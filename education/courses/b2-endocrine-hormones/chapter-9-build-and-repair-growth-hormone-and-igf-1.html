Build and Repair: Growth Hormone and IGF-1

A command hormone, a downstream effector, and the most reliable lever you already own: deep sleep.

60 min read 8 cited sources

Nothing dramatic happens in the room. A person falls asleep, drifts down through the lighter stages, and reaches deep slow-wave sleep maybe twenty minutes in. The lights are off. Nobody is lifting anything, eating anything, or trying anything. And right then, in that quiet, the pituitary fires off the single largest, most reproducible burst of growth hormone of the entire day.

That is the chapter in one image. The hormone most associated with building and repairing your body does most of its important work while you are unconscious. Not in the gym, not at the supplement counter, not in a clinic. In bed. By the time you finish this chapter you will understand why that is true, how growth hormone actually does its job (mostly through a second messenger called IGF-1), and how to separate what this system genuinely does from the inflated reputation it carries in popular culture.

The same command gland, doing a familiar thing

You already met the main character. Back in Chapter 2 we called the pituitary the master gland, the small structure at the base of the brain that takes orders from the hypothalamus and sends out command hormones to the rest of the body. Growth hormone (GH, also written hGH for human growth hormone) is one of those commands. It is made and released by cells in the anterior pituitary called somatotrophs.

Here is the structural pattern you should recognize immediately, because it rhymes with everything we have built so far. The hypothalamus runs growth hormone with two opposing signals: GHRH (growth hormone releasing hormone), which tells the pituitary to release, and somatostatin (SST), which tells it to hold back (Weltman et al., 2017; Al-Samerria & Radovick, 2021). One pedal pushes, one pedal brakes. The interplay of those two signals at the somatotroph is what produces growth hormone's most distinctive feature: it does not trickle out steadily. It comes in pulses.

That accelerator-and-brake setup is the same logic you saw with the thyroid axis and the stress axis. A releasing hormone, an inhibiting influence, and a command hormone that comes out in bursts rather than a constant stream. If that feels familiar, good. It should. We are not learning a new kind of machine here. We are learning a new application of a machine you already understand.

Think About It

Two hypothalamic signals control growth hormone release. One says "go," one says "stop." Can you name both, and say which produces a pulse when it dominates? If you can answer without scrolling up, the axis pattern has stuck.

Coaching translation

Growth hormone is not a tap that runs all day at a set rate. It is more like a sprinkler on a timer that fires in short bursts. The most important burst is at night. Keep that picture. Almost everything practical in this chapter flows from it.

Pulsatile release, and why night is the headline

If you sampled someone's blood every few minutes across a full day, growth hormone would look like a flat line interrupted by occasional spikes. Between pulses, levels are very low. During a pulse, they jump sharply and then fall again. Researchers use a technique called deconvolution analysis to pull these bursts apart and measure their frequency and amplitude (Weltman et al., 2017). Several things shape that pulse pattern: age, sex, body composition, nutrition, exercise, and sleep.

Of those, the one that dominates is sleep, and specifically deep sleep. The foundational work here belongs to Eve Van Cauter and colleagues, whose review of growth hormone secretion during sleep established a tight temporal coupling between growth hormone release and slow-wave sleep (SWS), the deepest non-REM stages (Van Cauter & Plat, 1996). Two findings from that body of work are worth memorizing:

First, the largest and most reproducible growth hormone pulse of the day occurs shortly after you fall asleep, coinciding with your first episode of slow-wave sleep. Second, in adult men roughly 70 percent of sleep-related growth hormone pulses coincide with slow-wave sleep (Van Cauter & Plat, 1996). This is not a loose correlation. The sleep-onset growth hormone surge is primarily driven by a burst of GHRH that lines up with the descent into deep sleep.

A later landmark study drove the point home across the lifespan. Using data from 149 healthy men aged 16 to 83, Van Cauter and colleagues showed that the decline in slow-wave sleep from young adulthood into midlife was paralleled by a major fall in growth hormone secretion, on the order of 372 micrograms per decade (Van Cauter et al., 2000). The striking part: independent of age, the amount of growth hormone a man secreted tracked with how much slow-wave sleep he got. The authors concluded plainly that strategies to enhance sleep quality could carry hormonal benefits.

If most of the day's growth hormone is released during the first deep-sleep episode, what happens to that surge in a person who repeatedly wakes during the first hour of the night? Predict the consequence before you read on. The first widget will let you test it.

Coaching translation

Here is where people get this wrong. They treat growth hormone as something you go out and "boost" with a product or a protocol. The data say the opposite. The most reliable, freely available, evidence-backed lever on your own growth hormone is the deep sleep you get in the first part of the night. That is not a soft wellness slogan. It is what the deconvolution data and the lifespan study actually show. Protect the first sleep cycle and you protect the biggest pulse.

The two-step trick: growth hormone makes IGF-1 do the work

Now the part that surprises most people. Growth hormone does a lot of its building and repairing without touching the target tissue directly. Instead, growth hormone travels to the liver and other tissues and tells them to make a second hormone: IGF-1, insulin-like growth factor 1. IGF-1 is then released into the blood and carries out a large share of growth hormone's growth and repair effects (Fernández-Garza et al., 2025; Al-Samerria & Radovick, 2021).

This is the command-to-effector pattern again, and you have seen it across this whole course. A command hormone does not always act on the final target itself. Sometimes it triggers production of a downstream molecule that does the on-the-ground work. Think of growth hormone as the foreman who never personally swings a hammer. He walks the site and issues an order, and the order is carried out by a crew the order itself recruits: IGF-1. If the crew never shows up, the foreman's instructions accomplish very little.

There is real history in the name. IGF-1 was originally called somatomedin, literally "the substance that mediates the soma," because researchers recognized it as the thing carrying out growth hormone's effects (Laron, 2001). In 1976 it was renamed insulin-like growth factor because of its striking structural resemblance to proinsulin. IGF-1 is a single 70-amino-acid peptide that folds in a way closely echoing the insulin molecule (Laron, 2001).

The insulin tie-back from Chapter 3

That resemblance is not a trivia note. It explains genuine overlap in function. Because IGF-1 looks so much like insulin, it can actually bind to the insulin receptor, though with lower affinity, and insulin can nudge IGF-1 receptors in return (Al-Samerria & Radovick, 2021; Laron, 2001). This is why the two hormones share some metabolic effects, particularly around tissue growth and glucose handling. When you studied insulin back in Chapter 3 as the storage-and-build signal, you were already learning part of the IGF-1 story without knowing it. The family resemblance is structural, and structure drives function.

Suppose a person releases a perfectly normal growth hormone pulse, but their liver cannot produce IGF-1. Would you expect normal growth and repair? Use the foreman analogy to reason it out before the next widget shows you the answer.

Coaching translation

When you hear "growth hormone builds muscle and repairs tissue," translate that mentally to "growth hormone tells the liver to make IGF-1, and IGF-1 does much of the building." The two-step matters because it means the effect depends on the whole chain working, not just on how much growth hormone you put out in a single pulse. More signal at the top does not automatically mean more result at the bottom.

What growth hormone genuinely does

Strip away the hype and the real roles are still substantial. Across the lifespan, the growth hormone and IGF-1 system supports tissue maintenance, body composition, and recovery. Clinical reviews describe growth hormone increasing lean body mass, reducing adiposity, promoting bone formation, and participating in the regulation of protein, lipid, and glucose metabolism (Fernández-Garza et al., 2025; Bartke, 2019). In plain terms: it leans toward building and maintaining muscle and bone, and toward releasing stored fat for use rather than parking it.

Notice that these are overview-level roles. This is a foundations course, and the honest summary is that growth hormone and IGF-1 are part of the background machinery of repair and maintenance, working alongside the other systems you have studied rather than acting as a standalone "build" button. The system matters most when it is chronically deficient or chronically excessive. In the normal middle range, day-to-day variation in your pulses is one input among many.

The body-composition piece, carefully

Growth hormone's reputation as a body-composition agent traces in large part to a single influential event. In 1990, a report describing reduced fat and increased lean mass in elderly men given growth hormone attracted enormous attention and effectively launched the idea of growth hormone as an "anti-aging" agent (Bartke, 2019). The headline was seductive: a hormone that declines with age, replaced, appearing to reverse some markers of aging.

The follow-up evidence was far more sober, which is exactly where the next section lives.

Honest about scope: the reputation versus the evidence

Let me be blunt, because this is where the popular story and the scientific record split hard. Growth hormone cannot live up to its reputation. The careful reviews are direct about it.

When you look past that 1990 report to well-controlled studies, the body-composition changes from growth hormone in healthy older adults are modest, and they come with meaningful adverse effects, including fluid retention, joint pain, insulin resistance, and others (Bartke, 2019; Fernández-Garza et al., 2025). Meta-analyses conclude that growth hormone cannot be recommended as an anti-aging therapy, and multiple major medical societies advise against growth hormone administration for anti-aging purposes in healthy older people (Bartke, 2019; Fernández-Garza et al., 2025).

Then comes the genuinely strange finding that should make anyone pause before treating "more growth hormone" as obviously good. In animal models, mice that are growth hormone deficient or growth hormone resistant, meaning they have less growth hormone signaling, show remarkable extension of lifespan (Bartke, 2019). More of this hormone is not a simple dial toward "younger and healthier." Biology rarely cooperates with that kind of single-lever thinking.

Think About It

The same hormone is marketed as anti-aging, yet reduced growth hormone signaling extends lifespan in mice and major societies advise against it for healthy older adults. What does this tell you about treating any single hormone as universally "good" or "bad"?

Coaching translation

Here is the myth, stated plainly so we can kill it: growth hormone is not a fountain of youth, and chasing higher growth hormone is not a health strategy. The popular version oversells a real but modest system and ignores both the side effects and the genuinely counterintuitive longevity data. This is an education chapter, not a sales pitch, so the takeaway is not "go get more." It is "understand what this system actually does and stop being impressed by the marketing." The thing you actually have control over, supported by real data, is your sleep.

A secondary layer: training and the exercise-induced pulse

Sleep is the headline lever. Exercise is a real but secondary one, and it is worth understanding without turning this chapter into a performance manual. Exercise produces a genuine growth hormone pulse, the exercise-induced growth hormone response (EIGR). Resistance training reliably triggers it, with load and frequency acting as key regulators, and endurance work shows an intensity threshold: the largest endurance response tends to require working above the lactate threshold for at least about ten minutes (Wideman et al., 2002).

But notice how the chapter's central lesson reappears even here. Wideman and colleagues point out that despite the exercise-induced growth hormone pulse, much of the stimulus for protein synthesis is attributed to IGF-1 rather than to growth hormone binding its receptor directly (Wideman et al., 2002). The two-step logic holds in the gym just as it holds at night. The command produces a pulse; the effector does much of the building.

For most people reading this, the practical point is simple. Training and good sleep quality both shape the growth hormone pulse pattern, and they tend to reinforce each other rather than compete. You do not need to engineer your workouts around a hormone you cannot measure. You need to train in a way you can sustain and recover from, and then sleep well enough to capture the night pulse. The trained-athlete details are a refinement on top of that, not a replacement for it.

Coaching translation

In practice, this means you do not have to pick between "sleep" and "training" as your growth hormone lever. They work together. But if you are only going to protect one, protect sleep, because the nightly slow-wave pulse is the largest and most consistent one you have, and because the exercise pulse still routes much of its benefit through the same IGF-1 effector anyway.

Key Takeaways

  • Growth hormone is a pituitary command hormone released in pulses, controlled by the opposing hypothalamic signals GHRH (go) and somatostatin (stop), the same accelerator-and-brake axis logic from earlier chapters.
  • The largest, most reproducible daily pulse occurs shortly after sleep onset, tied to the first episode of slow-wave sleep; in adult men roughly 70 percent of sleep-related GH pulses coincide with deep sleep (Van Cauter & Plat, 1996).
  • Growth hormone acts largely indirectly: it prompts the liver and other tissues to make IGF-1, the downstream effector that carries out much of the growth and repair, including much of the protein-synthesis stimulus.
  • IGF-1 was renamed in 1976 for its structural resemblance to proinsulin, which lets it bind the insulin receptor with low affinity and explains real overlap with insulin's actions, a direct tie-back to Chapter 3 (Laron, 2001).
  • Genuine roles include tissue maintenance, lean mass, bone formation, reduced adiposity, and recovery, but the anti-aging reputation is not supported: well-controlled studies show modest effects with meaningful side effects, and reduced GH signaling extends lifespan in mice (Bartke, 2019).
  • The single most reliable, freely available lever on your own growth hormone is deep sleep, especially in the first sleep cycle. Exercise is a real but secondary layer.
