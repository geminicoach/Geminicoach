The Sex Hormone Network: Testosterone, Estradiol, and the HPG Axis

Both sexes make both. The difference is amount and ratio, not presence.

60 min read 8 cited sources

Ask almost anyone on the street which hormone is male and which is female, and you will get the same confident answer: testosterone for men, estrogen for women. It is one of the most widely held beliefs in all of human biology. It is also wrong. Not partly wrong, not wrong in the details. Wrong at the level of the basic premise.

Every healthy man carries measurable estradiol in his blood and needs it for his bones, his brain, his metabolism, and his fertility. Every healthy woman makes and depends on testosterone. These are not trace contaminants that leaked in by accident. They are essential, load-bearing parts of normal physiology in both bodies. The sexes differ in the concentrations and the ratio of these hormones, not in whether they possess them. Once you absorb that single correction, an enormous amount of confusion about hormones, aging, body composition, and mood simply dissolves.

Why this chapter is a convergence point

This is where three threads you have already learned tie together into one knot. From Chapter 1, you know that steroid hormones are lipophilic molecules that slip through the cell membrane, bind a receptor, travel to the nucleus, and change which genes get transcribed. That is the slow, powerful timescale: effects measured in hours and days, not seconds, but effects that reshape tissue and behavior at a deep level. From Chapter 2, you know the template for an endocrine axis: the hypothalamus pulses a releasing signal, the pituitary relays its own messengers, a downstream gland produces the final hormone, and that final hormone feeds back to shut the whole thing down. And from Chapter 5, on the adrenal glands, you met DHEA, a steroid precursor sitting upstream in the biosynthesis tree.

The sex hormones are the best place in the entire course to see how these pieces interlock, because all three show up at once. The gonadal axis is just another instance of the Chapter 2 template. The sex hormones are steroids built from cholesterol, so they act exactly the way Chapter 1 described. And adrenal DHEA, from Chapter 5, feeds raw material into the same pool the gonads are drawing from. Nothing here is a new mechanism. It is the same machinery you already own, applied to a new set of players.

Here is the scope, stated plainly so nobody gets the wrong expectation. We are building the network, the axis, and the feedback. We are not covering the female menstrual cycle in any depth. That material belongs to a dedicated physiology course and deserves its own full treatment, not a rushed paragraph here. We also give no clinical, dosing, or prescribing guidance anywhere in this course. This is education, not medical advice. What you will walk away with is the architecture: how the system is wired, who the main hormones are, why both sexes need both, and where the adrenal precursors enter. That foundation is what makes the next chapter, on the body-wide roles of estradiol, land on solid ground.

Think About It

Before you read further, try to recall the Chapter 2 axis template from memory. What are the three tiers, and what does the final hormone do when its level gets high? If you can reconstruct that on a napkin, the HPG axis will feel like a re-skin of something you already know.

The HPG axis: same template, new gland

HPG stands for hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal. It is the gonadal axis, and it follows the now-familiar three-tier structure exactly.

Tier one: the hypothalamus and its pulse

At the top, a small population of neurons in the hypothalamus releases gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) into the local portal blood supply that feeds the anterior pituitary. The single most important fact about GnRH is that it is not released as a steady drip. It comes out in pulses. This pulsatility is not a quirk. It is the whole mechanism.

Herbison (2018), in the definitive review of what is now called the GnRH pulse generator, makes the point sharply: pulsatile GnRH secretion is essential for fertility in every mammal studied, and the pulse is not generated by the GnRH neurons themselves. It is driven by an upstream network of neurons in the arcuate nucleus that express kisspeptin, neurokinin B, and dynorphin, collectively nicknamed KNDy neurons. These cells act as the clock. They fire in coordinated bursts, and each burst tells the GnRH neurons to release a pulse. Disrupt the clock and you disrupt the entire axis downstream, no matter how healthy the lower tiers are.

The frequency of these pulses carries real information. Herbison documents that luteinizing hormone pulses, which track GnRH pulses closely, occur roughly every hour in follicular-phase females and every two to three hours in males. The frequency and amplitude of the pulse directly shape how much testosterone, estradiol, and progesterone end up circulating. This is a deep idea worth pausing on: the body encodes a command not just in the presence of a signal but in its rhythm. A constant, non-pulsing GnRH signal actually shuts the axis down rather than driving it harder, which is exactly backwards from what intuition predicts and exactly why certain clinical drugs that flood the system with steady GnRH-like signal are used to suppress, not stimulate.

Tier two: the pituitary and the gonadotropins

Each GnRH pulse hits the anterior pituitary and triggers the release of two messenger hormones, collectively called the gonadotropins: luteinizing hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH). These are protein hormones, not steroids, so they act fast and at the cell surface, consistent with what you learned about water-soluble messengers in Chapter 1. They travel through the general circulation to the gonads.

In broad strokes, LH drives the gonadal cells that manufacture sex steroids, and FSH supports the maturation of gametes (sperm and eggs) and works alongside LH to fine-tune steroid output. The two gonadotropins are the pituitary's way of converting the hypothalamic rhythm into a chemical instruction the gonads can read.

Tier three: the gonads and the final hormones

The gonads, the testes and the ovaries, are the steroid factories. Stimulated by LH and FSH, they produce the three final hormones this chapter is about: testosterone, estradiol, and progesterone. We will look at each of these in detail in a moment. For now, hold onto the structural point: the gonads are the third tier, the same position the thyroid held in the thyroid axis and the adrenal cortex held in the cortisol axis.

Closing the loop: feedback

The final hormones do not just go out and act on tissue. They also circulate back to the brain and the pituitary and report on their own levels. When testosterone and estradiol rise high enough, they suppress GnRH pulsing and gonadotropin release, which lowers their own production. When they fall, the brakes come off and the axis ramps back up. This is negative feedback, the same self-correcting loop you saw in every axis in Chapter 2. It is what keeps the system humming within a range instead of spiraling out of control.

The female cycle adds a famous and important wrinkle here, a window in which estradiol switches from suppressing the axis to briefly stimulating it, producing the surge that triggers ovulation. That positive-feedback episode is precisely the menstrual-cycle material we are deferring to the physiology course. For our purposes, the take-home is that the default mode of the HPG axis, the one shared by both sexes and operating most of the time, is negative feedback, exactly the template you already know.

Coaching translation

Here is what this means in real life. The HPG axis is not a tap you turn up by demanding more. It is a thermostat that reads its own output and self-corrects. That is why hammering one tier from outside, whether through extreme overtraining, severe underfueling, or chronic sleep deprivation, can drag the whole axis down: you are not adding to the system, you are disrupting the rhythm at the top. The rhythm is the message. Protect the clock, and you protect the output.

The three players, traced on the biosynthesis tree

Now let us meet the final hormones properly. The crucial thing to understand is that they are not three unrelated molecules. They are stops on a single assembly line, and that line begins with cholesterol. This is the same precursor-tree logic you saw in Chapter 5 with the adrenal steroids, because it is literally the same chemistry happening in a different room.

Miller and Auchus (2011), in the standard reference on human steroidogenesis, make an argument that should reshape how you think about all of this: steroidogenesis is best understood as a single process repeated with cell-type-specific variations across the adrenal, the gonads, and peripheral tissues. There is not a separate "adrenal pathway" and a separate "gonadal pathway." There is one pathway, and different tissues express different enzymes, so they stop the assembly line at different points and pour out different final products.

From cholesterol to the sex steroids

The line starts when cholesterol is delivered into the mitochondrion and the enzyme CYP11A1 (with the help of the transport protein StAR) cleaves its side chain to make pregnenolone. From pregnenolone, the pathway branches. A pivotal enzyme called CYP17A1 (also called P450c17) determines, by which of its activities it runs, whether a cell heads toward cortisol-type products or toward the sex steroids. Miller and Auchus emphasize that this qualitative regulation by P450c17 is what decides the steroid class a tissue produces. The gonads are equipped to run this enzyme in the direction that yields androgens and, from there, estrogens.

The simplified order of the sex-steroid branch goes like this. Pregnenolone leads, through several steps, to DHEA, then onward to androstenedione, then to testosterone. Testosterone is itself a finished, active hormone, but it is also a precursor. An enzyme called aromatase (CYP19A1) converts testosterone into estradiol. Read that sentence again, because it is the hinge of the entire chapter: estradiol is made from testosterone. The most potent estrogen is one enzymatic step downstream of the so-called male hormone. Progesterone sits on a parallel early branch off pregnenolone and serves both as an active hormone and as an intermediate toward other steroids.

Spend a second on what this architecture implies. Because estradiol is made from testosterone by aromatase, any tissue that expresses aromatase can produce its own estradiol locally, regardless of which sex it belongs to. Hammes and Levin (2019) document exactly this: aromatase converts testosterone to 17-beta-estradiol in both sexes across many tissues, and 17-beta-estradiol is the common form of estrogen in men and women alike. There is no separate male estrogen and female estrogen. It is the same molecule, E2, made by the same enzyme, in both bodies.

Think About It

If estradiol is made from testosterone by aromatase, what would you predict happens to a man's estradiol if his testosterone drops to near zero? And what would happen if you blocked aromatase entirely in a man with normal testosterone? Hold your answers; the next section tests them against real data.

Estradiol as the primary estrogen, in both sexes

Let us be precise about a word. "Estrogen" is a class, not a single molecule. The class includes estrone (E1), estradiol (E2), and estriol (E3). When this course says estradiol is the primary estrogen, we mean that 17-beta-estradiol (E2) is the most potent and physiologically dominant member of the class outside of pregnancy, and it is the form that matters most for the body-wide actions we will explore in the next chapter.

The claim that estradiol matters in males is not a hedge or a footnote. It is one of the best-documented findings in modern endocrinology. Cooke et al. (2017), in a landmark treatise in Physiological Reviews, lay out the evidence: estradiol is measurable in the blood of men, reaches high concentrations in the fluid of the rete testis, and acts through the estrogen receptors ESR1 and ESR2 and the membrane receptor GPER in numerous male tissues. Aromatase, the enzyme that makes estradiol, is expressed in the Leydig cells of the testis, in the seminiferous epithelium, and in the brain, adipose tissue, bone, and skin of males. Their conclusion is unambiguous: loss of ESR1 or of aromatase impairs male fertility, bone density, body composition, and cardiovascular function. Estrogen is physiologically essential in men.

Now check your predictions from the callout. A man whose testosterone drops to near zero also loses his estradiol, because the substrate for aromatase is gone, and he suffers estrogen-deficiency consequences in bone and metabolism even though we usually frame low testosterone as a "low T" problem. And a man with normal testosterone whose aromatase is blocked loses estradiol despite plenty of substrate, with the same downstream costs. Both natural experiments point the same way: men need estradiol, and they make it from their own testosterone.

Mauvais-Jarvis and Lindsey (2024) sharpen this into a principle that will matter for the next chapter. In males, testosterone's aromatization to estradiol is fundamental to bone health and to the prevention of visceral fat accumulation. And estradiol in males operates within a tight physiological window that requires the co-presence of physiological testosterone. In other words, it is not "more estradiol is better" or "less is better." It is a balance, and the balance depends on both hormones being present together in the right proportion.

And testosterone in females

The mirror image is equally true. Hammes and Levin (2019) review the actions of androgens in female bone, ovary, and metabolism. Testosterone is not a foreign substance in a woman's body; it is a normal, functional hormone with roles in bone, in the ovary itself, and in metabolic regulation, and it is also, of course, the precursor from which a woman's estradiol is built. Mauvais-Jarvis and Lindsey (2024) note that in females, estradiol is central to bone health, fat distribution, insulin sensitivity, and mitochondrial health, while testosterone also contributes. Both hormones, both sexes, doing real work.

Here's where people get this wrong

The myth says testosterone is masculine and estrogen is feminine, as if they were opposites on a seesaw. The biology says they are sequential: estradiol is built from testosterone, one enzyme step downstream, in everybody. They are not opponents. They are a supply chain. A man does not protect his masculinity by crushing his estradiol; he can wreck his bones and his metabolism doing it. The goal in both sexes is appropriate levels of both, in the appropriate ratio for that body.

DHEA and cholesterol as upstream precursors

We have been tracing the line from cholesterol forward. Now connect it explicitly back to Chapter 5, because the adrenal glands are not separate from this story. They feed it.

Recall that the adrenal cortex has a zone, the zona reticularis, dedicated largely to producing DHEA and its sulfated storage form, DHEAS. Lin et al. (2025), in a current comprehensive review, trace DHEA's biosynthesis from cholesterol via the same enzymes, CYP11A1 and CYP17A1, and describe DHEA as a precursor to both androgens and estrogens in peripheral tissues. The adrenal pumps DHEA into the circulation, and tissues throughout the body take it up and finish the synthesis locally, converting it into active androgens and estrogens on site.

This local, on-site manufacturing has a name: intracrinology. The concept was established by Labrie and colleagues (2003), who showed that DHEA from the adrenals is the dominant precursor for tissue-level synthesis of sex steroids in peripheral tissues. The numbers they report are striking. By their analysis, DHEA metabolism provides roughly 75 percent of estrogens in postmenopausal women and about 40 percent of androgens in men. In other words, a large fraction of the sex steroid action happening in your tissues is not coming straight from the gonads as finished product. It is being assembled in the tissue itself from adrenal raw material.

This is why the gonadal axis cannot be drawn in isolation. The gonads are the headline producers, the LH-driven factories of the HPG axis. But there is a second, parallel supply chain running from the adrenal cortex: DHEA flowing out, tissues finishing the build. Both feed the same pool of circulating and locally active sex steroids. When you wired DHEA into the axis builder above, that connector was not decoration. It is a real and quantitatively significant input.

Lin et al. (2025) add a clinically meaningful detail: DHEA levels decline steadily with age, a phenomenon called adrenopause. As that adrenal precursor supply falls, the raw material available for tissue-level sex steroid synthesis falls with it. This matters most in life stages and tissues where the gonadal supply is also low, because then the adrenal-derived pool is doing a larger share of the work. That is exactly Labrie's point about postmenopausal women, where the ovarian output has dropped and intracrine synthesis from adrenal DHEA becomes the dominant source of estrogen.

Coaching translation

In practice, this means the sex hormones are not produced by a single faucet you can point to. They come from a gonadal faucet (the HPG axis) and an adrenal-fed local synthesis system running in the background of nearly every tissue. That is one reason hormone status is more robust than people fear in some respects and more interconnected than they expect in others. It also explains why chronic stress, which loads the adrenal system, and aging, which lowers DHEA, both ripple into sex-hormone physiology. Different rooms, one building.

The mechanism, revisited: why these act slowly and deeply

One last thread to close, back to Chapter 1. Every hormone in this chapter, testosterone, estradiol, progesterone, DHEA, is a steroid built on the cholesterol backbone, and steroids share a signature mechanism. They are lipophilic, so they cross the cell membrane rather than knocking on a surface receptor. Inside the cell, as reviewed in the canonical-pathway literature (Frontiers in Endocrinology, 2018), a steroid binds its nuclear receptor, the receptor changes shape and moves into the nucleus, and the hormone-receptor complex docks onto specific DNA response elements to turn genes on or off.

That is the genomic pathway, and it is why these hormones operate on the slow, powerful timescale: changing which proteins a cell builds takes hours to days, but the result is durable remodeling of the tissue, not a momentary blip. The same review notes that steroids also have faster, non-genomic actions through membrane-associated receptors, and that the two modes crosstalk. The membrane receptor GPER for estradiol, mentioned by Cooke et al. (2017), is one example. But the headline mechanism, the one that explains the deep, body-wide, slow-building effects of estradiol you will study next chapter, is genomic action at the level of the gene.

So the full picture stands assembled. A pulse generator in the hypothalamus sets the rhythm. The pituitary translates the rhythm into LH and FSH. The gonads build testosterone, aromatize some of it to estradiol, and make progesterone, all from cholesterol along a single branching pathway. The adrenal cortex feeds DHEA into the same pool, and peripheral tissues finish the synthesis locally. The final hormones feed back to the brain to keep the loop in range, and they act on tissue by entering cells and rewriting gene expression on the slow, powerful timescale. Three earlier chapters, one integrated system.

Key Takeaways

  • The HPG axis is the Chapter 2 template applied to the gonads: hypothalamus (pulsatile GnRH) to pituitary (LH and FSH) to gonads (testosterone, estradiol, progesterone), with the final hormones feeding back by negative feedback.
  • GnRH must be released in pulses, driven by upstream arcuate kisspeptin (KNDy) neurons; pulse frequency and amplitude shape circulating sex steroid levels (Herbison, 2018).
  • Testosterone, estradiol, and progesterone are not separate molecules but stops on one cholesterol-derived assembly line; CYP17A1 routes a cell toward sex steroids, and aromatase (CYP19A1) converts testosterone into estradiol (Miller & Auchus, 2011).
  • Estradiol (17-beta-estradiol, E2) is the primary, most potent estrogen and is the same molecule in both sexes; it is physiologically essential in males for bone, brain, metabolism, and fertility (Cooke et al., 2017; Hammes & Levin, 2019).
  • Both sexes make and require both testosterone and estradiol; the difference is concentration and ratio, not presence, and estradiol in males operates within a tight window requiring physiological testosterone (Mauvais-Jarvis & Lindsey, 2024).
  • Adrenal DHEA is a major upstream precursor feeding tissue-level (intracrine) sex steroid synthesis, supplying roughly 75 percent of estrogens in postmenopausal women and about 40 percent of androgens in men, and it declines with age (Labrie et al., 2003; Lin et al., 2025).
  • As steroids, all of these act mainly through the genomic nuclear-receptor pathway on the slow, powerful timescale from Chapter 1 (Frontiers in Endocrinology, 2018).
