Hunger's Two Voices: Leptin and Ghrelin

The short-term hunger alarm and the long-term fuel gauge, and why appetite is physiology, not character.

60 min read 8 cited sources

It is 12:45 in the afternoon and your stomach growls on cue. You did not decide to feel hungry. You did not summon it through a failure of discipline. A peptide hormone secreted mostly by the cells lining your stomach climbed steadily over the last hour, crossed into your bloodstream, reached a specific cluster of neurons at the base of your brain, and flipped a switch labeled "go find food." That hormone is ghrelin. Forty minutes after you eat, it will fall, and the urgency will quietly dissolve, not because you talked yourself out of it, but because the chemistry changed.

Now zoom out from that single afternoon to the slow background hum of your entire energy reserve. A second hormone, leptin, leaks out of your fat cells in rough proportion to how much fat you are carrying, and it whispers a much slower message to the same brain region: "we have plenty in storage" or "reserves are getting thin." These are the two voices of hunger. One is a stopwatch. The other is a fuel gauge. This chapter is about how they argue, how the brain listens, and what happens when the listening breaks down.

Two timescales, one question

Every regulated system in this course has been built around the same architecture, and by now you should be able to predict the shape of it before I draw it. There is a quantity the body wants to defend, a sensor that reads the current value, and an opposing pair of signals that push the value back toward target whenever it drifts. You met this with blood glucose in Chapter 3: insulin pushes glucose down into storage, glucagon pulls it back up out of storage, and between them they hold a setpoint. Appetite runs on the same logic, with one important twist. The "quantity being defended" is not a single number you can measure with a finger-prick. It is energy balance itself, and it has to be defended across two completely different timescales at once.

On the scale of hours, the question is "should I start a meal right now?" That is a fast, twitchy, meal-to-meal decision, and it belongs to ghrelin. On the scale of weeks and months, the question is "do I have enough stored fuel to keep this whole organism running, reproducing, and staying warm?" That is a slow, smoothed, integrated decision, and it belongs to leptin. The genius of the system is that one small region of the brain, the hypothalamus, reads both signals at once and produces a single felt experience we call hunger (Yu & Kim, 2012).

Here is the framing that anchors the whole chapter, and I want you to sit with it before we get into mechanism: appetite is a regulated physiological output. It is not a referendum on your willpower. The same way your body temperature is held near 37 degrees by machinery you do not consciously run, your drive to eat is produced by hormones doing their job. This is not an excuse. It is the opposite of an excuse. It is the reason that understanding the machinery gives you actual leverage, where moralizing never could.

Ghrelin: the voice that says "now"

Ghrelin holds a distinction worth pausing on. Among all the circulating hormones that influence how much you eat, it is the only one known to increase appetite. Everything else in the system, leptin, insulin, the gut hormones released after a meal, pushes toward fullness. Ghrelin alone pushes toward eating. Cummings (2006), in the review that most cleanly laid out its role, calls it the only known orexigenic circulating hormone, orexigenic meaning literally appetite-generating. That makes it special. The "stop eating" side of the ledger is crowded with redundant signals. The "start eating" side has essentially one loud voice, and ghrelin is it.

It is produced mainly by cells in the stomach lining, and its behavior across a day is strikingly clean. Ghrelin rises in the one to two hours before each habitual meal, peaks right around the time you would normally eat, and then drops sharply once food arrives in the gut (Cummings, 2006). This is the pre-prandial surge, pre-prandial just meaning "before the meal." If you eat lunch at noon every day for a month, your ghrelin will start climbing around 11 a.m. whether or not anyone rings a bell, because the system has learned the schedule. That detail matters enormously for the practical advice later: ghrelin is partly trained by your habits.

What actually shuts ghrelin off

The drop after eating is not instant and it is not driven simply by your stomach stretching. The strongest suppressors of ghrelin are the nutrients themselves arriving in the small intestine, with carbohydrate and protein suppressing it more durably than fat for a given number of calories. This is why a meal of mostly fat can leave you hungry again surprisingly soon, and why protein has a reputation for "keeping you full" that is grounded in real endocrinology rather than diet folklore. The signal that says "now" goes quiet because the body detects real fuel coming in, not because the tank is mechanically full.

Coaching translation

Ghrelin is your meal-timer, and it is partly a creature of habit. If you eat at chaotic, unpredictable times, you get chaotic, unpredictable hunger surges. If you eat at roughly consistent times, ghrelin learns the schedule and stops ambushing you at random. "Eat on a regular rhythm" is not discipline advice. It is signal-shaping advice. In practice, this means a predictable meal pattern often reduces the sense of hunger coming out of nowhere.

Leptin: the voice that says "how much we have"

If ghrelin is the stopwatch, leptin is the fuel gauge welded to the dashboard. It is secreted by adipocytes, your fat cells, and the more fat mass you carry, the more leptin circulates. That proportionality is the entire point. Leptin is the brain's way of knowing, without direct access, how much energy is banked in storage (Klok et al., 2006). When fat stores are ample, leptin is high and the hypothalamus reads "reserves full, you can afford to suppress appetite and spend energy freely." When fat stores fall, during prolonged undereating or extended energy deficit, leptin drops, and the brain reads "reserves low, conserve energy and seek food."

Notice the timescale. Your fat mass does not swing from meal to meal. It changes over weeks and months. So leptin is a deliberately slow, smoothed signal. It is not trying to time your lunch. It is trying to defend your long-term survival against starvation. This is why leptin's most dramatic effects show up not in the well-fed state but in deficit. When you lose a meaningful amount of fat, leptin falls, and the falling leptin is one of the strongest drivers of the increased hunger and the slowed metabolism that make weight loss so stubborn to maintain. The system is far more aggressive about defending against loss than about preventing gain, an asymmetry that makes brutal sense if you evolved in an environment where famine killed and surplus rarely did.

Where the signals actually land

Both hormones converge on the arcuate nucleus of the hypothalamus, a region positioned to sample the blood with unusual directness. There, two opposing neuron populations do the integrating. One set, the NPY/AgRP neurons, drives appetite up. The other set, the POMC/CART neurons, drives appetite down (Yu & Kim, 2012). Leptin and insulin both inhibit the appetite-promoting NPY/AgRP neurons and stimulate the appetite-suppressing POMC neurons. Ghrelin does the reverse, firing up the NPY/AgRP "eat now" population. So the same neural circuit hears all the voices and resolves them into a single output. That output is what you feel as hunger, and it then radiates out to reward and motivation circuits in the limbic system that make food look more or less appealing (Yu & Kim, 2012).

Two people weigh the same and have eaten the same lunch at the same time. One has noticeably more body fat. Which one likely has higher circulating leptin right now, and does that mean they feel less hungry? Hold that question. The answer is where popular understanding falls apart.

The setpoint, revisited on purpose

I told you in Chapter 3 that the insulin and glucagon pairing was not a one-off fact to memorize but the first clean instance of a general pattern. Here is the second instance, and I want you to feel the symmetry, because that recognition is the whole reason this chapter sits where it does in the course.

Insulin and glucagon defend a glucose setpoint: one lowers, one raises, opposed forces holding a target. Leptin and ghrelin defend an energy-balance setpoint: leptin signals surplus and suppresses intake, ghrelin signals the moment to refuel and promotes intake. Opposed forces, holding a target. The variables differ. The architecture is identical. Once you can see one regulated system as an instance of "sensor plus opposing pair plus defended setpoint," you can see all of them, and you stop being surprised by the next one. That transfer is more valuable than any single hormone fact.

There is one honest complication worth stating plainly. The energy-balance setpoint is not a fixed number stamped at birth, and it can drift, especially upward, over a lifetime. The body defends wherever the setpoint currently sits much more fiercely on the downside than the upside. This is part of why losing fat and keeping it off is hard in a way that is not explained by motivation. The defended target itself fights back. That is not a moral failing. It is a setpoint doing exactly what setpoints do.

Leptin resistance: where popular understanding breaks

Now we get to the nuance that nearly everyone gets backwards. Logic seems to say: more body fat means more leptin, and leptin suppresses appetite, so people with more fat should feel less hungry and naturally eat less. The system should be self-correcting. Gain fat, leptin rises, appetite falls, problem solved. If that were true, obesity would be nearly impossible.

It is not true, and the reason is leptin resistance. In obesity, circulating leptin is indeed high, often very high, a state called hyperleptinemia. But the brain's response to that leptin is blunted. The signal is shouting, and the receiver has turned down the volume. Klok et al. (2006) describe obese patients explicitly as leptin-resistant despite elevated leptin. Obradovic et al. (2021) characterize the consequence bluntly as reduced satiety and over-consumption of nutrients despite high circulating leptin levels. The fuel gauge is reading "full," and the dashboard light that should respond is disconnected.

If this sounds familiar, it should

Hold leptin resistance up next to insulin resistance from Chapter 3 and the two are nearly the same shape. In insulin resistance: lots of insulin in the blood, but tissues respond weakly, so the pancreas pumps out even more to get the same effect, and the high insulin itself becomes part of the problem. In leptin resistance: lots of leptin in the blood, but the hypothalamus responds weakly, so the satiety message that should say "enough" stops landing. More hormone, less listening, and a louder signal needed to accomplish what a quiet one used to. That is the universal signature of a resistance state, and you now have two clean examples of it. This is the second consolidation point of the chapter: resistance is a pattern, not a peculiarity of one gland.

What "the volume is turned down" means at the cell

For those who want the mechanism, the molecular story is well mapped. Leptin acts through its receptor, LRb, which signals largely via the JAK2/STAT3 pathway to change gene expression in arcuate neurons. In obese states, several brakes get applied to that pathway. SOCS-3, a feedback inhibitor, is upregulated and dampens LRb signaling. Phosphorylation at the Tyr985 site recruits further inhibition. Together these attenuate the leptin signal inside the cell even when leptin outside the cell is abundant (Myers et al., 2008). The arcuate nucleus, the very integration hub we described earlier, is a key site where this attenuation happens. So "the brain stops listening" is not a metaphor for weak character. It is a describable set of intracellular brakes on a specific signaling cascade.

Here is where people get this wrong

People assume a heavier person with high leptin must "just not feel hungry" and therefore overeating proves a lack of discipline. The endocrinology says closer to the opposite: the satiety signal is loud but unheard, so the brain can perceive a state of relative need even in the presence of ample stores. The drive is real. The signal is muffled. Calling that a character flaw is both unkind and factually wrong.

Cause, effect, or both?

Honesty requires one more layer, because the field itself is careful here. It is tempting to declare leptin resistance the cause of obesity, but Myers et al. (2010) explicitly warn against assuming that direction. Elevated leptin with reduced signaling is reliably observed in obesity, and it is widely interpreted as leptin resistance, but distinguishing whether the attenuated signaling drives the weight gain or largely results from the obese state is genuinely difficult. The most defensible reading is that leptin resistance is at minimum a powerful contributor to the maintenance of a higher fat mass, helping defend an elevated setpoint, whether or not it lit the fuse. As a coach or an educated learner, you do not need to resolve that debate. You need to respect it, and avoid the overconfident "leaky" claims that fill the wellness internet.

Hunger, sleep, and stress: the levers you actually control

So far the picture might feel a little fatalistic. Setpoints defend themselves, resistance muffles the off-switch, the system is rigged toward eating. But there are real, modifiable inputs that move ghrelin and leptin in your favor, and the cleanest of them is sleep. This is also a preview of the lifestyle chapter coming later, so treat it as a first look at a theme we will develop.

The landmark experiment here is Spiegel et al. (2004), a tightly controlled randomized crossover study in healthy young men. The design is what makes it powerful: same people, two conditions, caloric intake and physical activity held constant, only sleep changed. Two nights of sleep restricted to 4 hours, compared with nights of 10 hours, produced a coherent and unwelcome pattern. Leptin fell by about 18 percent. Ghrelin rose by about 28 percent. And subjective hunger climbed by about 24 percent, with the sharpest cravings for calorie-dense, carbohydrate-rich food. Read that combination again. The "you have enough" signal got quieter and the "go eat now" signal got louder, at the same time, from nothing but lost sleep.

Because that was a small mechanistic study, you want to know whether it holds in the real world at scale. It does. Taheri et al. (2004) examined 1,024 participants in the Wisconsin Sleep Cohort and found that habitual short sleep duration was associated with reduced leptin, elevated ghrelin, and higher BMI in a general population. The experimental study tells you the direction and the mechanism. The epidemiological study tells you it shows up across ordinary people living ordinary lives. That is exactly the pairing of evidence you should look for before you trust a claim: a controlled trial that establishes causation in a small sample, and a population study that confirms relevance at scale.

Stress on the same lever

Stress pushes in a similar direction, partly through overlapping pathways and partly through the behavioral knock-on effects of elevated cortisol and disrupted sleep. Chronic stress tends to nudge appetite hormones and reward circuitry toward seeking energy-dense food, which is why high-stress periods so often coincide with eating that feels out of proportion to actual energy need. We will treat the stress system properly in its own context, but the headline for now is that stress and short sleep are not separate problems. They frequently travel together, and they pull the same appetite levers in the same unhelpful direction.

Coaching translation

If someone is fighting relentless hunger and reaching for the most calorie-dense food they can find, the first question is not "where is your willpower." It is "how are you sleeping." A run of short nights measurably raises ghrelin and lowers leptin, which is exactly the recipe for feeling hungrier and craving denser food. In practice, this means that protecting sleep is appetite management, not a separate wellness nicety. You are not being weak at night. Your hormones were tilted hours earlier.

The other direction: energy availability and the lean case

We have spent most of this chapter on surplus and resistance, because that is where most learners and most coaching questions live. But the leptin system reveals its deeper purpose at the other end, in states of low energy availability, and this connects directly back to the energy theme from the thyroid chapter.

Remember that leptin is proportional to fat mass, and that the system defends much harder against loss than gain. In individuals who are lean, training hard, or chronically under-eating relative to their expenditure, fat mass and energy availability drop, and leptin falls with them. Falling leptin is read by the hypothalamus as a genuine energy emergency, and the downstream response is broad: increased hunger, but also down-regulation of energy-expensive functions the body deems non-essential under famine conditions. This is part of how the thyroid axis and reproductive function can be suppressed when energy availability stays low for a long time. Leptin is, in effect, one of the body's permission signals: when it is adequate, the body feels it can afford to spend energy on metabolism, heat, and reproduction. When it falls too far, those systems get throttled.

For the general learner this matters because it dismantles a tidy myth: that less body fat is always and only better. The leptin system treats very low energy availability as a threat to be defended against, and it will fight you with hunger and with metabolic slowing. For coaches working with people who train hard or eat sparingly, low leptin is a flag worth recognizing, not a trophy. We keep food unmoralized here for the same reason we keep body fat unmoralized: these are signals and setpoints, not verdicts on virtue.

Think About It

You now have leptin moving in two opposite real-world situations: high but unheard in obesity, and low and very much heard in chronic energy deficit. In which of these does the leptin signal actually accomplish its evolved job of defending energy stores? What does that tell you about which direction the system was built to fight hardest?

A worked example: reading a real morning

Let us put the whole system together on one ordinary person across one ordinary morning, so the abstractions become a story you can trace.

It is 7 a.m. and our subject slept four hours after a stressful week. Already the deck is stacked: the short sleep has pushed leptin down and ghrelin up relative to a rested baseline (Spiegel et al., 2004). They skip breakfast because they "are not really a breakfast person," which means no nutrient arrival to suppress ghrelin, so the pre-prandial ghrelin surge that would have come before a habitual breakfast either gets blunted or, more often, the hormone simply keeps climbing toward the next eating opportunity. By 11 a.m., ghrelin is high, leptin is low, and the integrated signal at the arcuate nucleus is reading "find dense food, now." The reward circuitry obliges by making a pastry look spectacular (Yu & Kim, 2012).

None of that sequence required a moral failing. It required two short nights, a skipped meal, and a stress load, all of which moved real hormones in the documented directions. Now run the counterfactual. Same person, after a normal night of sleep, with a protein-containing breakfast on a consistent schedule. Ghrelin gets durably suppressed by the nutrients (especially the protein), leptin sits at its rested baseline rather than a sleep-deprived trough, and the 11 a.m. signal is far quieter. Same body, same willpower, completely different felt hunger, because the inputs to a regulated physiological system were different. That is the entire thesis of the chapter in one comparison.

Sleep curtailment in healthy young men is associated with decreased leptin levels, elevated ghrelin levels, and increased hunger and appetite.

What this does and does not let you conclude

Be careful at the edges, because this is where well-meaning explanations overreach. The appetite hormones explain drives and signals. They do not, by themselves, dictate every bite. The hypothalamic system is integrated with conscious decision-making, food environment, habit, and reward, and people do successfully override hunger signals all the time. The point of understanding the hormones is not to declare behavior helpless. It is to locate effort where it actually pays: shaping the inputs, sleep, meal rhythm, protein, stress, that set the baseline difficulty of the day, rather than relying on raw resistance against a tilted signal.

And keep the cause-and-effect humility from the resistance discussion. We can say leptin resistance is real and reliably observed in obesity (Klok et al., 2006; Obradovic et al., 2021). We should not casually claim it is the sole cause of weight gain, because the field itself has not settled that (Myers et al., 2010). Good education holds both the strong claim and the honest uncertainty at once. That is the register you want as a credible coach: confident about the mechanism, careful about the causal story.

Key Takeaways

  • Appetite is governed by an opposing pair on two timescales: ghrelin is the short-term, meal-initiating hunger signal that rises before meals and falls after eating; leptin is the long-term energy-status signal released in proportion to fat mass.
  • This is the same setpoint-and-balance architecture you met with insulin and glucagon. The variables differ, the logic is identical, and recognizing the pattern is the point.
  • Leptin resistance is the central nuance: in obesity, leptin is high but the brain's response is muffled, so the satiety signal stops landing. It mirrors insulin resistance almost exactly: more hormone, less listening, louder signal needed.
  • The mechanism is real and cellular (SOCS-3, Tyr985 attenuation of LRb/STAT3 signaling in the arcuate nucleus), not a metaphor for weak willpower.
  • Short sleep measurably lowers leptin and raises ghrelin and hunger, shown in a controlled crossover study and confirmed in a 1,024-person cohort. Stress pulls the same levers.
  • At the other extreme, low energy availability drops leptin and triggers defense of stores, linking appetite to thyroid and reproductive throttling. Less fat is not always better.
  • Appetite is regulated physiology, not virtue. Understanding the machinery tells you where effort actually pays: shaping the inputs, not battling a tilted signal.
Looking Ahead

You now hold two clean instances of the setpoint-and-resistance pattern.
