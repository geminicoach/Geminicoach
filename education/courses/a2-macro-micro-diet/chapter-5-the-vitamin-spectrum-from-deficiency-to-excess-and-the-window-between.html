The Vitamin Spectrum: From Deficiency to Excess and the Window Between

Why a diet can pass every macro check and still fail a person quietly, one month at a time.

35 min read 11 cited sources

Imagine two clients with identical food logs on paper. Same calories, same protein, same carbs and fats, hit to the gram. One feels fine. The other, six months in, is dragging through afternoons, bruising oddly, and noticing their gums bleed when they brush. Nothing in the macro math explains it. The macro math was never going to explain it, because the problem lives one layer down, in a part of the diet that moves on a different clock entirely.

Macronutrients give you feedback fast. Eat too little protein for a week and you feel it. Micronutrients are patient. A vitamin gap rarely announces itself today, or this week. It accumulates in the background, below the threshold of daily awareness, and then surfaces as something vague and easy to misattribute: fatigue, mood, skin, slow recovery. This chapter is about learning to see that slow layer clearly, so you can spot when a diet pattern is structurally set up to come up short long before the person feels it.

What Vitamins Actually Do (And What They Do Not)

Start with the most common misunderstanding, because clearing it up changes how you think about everything after it. People talk about vitamins as if they were a kind of fuel, something that "gives you energy." They do not. A vitamin carries no usable energy of its own. Protein, carbohydrate, and fat are the energy-yielding nutrients, the ones measured in calories. Vitamins contribute zero calories. If a supplement label promises "energy" from a B-complex, the honest version of that claim is narrow: B vitamins help your body release energy that is already locked in the food you ate. They are not adding energy to the system. They are part of the machinery that unlocks it.

Here is the mechanism, and it is the one to hold. Most vitamins work as coenzymes and cofactors: small helper molecules that enzymes need in order to function. An enzyme is a protein that runs a specific chemical reaction in the body, and many enzymes are useless on their own. They need a partner molecule that sits in the active site and makes the chemistry possible. That partner is often a vitamin, or a molecule the body builds out of a vitamin. Take the vitamin away and the enzyme stalls, and every downstream process that depended on that reaction slows or stops.

The B vitamins are the clearest illustration, because nearly all of them are converted into coenzymes that drive metabolism. Thiamin (B1) becomes a coenzyme central to pulling energy out of carbohydrate. Riboflavin (B2) and niacin (B3) become the electron-carrying coenzymes (FAD and NAD) that the energy-producing pathways run on continuously. Pantothenic acid (B5) becomes coenzyme A, which sits at the hub of fat and carbohydrate metabolism. Vitamin B6, in its active form pyridoxal 5'-phosphate, is a coenzyme for more than 150 distinct enzymatic reactions, most of them in the handling of amino acids (Ueland et al., 2016). Folate (B9) and B12 run the one-carbon reactions that build DNA and recycle key molecules. None of these vitamins is burned for fuel. Each is a reusable tool that lets a reaction happen.

Mechanism to real life

Think of an enzyme as a worker and the coenzyme vitamin as a specialized tool that worker needs to do one specific job. The worker can show up, but without the tool, nothing gets built. This is why a deficiency does not just cause one tidy symptom. When a coenzyme runs short, every reaction that needed it slows at once, which is exactly why vitamin deficiencies tend to show up as broad, vague, multi-system complaints (fatigue, poor recovery, mood, skin) rather than one clean signal. In practice, this means: when you take a vitamin in, you are not topping up an energy tank. You are restocking the tools the body uses to run its own chemistry. That distinction is the reason "more is better" fails, because once the toolbox is full, extra tools do not speed anything up. They just pile up, and for some vitamins, piling up is the problem.

Two fat-soluble vitamins break the coenzyme mold and are worth naming, because they show the category is broader than "metabolic helper." Vitamin A is essential for vision (it is part of the light-sensing pigment in the retina) and for regulating which genes are switched on in growth and immune cells. Vitamin D, once activated, behaves more like a hormone than a classic vitamin: it binds a receptor inside cells and controls the machinery of calcium absorption and bone handling. Vitamin K is a cofactor for the enzymes that finish blood-clotting proteins. So the unifying idea is not "every vitamin is a coenzyme." It is that vitamins are regulators and enablers: tiny amounts that make essential machinery run. You need them in milligram or microgram quantities, not the gram quantities of the macronutrients, and that tiny scale is part of why deficiency and excess are both surprisingly easy to reach.

One Distinction That Organizes Everything

There are thirteen vitamins, and you could try to memorize them as a list of thirteen unrelated facts. Don't. There is one split that does most of the explanatory work, and once you have it, almost everything else follows: vitamins are either fat-soluble or water-soluble.

Four are fat-soluble: vitamins A, D, E, and K. The other nine are water-soluble: vitamin C and the eight B-complex vitamins (B1 thiamin, B2 riboflavin, B3 niacin, B5 pantothenic acid, B6 pyridoxine, B7 biotin, B9 folate, and B12 cobalamin). That is the whole roster. The useful move is not the labels but what the labels predict.

Solubility is not a chemistry-class footnote here. It governs four things that matter directly to anyone steering a diet: how the vitamin gets absorbed, whether the body can store it, how fast a deficiency develops when intake stops, and whether you can overdose on it. Get the solubility right and you can reason your way to the rest without a chart.

Why "soluble in what" decides "stored or not"

Your body is mostly water. Blood, the inside of cells, the fluid between them: aqueous environments. A water-soluble vitamin dissolves into that environment freely, circulates, does its job, and when there is more than the body needs at that moment, the kidneys filter the excess into urine and it leaves. There is no good place to stash a surplus of something that dissolves in the very fluid your kidneys are constantly cleaning. So water-soluble vitamins largely operate on a flow-through basis: in, used, out. The body keeps small working pools, but it does not bank them in any meaningful quantity (with one important exception we will get to, B12).

Fat-soluble vitamins are the opposite. They do not dissolve in water, which means the kidneys cannot easily flush them. Instead they dissolve in fat, and the body has a great deal of fat: adipose tissue, cell membranes, and the liver, which is a major storage depot. So when you take in more fat-soluble vitamin than you need today, it does not wash out. It gets tucked away in fat stores and the liver, held in reserve (Lykstad & Sharma, 2022). This is genuinely useful. You can go weeks or months without dietary vitamin A or D and draw down the reserve. But the same property that protects you against short-term shortfall is exactly what makes excess dangerous: what accumulates can keep accumulating.

Think About It

Before reading on: if a vitamin washes out in urine when you take too much, what does that predict about (a) how often you need to eat it and (b) how easy it is to overdose on it from food? Hold your answer. We will check it in a moment.

Absorption: The Fat-Soluble Vitamins Need a Ride

Here is the part that reconnects directly to Chapter 4, and it is one of the quiet reasons a too-low-fat diet costs you. Water-soluble vitamins are relatively easy to absorb. They dissolve in the watery contents of your gut and are taken up without much ceremony.

Fat-soluble vitamins are not so simple. Because they do not dissolve in water, they cannot just float across the gut lining. They have to be packaged. When you eat fat, your gallbladder releases bile salts, which emulsify that fat into tiny droplets called micelles. The fat-soluble vitamins dissolve into those micelles and ride along. From there they are absorbed into intestinal cells, repackaged into transport particles called chylomicrons, and sent into the lymphatic system rather than straight into the bloodstream (Lykstad & Sharma, 2022). It is a multi-step process, and the entry ticket is dietary fat.

This is not a vague "fat helps." It is mechanical. No fat in the meal means little bile release, means poor micelle formation, means fat-soluble vitamins pass through largely unabsorbed. Goncalves and colleagues (2015) mapped this experimentally and showed something even more specific: the four fat-soluble vitamins are absorbed at different sites along the intestine (vitamin A more proximally, vitamin D in the middle, vitamins E and K further down), they share cholesterol transporters rather than diffusing passively, and they actually compete with one another for uptake. Absorption is an active, contested, fat-dependent process, not a passive trickle.

Coaching translation

This is where good intentions backfire. Someone eats a big salad of leafy greens and orange vegetables, dressing on the side because dressing is "extra calories," and assumes they are loading up on vitamins A, K, and E. But those vitamins need fat to be absorbed. Dry salad plus fat-free everything plus an aggressively low-fat diet means a meaningful chunk of those fat-soluble vitamins never makes it in. In practice, this means: put fat with your vegetables. Olive oil, avocado, nuts, full dressing. It is not indulgence, it is the absorption mechanism.

And this is why malabsorption matters clinically

Because the fat-soluble vitamins depend on the whole fat-handling apparatus, anything that disrupts fat digestion or absorption disrupts them too. Andrès and colleagues (2024) emphasize that conditions involving fat malabsorption (certain digestive tract disorders, problems with bile flow, surgeries that alter the gut) can produce fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies even when dietary intake looks adequate on paper. That is firmly in clinical territory, and we name it here only so you understand the boundary: when the issue is the plumbing rather than the menu, that is a clinician's problem, not a coach's.

The Dose-Response Spectrum

Now we get to the central mental model of this chapter, and it is the one to keep. Think of any vitamin as living on a single axis of intake, from too little on the left to too much on the right. On that axis there are three zones.

On the far left is deficiency: intake is too low to support function, and over time something breaks. In the middle is a broad region of sufficiency, the healthy window where the body has what it needs and any modest surplus is handled without trouble. On the far right is toxicity: intake is so high that the vitamin itself causes harm. Both ends are bad. The whole game of micronutrition is staying in the window.

This reframes a stubborn myth. People treat vitamins as unconditionally good, where more is automatically better and a supplement is automatically a safety net. That is dose-blind thinking. A vitamin is a tool with a working range. Below the range it fails you; above it, it can hurt you. "More" is not a direction, it is a position on a curve, and the curve turns down at both ends.

Here is the crucial asymmetry, and it falls right out of the solubility split:

  • Water-soluble vitamins have a narrow risk of toxicity because surplus washes out. Their main hazard is on the left side of the axis: deficiency, and it can develop relatively fast because there is little stored buffer.
  • Fat-soluble vitamins are buffered against short-term deficiency because of storage, so their deficiency timelines are longer. But that same storage means the right side of the axis (toxicity) is a real and present risk, because excess accumulates instead of leaving.

Nicolov and colleagues (2021) put a chemistry-level backbone under this. Using computational ADMET profiling (a structured way of comparing how compounds are absorbed, distributed, metabolized, excreted, and how toxic they are), they found that fat-soluble vitamins fall systematically into higher-toxicity regions purely by their structural chemistry, while most water-soluble vitamins show high bioavailability and low toxicity. The safety asymmetry is not folklore. It is written into the molecules.

Think About It

Did your earlier answer hold up? If a vitamin washes out when you overdo it, you need to eat it regularly (little storage) and it is hard to poison yourself from food (excess leaves). That is the water-soluble profile in one sentence. Now flip every clause and you have described the fat-soluble profile.

Putting Numbers on the Window: RDA and the Upper Limit

The spectrum is the right mental picture, but coaches and clinicians need anchors on that axis, not just a vibe of "enough" and "too much." Nutrition science gives us two that matter most here, and knowing what each one means (and does not mean) keeps you from misreading a supplement label or a client's intake.

The Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) sits on the left-of-center part of the axis. It is the daily intake estimated to meet the needs of nearly all healthy people in a group (about 97 to 98 percent). It is deliberately set with a margin above the bare minimum, so hitting the RDA is a comfortable "in the window" target, not a knife's edge. Read it as a floor with cushion, not a ceiling.

The Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL) sits on the right side. It is the highest daily intake judged likely to pose no risk of harm for almost everyone. Below the UL, you are in safe territory. Above it, the risk of adverse effects starts to climb, and it climbs faster the further you go. The UL is the guardrail at the top of the curve. The gap between the RDA and the UL is, in plain terms, the width of the window. For some vitamins that gap is enormous (vitamin C: easy to meet the RDA, very hard to reach harm). For others it is narrow enough that routine high-dose supplements can push a person past the guardrail without anything dramatic happening on the day they cross it.

Two practical points fall out of this framing. First, the RDA and the UL describe total intake, food plus supplements combined. A person eating a fortified breakfast cereal, drinking a fortified beverage, and taking a multivitamin and a separate high-dose single-nutrient capsule can stack past the UL on a vitamin without ever feeling like they are "overdoing it," because each individual product looked modest. Second, the UL is a population guardrail, not a personalized poison threshold. It is built with safety margins, so crossing it once is not an emergency, and for water-soluble vitamins the body usually handles modest overshoot by excreting it. The number matters most for the fat-soluble vitamins and the megadose supplement habits we are about to examine, where "a little over, every day, for months" is exactly the pattern that accumulates.

This is why it matters

When you look at a supplement bottle, the useful question is not "does this contain the vitamin," it is "how does this dose compare to the RDA and the UL." A capsule delivering 100 percent of the RDA is a sensible top-up. A capsule delivering 2,000 percent of the RDA is not ten times more helpful, it is a dose aimed at the right edge of the curve, and for a fat-soluble vitamin taken daily, that edge is where harm lives. You do not need to memorize every number. You need the habit of asking where on the axis a given dose actually lands.

Deficiency Timelines: Why the Clock Runs Differently

Because the two classes store differently, they fail differently in time. This is one of the most practically useful things in the chapter, because it tells you which gaps show up in weeks and which take months or years.

Water-soluble: fast in, fast empty

With little storage buffer, water-soluble deficiencies can develop in weeks to a few months once intake drops. The 2025 Nutrients review on water-soluble vitamins makes the principle explicit: these vitamins require consistent intake precisely because storage is limited, and their deficiencies produce neurological, hematological, and immune consequences (Various, 2025). Vitamin C is the classic teaching case. Historically, sailors on long voyages without fresh produce developed scurvy in a matter of weeks to a couple of months: fatigue, bleeding and swollen gums, poor wound healing, bruising. The body simply ran out, because there was no large reserve to draw on.

The standout exception is vitamin B12. Despite being water-soluble, B12 is stored in the liver in surprisingly large amounts, enough that a deficiency can take years to surface after intake stops. This matters enormously for one population in particular, which we will get to, because the long lag means the deficiency is well advanced before symptoms appear, and some of the neurological damage can be slow to reverse. Keep B12 flagged as the water-soluble vitamin that breaks the "washes out fast" rule.

Fat-soluble: slow to empty, slow to show

Fat-soluble deficiencies generally take longer to develop, because the body draws down its stores first. An adult with good liver reserves of vitamin A can go a long time on a deficient diet before running dry. The upside is a buffer against short-term dietary gaps. The downside is that by the time a fat-soluble deficiency is symptomatic, intake has usually been inadequate for an extended period. Andrès and colleagues (2024) catalog the deficiency syndromes: vitamin A deficiency impairs vision (notably night vision) and immune function; vitamin D deficiency compromises bone mineralization and calcium handling; vitamin E deficiency, which is rare, can produce neurological signs; vitamin K deficiency impairs blood clotting.

This is why it matters

The timeline tells you what kind of problem you are looking at. A vitamin issue that appears within a few weeks of a sudden dietary change points you toward the water-soluble group. A vague decline over many months on a structurally narrow diet, with no acute trigger, fits the fat-soluble group or the slow-burning B12 picture. You are not diagnosing. You are reading the clock to know which conversation to have, and when to point someone toward testing.

Recognizable Signs: Patterns, Not Diagnoses

Let's be very clear about the lane before we drive in it. What follows are educational patterns of deficiency and excess, the kind of thing that helps you understand why a diet might be structurally risky. They are not a diagnostic checklist. Fatigue has a hundred causes. Bleeding gums can be gum disease. The signs below overlap with countless other conditions, and confirming a vitamin problem requires a clinician and usually a blood test. Your job is fluency in the patterns so you know when to steer food choices and when to refer. Diagnosis is not your job, and pretending otherwise is how coaches get people hurt.

Signs on the deficiency (left) side

  • Vitamin C: fatigue, bleeding or swollen gums, easy bruising, slow wound healing, rough skin.
  • Vitamin D: bone pain or weakness, muscle aches; in severe pediatric cases, impaired bone development.
  • Vitamin A: impaired night vision, dry eyes, increased susceptibility to infection.
  • Vitamin K: easy bruising and prolonged bleeding (it is essential for coagulation).
  • B12 and folate: a particular type of anemia plus, for B12, neurological signs such as numbness, tingling, balance problems, and cognitive changes.
  • B1 (thiamin): neurological and cardiovascular signs (the deficiency disease is beriberi).
  • B3 (niacin): the classic triad of dermatitis, diarrhea, and dementia (pellagra).

Signs on the toxicity (right) side

This is where the fat-soluble group earns its caution. Because excess accumulates:

  • Vitamin A toxicity: headache, nausea, skin changes, liver problems, and in severe chronic cases bone and neurological effects. Pregnancy is a special high-stakes case because excess vitamin A is a known teratogen.
  • Vitamin D toxicity: the clearest cautionary tale of the supplement era, which we cover in detail next.
  • Vitamins E and K: toxicity from these is less common, though high-dose vitamin E supplementation has its own concerns and vitamin K interacts with anticoagulant medication (Lykstad & Sharma, 2022).

For water-soluble vitamins, toxicity from food is essentially a non-issue, because surplus is excreted. But "essentially never from food" is not the same as "never," and the exceptions matter precisely because they prove the rule, which we will hit in the B6 section.

Worked Example: Vitamin D and the Top of the Curve

Vitamin D is the perfect case study because it lives at the intersection of two modern realities: a lot of people genuinely run low, and a lot of people respond by megadosing supplements without supervision. Both ends of the spectrum are crowded.

On the deficiency side, vitamin D is unusual in that diet is not the main supplier for most people; skin synthesis from sunlight is. So low sun exposure (high latitudes, indoor lifestyles, heavily covered skin, darker skin pigmentation which reduces synthesis) plus limited dietary sources sets up a predictable shortfall. The signs are slow and skeletal, in keeping with its fat-soluble timeline.

On the toxicity side, Marcinowska-Suchowierska and colleagues (2018) reviewed hypervitaminosis D and laid out the mechanism plainly: excess vitamin D drives excessive calcium absorption, producing hypercalcemia, too much calcium in the blood. The downstream symptoms include confusion, vomiting, frequent urination, dehydration, and, if severe and sustained, kidney damage and calcium deposits in soft tissue. Critically, they note that the rising use of high-dose over-the-counter supplements is driving an increase in toxicity cases. This is not a theoretical hazard from a lab. It is people buying very high-dose capsules, taking them daily for months under a "more is better" assumption, and landing in genuine trouble.

A methodological caution belongs here, because this is upper-level material: the evidence base for vitamin D toxicity rests heavily on case reports, accidental poisonings, and animal studies, because you cannot ethically run a trial that poisons people (Marcinowska-Suchowierska et al., 2018). That is a real limitation. It means our picture of exactly where toxicity begins is fuzzier than we would like. But the direction is unambiguous: a fat-soluble vitamin, taken in large doses over time, accumulates and can cross into harm.

Coaching translation

In practice, this means: vitamin D is one of the few supplements where a deficiency is common enough that supplementation is often reasonable, but it is also one where the megadose instinct can backfire. The move is not "guess high." The move is "test, then dose, then retest if you are using meaningful amounts," and that testing is a clinician's call. Your job as a coach is to flag the pattern (low sun, narrow diet) and to push back hard on the bro-science idea that if a normal dose is good, ten times the dose is ten times as good. With a fat-soluble vitamin, that logic is exactly backwards.

Worked Example: B6 and the Exception That Proves the Rule

We keep saying water-soluble vitamins are hard to overdose on. That is true, and it is also not absolute, and a confident coach knows the edge cases. Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) is the canonical example.

Lykstad and Sharma (2023) lay out the picture cleanly. From food, B6 toxicity essentially does not happen; ordinary dietary intake (on the order of 1.9 mg per day) carries no toxicity risk. The problem is supplements taken at supratherapeutic doses for prolonged periods. Sensory neuropathy (numbness, tingling, and problems with coordination, because the nerves carrying sensation are affected) typically appears above 1000 mg per day, which is hundreds of times the dietary amount, though cases have been documented below 500 mg per day with long-term use. Nicolov and colleagues (2021) flagged the same point from the chemistry side: B6 and B3 are the water-soluble vitamins that show non-trivial toxic potential at high doses.

So the rule still holds, but with a refinement. Water-soluble does not mean infinitely safe. It means the toxic dose from food is effectively unreachable, and trouble only appears when someone deliberately takes massive supplemental doses for a long time. The 2025 pediatric review reinforces this: even water-soluble vitamins can produce mild toxicity, usually gastrointestinal or neurological, at high intakes, while still confirming the broad asymmetry between the two classes (Various, 2025).

Myth, busted

"You can't overdose on B vitamins, they just come out in your pee." Mostly true, dangerously incomplete. You will not overdose from food, and most B vitamins really do flush. But B6 in megadose supplement form can cause nerve damage, and some of that damage is slow to reverse. The lesson is not "fear B vitamins." It is "the dose-response curve has a right edge for everything, and high-dose supplements are how people find it."

Worked Example: Folate, B12, and Why Two Vitamins Travel Together

Folate (vitamin B9) is the best case for showing that "deficiency" is not only about how a person feels today. Sometimes the highest-stakes consequence lands on someone else entirely, and it lands early.

Folate is a coenzyme in one-carbon metabolism, the set of reactions that build and repair DNA and that let cells divide. When a tissue is growing fast, demand for folate spikes, because rapid cell division means rapid DNA synthesis. Nowhere is growth faster than in a developing embryo. In the first few weeks of pregnancy, the neural tube (the structure that becomes the brain and spinal cord) closes, and adequate folate is required for that closure to happen normally. The decisive detail is timing: this happens so early that it is often complete before a person knows they are pregnant. That is why the public-health recommendation targets women who could become pregnant, not only those already trying.

The evidence here is unusually strong for nutrition. According to PubMed, the US Preventive Services Task Force evidence review found that folic acid supplementation taken before and during early pregnancy is associated with a substantially reduced risk of neural tube defects, with no evidence of the harms that had been raised as concerns (Viswanathan et al., 2023). This is one of the clearest examples in all of micronutrition where a specific, well-timed supplement genuinely prevents serious harm. It is the opposite of vague "insurance" supplementing. It is targeted, mechanism-based, and supported by large data.

Now the link to B12, because folate and B12 are tied together by chemistry and you cannot reason about one without the other. Both feed the same one-carbon machinery. A shortage of either produces the same distinctive picture in the blood: a megaloblastic anemia, in which red blood cells come out abnormally large because DNA synthesis (which needs folate and B12) cannot keep pace with the cell's growth in size. Here is the trap that has real clinical consequences. If someone is low in B12 but takes high-dose folic acid, the folate can correct the blood picture (the anemia improves) while the B12 deficiency keeps quietly damaging the nervous system underneath (Scaglione & Panzavolta, 2014). The blood test looks better, the nerve damage continues, and because B12 nerve damage can be slow or incomplete to reverse, masking it is genuinely dangerous.

This is why it matters

Folate is the rare case where supplementation is not just defensible but strongly recommended for a specific group, women who could become pregnant, on a clear mechanism and strong evidence (Viswanathan et al., 2023). At the same time, folate and B12 show why "just take more of one vitamin" is risky reasoning: pour in folate while B12 is low, and you can hide a problem that keeps doing harm. For a coach, the takeaways are concrete. Encourage folate-rich foods and, for anyone who could become pregnant, support the standard pre-pregnancy supplementation conversation with their clinician. And treat unexplained anemia, numbness, or tingling, especially in someone at B12 risk, as a referral, not a supplement to guess at. The interaction between these two is exactly the kind of thing that belongs in a clinician's hands.

How Dietary Patterns Create Predictable Vitamin Risks

This is where the chapter pays off for a coach, because you do not analyze vitamins one by one in real life. You look at a dietary pattern and reason forward to its likely gaps. The single most useful principle comes straight from Bogataj Jontez and colleagues (2024), and it is worth quoting: "omitting a whole food group raises the risk of low intake of micronutrients abundant in the omitted group." That sentence is your whole framework. Find what a diet leaves out, and you have found its risks.

Their cross-sectional study of 130 adults across four dietary patterns (omnivore, vegetarian, vegan, and low-carbohydrate high-fat) found exactly this structure. And here is the part that should stick: they found supplement use did not fully correct all the deficiencies. Supplements are not an automatic patch. The pattern of the underlying diet still matters.

Pattern by pattern

  • Vegan diets are at high risk for vitamin B12, because B12 is essentially absent from plant foods, and Bogataj Jontez and colleagues (2024) also flagged vitamin D and other micronutrient gaps. B12 is the headline because of its long, silent timeline: the liver store masks the deficiency for years, then neurological signs appear. This is the one nutrient where a well-run vegan diet genuinely requires reliable supplementation, not as a nicety but as a structural necessity. We will return to this in Chapter 8.
  • Very low-fat diets quietly compromise the fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) through the absorption mechanism we covered: no fat in the meal, no efficient uptake. This is the direct callback to Chapter 4. A low-fat diet is not just a macronutrient choice. It is a micronutrient absorption choice.
  • Diets very low in fresh produce (long stretches of minimal fruits and vegetables, common in restrictive or convenience-driven eating) put vitamin C and folate at risk. The water-soluble timeline means these can show up in weeks to months, not years.
  • Diets that omit dairy and fortified foods, combined with low sun exposure, stack the odds against vitamin D.
  • Low-carbohydrate high-fat diets, per Bogataj Jontez and colleagues (2024), have their own specific gaps, often tied to reduced intake of fruits, grains, and certain vegetables, which can pull down vitamin C and some B vitamins.

Notice what this gives you. You do not need a nutrient database to start. You need to ask: what food groups does this person almost never eat? Then you reason to the vitamins that group supplies. That is structural risk assessment, and it is squarely in your lane, because it is about food choices, not diagnosis.

This is why it matters

The most common coaching failure here is treating supplements as the answer to a structural diet problem. A vegan who refuses to supplement B12 is not "covered" because they take a multivitamin that happens to be low in it. An LCHF dieter is not safe from low vitamin C because they take fish oil. Bogataj Jontez and colleagues (2024) showed supplements often do not fully close the gaps. The first move is always the food pattern itself: what is missing, and can we add it back through food? Supplementation, where it is warranted and especially for B12 in vegans, is a deliberate decision, ideally informed by testing, not a vague safety blanket.

Who Is Actually at Risk, and Where Food Comes First

It is easy to read a chapter like this and conclude that everyone is teetering on the edge of some deficiency. That is not the reality, and overstating risk is its own kind of error, the one that sells unnecessary supplements. In a person eating a reasonably varied diet with adequate calories, most vitamin needs are met without much thought, because vitamins are spread across a wide range of foods and the required amounts are small. The point of understanding risk is to spot the specific situations where the odds genuinely shift, not to treat the whole population as deficient.

Real elevated risk clusters in identifiable groups. Restrictive or narrow diets that omit whole food groups raise the odds for whatever those groups supply, which is the structural logic we will detail next. People eating in a chronic calorie deficit have less total food coming in, so micronutrient density has to do more work and gaps open more easily. Older adults absorb some vitamins less efficiently, B12 in particular, because the stomach changes that come with age reduce its release from food. Pregnancy and lactation raise demand for several nutrients at once. Limited sun exposure plus few fortified foods sets up vitamin D shortfall regardless of how "clean" the rest of the diet looks. And certain medications and medical conditions alter absorption or requirements, which is squarely clinical ground. These are the real-world flags. Outside of them, a varied, adequate diet does most of the job on its own.

The food-first default, and why it is not just a slogan

Food-first means exactly what it says: the first move for closing a nutrient gap is almost always food, not a pill. There are concrete reasons this is the default rather than a preference. Whole foods deliver vitamins alongside the fats and the food matrix that aid their absorption, which a standalone capsule taken on an empty stomach may not. Food makes overdosing essentially impossible for the water-soluble vitamins and very hard for the fat-soluble ones, because you are not delivering a concentrated megadose. And food supplies the nutrients together, in combinations and ratios that often work better than any single isolated dose, which matters for the folate and B12 pairing you just saw. Adding back a food group, or adding fat to a vegetable-heavy meal so the fat-soluble vitamins actually absorb, frequently solves the problem more safely than supplementation does.

Supplementation is a tool, and it has genuine indications. The honest stance is neither "supplements are pointless" nor "everyone should take a stack." Supplementation is warranted when the food pattern cannot reasonably close the gap. The cleanest examples are the ones already in this chapter. Vitamin B12 for someone on a strict vegan diet is not optional polish, it is a structural requirement, because B12 is essentially absent from plant foods and the deficiency is slow, silent, and partly irreversible. Folic acid for women who could become pregnant is strongly recommended on strong evidence (Viswanathan et al., 2023). Vitamin D is commonly reasonable where sun exposure is genuinely limited, ideally guided by testing rather than guesswork. Documented deficiencies, confirmed by a clinician, are treated with targeted supplementation. In each case the logic is the same: a specific gap, a clear reason food will not close it, and where possible a confirmation rather than a guess.

Myth, busted

"A daily multivitamin is cheap insurance, so everyone should take one." For a person already eating a varied, adequate diet, a multivitamin mostly produces expensive urine and, for the fat-soluble vitamins, a small standing nudge toward the right edge of the curve. It is not a moral failing to take one, and in specific at-risk situations a targeted supplement is genuinely smart. But a multivitamin is not a substitute for a food pattern that is missing a whole group, and the research backs this up: supplement use did not fully close the deficiencies created by restrictive dietary patterns (Bogataj Jontez et al., 2024). The supplement is the patch. The food pattern is the structure. Fix the structure first.

Why megadosing is not "more is better"

Pull the whole chapter together and the case against routine megadosing is not an opinion, it is what the dose-response curve already told us. A vitamin is a coenzyme or a regulator that works within a range. Once the relevant enzymes have the coenzyme they need, adding more does not make those reactions run faster, because the reaction was never limited by a lack of the tool once the toolbox was stocked. So the upside of megadosing, for a person who is not deficient, is essentially flat. The downside is not flat. For fat-soluble vitamins, surplus accumulates and can cross into toxicity (vitamin D is the modern cautionary tale). For a few water-soluble vitamins, very high chronic supplemental doses cause their own harm (B6 and nerve damage is the proven case). And megadosing one nutrient can disturb others, as the folate-masking-B12 problem shows. "More" is a position on a curve that bends downward at both ends. Aiming for the high end, by default, points a person at the wrong edge.

Putting the Spectrum to Work

Let's assemble the whole model into the way you will actually use it. When you look at a person's diet, run three quick passes.

First, classify by solubility. Is the vitamin you are worried about fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) or water-soluble (C and the Bs)? That immediately tells you whether storage buffers the timeline and whether excess is a real risk.

Second, locate the timeline. A sudden dietary change with symptoms in weeks points water-soluble. A slow decline over many months on a narrow diet points fat-soluble, or the slow-burn B12 exception. This tells you how urgent and how recent the issue likely is.

Third, read the pattern for structural gaps. What food groups are missing? Vegan without B12 supplementation. Very low-fat starving the fat-soluble four. Minimal produce dropping vitamin C. Then steer the food, and refer the diagnosis.

That last clause is the whole ethic of this chapter. You can become genuinely fluent in vitamin patterns, fluent enough to spot a diet that is structurally set up to fail someone over months, and that fluency makes you a better coach. But fluency in patterns is not authority to diagnose. The moment a case involves confirming a deficiency, interpreting blood work, dosing therapeutically, or anything touching pregnancy, medication interactions, or malabsorption, you are at the edge of your lane. Knowing exactly where that edge is, and saying so clearly to a client, is not a weakness. It is the mark of someone who actually understands this material.

Case study: the diet that passed every macro check

Case

Noor is 29, switched to a strict vegan diet two years ago, and trains hard. She tracks meticulously: calories on target, protein hit every day through legumes, tofu, and a plant protein powder, plenty of vegetables. To keep the diet "lean and clean," she eats very little added fat, dressings on the side, minimal oil. She takes a general multivitamin "to be safe" but never looked closely at what is in it. Lately she is more tired than her training load explains, and in the past couple of months she has noticed tingling in her hands and the occasional balance wobble that unsettles her. Her food log looks textbook. Nothing in the macros explains how she feels.

Run Noor through the three passes from this chapter and the picture stops being mysterious. Start by reading the pattern for structural gaps. Her diet omits two things that matter: animal foods entirely, and most dietary fat. Each omission predicts a specific risk, and the symptoms she is reporting line up with one of them in particular.

The headline is vitamin B12. It is essentially absent from plant foods, so a strict vegan diet without reliable B12 supplementation is structurally set up to run short (Bogataj Jontez et al., 2024). Her multivitamin "to be safe" is exactly the false comfort this chapter warned about: a general multi is not guaranteed to deliver adequate, well-absorbed B12, and supplement use does not automatically close the gaps a restrictive pattern creates. Now layer in the timeline. B12 is the water-soluble vitamin that breaks the fast-empty rule, because the liver stores a large reserve. That reserve is why she felt fine for a long stretch and is only now, two years in, noticing problems. The tingling and balance issues are the part that should move a coach to act, because those are neurological signs, and B12-related nerve damage can be slow or incomplete to reverse. This is not a "try more spinach" situation. It is a referral.

Her very low-fat approach is the quieter second problem, and it works through the absorption mechanism from earlier in this chapter. Eating fat-free, dressing-on-the-side, with plenty of vegetables feels virtuous, but the fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) need dietary fat, bile, and micelle formation to be absorbed. A diet that is both plant-based and aggressively low-fat can take in vitamin A precursors and vitamin K from all those vegetables and still absorb a disappointing fraction of them. Combined with limited sun exposure and few fortified foods, her vitamin D is also a fair bet to be low. None of this shows up in her macros, because absorption is invisible to a food log.

What does a coach actually do here? Stay in lane and steer the food while routing the rest correctly. The food moves are clear and squarely yours: add fat back to those vegetable-heavy meals (olive oil, avocado, nuts) so the fat-soluble vitamins actually absorb, and treat reliable B12 supplementation as a structural requirement of her chosen diet, not an optional extra. The clinical moves are not yours to make: the neurological symptoms and the question of confirming and correcting a B12 deficiency belong with a clinician and a blood test, and you say so plainly. That combination, fluent enough to see the structural gaps and disciplined enough to refer the diagnosis, is the entire posture this chapter has been building toward.

Omitting a whole food group raises the risk of low intake of micronutrients abundant in the omitted group.

Bogataj Jontez et al. (2024)

Key Takeaways

  • The single most useful split is fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) versus water-soluble (C and the eight B vitamins). It predicts absorption, storage, deficiency timeline, and toxicity risk.
  • Fat-soluble vitamins need dietary fat to be absorbed (bile, micelles, chylomicrons), they are stored in liver and fat, deplete slowly, and can accumulate to toxic levels. A too-low-fat diet quietly impairs their uptake.
  • Water-soluble vitamins are not stored in quantity (B12 is the major exception), so they need consistent intake, deplete faster, and rarely reach toxic levels from food. B6 in megadose supplements is the notable exception that can cause nerve damage.
  • Every vitamin lives on a dose-response spectrum: deficiency on the left, a broad sufficiency window in the middle, toxicity on the right. More is not a direction; it is a position on a curve that turns harmful at both ends.
  • Signs of deficiency and excess are patterns to understand, never a diagnostic checklist. They overlap with countless other conditions, and confirming them is a clinician's job.
  • Read dietary patterns for structural gaps: vegan diets risk B12 (and vitamin D); very low-fat diets risk A, D, E, K; low-produce diets risk vitamin C. Supplements do not automatically close these gaps.
Looking Ahead

Vitamins are only half of the micronutrient layer. In Chapter 6 we turn to minerals, where the central plot twist is bioavailability: it is not just how much iron or calcium is on the plate, but how much your body can actually absorb, and how the components of a meal compete, block, and enhance one another. The dose-response spectrum you learned here carries straight over, but the absorption story gets considerably more interesting.
