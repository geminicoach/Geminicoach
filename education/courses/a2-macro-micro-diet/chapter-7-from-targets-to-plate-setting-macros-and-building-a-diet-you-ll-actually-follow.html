Class 7 · Module 3

From Targets to Plate: Setting Macros and Building a Diet You'll Actually Follow

Everything converges here: turning a maintenance estimate into goal targets, macros into meals, and a clever plan into a livable one.

31 min read 11 cited sources

Two people walk into a coaching session on the same morning. The first hands you a spreadsheet so precise it specifies grams of almonds to the decimal, calories down to the single digit, and a meal schedule built around six exact eating windows. The second person shrugs and says, "I eat lunch out most days, I hate cooking, and I'm wrecked by the time dinner happens." Here is the uncomfortable truth that will run through this entire chapter: the second person is far more likely to succeed, because their coach is forced to design for the human who actually exists rather than the robot who lives only on paper.

We have spent six chapters building the parts. Energy from Chapter 1. Protein from Chapter 2. Carbohydrate from Chapter 3. Fat from Chapter 4. Fiber, satiety, and food-matrix thinking from the chapters after. Now we assemble them into one workflow: take a person's stats and goal, produce a calorie and macro target with sensible ranges, and translate that into a structure of meals they can repeat on a Tuesday that went badly. This is the synthesis chapter. By the end, you will be able to do the whole job, from numbers to plate.

Step one: turn maintenance into a goal target

Back in Chapter 1 you produced a maintenance estimate: the number of calories that holds bodyweight roughly steady over time. We treated it not as a fact carved in stone but as a working hypothesis to be tested against the scale and the mirror over two to four weeks. That estimate is the hinge on which everything else turns. Every goal is just a deliberate, sized adjustment away from it.

There are only three destinations. You eat below maintenance to lose fat (a deficit), at maintenance to hold steady, or above it to gain (a surplus). The art is in sizing the gap, because the gap has consequences that go well beyond how fast the number on the scale moves.

The fat-loss deficit: moderate beats aggressive

The instinct is to make the deficit as large as you can tolerate, on the logic that bigger deficit equals faster results. The problem is that a larger deficit costs you more than time. It costs you lean mass, training quality, sleep, mood, and, most decisively, your ability to keep doing it. A practical default is a deficit of roughly 15 to 25 percent below maintenance, which for many people lands somewhere around 300 to 600 calories per day.

Why not deeper? Murphy and Koehler (2022) conducted a meta-analysis of resistance-training studies run under an energy deficit and found that deficits significantly blunted gains in lean mass compared to energy-balanced controls. Their meta-regression put a useful number on it: a deficit of roughly 500 kcal per day was the point at which lean mass gains were essentially prevented. Strength held up better than mass, which tells us the body protects function even while it struggles to build tissue under-fueled. The coaching translation: a moderate deficit lets you lose fat while defending the muscle that keeps your metabolism and your physique intact. An aggressive deficit buys you a faster scale and a worse outcome.

Think About It

If a moderate deficit and an aggressive deficit both lose the same fat over a month, but the aggressive one costs more lean mass and is harder to sustain, what is the aggressive deficit actually buying you? Be honest about who it serves: the client, or the client's impatience.

Maintenance: the most underrated goal

Maintenance is not the absence of a goal. It is energy-matched intake, and it is the correct prescription more often than people think: after a fat-loss phase to consolidate the result, during a stressful life season when adding any other demand is reckless, or for someone whose real problem is consistency rather than calories. Coaches under-prescribe maintenance because it does not feel like progress. In practice, learning to hold a result is the skill that separates people who keep weight off from people who ride the same ten pounds up and down for a decade.

The gain surplus: smaller than you'd guess

To build tissue you need to be in positive energy balance, but the size of the surplus required is genuinely uncertain. Slater et al. (2019) reviewed the question directly and concluded that while we have clarified optimal protein intake, the specific energy surplus needed to maximize hypertrophy remains unknown and has never been validated in resistance-trained populations. That is a striking admission from a careful review. The practical consequence: a modest surplus, on the order of 5 to 15 percent above maintenance (often 200 to 400 calories), supplies the raw material for growth without piling on excess fat you will only have to diet off later. "Bulking" at a thousand calories over maintenance does not build muscle faster; it builds fat faster and muscle at the same rate the raw materials allow.

The coaching takeaway across all three: pick the smallest adjustment that still produces the result you want. Bigger is not better. Sustainable is better.

One more idea has to travel with the target, or the whole workflow turns brittle: the number you set is a starting hypothesis, not a verdict. The maintenance estimate from Chapter 1 carries real error, so the deficit or surplus you build on it is your best opening guess, and the scale and the mirror over two to four weeks are how you check it. If a person on a planned 20 percent deficit is not losing after three honest, consistently tracked weeks, the answer is rarely to slash calories further. It is to confirm the tracking is accurate, then make a small adjustment, a few hundred calories, and re-check. If they are losing faster than expected and feeling wrecked, the deficit is too steep and you ease it up. This is the difference between coaching a living system and obeying a spreadsheet. Bodies adapt, life intervenes, and the number that was right in week one may need a nudge by week six. The skill is reading the feedback and adjusting in small, deliberate steps rather than overhauling the plan every time the scale has a bad morning.

Step two: set macros in priority order, as ranges

Once the calorie target is set, you allocate it across the three macronutrients in a specific order. The order matters because the macros are not equal partners. One has a job that depends on hitting a floor, one has a job that depends on not falling below a floor, and the third gets whatever is left. Set them in the wrong order and you can build a target that is internally contradictory.

Protein first

Phillips (2014) made the case plainly: dietary protein should be "the macronutrient around which changes in macronutrient composition should be framed." This is exactly right and it is why protein goes first. During a deficit, protein requirements actually rise, because adequate protein is what spares lean mass when total energy is low, and it does double duty by driving satiety so the diet feels less punishing. A common, defensible target is roughly 1.6 to 2.2 grams per kilogram of bodyweight (about 0.7 to 1.0 grams per pound), skewing toward the higher end in a deficit and for people doing serious resistance training.

Notice that we set protein in grams per unit of bodyweight, not as a percentage of calories. This is deliberate. A percentage target makes protein shrink exactly when you cut calories, which is precisely backward.

Fat to a sensible floor

Fat goes second, and the key idea is a floor, not a target. Fat is essential for hormone production, fat-soluble vitamin absorption, and the simple matter of food tasting like food. A reasonable floor is around 0.5 to 0.8 grams per kilogram of bodyweight, or roughly 20 to 30 percent of calories for most people. Below that floor you start trading away function and palatability for no real benefit. Above it, fat is largely a lever you can move up or down based on preference. Some people feel and eat better with more fat and fewer carbs; others the reverse. The floor is non-negotiable; the ceiling is preference.

Meeting that floor with real food is straightforward once you know roughly what carries fat. Cooking oils and butter are nearly pure fat, about 14 grams per tablespoon, so they move the number fast. Nuts, seeds, and their butters, fatty fish like salmon, whole eggs, full-fat dairy, and avocado all contribute meaningfully while bringing other things along (omega-3s from the fish, fat-soluble vitamins from the dairy and eggs, fiber from the nuts and avocado). A practical way to hit the floor without obsessing: let the protein sources you already chose carry some of it (salmon, whole eggs, and a fattier cut of beef are protein and fat at once), add a thumb of fat to a meal or two, and the floor takes care of itself. The reason it is a floor and not a target is functional. Below it you risk blunting hormone production and fat-soluble vitamin absorption and stripping flavor out of the diet, all for no benefit. Above it, within the calorie budget, fat is simply a preference lever you can trade against carbohydrate based on what makes the person feel and eat best.

Carbohydrate fills the remainder

Carbohydrate comes last, and that is not an insult. It is a feature. Once protein is set and fat is above its floor, whatever calories remain go to carbohydrate. Carbs fuel training, support recovery, feed the gut through fiber-rich sources, and frankly make most diets enjoyable enough to follow. Filling the remainder with carbohydrate means your hardest-working, most flexible macro absorbs the day-to-day variation while protein and the fat floor stay protected.

One detail to nail down before you build a plate: the energy each macro carries, because the whole remainder calculation runs on it. Protein and carbohydrate supply about 4 calories per gram. Fat supplies about 9. Those numbers are the exchange rate between the grams you prescribe and the calories you budgeted, and they are why the priority order works arithmetically and not just as a philosophy. You lock protein in grams and multiply by 4 to spend its share of the budget. You set fat at its floor in grams and multiply by 9 to spend its share. Whatever calories remain, you divide by 4 to get the grams of carbohydrate that fit. Carbohydrate is the remainder precisely because it is the macro you can move most freely without breaking anything structural, so it is the natural place for the leftover energy to land.

Here is that arithmetic made concrete. Say the budget is 2,000 calories and the person weighs 75 kilograms. Protein at 1.8 g/kg is 135 grams, which costs 540 calories. A fat floor at 0.7 g/kg is about 53 grams, which costs roughly 475 calories. Those two together spend 1,015 calories, leaving 985 for carbohydrate. Divide by 4 and you get about 246 grams of carbohydrate. Change the goal and only one number moves: drop the budget to 1,600 for a deficit, and protein stays put (you defend it), the fat floor stays put (you defend it), and carbohydrate alone takes the hit, falling to about 146 grams. That is the priority order doing its job. The two macros with non-negotiable floors hold steady, and the flexible one flexes.

Think About It

Why do we express protein as grams per kilogram of bodyweight but fat as a floor and carbohydrate as a remainder? What does each choice protect? (Hint: think about what happens to each macro when you suddenly cut 400 calories from the plan.)

Why ranges beat fragile exact numbers

Here is where people get this wrong. They write down 187 grams of carbohydrate as if the universe will notice the difference between 187 and 174. It will not. Your TDEE estimate has error bars. Food labels are allowed to be off by up to 20 percent. Your activity varies day to day. A target stated as a single exact number is a lie about precision you do not have, and worse, it sets the client up to feel like a failure for landing at 170 instead of 187.

State every target as a range. Protein "at least 150 grams." Fat "between 60 and 80 grams." Carbohydrate "roughly 180 to 220 grams." A range is honest about the underlying uncertainty, and it gives the human room to live inside the plan instead of constantly violating it by a rounding error. This is not sloppiness. It is matching the precision of the prescription to the precision of the measurement, which is exactly what good science demands.

How the person will actually track

A target is only useful if the person can tell, day to day, whether they are hitting it. That is the job of tracking. There is no single correct method. There is a spectrum running from precise-and-demanding to loose-and-easy, and the right choice depends on who you are coaching and what they will sustain. Choosing the method is part of the prescription, not an afterthought.

Weighing and logging: most precise, most demanding

At the precise end, the person weighs food on a kitchen scale in grams and logs it in an app that sums the macros. This is the most accurate method, and the only one that cleanly separates "the plan is wrong" from "the plan is not being followed," because it removes guesswork from the measurement. It also has the highest cost in time, mental load, and friction, which is exactly why it is the wrong default for many people. Weighing earns its place in two situations: a defined, time-limited phase where precision matters (the final weeks before a physique goal, or troubleshooting a stall), and an education phase, where a few weeks of weighing teaches the person what portions actually look like so they can eyeball them later with some accuracy.

Hand portions: the no-equipment middle ground

In the middle sits hand-portion estimation, which uses the person's own hand as a measuring tool that travels everywhere and needs no app. A common system: a palm of protein (roughly 20 to 30 grams of protein), a cupped handful of carbohydrate, a thumb of fat, and a fist of vegetables, repeated across meals to reach the day's targets. The hand scales with the person, so a larger body gets larger portions automatically, which is a feature rather than a flaw. The tradeoff is precision: hand portions land you in the right neighborhood, not at the exact address. For most general-population clients chasing health and a sustainable physique, the right neighborhood is plenty.

Habit-based: no numbers at all

At the loose end is habit-based eating, with no counting whatsoever. Instead the person follows a few structural rules: a protein source at every meal, vegetables at two meals, a fixed number of meals, water before reaching for a snack. This is the lowest-friction method and, for many people, the most durable, because rules you can run on autopilot survive the bad days that a spreadsheet does not. The cost is feedback: when progress stalls you have less data to diagnose why, and you may have to tighten to a more precise method temporarily to see what is actually going in.

The decision rule across all three is the same one used for the calorie deficit: pick the least demanding method that still gives the feedback the goal requires. And tracking is not only measurement. It is itself an intervention. Peterson et al. (2014) followed 220 women through a behavioral weight-loss program and an extended-care year and found that the people who kept the weight off were the ones who self-monitored frequently and consistently, more than three days a week. The benefit came specifically from frequency combined with consistency, and it worked by helping people actually meet their intake goals. Sporadic logging did little. Whichever method you pick, consistency is the active ingredient. A loose method done every day beats a precise method done twice a week.

Coach's Note

When someone says "I track but I'm not losing," the first question is not "are you tracking?" It is "are you tracking accurately and consistently?" Untracked bites, weekend gaps, and eyeballed cooking oils are where the math quietly breaks. Often the fix is not a smaller target. It is a few weeks of tighter measurement to see the truth, then a return to a looser method once the picture is clear.

Step three: translate targets into a plate

Numbers on a screen do not feed anyone. The next move is to turn the daily targets into a structure of meals that a real person can hit most days without thinking too hard. The structure rests on three ideas: protein anchors, satiety levers, and built-in room for foods people enjoy.

Anchor every meal with protein

The simplest, most robust meal-building rule is to start each eating occasion with a protein source and build the rest of the plate around it. If a person eats three meals, dividing the daily protein target into three roughly equal anchors of 30 to 50 grams each makes the daily total almost automatic. Anchoring protein also smooths satiety across the day rather than back-loading it, and it is the one macro most people under-hit by accident.

Here is where grams stop being abstract and start being food. A coach has to be able to translate "165 grams of protein" into things a person recognizes on a plate, because nobody eats a number. Round, usable references help: a palm-sized portion of cooked chicken breast, fish, or lean beef carries roughly 25 to 35 grams of protein; a large egg about 6; a cup of Greek yogurt around 20 to 25; a cup of cooked lentils about 18; a standard scoop of whey roughly 25. On the carbohydrate side, a cup of cooked rice or pasta runs about 45 grams, a medium potato around 35, a slice of bread about 15, a banana about 25. For fat, a tablespoon of oil is about 14 grams, a thumb of nut butter around 8, half an avocado about 15. You do not memorize a database. You learn a handful of anchor foods well enough that you can rough out a day in your head, then check it against an app until the estimates calibrate.

There is also a real reason to spread protein across meals rather than saving it for one big dinner. Mamerow et al. (2014) fed healthy adults the same total daily protein either evenly across breakfast, lunch, and dinner (about 30 grams each) or skewed toward the evening meal (about 10, 16, and 63 grams). The even distribution produced 24-hour muscle protein synthesis roughly 25 percent higher than the skewed pattern, and the effect held after a week of habituation. The applied lesson reinforces the anchoring rule: getting a meaningful dose of protein at each meal is not just a convenience trick for hitting the total, it appears to use that protein more effectively for building and maintaining tissue. Three or four protein anchors of 25 to 45 grams beats one giant evening load carrying the same grams.

Meal frequency: fit the schedule, not the dogma

How many meals? The honest answer is: whatever number the person can actually repeat. Leidy et al. (2011) tested this directly in a 12-week trial of overweight and obese men on a 750-calorie deficit, comparing higher protein (25 percent of energy) against normal protein (14 percent), each delivered at either three or six meals per day. The higher-protein diet produced significantly greater daily fullness and reduced late-night appetite and food preoccupation. But eating frequency, three versus six meals, did not independently change appetite outcomes. Read that again: protein amount mattered; meal count did not.

This is liberating. It means you can build around the person's life. A shift worker eats differently from an office worker who eats differently from a parent feeding three kids. Pick the frequency that fits the schedule, then make sure each of those meals carries a protein anchor. The myth that you must eat every three hours "to stoke the metabolic fire" has no support here.

In practice this gives you a small set of structures to offer, not one rigid template. A three-meal day suits most office schedules: three plates, each anchored with protein, carbohydrate leaning toward fiber-rich sources, fat sources the person enjoys. A two-meal compressed window suits people who genuinely are not hungry in the morning or whose schedule forbids a midday meal; the catch is that two meals must each carry a larger protein anchor to reach the daily total, which some people find hard to eat in one sitting. A grazing pattern of four to six smaller eating occasions suits people who prefer to nibble or who train in the middle of the day. None of these is metabolically superior. They are scheduling options, and you pick the one the person can repeat without willpower. The constant across all of them is the protein anchor at each occasion and the daily totals landing in range.

Use fiber and fat as satiety levers

Within the calorie budget, two levers make a diet feel fuller than its numbers suggest. Fiber-rich carbohydrates (vegetables, fruit, legumes, whole grains) add volume and slow digestion, so the plate fills the stomach and the bloodstream gradually. Fat adds satisfaction and flavor and slows gastric emptying. Neither is magic, and both cost calories, so they are levers to pull thoughtfully, not commands to maximize. A practical move in a deficit: lean toward higher-volume, higher-fiber foods so the person eats more grams for fewer calories and feels it.

There is real mechanism here, not just folk wisdom about "filling foods." The property that matters is energy density, the number of calories packed into a given weight of food. Water and fiber add weight and volume without adding calories, so foods high in both (vegetables, fruit, broth-based soups, legumes) let a person eat a large, stomach-filling quantity for a small calorie cost. Fat does the opposite: at 9 calories per gram it is the most energy-dense thing on the plate, so fat-heavy foods deliver a lot of calories in a small, easy-to-overeat package. This is why a deficit built on high-volume, lower-energy-density foods feels less like deprivation. The person is genuinely eating more food, just food that weighs more per calorie.

Physical form matters too, independent of the macros. Zijlstra et al. (2007) gave people products matched on calories, macronutrients, and palatability but differing in viscosity, from liquid to semi-solid, and let them eat freely. People ate up to 30 percent more of the liquid version than the solid one, partly because liquids go down faster and the body registers them less effectively as a meal. The lesson lands hard on the modern diet: calories you drink (juice, soda, sweetened coffee, even smoothies) tend to slip past the satiety system that solid food trips. When you are designing for fullness, favor foods the person has to chew, and treat liquid calories as something to spend deliberately rather than by accident.

Leave room for foods people enjoy

This is the lever most spreadsheets forget. Once protein is anchored and the fat floor is met, a portion of the remaining calories should be deliberately reserved for foods the person actually likes, including foods with no nutritional "justification" beyond enjoyment. This is not a cheat or a compromise. It is the design feature that makes the other 85 percent of the diet adherable. This is the practical meeting point of two camps usually framed as enemies. The "if it fits your macros" approach says any food is allowed as long as the daily macro totals land in range, which is liberating and flexible but, taken to an extreme, ignores that two diets hitting identical macros can deliver wildly different micronutrients, fiber, and fullness. The "whole-food-first" approach prioritizes nutrient-dense, minimally processed foods, which covers micronutrients and satiety well but, taken to an extreme, hardens into the rigid, all-or-nothing rule set the restraint literature warns against. The resolution is not to pick a side. It is to use whole foods as the default that fills most of the plate (for the fiber, the micronutrients, and the satiety they buy) and to use the flexibility of "it fits" for the remaining slice, so the diet stays both nourishing and livable. Most of the plate is chosen for what it does for the body; a deliberate minority is chosen for enjoyment, and the macros hold either way. We will defend this with hard data in the next section, but state the principle now: a plate with zero room for pleasure is a plate that gets abandoned.

Step four: the variable that beats every spreadsheet

We have arrived at the heart of the chapter. You can produce a flawless calorie target, allocate macros in perfect priority order, and design an elegant meal structure, and none of it matters if the person does not do it. Adherence is the decisive variable in diet design. A diet a person will not follow is not a good diet, no matter how beautiful the math.

The evidence: composition loses, adherence wins

Two landmark trials settle this more cleanly than most debates in nutrition ever get settled. The POUNDS LOST trial (Sacks et al., 2009) randomized 811 overweight and obese adults to four diets that varied systematically in fat, protein, and carbohydrate across a two-by-two factorial design, and followed them for two years. The result: weight loss was similar across all four macronutrient compositions. The thing that predicted who lost weight was not which diet they were assigned. It was how many sessions they attended and how well they stuck to it. Adherence, not ratio, drove the outcome.

Dansinger et al. (2005) ran the test from a different angle, comparing four popular named diets (Atkins, Ornish, Weight Watchers, and Zone) in 160 adults over a year. Adherence was low across all groups, but here is the finding that should be tattooed on every coach's forearm: for every single diet type, greater adherence was associated with greater weight loss and greater improvement in cardiac risk factors. The diet you can stick to beats the diet that is theoretically optimal but abandoned by week six.

This is why it matters. The entire industry argues about keto versus low-fat versus carnivore versus whatever is trending, and the best available randomized evidence says the argument is largely beside the point. The macro ratio is a rounding error compared to whether the human keeps doing it. Your job as a coach is not to find the magic composition. It is to engineer adherence.

Flexible control beats rigid control

So how do you engineer adherence? A large body of work on dietary restraint points to one answer: flexibility. Westenhoefer et al. (1999), working with an enormous sample of over 54,000 participants, validated two distinct styles of eating control. Rigid control is all-or-nothing: foods are forbidden, rules are absolute, and a single deviation means the day is "blown." Flexible control is graded: a larger portion today is balanced by a smaller one tomorrow, no food is forbidden, and a deviation is just a data point. Rigid control was associated with higher BMI, more disinhibition, and more frequent binge eating. Flexible control was associated with lower BMI, less disinhibition, and more successful long-term weight management.

Conlin et al. (2021) tested this head to head in resistance-trained men and women under a roughly 20 percent deficit, comparing a flexible diet (any foods, hit your macros) against a rigid diet (specific prescribed foods). Both produced equivalent weight loss during the diet. After the diet, fat-free mass gains were numerically greater in the flexible group. The conclusion echoed the bigger trials: adherence, not dietary structure, was the critical variable.

No clean foods, no dirty foods

Here is a place to be blunt. Moralizing food as "clean" or "dirty," "good" or "bad," is not just unscientific, it is actively counterproductive. It manufactures the exact rigid, all-or-nothing mindset that the restraint literature links to worse outcomes and disordered eating. There are foods that are more nutrient-dense per calorie and foods that are less so. There are smart sources for hitting your protein, your fiber, your micronutrients. And there is room in a well-built diet for foods chosen purely because you enjoy them. That is the entire vocabulary you need. "Smart sources" and "balance," not "clean" and "cheat."

In practice, this means you design for the human who exists. You ask what they like to eat, what their week actually looks like, where the predictable failure points are (the work lunch, the Friday social, the bad-sleep day when hunger spikes), and you build slack into the plan for exactly those moments. A perfect day is easy. A sustainable month is the goal. The difference between them is whether you designed for an ideal robot client or a real person, and then whether you blame the human when the robot's plan fails them.

Putting it together: a worked example

Let us run the whole workflow once, start to finish. Meet a client: 170 pounds (about 77 kg), maintenance estimated at 2,400 calories from Chapter 1's logic, goal is fat loss, trains three times a week.

Calorie target. A moderate 20 percent deficit puts intake near 1,920 calories. We round to a target of "about 1,900, give or take." Staying out of the deep-deficit zone respects the Murphy and Koehler (2022) finding that large deficits sabotage lean mass.

Protein first. At roughly 1.0 gram per pound for someone in a deficit, we set protein at about 170 grams, stated as "at least 165 grams." That is 680 calories.

Fat to floor. A floor near 0.7 grams per kilogram gives about 54 grams. We set a range of 55 to 70 grams. Call it 60 grams as a working figure: 540 calories.

Carbohydrate by remainder. 1,900 minus 680 minus 540 leaves 680 calories, or about 170 grams of carbohydrate. State it as "roughly 155 to 185 grams."

Meal structure. The client prefers three meals and a snack. We build three protein anchors of about 45 grams each plus a 35-gram snack. We lean the carbohydrate toward fiber-rich, higher-volume sources to make the deficit feel fuller, meet the fat floor with sources the client enjoys, and reserve roughly 150 to 200 calories of flexible room each day for foods chosen purely for pleasure. That reserved room is not the weak point of the plan. It is the load-bearing wall.

That is the entire job: stats and goal in, defensible calorie and macro ranges out, and a meal structure built around the person rather than against them.

Case study: the perfect plan nobody could follow

Case

Lena is 38, a nurse working rotating twelve-hour shifts, and a mother of two. She comes in wanting to lose about fifteen pounds. Her previous coach handed her a spreadsheet: 1,450 calories, six exact meals spaced every three hours, specific named foods weighed to the gram, and a hard rule that nothing off the list ever touches her plate. For three weeks Lena was perfect. Then a night shift blew up the meal timing, she missed two of the six meals, decided the day was ruined, and ate freely to compensate. The all-or-nothing pattern set in: rigid for a stretch, then a collapse, then guilt, then a rigid restart. Six weeks later she had lost almost nothing, felt like a failure, and was eating worse than before she started.

Run Lena through this chapter and the failure was designed in from the first line of that spreadsheet. The plan was not too generous or too strict on calories alone. It was built for a robot who lives on a fixed schedule, and Lena is a shift-working parent whose schedule is the one thing she cannot control. The deficit itself was part of the problem: at 1,450 calories against her real maintenance, it sat far below the moderate 15 to 25 percent this chapter argues for, dropping her into the deep-deficit zone Murphy and Koehler (2022) link to lost lean mass and the kind of hunger that makes a collapse nearly inevitable.

The six-meals-every-three-hours rule was worse than useless for her. Leidy et al. (2011) showed meal frequency does not independently drive satiety or results; protein amount does. So the rule bought Lena nothing physiologically while costing her enormously in feasibility, because hitting six timed meals across a rotating shift is close to impossible. The fix is to invert it: build around the two or three eating occasions her shift actually allows, make sure each carries a real protein anchor, and lean on the Mamerow et al. (2014) finding that spreading protein across those occasions uses it better than dumping it into one meal. Same protein total, a structure that survives a night shift.

The rigid "nothing off the list" rule is the part that turned a hard week into a spiral. This is textbook rigid control, the all-or-nothing style Westenhoefer et al. (1999) tied to higher BMI, more disinhibition, and more binge eating, and the opposite of the flexible control Conlin et al. (2021) found preserves results without the crashes. One missed meal should be a data point, not a verdict on the day. And the larger evidence says the precise composition Lena agonized over barely mattered: POUNDS LOST (Sacks et al., 2009) and Dansinger et al. (2005) both found that adherence, not the exact ratio or the exact foods, predicts who succeeds.

The rebuild is almost boring by comparison. Set a moderate deficit she can live in. State the macros as ranges, protein first as a daily minimum she anchors across whatever meals her shift allows. Pick a tracking method she can sustain on a chaotic week, likely hand portions rather than gram-weighing, so the method does not collapse the first busy day. Reserve a couple hundred calories of deliberate flexible room for foods she enjoys, including the coffee she drinks on shift. Lena does not need more discipline. She needs a plan designed for her actual life instead of one that blames her for not living a different one.

Key Takeaways

  • Every goal is a sized adjustment from your maintenance estimate: a moderate deficit (about 15 to 25 percent) for fat loss, energy-matched intake for maintenance, and a modest surplus (about 5 to 15 percent) for gain. Pick the smallest adjustment that produces the result.
  • Set macros in priority order: protein first (1.6 to 2.2 g/kg, in grams of bodyweight, not a percentage), fat to a floor (about 0.5 to 0.8 g/kg), and carbohydrate filling the remainder.
  • State every target as a range, not a fragile exact number. Your TDEE estimate and food labels both carry real error, so false precision sets clients up to "fail" by a rounding error.
  • Anchor every meal with protein. Meal frequency should fit the person's schedule; Leidy et al. (2011) showed protein amount drives satiety while three versus six meals does not.
  • Adherence, not macro ratio, is the decisive variable. POUNDS LOST (Sacks et al., 2009) and Dansinger et al. (2005) both found that sticking to the plan predicted success, regardless of composition.
  • Flexible control beats rigid control (Westenhoefer et al., 1999; Conlin et al., 2021). Design slack into the plan, reserve room for enjoyed foods, and never moralize foods as clean or dirty.
  • Design for the human who exists, not the ideal robot client, then never blame the human for a plan that ignored their real life.
Looking Ahead

You now have a complete workflow: stats and goal to calorie and macro ranges to a followable plate. Chapter 8 stress-tests that workflow against real human variation, the menopausal client, the shift worker, the person with a history of disordered eating, the trained athlete in a peaking phase, so you can adapt the framework instead of breaking it when the textbook case walks out and a complicated human walks in.
