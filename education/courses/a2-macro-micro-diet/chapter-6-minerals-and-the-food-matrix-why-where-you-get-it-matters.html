Minerals and the Food Matrix: Why Where You Get It Matters

It is not what you eat. It is what you absorb.

34 min read 13 cited sources

Two people eat the exact same number of milligrams of iron at lunch. One eats a plate of lentils and brown rice with a glass of black tea. The other eats a smaller portion of beef with a side of bell peppers and a squeeze of lime. Read the food labels and you would swear the lentil plate "wins." It has more iron on paper. But run the tape forward eight hours into the intestine, and the beef eater has quietly absorbed several times more usable iron, while a good chunk of the lentil eater's iron walks out the other end untouched.

That gap, between the iron printed on a label and the iron a body actually keeps, is the single most important idea in this chapter. Minerals force the issue more than any other nutrient class, and once you see it, you cannot unsee it. The number on the package is the offer. Your gut decides how much of the offer to accept.

The mineral cast: major and trace

Minerals are the inorganic part of nutrition. Unlike carbohydrates, fats, proteins, and vitamins, they are not built from carbon chains your body assembles and breaks down. They are elements, the same calcium and iron and zinc you would find on a periodic table, pulled out of soil and water by plants and animals and passed up the food chain to you. You cannot synthesize a single one of them. Every atom of iron in your blood was once in the ground.

We sort them by quantity, not by importance. Major minerals (sometimes called macrominerals) are the ones you need in larger amounts, conventionally more than 100 milligrams a day: calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, sodium, potassium, chloride, and sulfur. Trace minerals (microminerals) you need in tiny amounts, often just a few milligrams or even micrograms daily: iron, zinc, copper, selenium, iodine, manganese, molybdenum, and a handful of others.

Here is the trap in that vocabulary. "Trace" sounds optional, like a rounding error. It is not. You need only about 8 to 18 milligrams of iron a day depending on who you are, but run short and you get anemia, fatigue, cold hands, and a brain that feels like it is running through wet sand. You need only 150 micrograms of iodine, an amount you could barely see, but run short during pregnancy and you compromise a developing brain permanently. Trace means small dose, not small consequence.

What the major minerals actually do

Before we talk about absorption, it helps to know what these elements are for. A list of names is forgettable. A list of jobs sticks, because once you know the job you can reason about who is at risk and why a shortfall shows up the way it does. We will keep this tight and functional, major minerals first.

Calcium is the structural mineral. Roughly 99 percent of the calcium in your body sits in bone and teeth, where it is the raw material of the skeleton. The other 1 percent does work that is arguably more urgent moment to moment: it triggers muscle contraction (including the heartbeat), carries nerve signals, and helps blood clot. That 1 percent is so important that the body will pull calcium out of bone to keep blood levels steady if the diet runs short. So a low-calcium diet does not announce itself as a blood problem. It quietly taxes the skeleton over years, which is why bone density is the long game calcium plays.

Phosphorus is calcium's structural partner in bone, but it moonlights everywhere else. It is in the backbone of your DNA, in the membranes of every cell, and in ATP, the energy currency your cells spend to do anything at all. Phosphorus is so widespread in food (it rides along with protein in meat, dairy, grains, and legumes) that dietary deficiency in a normally fed person is rare. The more common modern concern runs the other way: heavily processed foods and colas add phosphate as an additive, and very high intakes can nudge the calcium-to-phosphorus balance in unhelpful directions, especially for people with kidney disease.

Magnesium is the quiet workhorse. It is a required cofactor for more than three hundred enzyme systems, including every reaction that makes or spends ATP, which means magnesium is involved any time a cell produces energy. It also helps regulate muscle and nerve function, blood glucose, and blood pressure, and it works in partnership with calcium: calcium signals a muscle to contract, magnesium helps it relax. Magnesium lives in green leafy vegetables, nuts, seeds, legumes, and whole grains, which is exactly the part of the diet that thins out when people eat highly refined food. We will come back to magnesium, because subclinical shortfall is more common than most people assume (Nielsen, 2014).

Sodium, potassium, and chloride are the electrolytes, the charged minerals that manage fluid balance, blood pressure, nerve signaling, and muscle contraction. They work as a balanced pair more than as individuals, and that balance, not either one alone, is what your physiology actually cares about. They get their own section later in this chapter, because the modern diet pushes that balance hard in one direction.

What the trace minerals actually do

Trace minerals show up in tiny amounts and run outsized jobs. Think of them less as building material and more as the spark plugs and specialized tools of metabolism.

Iron sits at the center of oxygen transport. It is the business end of hemoglobin, the protein in red blood cells that ferries oxygen from your lungs to your tissues, and of myoglobin, which stores oxygen in muscle. It is also built into the enzymes that let cells produce energy. Run short and the symptoms map directly onto that job: fatigue, breathlessness, cold hands, pale skin, and a foggy, sluggish brain, because tissues are not getting the oxygen they need. Iron gets the most attention in this chapter precisely because it is both critically important and one of the hardest minerals to absorb.

Zinc is the multitool. It is a structural and catalytic component of hundreds of enzymes and of the proteins that switch genes on and off, which is why zinc touches so many systems at once: immune function, wound healing, growth, taste and smell, and the synthesis of DNA and protein. A zinc shortfall tends to show up as a slow erosion across several fronts: more frequent infections, slow-healing cuts, blunted appetite, and in children, stalled growth. Maret (2013) catalogs how broadly zinc reaches, noting that it interacts directly with the metabolism of copper and iron, a point that becomes practical when we talk about supplements.

Iodine has one famous, non-negotiable job: it is a building block of thyroid hormone, which sets the metabolic rate of nearly every cell in the body. There is no workaround. Without iodine the thyroid cannot make enough hormone, and it enlarges trying (the classic goiter). The stakes are highest in pregnancy and early life, because thyroid hormone drives brain development. Iodine deficiency during those windows remains the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability worldwide, which is why salt iodization has been one of the most successful public health interventions of the last century (Doggui et al., 2020).

Selenium works mostly through a family of antioxidant enzymes (the selenoproteins, most famously glutathione peroxidase) that protect cells from oxidative damage, and it is also required to activate thyroid hormone. Selenium content in plant foods depends heavily on the selenium in the soil where they grew, so intake varies by region in a way few other nutrients do. A couple of Brazil nuts can supply a day's worth; a diet from selenium-poor soil can run chronically low.

Copper partners with iron in making red blood cells, helps build connective tissue and the protective sheath around nerves, and is part of the same antioxidant defenses that handle reactive oxygen. Copper deficiency from diet alone is uncommon, but it can be induced, and the most common modern cause is self-inflicted: long-term high-dose zinc supplementation, which we will cover directly. Copper and zinc compete for the same absorption machinery, so flooding the system with one can starve you of the other (Maret, 2013).

This is why it matters: you do not need to memorize this like a chemistry exam. You need the functional map, because it tells you who is at risk and what a shortfall feels like. Iron is oxygen delivery. Iodine is thyroid and brain development. Zinc is immune and repair. Calcium is the skeleton's long game. Magnesium is energy and relaxation. Once the jobs are in your head, the rest of this chapter is about getting these elements out of food and into the body, which is a different problem entirely.

Why minerals are harder to absorb than they look

Minerals exist in food as charged ions or bound into compounds, and that chemistry makes them needy. They are highly susceptible to being grabbed, bound, and locked up by other things in the meal before they ever reach an absorption site (Frontiers in Nutrition, 2025). A vitamin like riboflavin is a defined molecule that mostly gets absorbed as itself. A mineral like iron is a reactive ion that other food components are constantly competing to bind. That reactivity is exactly why context matters so much for minerals and less so for many vitamins.

Coaching translation: think of vitamins as guests who mostly show up and walk in. Minerals are guests who show up, then have to get past a bouncer, a coat check, and a crowd of people trying to grab their arm on the way to the door. How many actually make it inside depends entirely on who else is at the party.

Bioaccessibility and bioavailability: the two numbers that aren't on the label

To reason about minerals well, you need two terms that sound similar and mean different things. The food matrix review by Weaver and colleagues (2025) draws the line cleanly, so we will borrow their framing.

Bioaccessibility is the proportion of a nutrient that gets released from the food during digestion and becomes available for absorption. A mineral locked inside an intact plant cell wall, or chelated to a binding compound, is present in the food but not bioaccessible. Digestion has to free it first.

Bioavailability is the proportion that is actually absorbed across the gut wall and made available for the body to use. This is the number that matters for your physiology, and it is always equal to or smaller than bioaccessibility. A nutrient has to be released before it can be absorbed, but being released is no guarantee it will be.

So the full chain looks like this: the label tells you total content. Digestion determines bioaccessibility (how much breaks free). Then the gut, the competing minerals, the enhancers, and your own physiological state determine bioavailability (how much crosses over). The number printed on the package sits at the very top of that funnel, and a lot leaks out at every stage below it.

The food matrix: the package changes the contents

The food matrix is the physical and chemical structure of a food: how its nutrients are arranged, what they are bound to, what cells and fibers and fats and proteins surround them. The central thesis of Weaver and colleagues (2025), and the conceptual hinge of this entire course, is that a nutrient's effect on the body depends profoundly on the matrix it arrives in. The same milligram of calcium behaves differently dissolved in milk than crushed into a fortified cracker, because the matrix changes whether and how it is released and absorbed.

Dima and colleagues (2020) make this vivid by zooming in on whole foods. In dairy, fruits, and vegetables, the proteins, lipids, and minerals interact with each other, and the microstructure of the food (how tightly the cells are packed, how the fats are emulsified) changes the kinetics of absorption. Their conclusion is blunt and important: whole food matrices produce different health effects than the same nutrients in isolated form. A mineral in a food is not the same as a mineral in a pill, because the food brings its whole supporting cast along.

This is why it matters: the food matrix is the scientific reason we build diets from foods rather than from spreadsheets of isolated nutrients. If nutrients acted independently, you could just total up milligrams and call it a day. They do not. The packaging is part of the dose.

Enhancers and inhibitors: who helps and who blocks

If bioavailability is the outcome, enhancers and inhibitors are the levers. These are the other components in a meal that push absorption up or drag it down. We will work through the big ones, because once you know the cast you can predict the behavior of almost any meal.

Vitamin C: the iron amplifier

Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is the headline enhancer for non-heme iron, the form of iron found in plants and in fortified foods. It works through chemistry: it reduces ferric iron (Fe³⁺) to ferrous iron (Fe²⁺), the form the gut absorbs far more readily, and it forms a soluble complex that keeps iron from being grabbed by inhibitors. In practical terms, adding a vitamin C source to a plant-based iron meal can multiply the absorbed iron substantially.

This is the "citrus trick" your grandmother may have stumbled onto without the biochemistry. A squeeze of lime on the lentils, bell peppers in the stir-fry, strawberries with the iron-fortified cereal, tomato sauce on the beans. These are not garnishes. They are absorption engineering.

Phytates: the plant's mineral vault

Phytate (phytic acid) is the storage form of phosphorus in seeds, and it is abundant in whole grains, legumes, nuts, and seeds. It is a powerful mineral binder: it grabs iron, zinc, and calcium and forms complexes your gut cannot easily break apart, dragging bioavailability down (Frontiers in Nutrition, 2025). This is the great irony of whole, plant-rich foods. The same legumes and whole grains that are nutritional powerhouses also carry the very compounds that limit how much of their minerals you absorb.

Researchers quantify this with the phytate-to-mineral molar ratio. The more phytate per unit of mineral, the lower the expected absorption. Hunt (2003) categorized vegetarian diets exactly this way, linking phytate-to-zinc molar ratios to zinc bioavailability ranging from roughly 15 to 55 percent. A high-phytate diet sits at the bottom of that range; a lower-phytate one climbs toward the top. Same zinc on the label, dramatically different zinc in the body.

Polyphenols: tea and coffee at the wrong time

Polyphenols, especially the tannins in black tea, coffee, red wine, and some herbal infusions, bind non-heme iron and blunt its absorption. This is the tea-timing pitfall. A strong cup of black tea with an iron-rich plant meal can cut iron absorption sharply. The fix is not "never drink tea." The fix is timing: enjoy the tea between meals rather than alongside the meal you are counting on for iron.

Calcium and iron: two minerals that compete

Here is one that surprises people, because we usually think of minerals as a team. Calcium inhibits iron absorption. Hallberg and colleagues (1992) established this with clinical single-meal studies, showing the effect is dose-dependent and molar-ratio dependent, occurs within the intestinal mucosal cells, and affects both heme and non-heme iron. That last point is striking: calcium is the one inhibitor that can dent even heme iron, which is otherwise hard to touch.

But before you panic about your latte, read the rest of the literature. Lynch (2000) reviewed both single-meal and long-term studies and drew a more measured conclusion. Single-meal studies show a clear inhibitory effect, maximal somewhere around 150 to 300 mg of calcium. But multiple-meal and long-term studies suggest that in populations with adequate calcium intake, calcium supplementation has only a small effect on overall iron status, likely because the body's regulatory mechanisms compensate over time.

Here is where people get this wrong: they take a true single-meal finding and inflate it into a permanent rule, like "never eat dairy and iron together or you'll go anemic." The honest read is that the interaction is real and matters for a single critical meal (say, an iron-deficient person trying to maximize iron at lunch), but for most people eating a varied diet over weeks and months, the body adapts and the long-term effect on iron status is modest. The single-meal effect is real; the catastrophe is not.

Heme versus non-heme iron: the source story

Iron deserves its own deep dive, because it is the cleanest example of why the source, not just the amount, determines what you get. Dietary iron comes in two forms, and they travel completely different roads into the body.

Heme iron comes from the hemoglobin and myoglobin of animal tissue: meat, poultry, fish. The iron sits inside a heme ring, a protective porphyrin structure. Non-heme iron is the free ionic iron found in plants, dairy, eggs, and fortified foods. It is the form most exposed to the binding and competition we have been discussing.

Two different doors

Kalman and colleagues (2025) lay out the mechanism. Heme iron enters the enterocyte largely intact, taken up by a dedicated pathway and then cleaved inside the cell by heme oxygenase to release its iron. Because it travels protected inside the heme ring, it is shielded from the phytates, tannins, and competing minerals in the meal. Its absorption is high and relatively stable, on the order of 25 to 30 percent, and it does not lean on vitamin C or other enhancers to do its job.

Non-heme iron uses a different, more exposed pathway. It must first be reduced from ferric to ferrous form, then transported across the membrane, and at every step it is vulnerable to the inhibitors swimming around it. Its absorption is far more variable, swinging anywhere from about 2 percent to 20 percent depending entirely on the rest of the meal. That is a huge range, and the range is precisely the point: non-heme iron absorption is something you can engineer, for better or worse.

The "meat factor"

There is a bonus hiding in animal foods. Kalman and colleagues (2025) describe the meat factor: peptides released from animal tissue during digestion that enhance the absorption of non-heme iron eaten in the same meal. So a little meat does double duty. It delivers highly available heme iron itself, and it boosts the absorption of the non-heme iron from the plants on the same plate. This is one reason a mixed meal of beef and beans can outperform either food alone.

What this means for plant-based eaters

Hunt (2003) studied this directly. Eliminating meat and increasing intake of phytate-rich legumes and whole grains significantly lowers iron and zinc absorption compared with diets that include meat. On the surface, that sounds like a verdict against plant-based eating. It is not, and Hunt's own data are careful here: although vegetarians tend to have lower iron stores, adverse health effects from this have not been demonstrated in varied vegetarian diets in developed countries. The body adapts. When iron stores fall, the gut up-regulates absorption to compensate.

In practice, this means a plant-based eater is not doomed to deficiency, but they do have to be a more deliberate absorption engineer than someone eating meat. The same meal-design tools we have covered (vitamin C with iron meals, phytate reduction, timing tea away from meals) carry more weight when the easy default of heme iron is off the table. Plant-based diets do not break the rules. They just raise the stakes on knowing them.

Closing the loop with fiber

Back in the carbohydrate chapter, fiber was the hero, and it should be. But honesty demands we connect a thread here. Fiber, along with the phytates that often travel with it in whole plant foods, can bind minerals and lower their absorption (Frontiers in Nutrition, 2025). For a moment that might read like a contradiction: eat more fiber, but fiber blocks minerals?

It is not a contradiction, and it is definitely not a reason to fear fiber. It is a reason to think in terms of context and the total dietary pattern instead of single foods. The mineral binding from a fiber-rich whole-food diet is modest, your body adapts to it over time, and the broader benefits of fiber for digestion, blood sugar, satiety, and the gut microbiome are enormous and well established. The answer is not less fiber. The answer is a varied diet with enough total mineral intake and a few smart pairings, so that even with some binding, you absorb plenty.

Here is where people get this wrong: they read one scary headline about phytates or fiber "blocking" minerals and start stripping whole grains and legumes out of their diet. That trades a small, manageable absorption penalty for the loss of some of the most protective foods you can eat. The smart move is not subtraction. It is preparation and pairing.

Preparation changes the math

This is the most empowering part of the chapter, because it puts the lever in your hands. Traditional food preparation methods that humans have used for thousands of years (soaking, sprouting, fermenting) physically reduce phytate and free up minerals. This is not folklore. It is measurable biochemistry.

Onyango and colleagues (2024) quantified it in maize. Spontaneous fermentation alone cut phytate by 51.8 percent. Combining lactic acid fermentation with soaking and germination reduced phytate by up to 85.6 percent and dropped the phytate-to-iron molar ratio by 85 percent, substantially raising the estimated bioavailability of iron and zinc. Read that again: the same maize, prepared traditionally instead of plainly, can deliver dramatically more usable minerals. The grain did not change. The matrix did.

This is why fermented and sprouted foods, sourdough bread, soaked beans, and traditional grain preparations are not just culinary preferences. They are absorption technology that predates the science explaining it.

Why a varied whole-food diet beats the spreadsheet

Pull all of this together and a principle emerges that will carry through the rest of the course. Bioavailability problems are mostly solved by variety and food synergy, not by chasing isolated numbers. A varied whole-food diet naturally mixes enhancers and inhibitors, includes some animal foods or well-prepared plant foods, and gives the body enough total mineral intake that even imperfect absorption lands you in a good place.

Supplements can fix a specific, diagnosed deficiency, and they have a real role. But they solve bioavailability clumsily. The Frontiers in Nutrition review (2025) notes that supplemental mineral forms vary wildly in how well they are absorbed (ferrous versus ferric iron, zinc sulfate versus zinc oxide), and host factors like your gut microbiota and life stage shift the outcome further. A pill is an isolated nutrient stripped of its matrix, which means it loses exactly the supporting cast that food brings for free. Dima and colleagues (2020) showed that whole food matrices produce different effects than isolated nutrients precisely because the components interact. Food is not just a delivery vehicle for minerals. The vehicle is part of the medicine.

This is the conceptual hinge of the whole course. The food matrix is the reason we build diets from foods, not from spreadsheets of isolated nutrients. You cannot optimize a meal by summing milligrams, because the milligrams interact. You optimize by understanding which foods help which other foods, and arranging them accordingly. That is what a good coach does, and that is what the next two chapters build on directly.

Sodium and potassium: the balance that runs sideways

Sodium and potassium deserve their own treatment, because the way the modern diet handles them is the clearest example of a mineral problem that is about ratio, not just amount. These two electrolytes manage fluid balance and blood pressure together, working in opposition the way the gas and brake pedals work in a car. Sodium tends to pull water into the bloodstream and raise pressure; potassium helps the kidneys clear sodium and relaxes blood vessel walls, easing it back down. Your body cares about the relationship between them.

Here is the modern mismatch. For most of human history we ate diets high in potassium (from plants) and low in sodium (salt was scarce). Our physiology was tuned for that ratio. The industrial food supply flipped it. Processed and restaurant foods are loaded with added sodium, while fruits, vegetables, beans, and other potassium-rich whole foods get crowded out. So the average person now runs a high-sodium, low-potassium pattern, which is close to the opposite of what the system was built for.

This is not a small or theoretical concern. Jayedi and colleagues (2018), pooling sixteen studies and more than a quarter-million people, found that a higher dietary sodium-to-potassium ratio was associated with a steadily rising risk of stroke, with no safe plateau at the high end. The ratio mattered, and they concluded that lowering it (by cutting sodium and raising potassium together) is a useful strategy alongside cutting sodium alone. Read that carefully: the lever is not only "eat less salt." It is "shift the balance," and you have two ways to pull it.

In practice, this means the most effective move for most people is not obsessive salt-counting. It is adding potassium-rich whole foods, the produce and legumes that were missing in the first place, while easing off the heavily processed foods that carry most of the hidden sodium. You fix the ratio from both ends at once, and you do it by changing what is on the plate rather than by chasing a single number. One caution worth flagging: people with kidney disease or on certain blood pressure medications can have trouble clearing potassium, so for them, loading up on potassium is genuinely something to clear with a clinician first. For everyone else, the produce drawer is the lever.

Mineral against mineral: the zinc and copper seesaw

We have already met one mineral-versus-mineral fight, calcium blunting iron. There is a second one that matters even more in the supplement aisle, because people create it themselves: zinc and copper. The two compete for the same absorption pathway in the gut. When zinc intake is high, the intestinal cells ramp up a binding protein (metallothionein) that grabs copper and holds it inside the cell until it is shed, so copper never reaches the bloodstream. Push zinc high enough for long enough and you can drive a person into genuine copper deficiency (Maret, 2013).

This is not a rare textbook curiosity. It is one of the most common nutrient problems created by supplements rather than diet. Someone takes a high-dose zinc lozenge or tablet daily for months, often for immune support, and slowly develops a copper deficiency that can cause anemia and neurological problems, the kind of issue that is hard to trace back to its cause because the person did everything they thought was healthy. The dose is the difference. At intakes near what food provides, the seesaw barely tips. DiSilvestro and colleagues (2015) gave young women 60 mg of zinc a day for six weeks (a moderately high dose) and saw no measurable hit to copper status, which tells you the danger lives at sustained high doses over longer periods, not at the modest amounts in a normal diet or a basic multivitamin. From food, this competition is essentially a non-issue, because foods rich in zinc (shellfish, meat, legumes, seeds) tend to carry copper along too. The seesaw only tips hard when you isolate one mineral and take a lot of it.

Here is where people get this wrong: they treat single-mineral megadoses as harmless because "it is just a vitamin." Minerals interact, and more of one can mean less of another. That is the whole logic of this chapter, applied to the supplement bottle.

Who is actually at risk, and who is not

One of the most useful things a coach can do is tell the difference between a real risk and a manufactured worry. Most people eating a varied diet are not teetering on the edge of mineral deficiency, and feeding them anxiety (or a cabinet full of pills) helps no one. But specific groups carry genuine, predictable risk, and recognizing them is where this knowledge earns its keep.

Iron has the clearest risk map. Menstruating women lose iron with every cycle, which is why their requirement is roughly double that of men and why iron deficiency is the most common nutrient deficiency in the world, concentrated in this group. Pregnancy raises the demand further. Endurance athletes are a second at-risk group for reasons that surprise people: hard training can increase iron losses (through sweat, gut microbleeding, and the breakdown of red blood cells from repeated foot impact) while also triggering hepcidin, a hormone that temporarily clamps down on iron absorption after intense exercise. Add a plant-based diet on top of either of those, where only the lower-absorption non-heme iron is on the menu, and the risk compounds. None of this means doom. It means these are the people for whom the absorption engineering in this chapter is not optional polish but core strategy.

Iodine risk is geographic and quieter. Where iodized salt is used consistently, deficiency is rare. But the picture is shifting in ways worth watching: more people cook with non-iodized specialty salts (sea salt, kosher salt) that contain little iodine, more processed food is made with non-iodized salt, and people cutting dairy and seafood remove two of the few reliable iodine sources. Pregnant and breastfeeding women have the highest stakes, because of the role iodine plays in fetal brain development (Doggui et al., 2020). This is a case where the public health win of salt iodization can quietly erode if individuals opt out of it without realizing iodine was the reason it mattered.

Zinc risk concentrates in people eating high-phytate, plant-heavy diets with little animal food, where both the lower zinc content and the binding by phytate work against status. Older adults, who often eat less overall and absorb less efficiently, are a second group. Magnesium is the sleeper: because so much of the diet has shifted toward refined foods stripped of the green vegetables, nuts, and whole grains that carry it, a meaningful share of the population runs a subclinical shortfall, intakes low enough to be associated with markers of chronic inflammation even without an obvious clinical deficiency (Nielsen, 2014). And calcium risk rises for anyone who avoids dairy without deliberately replacing it, and for older adults whose absorption efficiency falls with age.

This is why it matters: risk is not random, and it is not universal. It clusters in identifiable people: menstruating and pregnant women, endurance athletes, plant-based eaters, older adults, and people who have quietly removed a key source (dairy, seafood, iodized salt) without replacing what it provided. If none of those describe a person and they eat a varied diet, blanket mineral supplementation is mostly solving a problem they do not have. If one or more do describe them, that is exactly where attention, and sometimes testing, pays off.

Supplements: when they help and when they harm

Everything in this chapter points to a clear stance on supplements, and it is neither "always" nor "never." A mineral supplement is a tool. Used on the right problem it is excellent. Used as a blanket insurance policy it ranges from waste to genuine harm.

Supplements help when there is a specific, identified shortfall. A menstruating woman with diagnosed iron-deficiency anemia, a pregnant woman needing reliable iodine and iron, a person with a malabsorption condition, someone whose blood work shows low status: these are the situations where a targeted supplement does exactly what food sometimes cannot do fast enough. Correcting a real deficiency can be transformative, and refusing a supplement out of food-purism would be its own kind of mistake.

The harm comes from three directions, and all three trace back to ideas already in this chapter. First, megadosing creates the mineral-against-mineral problems we covered: high-dose zinc inducing copper deficiency is the textbook case, but the same logic applies any time you flood the system with one isolated element. Second, minerals have a real ceiling. Unlike many water-soluble vitamins, several minerals are toxic in excess, and the gap between helpful and harmful can be narrower than people assume, iron and selenium especially. Iron overload is dangerous, which is why iron supplements should be taken for a reason, not by default, and kept well away from children, for whom iron pills are a leading cause of poisoning. Third, the supplement strips away the matrix. A pill is an isolated nutrient without the supporting cast that food brings for free, and the form matters: the Frontiers in Nutrition review (2025) notes that mineral salts vary widely in how well they are absorbed, so two bottles claiming the same milligrams can deliver very different amounts.

In practice, this means the default for a varied eater is food first, supplement on purpose. Match the supplement to a real, ideally tested, need. Be especially careful with single-mineral megadoses, respect the upper limits for iron and selenium, and remember that "more milligrams in the bottle" was never the same as "more in the body." That was the first idea in this chapter, and it is the last word on supplements too.

Case study: the marathoner who did everything right and still ran out of gas

Case

Greta is 31, training for her second marathon, and over the past year she shifted to a mostly plant-based diet she is proud of: lentils, tofu, whole grains, big spinach salads, lots of legumes. She eats more iron on paper than she ever did when she ate meat. She has heavy periods. She drinks a strong cup of black tea with breakfast and another with lunch, and tops her lunch salad with a generous handful of cheese and a glass of milk because she wants the calcium for her bones. Over three months her training paces have slipped, her legs feel heavy on easy runs, she is cold at her desk, and she is foggy by mid-afternoon. Her diet "looks great." She is quietly developing iron deficiency.

Run Greta through this chapter and nothing about her decline is mysterious. She has stacked nearly every iron risk factor on top of nearly every absorption inhibitor, and the milligrams on her food labels are hiding it. Her symptoms (heavy legs, cold hands, fog, falling performance) map cleanly onto iron's job: oxygen delivery has quietly degraded, and a body short on usable iron cannot move oxygen the way a marathoner needs.

Start with the risk side. Greta is in the single highest-risk group for iron deficiency (menstruating, with heavy losses), she layered endurance training on top (which increases iron losses and, through hepcidin, can blunt absorption after hard sessions), and she removed heme iron from her diet entirely. Her iron is now all non-heme, the low-absorption form that swings from 2 to 20 percent depending on the meal. So the very number she is proud of, "more iron than when I ate meat," is the label, not the dose. The question is how much she keeps, and almost everything in her routine pushes that down.

Now the meal design, which is where it gets fixable. Her two strong teas a day deliver tannins that bind non-heme iron, and she is drinking them at meals, the worst possible timing. Her lunch is a near-perfect inhibitor stack: spinach brings oxalate, the legumes and whole grains bring phytate, and the cheese-plus-milk adds a calcium load right when she is trying to absorb iron. She did the calcium for a good reason (her bones), but she put it at exactly the wrong meal. None of these foods is a mistake on its own. Assembled together, at the same sitting, they form a wall between her and the iron on her plate.

The coaching plan writes itself from the tools in this chapter, and notice it is mostly rearranging, not restricting. Move the tea to between meals so it stops ambushing her iron. Add a vitamin C source directly to her iron meals (bell peppers in the salad, a squeeze of citrus, strawberries with breakfast) to multiply non-heme absorption. Shift the big calcium hit (the milk and cheese) to a different meal or a snack, so it is not competing at lunch, and keep the calcium, just not there. Lean on her own preparation: soaked, sprouted, and fermented versions of her grains and legumes carry far less phytate. And because she sits in a genuine high-risk group with real symptoms, this is precisely the case where testing iron status and considering a targeted, appropriately dosed iron supplement under guidance is warranted, not as a default for everyone, but as the right call for a person whose risk factors and symptoms line up this clearly. Greta does not need to abandon the diet she is proud of. She needs to become the deliberate absorption engineer that plant-based, high-output, high-loss eating requires.

The amount of a mineral in a food is the beginning of the question, not the answer. The matrix decides the rest.

The throughline of this chapter

Key Takeaways

  • Major minerals are needed in larger amounts (calcium, magnesium, potassium, and others); trace minerals in tiny amounts (iron, zinc, iodine, and others). "Trace" means small dose, not small importance.
  • Bioaccessibility is how much of a mineral digestion releases; bioavailability is how much you actually absorb and use. The food label reports neither, only total content.
  • The food matrix (what a nutrient is packaged with) profoundly changes absorption. A mineral in food behaves differently than the same mineral in a pill (Weaver et al., 2025; Dima et al., 2020).
  • Vitamin C is a powerful enhancer of non-heme iron. Phytates, polyphenols (tea, coffee), and competing calcium are inhibitors (Frontiers in Nutrition, 2025; Hallberg et al., 1992).
  • Heme iron from animal foods is absorbed at a stable 25 to 30 percent and resists inhibitors; non-heme iron from plants swings from roughly 2 to 20 percent depending on the meal, and the "meat factor" boosts it further (Kalman et al., 2025).
  • The calcium–iron interaction is real in a single meal but modest over the long term in well-fed populations, because the body adapts (Lynch, 2000).
  • Plant-based eaters absorb less iron and zinc by default but are not destined for deficiency in varied diets; they simply need to engineer meals deliberately (Hunt, 2003).
  • Soaking, sprouting, and especially fermentation can cut phytate dramatically (up to 85.6 percent in maize) and meaningfully raise mineral bioavailability (Onyango et al., 2024).
  • Fiber can bind minerals, but this is a reason to think in dietary patterns and preparation, not to fear or strip out whole plant foods.
Looking Ahead

You now have the full micronutrient layer, and you have the principle that ties it together: we build diets from foods because the matrix matters. In Chapter 7 we put that principle to work, assembling real meals and full days that account for enhancers, inhibitors, timing, and total patterns instead of isolated numbers. Then Chapter 8 individualizes the whole framework for specific dietary patterns and life stages, where plant-based eating and changing needs make absorption engineering not just useful but essential.
