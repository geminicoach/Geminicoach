Protein, Decoded: Muscle, Leucine, and the BCAA Myth

Why protein earns its priority, how much a meal actually needs, and the mechanistic reason your client's BCAA tub is mostly expensive flavoring.

32 min read 12 cited sources

A client slides a tub across the table. Forty dollars, fruit-punch flavored, "muscle-building" stamped on the front. She wants to know if it works. The honest answer is short and a little awkward: if she already eats enough decent protein, that tub is doing almost nothing she's hoping it does. It is sweetened amino acid powder dissolved in marketing.

To explain why, plainly and without hand-waving, you have to understand one of the most beautiful pieces of physiology in applied nutrition: how a meal flips a molecular switch in your muscle, why a single amino acid acts as the trigger, and why three amino acids cannot build a thing that requires twenty. In Chapter 1 we treated energy as the budget. Protein is the line item people get most wrong in both directions: too little to protect their tissue, or too much money spent on supplements that add nothing. This chapter fixes that.

Why protein is special

Carbohydrate and fat are primarily fuels. Your body burns them, stores them, and pulls them back out as needed. Protein can be burned for energy too, but that is not its main job and it is a wasteful way to use it. Protein is structural and functional: it builds and repairs muscle, skin, enzymes, antibodies, transport molecules, and the contractile machinery that lets you stand up from a chair at eighty. You are, quite literally, built from the protein you eat and the protein you recycle.

Here is the first thing that makes protein different from the other two macronutrients: there is no meaningful storage depot. Your body stockpiles carbohydrate as glycogen and fat as adipose tissue, but there is no "amino acid tank" sitting in reserve. The closest thing you have is your own muscle, which the body will break down to liberate amino acids when intake falls short. That single fact drives almost everything in this chapter. Because there is no buffer, the timing and the dose of protein you eat matter in a way that timing and dose of carbohydrate mostly do not.

The other distinguishing feature is what protein costs and what it returns. Protein has the highest thermic effect of food of any macronutrient, meaning your body spends a notable fraction of protein calories just digesting and processing it, and it produces the strongest satiety per calorie. Halton and Hu (2004), reviewing 48 studies, found convincing evidence that higher-protein diets increase both thermogenesis and satiety relative to lower-protein diets, and that high-protein meals reduce subsequent energy intake. Every study they reviewed that measured the thermic effect of food reported a greater effect for higher-protein meals than for fat or carbohydrate meals. We will return to those threads (satiety and thermic effect) when we build adherent diets later in the course. For now, hold the headline: protein earns its priority on the plate because it does structural work no other macronutrient does, and it happens to be the most filling and metabolically expensive calorie you can eat.

Before reading on: if your body has no dedicated amino acid storage depot, what do you predict happens to muscle when someone chronically under-eats protein, even while eating enough total calories? Muscle protein synthesis and the leucine trigger

Your muscle is in a constant tug-of-war. Muscle protein synthesis (MPS) builds new muscle protein. Muscle protein breakdown (MPB) tears it down. Whether you gain, maintain, or lose muscle over weeks and months is just the running total of synthesis minus breakdown. In a fasted state, breakdown wins slightly and you are in net negative balance. When you eat protein, synthesis spikes and you swing into net positive balance for a few hours. Repeat that swing across enough days and the muscle either grows, holds, or shrinks.

So the practical question becomes: what flips the synthesis switch, and how hard? The answer, surprisingly, is not "the more protein the better, forever." It is closer to a threshold. A meal needs to deliver enough of the right amino acids to push MPS up to its near-maximal rate, and past that point, adding more protein to the same meal does very little extra for synthesis. The leftover gets oxidized for energy or shunted toward other uses.

The dose-response evidence

Two studies anchor this. Moore and colleagues (2009) fed young men 0, 5, 10, 20, or 40 grams of whole egg protein after resistance exercise and measured the MPS response. The response climbed with dose, then flattened: MPS was maximally stimulated at 20 grams, with no further increase at 40 grams. Witard and colleagues (2014) ran the parallel experiment with whey protein in resistance-trained men, using 0, 10, 20, or 40 grams, both at rest and after exercise. Same conclusion: roughly 20 grams maximally stimulated myofibrillar MPS in their ~80-kilogram subjects. The 40-gram dose did not build more muscle. It increased amino acid oxidation and ureagenesis (the production of urea from excess amino acids), which is the body's polite way of saying "I cannot use this for building, so I will burn it and excrete the nitrogen."

This is the foundational evidence for what coaches loosely call the protein threshold, or more precisely the leucine threshold. Let me explain why leucine, specifically, sits at the center of it.

Why leucine?

Of the twenty amino acids, nine are essential amino acids (EAAs): your body cannot make them, so they must come from food. Three of those nine are branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs): leucine, isoleucine, and valine. And one of those three, leucine, does something the others do not. Leucine is not just a building block. It is a signal. Rising blood leucine activates a cellular pathway (the mTORC1 pathway) that effectively tells the muscle cell, "protein has arrived, start building." Leucine is the doorbell. The other amino acids are the materials the contractor needs once the door is answered.

This is the leucine trigger hypothesis: the amplitude and speed of the rise in blood leucine after a meal predicts how strongly MPS turns on. A meal that delivers enough leucine, fast enough, crosses the threshold and maximally stimulates synthesis. A meal that delivers too little leucine produces a weaker response, even if the total nitrogen looks adequate on paper. This is why a protein's leucine content, not just its gram count, matters.

The hypothesis is real but not absolute. Zaromskyte and colleagues (2021), in a systematic review across young and older adults, found that the leucine trigger explains the post-prandial MPS response reasonably well in some contexts but that recent evidence challenges a simple, mechanical "leucine alone decides everything" model. Leucine is necessary to flip the switch, but the full set of amino acids has to be present for the building to actually proceed. Remember that distinction. It is the entire basis for busting the BCAA myth a few pages from now.

Coaching translation

In real life this means a protein "dose" has a sweet spot. Roughly 20 to 40 grams of quality protein per meal, containing enough leucine, turns the building switch on hard. Eating 70 grams in one sitting does not turn it on harder. The extra is not wasted in the sense of being harmful, but it is not buying you extra muscle from that meal. Think of it like watering a plant: past a point, more water just runs out the bottom of the pot.

The threshold is not the same for everyone

The dose-response numbers above came with a quiet asterisk: the "20 grams maximally stimulates MPS" finding came from young men. Older adults are different. With age, muscle develops anabolic resistance: the same dose of protein produces a blunted MPS response. The muscle's doorbell gets harder to hear, so you have to ring it louder. Layman (2024) reviews evidence that adults over sixty need meals delivering at least about 2.8 grams of leucine, which translates to roughly 30 grams of high-quality protein, to robustly stimulate MPS, a threshold not seen in younger adults, who show a more linear, forgiving dose-response. Zaromskyte and colleagues (2021) reach the same conclusion: older adults need a higher leucine dose, around 3 grams, to overcome anabolic resistance.

Layman (2024) also adds a useful shape to the whole picture: across the full range of intakes, the MPS response follows a roughly logarithmic curve, rising steeply at first and then bending toward a plateau at higher protein intakes. That single curve quietly explains both the threshold and the diminishing returns. Each extra gram below the threshold buys a lot of synthesis. Each extra gram above it buys very little.

Net balance: why a single meal is only half the ledger

It is tempting to read the dose-response studies and conclude that building muscle is simply a matter of stacking up enough 20-gram synthesis spikes. That is part of it, but it leaves out the other side of the ledger, and the other side is where a lot of real-world muscle is won or lost. Recall the tug-of-war: net muscle change is synthesis minus breakdown, integrated over days and weeks. A meal pushes synthesis up. It also, more quietly, pushes breakdown down. Both moves shift the balance in the same direction, toward building, and the body is mostly responding to the amino acids and the insulin that arrive with food.

Two practical points fall out of this. The first is that the fasted state is not neutral. Between meals and overnight, breakdown runs ahead of synthesis and you sit in a small negative balance, drawing on your own tissue to keep the most essential processes supplied. This is normal and reversible. It only becomes a problem when the rebuilding side never gets enough material to catch up, which is exactly the chronic-underfeeding scenario the opening callout asked you to predict. Eat too little protein, too rarely, for long enough, and the day's negative stretches outweigh the positive ones. The running total turns into lost muscle even when bodyweight on the scale barely moves, because fat and water can mask what is happening underneath.

The second point is that the size of the synthesis response is not fixed by the protein alone. The muscle's sensitivity to a given dose changes with what you have been doing to it.

Resistance exercise turns up the volume

A resistance-training session does something elegant: it makes the muscle more responsive to the protein you eat afterward. The same 20 or 30 grams produces a larger and longer-lasting rise in synthesis when it lands on a recently trained muscle than when it lands on a rested one. Loading the muscle and feeding the muscle are partners, not substitutes. Training without enough protein leaves the repair signal shouting into an empty pantry. Protein without training rings a doorbell at a muscle that has no particular reason to remodel. Put the two together and the response is far greater than either alone, and that window of heightened sensitivity stretches for many hours, well beyond the old idea of a narrow post-workout rush.

The long-run, real-world version of this shows up in the training literature, not just in acute synthesis measurements. According to PubMed, Morton and colleagues (2018) pooled 49 controlled trials of people who lifted weights for at least six weeks and found that adding protein meaningfully increased the gains in lean mass, strength, and muscle size over training alone. The same analysis surfaced two patterns we keep meeting: the benefit of extra protein faded with age (older muscle is harder to push), and the gains in lean mass stopped improving once total intake passed roughly 1.6 grams per kilogram of bodyweight per day. That number is going to reappear when we set targets, and notice that it falls out of long-term body-composition data, not just acute lab measurements. The acute switch and the year-long outcome point at the same intake.

Coaching translation

In practice this means protein and training are a package. A client who lifts hard but eats 0.8 grams per kilogram is leaving real muscle on the table, and a client who eats 2 grams per kilogram but never loads the muscle is paying for a stimulus they are not using. The most common fixable mistake is not the supplement stack; it is one of these two halves missing. Pair adequate protein with progressive resistance work and you have the engine. Everything else in this chapter is tuning.

Distributing protein across the day

If a single meal maxes out MPS at around 20 to 40 grams and extra protein in that meal does little extra, an obvious implication follows. Stacking most of your daily protein into one giant dinner wastes most of it. You ring the doorbell hard once, the building runs for a few hours, and then the rest of the day your muscle sits in low gear. Spreading the same total across several meals rings the doorbell several times.

Areta and colleagues (2013) tested exactly this. They gave 24 trained men 80 grams of whey over a 12-hour recovery window in three patterns: large boluses (40 grams twice), intermediate doses (20 grams every 3 hours, four times), or small frequent doses (10 grams every 1.5 hours, eight times). The intermediate pattern, roughly 20 grams every 3 hours, produced the greatest stimulation of MPS over the full 12 hours. The big-bolus group overshot the threshold and oxidized the excess. The tiny-dose group never reliably crossed it. The middle pattern, hitting the threshold repeatedly without massive overshoot, won.

That is the clean evidence base for a simple coaching rule: aim for three to four protein-containing meals per day, each clearing the threshold, rather than one or two giant ones. Layman (2024) draws the same conclusion from the body-composition literature: both total quantity and per-meal distribution matter.

Where this matters more, and where it matters less

Be honest about the size of the effect. For a young, healthy person eating plenty of total protein, the distribution nuance is real but modest. They have a forgiving, near-linear response and a low threshold; they can be a bit sloppy and still do fine. The people for whom distribution genuinely matters are the two groups we keep returning to:

Older adults, because anabolic resistance raises their per-meal threshold. An older adult eating 25 grams of protein spread as 5 grams at breakfast, 5 at lunch, and 15 at dinner may fail to cross the threshold at any single meal, stimulating MPS poorly all day despite a number on paper that looks acceptable. The same total reorganized into three 25-to-30-gram meals could cross it three times. For preserving muscle into older age, this distinction is not academic. It is the difference between holding your strength and losing it.

Trained individuals chasing maximal muscle gain, because they are trying to extract every available stimulus and the difference between four good doses and one giant one compounds over a training year.

Coaching translation

In practice this means: do not obsess about timing for a 25-year-old who eats well. Do pay real attention to it for a 68-year-old client trying to keep her independence, or for a dedicated lifter optimizing for growth. The same total protein, intelligently spread, simply works harder. The headline rule is boring and effective: a solid protein source at most meals, three to four times a day.

The big myth: BCAAs build muscle

Now we have everything we need to bust the most lucrative myth in the supplement aisle. The pitch is seductive and half-true, which is what makes it dangerous. Leucine triggers MPS, the marketing says, and BCAAs contain leucine, so BCAA supplements build muscle. The first two clauses are correct. The conclusion does not follow.

This is the step the marketing quietly skips. Triggering protein synthesis and supplying the materials to complete it are two different jobs. Leucine rings the doorbell. But once the muscle cell decides to build new protein, it needs all twenty amino acids assembled in the right sequence, including all nine essential amino acids it cannot manufacture. BCAAs give you three of those nine. You have rung the doorbell and shown up to the construction site with only three of the nine bricks you cannot make on your own. The build cannot finish.

The mechanism, reasoned out

Wolfe (2017) lays out the logic in a way no marketing department wants quoted. When you ingest BCAAs alone, MPS does rise transiently, because leucine signals and because there is always some amino acid availability. But to build new muscle protein, the cell needs the other essential amino acids, and the only place they can come from when you have not eaten them is muscle protein breakdown: tearing down existing muscle to liberate the missing bricks. So BCAAs alone create a strange situation where you are stimulating synthesis using materials freed by breaking down the very tissue you are trying to build. It is a wash at best.

Wolfe (2017) puts a ceiling on it. Theoretically, BCAAs alone could produce at most about a 30 percent increase in MPS, and he argues even that is an overestimate, because obligatory oxidation of essential amino acids cannot be fully suppressed. A complete protein, by contrast, supplies all the materials and routinely drives MPS far higher. His conclusion is blunt: the claim that BCAA supplements are anabolic in humans is unwarranted.

If not BCAAs, what about EAAs?

A fair follow-up question, and one a sharp client will ask: if the problem with BCAAs is that they supply only three of the nine bricks, does a full essential amino acid (EAA) blend fix it? Mechanistically, yes, this is the more defensible supplement, because it supplies all nine bricks the body cannot make. The eleven non-essential amino acids, the ones your body can synthesize on demand, do not have to be eaten for the build to proceed; the cell manufactures them from raw material it already has. So a complete EAA dose can drive synthesis much harder than BCAAs alone, and a relatively small amount of EAAs can produce a strong response, which is genuinely useful in narrow clinical settings, such as feeding frail older patients who cannot eat much volume, or research feeding studies that need a clean stimulus.

For a healthy person who eats enough complete protein, though, the EAA tub runs into the same redundancy problem as the BCAA tub, just less severe. Whole-food protein already delivers all nine essential amino acids, in the right proportions, alongside the non-essentials, plus the digestion-slowing matrix that stretches the amino acid release and the satiety and micronutrients that come along for the ride. A scoop of isolated EAAs is, for that person, a more expensive and less satisfying way to eat amino acids they were already getting from chicken, eggs, dairy, soy, or a sensible mix of plants. The supplement is not the villain. It is simply solving a problem that a well-built plate has already solved.

Think About It

Suppose someone already eats 130 grams of mixed, high-quality protein per day across four meals. Mechanistically, what does adding 10 grams of BCAA powder contribute that their food has not already delivered, leucine and all? Reason from the brick model before reading the next paragraph.

The honest verdict

If your total protein intake is adequate and of decent quality, isolated BCAAs add little to nothing. Your meals already contain all nine essential amino acids, leucine included, in the amounts your muscle actually needs to build. The BCAA tub is redundant. It is supplying three bricks you already have in surplus, while charging you a premium and adding flavoring. The narrow cases where BCAAs might do something (fasted training, very low total protein intake, certain clinical situations) are exactly the cases that are better solved by eating enough complete protein in the first place. We are not reasoning from brand loyalty here. We are reasoning from mechanism: you cannot build a complete protein from an incomplete set of bricks.

Notice this does not make BCAAs harmful or fraudulent in some criminal sense. They are real amino acids. The problem is purely that they are sold as a special muscle-building tool when, for a well-fed person, they are an expensive way to consume something already on the dinner plate. That is the line to give a client: not "this is a scam," but "your food is already doing this job, for free, and better."

Protein quality: completeness and digestibility

If completeness is what makes a brick set usable, then "protein quality" is just a measure of how complete and how usable a given source is. Two things define it. First, amino acid completeness: does the protein contain all nine essential amino acids in roughly the proportions your body needs? Second, digestibility: how much of what you eat actually gets absorbed and made available, rather than passing through?

Animal proteins (eggs, dairy, meat, fish) tend to score high on both. They are complete and highly digestible, and they are leucine-rich, which is why they cross the threshold efficiently. Plant proteins are more variable. They are often lower in one or two essential amino acids (grains tend to be limited in lysine; legumes tend to be limited in the sulfur amino acids like methionine), lower in leucine, and somewhat less digestible because of fiber and antinutrients. van Vliet, Burd, and van Loon (2015) review this directly: plant proteins generally have lower anabolic properties than animal proteins due to lower digestibility, lower essential amino acid content (especially leucine), and these specific amino acid limitations.

How we score quality: PDCAAS and DIAAS

For decades the standard metric was the PDCAAS (Protein Digestibility-Corrected Amino Acid Score). It works, but it has two weaknesses: it measures digestibility crudely (using fecal rather than ileal measurements, which overestimates how much you really absorbed) and it is capped at a maximum of 1.0, which compresses the top of the scale and hides real differences between high-quality proteins.

The newer FAO-endorsed metric is DIAAS (Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Score). Marinangeli and House (2017) explain the upgrade: DIAAS measures ileal digestibility (at the end of the small intestine, before the gut microbiome muddies the picture) for each indispensable amino acid individually, and it is not capped at 1.0. The result is a more accurate descriptor of protein quality, and the improvement is largest exactly where it matters: distinguishing among lower-quality proteins, where PDCAAS tended to flatten everything to the same ceiling.

Coaching translation

You do not need to memorize DIAAS scores. You need to know what they imply: not all "20 grams of protein" are equal. Twenty grams from chicken or whey delivers more usable, leucine-rich amino acids than twenty grams from, say, rice. For someone eating mostly plants, this means leaning a little higher on total protein and being smart about variety. Which is the next point.

Mixing plant sources solves most "incompleteness"

The old idea that vegetarians must carefully combine complementary proteins at every single meal (rice plus beans, precisely paired, or you fail) is outdated. The body maintains a small circulating pool of amino acids, so completeness works out over the course of a day, not minute by minute. If grains are short on lysine and legumes are short on methionine, eating both across the day covers both gaps. van Vliet, Burd, and van Loon (2015) evaluate exactly these strategies for improving the anabolic potential of plant proteins, including blending different plant sources together and blending plant with animal proteins, and find them effective. Variety is the fix.

So a plant-based eater is not doomed to incomplete protein. They simply have to do three things: eat a bit more total protein to account for lower digestibility, get variety across the day so the amino acid gaps cover each other, and pay attention to leucine-rich plant sources (soy, in particular, is complete and a strong performer). None of this is exotic. It is just applying the brick logic at the scale of a day instead of a single meal.

What a real threshold-clearing dose looks like on a plate

All of this stays abstract until you can translate "20 to 40 grams of quality protein, with enough leucine" into actual food, so here is the rough map. A palm-sized portion of cooked chicken, turkey, lean beef, or fish (about 120 to 150 grams) lands around 30 to 40 grams of protein and clears the threshold comfortably on its own. A cup of Greek yogurt or cottage cheese, three to four whole eggs, a scoop of whey, or a large glass of milk plus a smaller protein source will each get most people there. On the plant side the portions run larger because the protein density and the leucine content are lower: a cup and a half of cooked lentils, a block of firm tofu, a generous serving of tempeh, or a couple of cups of cooked beans, often paired with a grain to round out the amino acid profile across the day. The common beginner mistake is counting a few nuts, a spoon of peanut butter, or a slice of bread as a meaningful protein source. They contribute, but they do not anchor a meal, and a plate built on them will quietly miss the threshold while looking reasonable.

This is also where the "total versus distribution" debate gets put in its proper place. Total daily protein is the heavyweight variable; it matters more than any timing detail, and a person who hits a strong daily total in two large meals will still do well. Distribution is the finishing move on top of an already-adequate total, and it earns its keep mainly for the two groups named earlier: older adults fighting anabolic resistance, and trained people chasing every available gram of muscle. Get the total right first. Then, if the person belongs to one of those groups, spread it so each meal clears the threshold. Doing the second without the first is polishing a foundation that is not there.

Protein's second job: satiety, the thermic effect, and dieting

So far the chapter has treated protein as building material. It also does something the building story does not capture, and it matters most exactly when people are trying to change their bodyweight. Protein is the most filling and the most metabolically expensive macronutrient you can eat, and those two facts make it the single most useful nutrient to get right during fat loss.

Start with the thermic effect. Recall that your body spends energy to digest, absorb, and process food, an effect called the thermic effect of food. That cost is not the same across the three macronutrients. Fat is cheap to handle. Carbohydrate is moderate. Protein is by far the most expensive: a meaningful slice of the calories in protein is spent simply turning that protein into usable amino acids and disposing of the leftover nitrogen. Halton and Hu (2004) found this pattern in every study they reviewed that measured it; higher-protein meals consistently raised thermogenesis above fat or carbohydrate meals. The practical consequence is small but real: the net energy you keep from a protein calorie is a little lower than from a fat or carbohydrate calorie, because your body skims more off the top to process it.

Satiety is the bigger lever. Protein produces the strongest fullness per calorie of the three macronutrients, through a mix of slower digestion, the release of fullness-signaling gut hormones, and the body's apparent drive to hit a protein target before it stops seeking food. The applied payoff is straightforward: a higher-protein meal tends to keep you fuller longer, which makes a calorie deficit easier to sustain without the gnawing hunger that wrecks adherence. According to PubMed, Leidy and colleagues (2015), reviewing two decades of work, report that higher-protein meals reliably increase perceived fullness and satiety hormones. They temper it with one limit that keeps this from becoming hype: improved fullness does not always translate into eating less at the very next meal. The benefit shows up over a day and a week, in easier adherence, more than in any single sitting.

Why protein is the dieter's anchor

Put the two jobs together and you see why protein is the nutrient to protect when calories drop. In a deficit, the body is under pressure to give up tissue, and without enough incoming amino acids it will give up muscle along with fat. Raising protein does two things at once. It keeps muscle protein synthesis stimulated often enough to defend lean mass while you lose fat, and it leverages satiety and the thermic effect to make the deficit livable. The same review by Leidy and colleagues (2015) concludes that higher-protein energy-restricted diets produce greater fat loss and better preservation of lean mass than lower-protein diets at the same calorie level, with their reviewed studies clustering around roughly 1.2 to 1.6 grams per kilogram per day and per-meal amounts of about 25 to 30 grams. They also flag the real-world limiter plainly: the benefit only materializes for people who actually stick to the higher-protein pattern. The mechanism is sound, but adherence is what cashes it in.

This is the cleanest example in the chapter of protein doing both of its jobs at once. The building job protects the muscle. The fuel-handling and appetite job protects the diet. A coach who understands both does not reach for a fat-burner or a special supplement when a client is struggling to lose fat without losing strength. They raise protein, distribute it sensibly, and let the physiology do the work.

In practice

When a client says they are always hungry on their diet and losing strength, the first lever is almost never a new supplement. It is protein: get it to roughly the upper end of the target, spread it across the day, and anchor each meal with a complete source. The hunger usually eases and the strength usually holds, because you have addressed both of protein's jobs at the same time.

Setting a defensible protein target

Let us turn all of this into numbers you can actually use, while staying honest that targets are ranges, not laws. The old RDA of 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight is a floor designed to prevent deficiency in sedentary people, not a target for someone who wants to preserve or build muscle, manage weight, or age well. The applied literature consistently supports more.

A defensible general range for active or body-composition-focused people is roughly 1.6 to 2.2 grams per kilogram of body weight per day. For an 80-kilogram person, that is about 128 to 176 grams daily. Divide that across three to four meals and you land naturally near the per-meal threshold: four meals of 32 to 44 grams each, every one of them ringing the doorbell. Notice how cleanly the daily target and the per-meal threshold reinforce each other. They were derived from different studies but they point to the same plate.

Adjust upward for older adults (to defeat anabolic resistance and to clear that higher ~30-gram, ~2.8-gram-leucine per-meal threshold), and for people in a calorie deficit (where higher protein protects muscle and leverages satiety, the very threads from Halton and Hu, 2004, that make protein the dieter's best friend). Adjust your expectations, not necessarily the gram target, for plant-based eaters: same target, more attention to variety and digestibility.

Worked example

A 70-kilogram, 64-year-old client wants to preserve muscle and lose some fat. Target the upper end for her age and deficit: about 2.0 g/kg, so roughly 140 grams per day. Split across four meals, that is 35 grams each, comfortably above the older-adult threshold of ~30 grams. We deliberately do not let her load 80 grams at dinner and skip protein at breakfast, because under anabolic resistance the skipped meals stimulate almost nothing. The BCAA tub she asked about? Redundant. Her four 35-gram complete-protein meals already deliver more leucine and more total essential amino acids than that powder, and they keep her fuller, which helps the deficit. That is the whole consult, reasoned from mechanism.

Case study: the lifter whose tub was doing nothing

Case

Hassan is 31, trains with weights four days a week, and is frustrated that his arms and shoulders have stalled for months. He weighs 82 kilograms. He shows up with a familiar setup: a 45-dollar tub of BCAAs he sips during every workout, because an ad promised it would "feed the muscle." When his actual eating is laid out, the picture is different from what he assumed. Breakfast is coffee and a banana. Lunch is a chicken wrap with maybe 25 grams of protein. He trains at 6 p.m. on a near-empty stomach, sipping BCAAs. Dinner is large, around 70 grams of protein, often the only serious protein of his day. His daily total lands near 110 grams, under 1.4 grams per kilogram, and it arrives almost entirely in one evening pile.

Run Hassan through this chapter and the stalled arms stop being a mystery. His problem is not that he is missing a magic supplement. It is that his protein is both too low and badly distributed, and the one product he is paying for addresses neither.

Start with distribution. Hassan rings the muscle's doorbell hard exactly once a day, at dinner, where most of that 70-gram bolus overshoots the synthesis threshold and gets oxidized rather than built. For the other twenty-some hours, his muscle sits in low gear: a protein-free breakfast, a borderline lunch, and a fasted training session feeding off BCAAs that supply three of the nine bricks. The dose-response work (Moore et al., 2009; Witard et al., 2014) says his single big dinner is mostly wasted past the first 30 to 40 grams, and the distribution work (Areta et al., 2013) says the same total spread into three or four threshold-clearing meals would stimulate building several times instead of once.

Now the tub. During his fasted workout, those BCAAs trigger a little synthesis, but with no other essential amino acids arriving, the cell has to liberate the missing six bricks by breaking down existing muscle (Wolfe, 2017). He is, in effect, paying to rob one part of the muscle to half-build another. The honest verdict from the brick model is that the tub is redundant the moment he eats enough complete protein. It is not poison; it is just an expensive way to consume three amino acids he should already be getting from food, and it is doing nothing for the leucine signal that a real meal would not do better.

The fix is unglamorous and entirely mechanism-driven. Raise his total to roughly 1.6 to 2.0 grams per kilogram, about 130 to 160 grams a day, which the long-term training data (Morton et al., 2018) supports for someone chasing growth. Put a complete, leucine-rich source at breakfast and lunch so those meals cross the threshold, and eat a real protein-containing meal around training instead of sipping powder on an empty stomach. Shrink the dinner overshoot. Retire the BCAA tub, or finish it and do not rebuy. Same training, far more of the daily stimulus actually captured, and 45 dollars freed up for food that does the job better. That is the whole consult, reasoned from the same physiology the chapter built.

Key Takeaways

  • Protein is structural, not just fuel, has no real storage depot, and carries the highest thermic effect and strongest satiety per calorie (Halton & Hu, 2004). That is why it earns priority on the plate.
  • Muscle is in constant turnover; net muscle change equals synthesis minus breakdown. A meal flips synthesis on, and leucine is the trigger that rings the doorbell.
  • MPS shows a dose-response that plateaus: roughly 20 grams of quality protein maximally stimulates it in young adults, with no further gain at 40 grams (Moore et al., 2009; Witard et al., 2014).
  • The per-meal threshold rises with age. Older adults need about 30 grams of protein (~2.8 g leucine) per meal to overcome anabolic resistance (Layman, 2024; Zaromskyte et al., 2021).
  • Spreading protein across three to four threshold-clearing meals beats one large bolus, especially for older adults and trained individuals (Areta et al., 2013).
  • BCAAs trigger but cannot build. Completing a new protein needs all nine essential amino acids; three bricks cannot finish the wall, so isolated BCAAs add little when total protein is adequate (Wolfe, 2017).
  • Protein quality combines completeness and digestibility; DIAAS measures it more accurately than PDCAAS (Marinangeli & House, 2017). Mixing plant sources across a day solves most apparent incompleteness (van Vliet et al., 2015).
  • Net muscle change is synthesis minus breakdown across days; resistance exercise makes muscle more responsive to the protein that follows it, and adding protein augments long-term gains up to about 1.6 g/kg/day (Morton et al., 2018).
  • Protein is also the most filling and most thermogenic macronutrient, which makes higher-protein diets preserve more lean mass and ease adherence during a calorie deficit (Leidy et al., 2015).
  • A defensible general target is ~1.6 to 2.2 g/kg/day, distributed so each meal clears the threshold.
