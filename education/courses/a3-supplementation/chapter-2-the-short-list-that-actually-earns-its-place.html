The Short List That Actually Earns Its Place

The handful of supplements with a real signal, and the hype layer sitting on top of each one.

38 min read 14 cited sources

In Chapter 1 you learned to run a supplement through the gap test: is there a real deficiency or shortfall in the person in front of you, does the supplement actually close that gap, and does closing it change an outcome you care about? Most products fail somewhere in that chain. They treat a gap you do not have, or they fill a gap that turns out not to matter for the thing you were promised. That is not cynicism. That is just what happens when you hold a product to a fair standard.

So the obvious question is: if almost everything fails, what survives? This chapter is the answer, and it is a short answer. A small set of names comes up again and again with evidence good enough to take seriously. Three of them earn a place because they fill a real nutritional gap for a lot of people: vitamin D, omega-3 fatty acids, and magnesium. Three more earn a place for a different reason, because they reliably change a performance or convenience outcome when you use them correctly: creatine, caffeine, and a protein supplement. We are not here to cheerlead any of them. Each has a genuine signal and a marketing halo bolted on top, and your job by the end is to tell those two layers apart with confidence.

What "earns its place" actually means

Earning a place on the short list is a higher bar than "has some evidence." Nearly every supplement on a store shelf has some study with its name in the title. The bar here is different. A supplement earns its place when we can name a real population that is plausibly short on the nutrient, point to a defensible dose region, and tie supplementation to an outcome that matters, with trial evidence rather than wishful mechanism.

That phrasing matters because the three nutrients in this chapter all clear that bar for someone, under some conditions, for some outcomes. They do not clear it for everyone, always, for everything. The work is in the qualifiers. A coach who can recite "vitamin D is good for you" is repeating a slogan. A coach who can say "vitamin D supplementation has the strongest case in adults over 75, in pregnancy, in children, and in people with prediabetes, and a much weaker case for healthy adults under 75" is actually using the framework.

Think About It

Before reading on, try to state the gap test in your own words for a friend who takes vitamin D "for immunity." What are the three links in the chain, and which one is most likely to break for that specific claim?

The distinction that runs through everything: evidence for an outcome versus evidence in general

This is the single most important idea in the chapter, so we are putting it first and building the rest on top of it. When someone says a supplement "has evidence," your reflex should be to ask: evidence for what? Strength of evidence is not a property of the pill. It is a property of the pill paired with a specific outcome.

Take omega-3 fatty acids. The evidence that a high dose lowers triglycerides is strong and consistent. The evidence that the same capsules lift your mood on an average day is thin and inconsistent. Same molecule, same bottle, completely different evidentiary footing depending on which outcome you name. If you let the strong claim borrow credibility for the weak one, you have been fooled, and you will fool other people.

People slip here constantly. They hear "studies support omega-3" and treat that as a blanket endorsement of every use. But a meta-analysis showing a triglyceride effect tells you nothing reliable about depression, joint pain, or memory. Each outcome has its own pile of trials, its own consistency, its own risk of bias. You have to evaluate them separately, every time.

The reason this carries weight: the flashier supplement categories covered later in this course lean hard on this exact trick. They show you a real effect on a surrogate measure, a blood marker or a lab outcome, and let your brain generalize it into the life outcome you actually wanted. The defense is a habit. Always finish the sentence. Not "this has evidence," but "this has evidence for which outcome, in whom, and how strong."

Coaching translation

In real life, when a label or a friend says "it's backed by science," your one-line response is a question: backed for what? If they cannot name the specific outcome and roughly how good the evidence is, the claim is doing more marketing than informing. That single question filters out most supplement nonsense before you ever check a study.

Vitamin D: a real population-wide shortfall, oversold for the wrong outcomes

Vitamin D is the cleanest example on the list of a genuine, widespread gap. Humans are built to synthesize it in skin exposed to ultraviolet B radiation. Modern life moved indoors, spread us across latitudes where winter sun cannot do the job, and added sunscreen and clothing on top. The result is that a large fraction of people in temperate countries run low at least seasonally. That part is not controversial.

The controversy is what to do about it and what supplementation actually buys you. This is exactly the place where a real gap gets oversold for outcomes the trials do not support.

What counts as low, and why the number is contested

Vitamin D status is measured as serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D, written 25(OH)D. Roughly, levels below about 30 nmol/L are widely agreed to be deficient, the range associated with rickets and osteomalacia. The fight is over the gray zone above that. Some bodies call below 50 nmol/L "insufficient," others treat the 30 to 50 range as adequate for most people. That definitional choice alone can move a population from "mostly fine" to "epidemic of deficiency," which tells you to be suspicious of any prevalence headline that does not state its cutoff.

Who the strongest evidence actually points to

In 2024 the Endocrine Society published a clinical practice guideline built primarily on randomized controlled trial evidence rather than the older observational associations (Demay et al., 2024). Its conclusions are bracing if you grew up hearing vitamin D recommended for everyone. The guideline recommends empiric supplementation, meaning supplementation without first testing blood levels, for a specific short list: children aged 1 to 18, adults aged 75 and older, people who are pregnant, and people with prediabetes. For healthy adults under 75, it concludes that supplementation above the current Dietary Reference Intakes is unlikely to provide meaningful benefit. It also explicitly recommends against routine 25(OH)D screening in the general healthy population.

Read that again, because it overturns a common habit. The most rigorous recent guideline says most healthy middle-aged adults do not need to test their vitamin D and do not need a supplement beyond ordinary dietary reference intakes. The strong cases cluster at the edges of life and in specific conditions: the very young, the old, pregnancy, and prediabetes.

The VITAL trial and the outcomes that did not pan out

Much of this caution traces to VITAL, a large, well-run trial that tested 2000 IU per day of vitamin D3 against placebo in 25,871 U.S. adults over a median 5.3 years, in a design that also tested omega-3s at the same time (Manson et al., 2020). For the headline primary outcomes, vitamin D did not significantly reduce total invasive cancer incidence or major cardiovascular events. There was a promising but secondary signal for reduced cancer mortality, the kind of finding that is worth following up but not strong enough to build a recommendation on.

This is the heart of the vitamin D story. The supplement reliably raises blood levels. What it does not reliably do, in healthy well-nourished adults, is move the big disease outcomes it has been sold for: cancer prevention, heart attack prevention, "immune support" in any dramatic sense. The gap is real. The downstream payoff for an already healthy adult is mostly not there.

Testing: when the blood test earns its cost, and when it does not

The serum 25(OH)D test is cheap and widely available, which is part of the problem. Easy access turns a useful clinical tool into a habit people reach for without a question to answer. The 2024 guideline is blunt about this. It recommends against routine screening in healthy adults, because for most of them the result will not change a sensible course of action: a healthy adult with adequate sun and diet does not need a test, and a healthy adult with low sun exposure can take a modest dose without one. Testing earns its cost when the answer would actually change management. That includes people with conditions that disrupt vitamin D handling, such as malabsorption disorders, chronic kidney disease, or osteoporosis under active treatment, and people on doses high enough that you want to confirm you are not overshooting.

There is a second reason to be skeptical of casual testing. A single 25(OH)D value swings with the season, with recent sun, and across different lab assays, so one number caught in late winter can look alarming and one caught in August can look reassuring for the same person. If you do test, interpret the number against the time of year and the person in front of you, not against a flat cutoff printed on a lab slip.

Dosing and the ceiling that matters

For someone who genuinely needs repletion, the workable region is roughly 1000 to 2000 IU of vitamin D3 per day, taken with a meal that contains some fat, because vitamin D is fat-soluble and absorbs better that way. That range raises serum levels into the adequate zone for the large majority of adults without flirting with the upper end. Going much higher does not buy more benefit, and it can eventually buy harm. Vitamin D is stored in fat and the liver rather than flushed out like a water-soluble vitamin, so chronic megadosing can accumulate. Sustained very high intakes, generally in the range that pushes well past 4000 IU per day for long periods, can raise blood calcium to the point of kidney and vascular trouble. This is not a reason for fear at sensible doses. It is a reason to ignore the "more is better" instinct that the supplement aisle quietly encourages, because with a stored, fat-soluble vitamin that instinct is exactly backwards.

The other side: a defensible baseline dose

To be fair to the nutrient, there is a reasonable counter-position. A 2024 narrative review argues that 2000 IU per day of vitamin D3 is a safe, effective dose that raises serum 25(OH)D above 50 nmol/L in more than 99 percent of adults, and cites a meta-analysis of 80 trials covering 82,210 participants suggesting a modest reduction in all-cause mortality (Pludowski et al., 2024). Note the register here. This is a narrative review arguing a position, which sits lower on the evidence hierarchy than a guideline built on systematic RCT appraisal, and "modest reduction in all-cause mortality" pooled across 80 heterogeneous trials is a softer claim than it sounds. But the dose itself, 2000 IU, is well within safe limits and is a sensible target for someone who genuinely has low sun exposure.

So the honest synthesis is this. Vitamin D supplementation has a strong case for filling a measured or highly likely deficiency, especially in the at-risk groups. It has a weak case as a disease-prevention pill for healthy adults who already have adequate status. The dose region that does the job for most people who do need it sits around 1000 to 2000 IU per day, and going far higher does not buy more benefit.

Think About It

A healthy 35-year-old who works outdoors in a sunny climate asks whether she should start vitamin D "to be safe." Walk through the gap test. Where does her case differ from a housebound 80-year-old at high latitude in winter?

Coaching translation

In practice, this means vitamin D is a context drug, not a universal one. If you have low sun exposure, dark skin at high latitude, you are older, pregnant, or have prediabetes, the case for a modest daily dose is genuinely good. If you are a healthy adult getting regular sun, the supplement is mostly buying you a slightly higher number on a test you probably did not need. Don't confuse "this raises my blood level" with "this changes my health."

Omega-3 fatty acids: a credible signal blurred by a source-and-dose story labels hide

Omega-3s are the second name on the short list, and they are interesting because the evidence is genuinely mixed in a way that rewards careful reading. There is a real cardiovascular and anti-inflammatory signal here. There is also a dosing and source story that most product labels smear over completely.

The two molecules that matter, and the one that mostly does not

When we say "omega-3," we usually mean three different fatty acids. EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) and DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) are the long-chain marine forms that do most of the physiological work. ALA (alpha-linolenic acid) is the plant form found in flax and walnuts, which your body converts to EPA and DHA only inefficiently, often in the low single-digit percentages. So when a label brags about omega-3 content but the source is flax, you are mostly getting a precursor your body converts poorly, not the active forms.

EPA and DHA are not interchangeable either. A systematic review of trials comparing them head to head found they have distinct effects on cardiometabolic risk factors: differing actions on triglycerides, LDL particles, inflammation, platelet reactivity, and endothelial function (Cottin et al., 2011). In nature and in most fish-oil capsules they arrive bundled together, which is fine, but it means that when a trial uses purified EPA only, you cannot assume a mixed EPA-plus-DHA product will behave identically.

The outcome where the evidence is strongest

This is the cleanest application of the evidence-for-an-outcome rule in the chapter. For lowering triglycerides, high-dose omega-3s have robust, consistent evidence. That effect is dose-dependent and reliable enough that prescription-strength omega-3 products exist specifically for hypertriglyceridemia. If that is your outcome, the case is strong.

For broader cardiovascular outcomes, the picture is genuinely contested, and you should hold both findings in your head at once. A 2021 meta-analysis of 38 trials covering 149,051 participants found that omega-3 supplementation was associated with reductions in cardiovascular mortality, non-fatal heart attack, coronary heart disease events, and major adverse cardiovascular events, with EPA-only formulations showing larger relative reductions than EPA-plus-DHA combinations (Khan et al., 2021). That is an encouraging result, but the same analysis found omega-3s increased incident atrial fibrillation, and EPA monotherapy carried higher bleeding risk. Benefit and harm in the same molecule, which is exactly why dose and individual context matter.

Pulling the other direction, a large Cochrane systematic review concluded that increasing EPA and DHA, based mainly on supplement trials, has little or no effect on overall mortality or cardiovascular health, and that earlier optimistic findings appeared to spring from trials with higher risk of bias (Abdelhamid et al., 2018). And recall that the vitamin D trial we already met, VITAL, also tested 1 gram per day of marine omega-3s and did not find a significant reduction in its primary cardiovascular endpoint (Manson et al., 2020).

So which is it? The honest reading is that the effect, if present in the general healthy population, is modest and sensitive to dose, formulation, and the baseline risk of the people studied. People who eat very little fish and are at higher cardiovascular risk have the most plausible upside. A healthy person already eating oily fish twice a week is in the territory where supplements add little.

Fish or algae? The source question with a clean answer

One practical question has a refreshingly clear answer. If you want EPA and DHA without fish, do algae-based supplements actually deliver? A 2025 randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in 74 adults directly compared plasma bioavailability of DHA and EPA from microalgal oil versus fish oil over 6 and 14 weeks, and found microalgal oil statistically non-inferior to fish oil (Bailey et al., 2025). Algae is where fish get their omega-3s in the first place, so this makes biological sense. For vegetarians, vegans, or anyone who dislikes fish, algae is a legitimate, equivalent source of the active forms, not a compromise.

Coaching translation

In practice, this means: if your goal is lower triglycerides, omega-3s have a strong case and the dose needs to be meaningful, often in the multi-gram range that food alone rarely reaches. If your goal is general "heart health" and you already eat oily fish regularly, the supplement is probably redundant. Match the source to the active forms, EPA and DHA from fish or algae, not ALA from flax, and remember that more is not automatically safer once you are at higher doses, given the atrial fibrillation and bleeding signals. Food first: a couple of servings of oily fish a week often does the job the capsule is sold for.

Magnesium: widely under-consumed, real for sleep and stress, easy to exaggerate

Magnesium is the third name, and it is the one where the gap is most clearly real at the population level while the outcome evidence is the softest. Both halves of that sentence are true at once, and a good coach holds them together.

The gap is genuine

A landmark review established that nearly half of the U.S. population, 48 percent in the 2005 to 2006 national data, consumed less magnesium from food than the requirement (Rosanoff et al., 2012). Magnesium is a cofactor in hundreds of enzymatic reactions, and the review synthesized associations between low magnesium status and a long list of conditions: type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, elevated C-reactive protein, hypertension, atherosclerotic vascular disease, sudden cardiac death, osteoporosis, and migraine. The authors argue, persuasively, that the health consequences of marginal magnesium intake are systematically underestimated, partly because magnesium has been studied far less than calcium.

So the gap test passes its first link easily. A real, common shortfall exists. Where the everyday wellness market overreaches is the second and third links: what supplementation actually fixes, and for which outcome.

Associations are not the same as trial-proven effects

Here is the trap. That impressive list of conditions tied to low magnesium is built largely on associations. People with low magnesium intake tend to have worse cardiometabolic profiles. That is real, but it does not tell you that swallowing a magnesium pill reverses any of it, because people who eat little magnesium also eat fewer vegetables, legumes, nuts, and whole grains and differ in dozens of other ways. The supplement marketing quietly upgrades "associated with" into "fixes," which is precisely the move the framework is built to catch.

The sleep evidence, read honestly

Sleep is where magnesium gets its biggest popular reputation, so look at the actual data. A 2021 meta-analysis pooled 3 randomized trials covering 151 older adults and found magnesium supplementation reduced sleep onset latency, the time it takes to fall asleep, by 17.36 minutes versus placebo, a statistically significant result (Mah & Pitre, 2021). That sounds great until you read the qualifiers the authors themselves stress. Total sleep time did not improve significantly. All three trials were at moderate to high risk of bias. The overall evidence quality was rated low to very low under GRADE. The authors' careful conclusion is that the literature is too weak for firm clinical recommendations, but that because magnesium is cheap, accessible, and low-risk, it may be reasonable to try for insomnia symptoms.

That is a textbook example of evidence-for-an-outcome thinking. "Magnesium helps you fall asleep faster" has weak but real, plausibly meaningful support in older adults with poor sleep. "Magnesium boosts your general energy" or "magnesium melts away stress" are much vaguer claims with much thinner trial support. Same mineral, very different evidentiary footing.

Stress, and the loop with sleep

The stress story is similar: biologically plausible, popularly inflated. Magnesium is involved in regulating the stress-response system and neuromuscular excitability, and low status is associated with markers of inflammation like elevated CRP (Rosanoff et al., 2012). But the leap from "involved in stress physiology" to "this pill calms your nervous system" outruns the trial evidence. Much of the felt benefit people report likely runs through the sleep channel: sleep a little better, feel less frayed. That is a real and worthwhile loop, but notice it is the modest, mechanism-honest version of the claim, not the "magnesium is nature's chill pill" version on the label.

Form and dose, briefly

Different magnesium salts differ in absorption and side effects. Magnesium oxide is cheap but poorly absorbed and tends to act as a laxative, which is its own useful clue about bioavailability. Citrate, glycinate, and malate are better absorbed. Effective supplemental doses in the sleep and general-repletion literature typically sit in the low hundreds of milligrams of elemental magnesium per day, comfortably below the level where loose stools become the limiting factor. And as always, food does much of the work: leafy greens, legumes, nuts, seeds, and whole grains are magnesium-dense, and someone eating those regularly may have no gap to fill at all.

Think About It

The magnesium-and-disease association list is long and alarming. Using the framework, explain to a skeptical friend why that list is a reason to eat more magnesium-rich food but a much weaker reason to expect a supplement to prevent those diseases.

Coaching translation

In practice, this means magnesium is a sensible, low-cost option to try if you eat few magnesium-rich foods and struggle to fall asleep, and you should judge it by whether your sleep onset actually improves, not by a vague sense that it should help everything. Pick a well-absorbed form, keep the dose modest, and remember that a plate with greens, beans, nuts, and seeds is doing the same job from a different direction. Don't let an honest, modest sleep benefit get inflated into a cure for stress, fatigue, and your cardiovascular risk all at once.

Creatine monohydrate: the best-evidenced performance supplement, and quietly a brain one too

The three nutrients above earn their place by filling a dietary gap. Creatine earns its place a different way. It is not correcting a deficiency in most people who use it, and it is not a vitamin. It is the single most rigorously studied performance supplement in existence, with a depth of trial evidence that almost nothing else in the supplement aisle can match. If you only ever trusted one performance product, the evidence says this is the one.

The mechanism, in plain terms

Your muscles run their fastest, most explosive efforts on a fuel called ATP, and they keep only a few seconds of it on hand. To extend that window, muscle uses a backup system: a stored compound called phosphocreatine donates a phosphate to rapidly rebuild ATP between efforts. Think of phosphocreatine as a small reserve battery sitting next to the main one, topping it back up between hard pushes. You make some creatine in your liver and kidneys and get more from meat and fish, but most people walk around with their muscle stores only partly filled. Supplementing creatine tops those stores up toward their ceiling, which means more rebuilt ATP, which means you can do a little more work before fatigue sets in: another rep or two, a slightly harder sprint, a touch more total volume across a session. Over weeks and months, that extra accumulated work is what drives the measurable gains in strength and muscle, not the powder itself. Creatine does not build muscle. It lets you train slightly harder, and the harder training builds the muscle.

Who actually benefits, and who does not

The clearest beneficiaries are people doing repeated high-intensity, short-burst work: resistance training, sprinting, jumping, and field or court sports with a lot of stop-and-go. According to PubMed, the International Society of Sports Nutrition position stand concludes that creatine monohydrate is the most effective ergogenic nutritional supplement available for increasing high-intensity exercise capacity and lean body mass during training (Kreider et al., 2017). Two groups see less of the muscle-and-strength effect. People who already eat a lot of meat and fish start closer to full, so they have less room to gain. And a minority of people are biological "non-responders" whose muscle stores are already near the ceiling, so topping up does little. For pure endurance athletes, the strength benefit is mostly beside the point, though the small added body water from creatine is occasionally a consideration in weight-class sports.

The more surprising frontier is cognition. The same energy logic that applies to muscle applies to the brain, which is metabolically expensive and uses phosphocreatine to buffer its own ATP. Based on articles retrieved from PubMed, a meta-analysis of randomized trials found that creatine supplementation improved measures of memory in healthy people, with the effect notably larger in older adults aged 66 to 76 than in younger adults (Prokopidis et al., 2023). A separate review of trials across adults reported benefits concentrated in memory, attention, and processing speed, and was larger in people under stress conditions such as sleep deprivation (Xu et al., 2024). Read those carefully and the pattern is consistent with the rest of this chapter: the effect is real but modest, it shows up most where the system is taxed (aging, sleep loss), and it is weaker or absent in well-rested young adults running at full capacity. This is a promising, honestly stated signal, not a "smart pill."

Dose: loading is optional, daily is the point

There are two routes to a full muscle store, and they end in the same place. The classic loading protocol uses roughly 20 grams per day, split into four doses, for 5 to 7 days, which saturates muscle quickly, followed by a maintenance dose of 3 to 5 grams per day. The slower route skips loading and simply takes 3 to 5 grams per day from the start; stores fill over about three to four weeks instead of one. Loading is faster, not better. The only real reason to load is impatience or a competition on the calendar. The downside of loading is that the larger split doses are the most common cause of the mild stomach upset some people report. Timing across the day barely matters for a compound that works by topping up a reservoir, so "take it whenever you will remember it" is sound advice. Monohydrate is the form with essentially all of the evidence behind it; the fancier, pricier forms marketed as superior have not shown a meaningful advantage and usually cost more for the same result.

Safety, and the myths that will not die

Creatine carries more long-term safety data than almost any supplement on the shelf. The position stand concludes that supplementation up to 30 grams per day for as long as five years is safe and well tolerated in healthy people, including across age groups from infants to the elderly in the clinical literature (Kreider et al., 2017). The persistent claims that it damages the kidneys or causes dehydration and cramping have not held up in controlled research in healthy individuals; the kidney myth largely traces to a misreading of creatinine, a normal byproduct of creatine that rises slightly and harmlessly on supplementation and can be mistaken for a kidney signal on a lab test. People with existing kidney disease are a reasonable exception who should clear it with a clinician, simply because they warrant caution with anything that shifts that chemistry. The honest summary is that the safety case here is unusually strong, which is exactly why it stands out on a list where most products cannot even establish a benefit.

Coaching translation

In practice, creatine monohydrate is the rare performance supplement that clears the bar this whole chapter sets. If you do resistance or stop-and-go training, 3 to 5 grams a day of plain monohydrate is a low-cost, well-evidenced way to get a little more out of the training you are already doing. Skip the loading phase unless you are in a hurry; it changes the speed, not the destination. Judge it over a couple of months by your training numbers, not by how you feel on day three. And ignore the cramping and kidney scares for healthy people, the controlled data does not support them.

Caffeine: a genuine performance and alertness tool with a tolerance catch

Caffeine is the most widely used drug on the planet, which makes it easy to forget that it is, in the performance sense, a real and well-evidenced ergogenic aid. It earns its place not by filling a gap but by reliably shifting an outcome you can measure, as long as you respect dose, timing, and the way the body adapts to it.

The mechanism, in plain terms

A molecule called adenosine builds up in your brain across the day and binds to receptors that slow you down and make you feel tired; it is part of how sleep pressure accumulates. Caffeine is shaped enough like adenosine to sit in those same receptors without activating them, blocking the "slow down" signal. The result is reduced perception of fatigue and effort, which is most of why a hard effort feels a little easier with caffeine on board. That perceived-effort effect is the through-line connecting the gym, the road, and the desk: the work is not actually lighter, your brain is just reporting it as less heavy.

Who benefits, and the outcome where the evidence is strongest

According to PubMed, the International Society of Sports Nutrition position stand on caffeine concludes that it acutely improves many aspects of exercise performance, with the most consistent, moderate-to-large benefits showing up in aerobic endurance (Guest et al., 2021). The benefits extend, generally smaller, to muscular endurance, strength, sprinting, jumping, and throwing, and they appear in both trained and untrained people. On the cognitive side, the same review notes caffeine reliably improves attention and vigilance in most people, and can rescue physical and mental performance under sleep deprivation. So the strongest single case is endurance performance and alertness, especially when you are tired; the case for raw maximal strength is real but more modest.

Who benefits least is just as informative. Response varies between individuals, partly down to genetics affecting how fast you clear caffeine, and partly down to habit. People anxious or jittery on caffeine, or who sleep poorly after it, get a worse trade. And the heaviest daily users have partly blunted the very effect they are chasing, which is the tolerance catch below.

Dose and timing

The position stand puts the performance-effective range at 3 to 6 milligrams per kilogram of body mass, with benefits potentially appearing as low as 2 mg/kg, and notes that very high doses around 9 mg/kg add side effects without adding benefit (Guest et al., 2021). For a 70 kilogram person, the effective range is roughly 210 to 420 milligrams, which is a meaningful jump above a single ordinary cup of coffee. The most common timing is about 60 minutes before the effort, which lines up with how long caffeine takes to peak in the blood from a capsule or coffee; faster-absorbing forms like gum can need less lead time. More is emphatically not better here: past the effective window you mostly add the jitters, the racing heart, and the sleep disruption without adding performance.

That sleep point deserves its own line, because it is where caffeine quietly costs people. Caffeine has a long half-life, commonly four to six hours and longer in slow metabolizers, so an afternoon dose can still be circulating at bedtime and erode the depth and quantity of your sleep even if you fall asleep fine. For most people, keeping caffeine to the first half of the day protects the sleep that does far more for next-day performance and mood than the caffeine itself ever could.

Tolerance: the catch that defines smart use

The body adapts to regular caffeine by adjusting its adenosine receptors, which is why the daily heavy drinker needs two cups just to feel normal and gets a smaller performance lift from a given dose than an occasional user. This is the single most important practical fact about caffeine as a tool. It is most powerful when used strategically rather than constantly. Some athletes deliberately taper their habitual intake down for several days before an event so that a competition-day dose lands on a more sensitive system. There is no single mandatory protocol, but the principle is durable: the more you lean on it every day, the less it does on the day you actually need it, and the more you are simply paying a withdrawal-management cost (the morning headache, the flatness) for no net gain.

Coaching translation

In practice, caffeine is a legitimate, well-evidenced tool for endurance, alertness, and getting through a session when you are tired, in a dose of roughly 3 to 6 mg per kilogram about an hour beforehand. Two habits separate smart use from a slow-leaking trap: keep it out of the second half of your day so it does not quietly wreck your sleep, and do not run your daily intake so high that tolerance steals the effect. Used occasionally and strategically, it works. Used as a constant crutch, you mostly end up managing your own withdrawal.

Protein powder: not a magic supplement, a convenience tool that earns its place

A protein supplement belongs on this list, but for a reason that breaks the usual supplement frame. It does almost nothing that food cannot. Its value is that it makes hitting a protein target easy, portable, and cheap when food is inconvenient. That is a smaller, more honest claim than the labels make, and it is exactly why the supplement is genuinely useful: it solves a logistics problem, not a biochemical one.

What protein supplementation actually does

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed, a meta-analysis of 49 randomized trials in nearly 1,900 adults found that protein supplementation significantly enhanced gains in strength and muscle size during prolonged resistance training (Morton et al., 2018). That sounds like a ringing endorsement until you read the key qualifier the same analysis found: the benefit of supplementation plateaued once total daily protein intake reached about 1.6 grams per kilogram of body mass. Past that point, adding more protein, from a shake or anything else, produced no further gains in muscle. The lesson is precise. Protein supports muscle when you are training and falling short of your total protein needs. The powder is one convenient way to reach that total. It is not a special muscle-building potion that works beyond the total your body can use.

Notice what that does to the marketing. A shake is not building muscle through some proprietary effect; it is helping you reach a daily protein number that food could also reach. Once you are at that number, the next shake is just expensive food. That reframes the entire category from "muscle supplement" to "a clean, fast way to hit a target," which is what it honestly is.

Whey, casein, or plant: do the differences matter?

The three common types differ in real but mostly minor ways. Whey is a fast-digesting milk protein, rich in the amino acid leucine that helps trigger muscle protein synthesis, which makes it a sensible default around training. Casein is the other milk protein and digests slowly, releasing amino acids over hours, which is why it is sometimes chosen before a long gap such as overnight. Plant proteins (soy, pea, rice, and blends) work too, with one wrinkle: a single plant source can be lower in one or another essential amino acid, so blends or a slightly larger serving close that gap. For the everyday goal of reaching a daily protein target, these distinctions are real but small. Total daily protein and overall diet quality outweigh which powder you pick. Someone reaching their target on plant blends is not at a meaningful disadvantage against someone using whey.

Who needs it and who does not

The people who get the most from a protein supplement are those with a high protein target and a hard time hitting it from food: people training hard for muscle or strength, older adults fighting age-related muscle loss who often under-eat protein, and busy people who would otherwise skip the protein in a rushed meal. The people who need it least are those already hitting their protein target comfortably from food, for whom a shake is simply a convenient option rather than a requirement, and anyone expecting the powder itself to do something a chicken breast or a bowl of lentils and yogurt would not. Food first remains the rule; the supplement is the tool you reach for when food first is losing to a busy day.

Coaching translation

In practice, treat protein powder as fast, portable food, not as a special supplement. If you are training and struggling to reach your daily protein from meals, a scoop or two makes the target easy to hit, which is the whole benefit. Once you are reliably at roughly 1.6 grams per kilogram, more does not add muscle; it just adds cost. Pick the type that fits your diet and your stomach, whey, casein, or a plant blend, and remember that the powder is convenience, while the daily total is what actually drives the result.

The general population versus the trained individual

One layer worth flagging, because this course serves both general learners and aspiring coaches. Everything above is framed for the general population, which is the right default. Trained or specific subgroups can differ, and it is a secondary layer, not the headline.

Heavy training can raise turnover and losses of some nutrients, and athletes who restrict food groups, train indoors, or sweat heavily can drift into shortfalls a sedentary person would not. Magnesium is lost in sweat, so very high training volumes can nudge requirements up. Vitamin D matters for the indoor-training athlete at high latitude just as it does for anyone else with low sun exposure, and there is interest, though not settled proof, in its role in muscle function. Omega-3s have a developing literature around exercise recovery and inflammation that is promising but not yet at the strength of the triglyceride evidence. The point is not to memorize athlete protocols. It is to notice that "who benefits" is a moving target, and the trained body is a different context that can change the gap analysis at the margins without overturning the framework.

The supplement does not have evidence. The supplement paired with a named outcome, in a named population, has evidence of a certain strength. Finish the sentence every time, and most of the hype dissolves on its own.

The operating rule of this chapter

Case study: Carmen and the cabinet full of "evidence-based" supplements

Case

Carmen is 41, lifts weights three times a week, works long indoor days at a high latitude, and reads enough to be suspicious of obvious junk. Her cabinet reflects that: she has bought only supplements she believes are backed by science. There is vitamin D, fish oil, magnesium for sleep, a tub of a premium creatine blend marketed as "advanced absorption," caffeine pills she takes whenever she remembers, including some afternoons, and three different protein powders. She feels vaguely virtuous about the collection and vaguely unsure whether any of it is doing anything. She wants to know what to keep, what to fix, and what is just expensive.

Run Carmen through the chapter and the cabinet sorts itself quickly. Start with the gap supplements. Her vitamin D has a strong case: long indoor days at high latitude is the exact context where endogenous synthesis falls short, so a modest daily dose around 1000 to 2000 IU is reasonable, and she does not need to keep testing her blood to justify it. Her magnesium is defensible if her diet is light on greens, beans, nuts, and seeds, and she should judge it honestly, by whether her sleep onset actually improves, not by a hope that it fixes everything. Her fish oil is the first thing to question: if she already eats oily fish a couple of times a week and her goal is vague "heart health" rather than lowering triglycerides, the capsules are likely redundant. The supplement is not wrong, it is just probably solving a gap she does not have.

Now the performance shelf, where her instincts are good but her execution leaks value. The creatine is worth keeping, but the "advanced absorption" blend is paying extra for a claim the evidence does not support; plain monohydrate at 3 to 5 grams a day does the same job for less, and as a lifter she is squarely in the group that benefits. The caffeine is a real tool she is using carelessly. Taken before a training session it can genuinely help, but the afternoon doses are quietly taxing the sleep that does more for her recovery and mood than the caffeine ever will, and dosing it "whenever" with daily use builds the tolerance that blunts the effect. Pulling caffeine into the first half of her day, and using it more deliberately around training, would make a smaller amount do more.

Finally the three protein powders, which is one logistics tool bought three times. For her goal, the type barely matters next to her daily protein total; one tub that suits her stomach and her diet is plenty, and the shake is simply a fast way to reach her target on busy days, not a muscle potion. The pattern across the whole cabinet is the lesson of the chapter in miniature. Carmen did not buy junk. She bought real supplements and then let the marketing decide the dose, the form, the timing, and the necessity. The fix is not more products or more discipline. It is finishing the sentence on each one: evidence for what, in her specifically, at what dose, and is the gap even hers to fill. Most of the cabinet survives that test in a leaner, cheaper, better-timed form, and that is what using the framework actually looks like.

Key Takeaways

  • Strength of evidence is a property of a supplement-plus-outcome pairing, never of the pill alone. Always ask "evidence for what, in whom, and how strong."
  • Vitamin D fills a genuine, widespread shortfall, but the strongest supplementation case is for children, adults 75 and older, pregnancy, and prediabetes; healthy adults under 75 are unlikely to gain from extra dosing, and routine screening is not recommended (Demay et al., 2024).
  • VITAL showed vitamin D reliably raises blood levels but did not significantly cut total cancer or major cardiovascular events in healthy adults (Manson et al., 2020). A defensible repletion dose is roughly 1000 to 2000 IU per day for those who need it.
  • Omega-3s have strong evidence for lowering triglycerides and a contested, modest signal for broader cardiovascular outcomes, with EPA and DHA acting differently and a real atrial-fibrillation and bleeding tradeoff at higher doses (Khan et al., 2021; Abdelhamid et al., 2018; Cottin et al., 2011).
  • Source matters: EPA and DHA from fish or algae are the active forms; ALA from flax converts poorly. Algae oil is bioequivalent to fish oil (Bailey et al., 2025).
  • Magnesium is genuinely under-consumed by roughly half the population (Rosanoff et al., 2012), with weak-but-plausible evidence for faster sleep onset in older adults (Mah & Pitre, 2021) and much thinner support for the broader stress and energy claims.
  • For all three, food often closes the gap, and "associated with" is not "fixes." Context decides whether each is a real baseline candidate for a given person.
Looking Ahead

You now have a fair, demanding evidentiary bar, set by three supplements that mostly clear it for specific people and specific outcomes. In the next chapter we turn that same lens on the flashier categories, the performance powders, the cognition pills, the "natural" mood and metabolism boosters, and ask them to clear the bar you just watched vitamin D, omega-3, and magnesium struggle to reach. Most will not. The framework will tell you why before the marketing gets a chance to.
