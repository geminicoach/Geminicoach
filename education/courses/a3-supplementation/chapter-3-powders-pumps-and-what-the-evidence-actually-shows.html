Powders, Pumps, and What the Evidence Actually Shows

Protein, creatine, and caffeine, treated with the same cold logic as everything else.

33 min read 16 cited sources

Walk into any supplement store and you are standing in a cathedral built on three molecules. The wall of tubs is mostly protein. The tubs next to it are mostly creatine. The brightly colored canisters by the register are mostly caffeine wearing a costume. Strip away the flavor systems, the proprietary blends, and the athletes glistening on the labels, and the entire category that dominates supplement spending comes down to those three things plus marketing.

This is the chapter where hype runs hottest, which means it is the chapter where our gap framework has to work hardest. The good news is that these three compounds have some of the strongest evidence in the whole supplement world. The bad news is that strong evidence and absurd marketing live in the same building, often on the same label. Your job is to learn to tell them apart.

Three things that are not the same kind of thing

Before we touch a single study, you need a mental partition, because the most common error in this category is treating protein, creatine, and caffeine as if they belonged to one bucket called "gym supplements." They do not. They answer different questions.

Protein powder is food. It is a convenient, concentrated way to hit a nutrient target you could also hit with chicken, lentils, eggs, or yogurt. The right question about protein powder is a gap question: do you have a protein shortfall, and is powder the most practical way to close it?

Creatine and caffeine are not food in any meaningful sense, and they are not closing a dietary gap. They are ergogenic compounds: substances that change performance through a pharmacological or physiological mechanism rather than by supplying a missing nutrient. The right question about them is not "do I have a gap" but "does the effect they produce matter to me, at a dose I can tolerate, for a cost I accept?"

Here is where people get this wrong. They buy protein powder hoping it will act like a drug, and they buy creatine hoping it will act like a meal that builds muscle while they sleep. Both expectations are backwards. Get the categories right and most of the confusion in this aisle evaporates.

Think About It

Before reading on: which of the three is a gap-filler, and which two are effect-producers? If you can state that distinction in one sentence, the rest of this chapter will click into place.

Protein powder is food wearing a label

Let us settle the central reframing first, because it does most of the work. A scoop of whey is roughly twenty to twenty-five grams of protein, some residual carbohydrate and fat, and flavoring. Nutritionally, it is closer to a small chicken breast than to a medication. It does not contain a hidden growth signal that whole food lacks. When researchers compare supplemented diets to whole-food diets matched for total protein, the supplement's advantage largely disappears. The variable that drives adaptation is how much protein you eat across the day, not whether some of it arrived as a powder.

What the evidence actually says about how much

The International Society of Sports Nutrition position stand places the optimal range for active people at roughly 1.4 to 2.0 grams per kilogram of body weight per day (Jäger et al., 2017). For a 70 kg person that is about 98 to 140 grams daily. For a general-population reader who is not training hard, the lower end of that range, or even the standard 0.8 g/kg adequacy floor scaled up modestly, is often plenty. The number is a target, not a moral obligation.

The most useful single finding comes from Morton and colleagues' meta-analysis of 49 randomized controlled trials covering 1,863 participants. Protein supplementation produced a real but modest boost to resistance-training gains: about 0.30 kg of additional fat-free mass on top of training alone, plus strength improvements (Morton et al., 2018). The critical detail is the dose-response curve. The benefit of added protein plateaued at approximately 1.62 g/kg/day, with the upper bound of the confidence interval reaching around 2.2 g/kg/day. Above that plateau, more protein did not buy more muscle.

Read that carefully, because it carries two messages at once. Protein does help if you are training and currently under-eating it. And there is a ceiling, beyond which the extra scoops are simply expensive redundancy. The same study found that total daily protein intake mattered more than the timing of the supplement or the specific source. The famous "anabolic window" turns out to be a barn door, not a keyhole.

Think About It

If the dose-response plateaus near 1.62 g/kg/day, what does that tell you about a person already eating 2.2 g/kg from food who adds two daily shakes "to be safe"? Where is that protein going?

Coaching translation

In practice, this means protein powder earns its place only when two things are true: you have a genuine protein gap, and powder is the most practical way to close it given your schedule, appetite, budget, or digestion. A shift worker who cannot stomach a full meal at 5 a.m. has a real use case. A busy student who reliably misses the mark on training days has a real use case. Someone already hitting their target with food who buys powder because the label has a flexing cartoon has bought convenient redundancy, which is fine if they enjoy it but should not be confused with necessity. Protein powder is a tool for hitting a number. First, find out if you are missing the number.

Whey, casein, and plant: a practical comparison, not a hierarchy

Once you have decided powder is useful, the next question is which kind, and this is where moralizing creeps in. People talk about whey as "superior" and plant as "incomplete" or "clean" as if these were virtues rather than properties. Drop the virtue language. These are three formats with practical tradeoffs.

Digestion speed

Whey is the sprinter. It digests fast, producing a rapid rise in blood amino acids and a sharp, short spike in muscle protein synthesis. Casein is the slow-release version: it forms a gel in the stomach and trickles amino acids over hours. This is why the ISSN position stand notes that 30 to 40 grams of casein before sleep can elevate overnight muscle protein synthesis (Jäger et al., 2017). The pre-sleep casein finding is real, but keep it in proportion: it is a marginal optimization stacked on top of an already adequate daily intake, not a requirement.

Completeness

Both whey and casein are complete proteins with a strong leucine content, leucine being the amino acid that most strongly triggers the synthesis signal. Many single-source plant proteins are lower in one or more essential amino acids: rice is modest in lysine, pea is modest in methionine. This is precisely why well-formulated plant powders are blends, typically pea plus rice, which complement each other and close the amino-acid gaps.

What the head-to-head data show

Here the recent evidence is more interesting than the marketing on either side. Pinckaers and colleagues pooled 12 studies making 26 direct comparisons of plant versus animal protein on muscle protein synthesis. In 75 percent of the comparisons there was no significant difference; 25 percent showed lower synthesis with plant protein. The meta-analysis found an overall edge for animal protein, but the authors describe it as modest and context-dependent, often a matter of dose and amino-acid profile rather than an unbridgeable gap (Pinckaers et al., 2025).

This is why context-dependent is the honest verdict. The animal-protein advantage tends to shrink or disappear when plant intake is a complete blend, the dose is adequate, and total daily protein is on target. A person eating a varied diet who hits their daily number with a pea-rice blend is not leaving meaningful muscle on the table. A person relying on a single under-dosed rice scoop might be.

Coaching translation

In practice, this means choose your powder on practical grounds and stop assigning it a personality. Lactose intolerant or plant-based by ethics? A complete blend works, just bump the dose slightly and confirm it is leucine-fortified or pea-rice based. Want the cheapest effective gram of complete protein and you tolerate dairy? Whey usually wins on cost per gram. Want something to sit overnight? Casein has a small, real edge. None of these is clean, none is dirty. They are formats. Pick the one you will actually use consistently, because consistency beats optimization every time.

Total daily protein intake is the primary driver of training adaptation. Timing and source are refinements layered on top of that, not substitutes for it.

Creatine: the strongest evidence, the silliest myths

Now we change categories entirely. Creatine monohydrate is not food filling a gap. It is an ergogenic compound, and it happens to be the single most well-supported sports supplement on the market. The ISSN position stand calls it "the most effective ergogenic nutritional supplement" available for increasing high-intensity exercise capacity and lean body mass (Kreider et al., 2017). That is a strong claim from a cautious body, and the data back it.

What it genuinely does, and the mechanism

Your muscles store energy for short, explosive efforts using the phosphocreatine system. When you sprint, lift, or jump, your cells regenerate ATP rapidly by donating a phosphate from phosphocreatine. Supplementing creatine enlarges the intracellular creatine pool, which means more phosphocreatine on hand, which means you can sustain maximal output slightly longer and recover between efforts slightly faster (Wax et al., 2021).

The downstream effects are consistent across the literature: improved maximal strength, improved power, better performance on repeated high-intensity efforts, and increased fat-free mass. Note what creatine does to body mass. Some of the early weight gain is intracellular water, drawn into muscle cells along with the creatine. This is not bloating and it is not "fake" muscle; it is a real change in cell volume that accompanies a real increase in work capacity. Over time, by enabling harder training, creatine also supports genuine gains in contractile tissue.

The general-population angle

You are not necessarily an athlete, so why should you care? Because the same mechanism that helps a sprinter helps an aging adult preserve strength and lean mass, and a growing literature points to cognitive and recovery roles, particularly under stress or sleep deprivation. For most general-population readers, creatine is best understood as a low-cost, well-tolerated insurance policy for strength and lean tissue, especially as you age. The dose is unglamorous: about 3 to 5 grams per day of plain monohydrate. No fancy form has been shown to beat it.

What it does not do

This is where strong evidence meets absurd marketing, our first sharp example of it. Creatine is blamed and credited for a long list of things it does not do. It does not damage healthy kidneys. The position stand reviews data on supplementation up to 30 g/day for as long as five years and finds it safe and well-tolerated in healthy individuals (Kreider et al., 2017). It is not a steroid; it is an amino-acid derivative your body already makes and you already eat in meat. It does not reliably cause muscle cramps; if anything, the hydration evidence points the other way. And the persistent rumor that it causes hair loss rests on a single small study showing a rise in a hormone marker, never replicated as actual hair loss.

Think About It

Creatine increases water inside muscle cells. A marketer calls this "explosive cell volumization for insane pumps." A myth-spreader calls it "dangerous water retention and bloating." Both describe the same finding. What does that tell you about reading supplement claims?

Coaching translation

In practice, this means creatine is one of the rare cases where the honest answer is genuinely positive: strong evidence, cheap, safe for healthy people, and useful well beyond the gym. The catch is that it does exactly one family of things, and the marketing inflates that into a personality transplant. It will not give you a six-pack, fix your diet, or make you a different athlete. It enlarges an energy buffer. That is the whole story, and the whole story is still worth telling.

Caffeine: a dose, not a habit

The third molecule is the one almost everyone already takes. Caffeine is consumed by more than 80 percent of U.S. adults, mostly without ever thinking of it as a supplement (McLellan et al., 2016). That familiarity is exactly the problem. People treat caffeine as a habit, a ritual, a personality. The evidence treats it as a dose with a measurable response curve, a tolerance story, and a timing problem that reaches all the way into your sleep.

The dose-response

The ISSN position stand on caffeine is clear: performance benefits show up consistently in the range of 3 to 6 mg per kg of body mass, taken about an hour before exercise (Guest et al., 2021). For a 70 kg person that is roughly 210 to 420 mg, or about two to four cups of coffee's worth. The most reliable benefits appear in aerobic endurance, where the effect is moderate to large, with smaller but real benefits to strength, muscular endurance, sprinting, and jumping. McLellan and colleagues put the ergogenic threshold at doses above roughly 200 mg, about 3 mg/kg, across many exercise modes (McLellan et al., 2016).

Notice the shape of the curve. Below the threshold, you get little ergogenic effect. In the effective range, you get the benefit. Push well above 6 mg/kg and you do not get proportionally more performance; you get jitteriness, elevated heart rate, gut distress, and anxiety. This is a classic case where more is not better, it is just more side effects. The dose has a ceiling, like protein, but for the opposite reason: not redundancy, but toxicity of experience.

The cognitive and occupational side

Caffeine is not only a gym tool. Moderate doses, roughly 32 to 300 mg, improve vigilance, learning, reaction time, and mood, and caffeine is genuinely useful for offsetting the cognitive and physical degradation caused by sleep loss (McLellan et al., 2016). This is why it is the default countermeasure for shift workers, drivers, and anyone fighting fatigue. For the general-population reader, this is the more relevant benefit: caffeine is a real, evidence-backed alertness tool, used deliberately.

Tolerance and the sleep tradeoff

Here is the part the marketing never mentions. Caffeine has a long half-life, commonly around five hours, meaning a meaningful fraction is still circulating long after you feel it. A recent Sports Medicine review frames this as a genuine conflict: the same dose that enhances afternoon performance can sabotage that night's sleep, and poor sleep degrades next-day performance, creating a loop where you chase the alertness you keep undermining (Silva et al., 2025).

The review notes substantial individual variability, including genetic differences such as the ADORA2A variant that makes some people far more sensitive to caffeine's sleep-disrupting effects. The practical recommendation, 3 to 6 mg/kg about an hour before exercise, has to be weighed against a hard timing rule: with a five-hour half-life, caffeine taken in the afternoon is still pharmacologically present at bedtime. Tolerance complicates this further. Regular use blunts the subjective jolt, so people escalate the dose, which lengthens the shadow it casts over sleep, without necessarily restoring the original effect.

Think About It

If caffeine has a five-hour half-life, roughly how much of a 4 p.m. dose is still in your system at 9 p.m.? At 2 a.m.? Now reconsider whether your afternoon coffee is a free alertness boost or a loan against tonight's sleep.

Coaching translation

In practice, this means treat caffeine like a tool you dose, not a tap you leave running. If you want the ergogenic effect, take it deliberately in the 3 to 6 mg/kg range before the effort that matters, not constantly throughout the day. Respect the half-life: set a personal cutoff time, often early afternoon, and move it earlier if you are a slow metabolizer or sleep poorly. And understand that tolerance means your daily habit is mostly buying back the alertness that caffeine itself stole the night before. Used as a strategic dose, caffeine is excellent. Used as a permanent background drip, it quietly becomes a sleep tax you pay every night.

The next tier down: modest, specific, real

Protein, creatine, and caffeine are the headliners because their evidence is strong and broad. Below them sits a second tier of compounds that are genuinely supported but in a narrow way. The effect is real, the effect is small, and the effect only shows up under specific conditions. This tier is where careful readers separate themselves from the crowd, because the marketing flattens "real but modest and situational" into "essential for serious athletes." Two ingredients earn a place here, and both of them tend to ride into your system inside a pre-workout tub.

Beta-alanine: a buffer for the burning-lungs zone

When you train hard enough that a muscle starts to burn, part of what you are feeling is a drop in pH inside the muscle fiber as hydrogen ions accumulate. Your muscles carry a built-in buffer against that acidity called carnosine, and the amount of carnosine you can store is limited by how much beta-alanine you have on hand. Supplementing beta-alanine for several weeks raises muscle carnosine, which raises your buffering capacity, which lets you push a little longer before that acidic burn forces you to stop.

Notice the conditions packed into that mechanism, because they define exactly when it helps. A meta-analysis of 40 studies, 65 exercise protocols, and 1,461 participants found a small but real overall benefit, with an effect size of about 0.18, and the benefit concentrated in efforts lasting roughly 30 seconds to 10 minutes (Saunders et al., 2016). A more recent meta-analysis in trained men found a comparable modest effect, around 0.39, again clustered in maximal efforts in the 4-to-10-minute range at doses of roughly 5.6 to 6.4 grams per day (Georgiou et al., 2024). That is the window where acidity is the limiting factor. Outside it, beta-alanine has little to offer. A single one-rep max is over before acidity builds, so beta-alanine does nothing for it. A marathon is paced well below the acidic zone, so beta-alanine does nothing for that either. The sweet spot is the middle: repeated hard intervals, a brutal set of twenty, a 2-minute row, the rounds of a combat sport. The usual dose is 3 to 6 grams per day, taken consistently to load the muscle over weeks, and the famous harmless side effect is paresthesia, a tingling of the skin that some people find alarming and others enjoy. The tingle is not the supplement working; it is just beta-alanine touching nerve receptors on its way through.

Citrulline: the "pump" ingredient with a thinner file than its reputation

Citrulline, usually sold as citrulline malate, is the ingredient most responsible for the "pump" pre-workouts promise. The logic is plausible on paper. Citrulline raises blood levels of arginine more effectively than arginine supplements do, and arginine feeds nitric oxide production, and nitric oxide widens blood vessels, so in theory you deliver more blood, oxygen, and nutrients to working muscle. That is a clean mechanistic story, and it is exactly the kind of clean story that should make you ask whether the data actually deliver on it.

They mostly do not, or at least not reliably. A critical review of citrulline malate and exercise performance concluded that the results are genuinely equivocal: some studies show a benefit to resistance-training volume or high-intensity work, many show nothing, and the field is muddied by inconsistent dosing, weak test-retest reliability in the protocols used, and quality-control problems where the actual citrulline-to-malate ratio in a product does not match the label (Gough et al., 2021). The most common research dose is a single 8-gram serving. Strip it down and citrulline lands as a "maybe, sometimes, modestly" ingredient, a long way from the engorged-vasculature spectacle on the canister. The visible pump you feel mid-workout is mostly the normal, temporary result of training itself, blood pooling in the muscle you are working, and it happens with or without the powder.

Pre-workout: a delivery vehicle, judged by its parts

Now put those pieces together, because a pre-workout is not a single thing with its own evidence. It is a delivery vehicle, a blend, and the only honest way to evaluate it is to ignore the brand name and read the ingredient panel as a list of separate bets. A review of multi-ingredient pre-workout supplements found that these blends typically contain caffeine, creatine, beta-alanine, citrulline or other nitric-oxide agents, and assorted amino acids, and that the acute performance benefit they produce is real but largely traceable to the caffeine, with the rest contributing smaller or less certain effects (Harty et al., 2018). The same review flagged two problems baked into the format. First, the widespread use of proprietary blends means the label often tells you the total weight of a mix without disclosing how much of each ingredient is inside, so you cannot tell whether the beta-alanine is at an effective 3-plus grams or a sprinkled-in pinch. Second, because the category is loosely regulated, some products contain banned or undisclosed stimulants as deliberate additions or as contaminants.

So here is the honest accounting of a typical scoop. The tingle is beta-alanine, real but modest and only for mid-length hard efforts. The "pump" claim rests on citrulline, whose evidence is thin and inconsistent. The actual kick, the thing you feel that makes you believe the product "works," is overwhelmingly the caffeine, which you could buy on its own for a fraction of the price and dose precisely. Everything else, the exotic-sounding aminos, the trademarked compounds, the colors and flavors, is either a low-evidence long shot or pure marketing. A pre-workout is not a scam, but it is rarely more than caffeine plus a couple of modest ingredients plus a large markup for the experience.

Think About It

A pre-workout lists a 6-gram "proprietary energy and pump matrix" containing caffeine, beta-alanine, citrulline, and four other ingredients. Beta-alanine alone needs about 3 grams to work. Citrulline studies use about 8 grams. What does the math tell you about how much of each is actually in that 6-gram blend?

Coaching translation

In practice, this tier is where you decide what a small, conditional effect is worth to you. If you do the exact kind of training beta-alanine helps, repeated hard intervals in that 1-to-10-minute burn zone, a standalone 3-to-6-gram dose is cheap and reasonable. If you want the caffeine kick of a pre-workout, you are usually better off buying caffeine by itself and skipping the proprietary blend, because then you control the dose and you are not paying premium prices for trace amounts of everything else. And if a label hides its amounts behind the word "proprietary," treat that as a reason for suspicion, not excitement. A blend that worked at real doses would be proud to print those doses.

The popular and the useless: why some best-sellers fail by design

The last group is the most important for your wallet, because it is full of products that sell extremely well and do almost nothing for the person buying them. These are not failing because the science has not caught up. They fail for mechanistic reasons you can understand, which means you can predict the failure before you ever read a study. Once you can see why something cannot work, no amount of label copy will fool you.

BCAAs: the right materials, fatally incomplete

Branched-chain amino acids, the trio of leucine, isoleucine, and valine, are sold on a half-truth. The half that is true: leucine is the strongest single trigger for muscle protein synthesis, the signal that says "start building." The half that sinks the product: a signal to build is useless if the building materials are not all present. Constructing new muscle protein requires all nine essential amino acids. BCAAs supply only three. The other six still have to come from somewhere, and in the fasted state the only available source is the breakdown of your own existing muscle.

This is not a subtle effect at the margins; it is the whole story. A detailed review of the evidence in humans concluded that taking BCAAs alone cannot maximally stimulate muscle protein synthesis, because the missing essential amino acids cap the response, and the two infusion studies that measured it directly found BCAAs actually reduced muscle protein turnover rather than driving net building (Wolfe, 2017). The author's verdict is blunt: the claim that BCAA supplements produce an anabolic response in humans is unwarranted. And here is the part that turns BCAAs from "weak" into "pointless" for most people: whey protein, regular chicken, eggs, and any complete protein already contain a generous dose of BCAAs plus the other six essentials. If you are eating enough total protein, you are already getting more leucine and BCAAs than a BCAA supplement provides, inside a complete package that can actually build tissue. The supplement sells you a fraction of what your food already delivers in full. The only narrow scenario where isolated BCAAs are even theoretically interesting is training fully fasted with no protein intake nearby, and even there, a small dose of complete protein or essential amino acids would do the job better.

Glutamine: real biology, no payoff for the gym-goer

Glutamine is the most abundant amino acid in your body and a genuinely important fuel for immune and gut cells, which is why it has a real role in specific clinical settings such as severe burns, trauma, or critical illness, where the body's demand outstrips supply. That legitimate medical use is the seed of the marketing, but it does not transfer to a healthy person who lifts weights. A randomized controlled trial gave young adults a substantial glutamine dose during six weeks of resistance training and found no advantage over placebo in strength, lean tissue mass, or muscle breakdown; both groups improved the same amount (Candow et al., 2001). The mechanistic reason is simple. A healthy, well-fed person already makes plenty of glutamine and eats more of it in any protein-containing meal, so supplementing adds to a pool that was never short. You cannot fix a shortage that does not exist. For the recreational athlete, glutamine is a solution searching for a problem.

"Test boosters": selling a lever that is already at the top

Testosterone boosters promise to raise the hormone most associated with muscle and drive, and the category leans on ingredients like D-aspartic acid and Tribulus terrestris. The problem is twofold, and both halves are fatal. First, the specific ingredients mostly do not raise testosterone in the people buying them. A randomized controlled trial of D-aspartic acid in resistance-trained men over twelve weeks found no increase in total or free testosterone and no improvement in strength or muscle size; if anything, one marker moved the wrong way (Melville et al., 2017). Tribulus has a similarly empty track record for raising testosterone in healthy men. Second, and more fundamental: even a real, modest bump in testosterone within the normal physiological range does not reliably translate into more muscle. The hormone is not a simple volume knob where turning it up a little produces more tissue. In men with normal levels, nudging testosterone slightly is not the lever that builds a physique, which is exactly why these products can sometimes truthfully claim a tiny hormonal change while still being useless for the result you actually want. They are selling you a lever that is already near the top of its useful range, and pretending the last millimeter of travel is where the magic lives.

Fat burners: stimulants and thermogenics dressed as a solution

Fat burners are the purest example of marketing exploiting a mechanism. Most are built on stimulants, primarily caffeine, sometimes alongside compounds like p-synephrine from bitter orange, which mildly raise metabolic rate and energy expenditure. The mechanism is real in the trivial sense that stimulants do slightly increase how many calories you burn and can blunt appetite a little. A review of bitter orange and p-synephrine concluded that at common doses it appears reasonably safe and does not produce the cardiovascular effects of the banned stimulant ephedra it replaced (Stohs, 2017). But "safe and mildly thermogenic" is a long way from "burns fat," and the gap is where the deception lives. The metabolic bump is small, the body adapts to stimulants quickly through tolerance, and none of it overrides the actual determinant of fat loss, which is an energy deficit sustained over time. A fat burner cannot create a deficit you are not already in; at best it nudges the math by a rounding error while encouraging you to believe the pill, rather than your diet, is doing the work. That belief is the real product, and it is the most expensive ingredient in the bottle.

Think About It

Each failure in this group has a different shape: BCAAs supply incomplete materials, glutamine fills a gap that is not empty, test boosters push a lever that does not move the outcome, fat burners exploit a real but trivial mechanism. Can you state, in one sentence each, the mechanistic reason every one of them fails?

Coaching translation

In practice, this means you can screen out most of the worst purchases in the supplement store before you read a single review, just by asking what the product is mechanically supposed to do and whether that mechanism can survive contact with how your body actually works. If you already eat enough complete protein, BCAAs and glutamine are redundant by definition. If you are a healthy man with normal hormones, a test booster is aiming at a lever that does not control the outcome. If you are not in an energy deficit, a fat burner has nothing to burn. None of these is dangerous in the usual sense, but money spent here is money not spent on the few things that genuinely earn their place, and attention spent here is attention stolen from the basics that actually move the needle.

Strong evidence meeting absurd marketing

Step back and notice the pattern across all three molecules, because it previews the two chapters ahead. Protein, creatine, and caffeine are among the best-supported compounds in the entire supplement world. And yet each is sold with claims that range from exaggeration to fantasy: protein that builds muscle "while you sleep" regardless of your daily total, creatine reframed as a steroid-adjacent miracle or a kidney-wrecking poison depending on who profits from the fear, caffeine packaged as a "clean energy" lifestyle rather than a dosed stimulant with a sleep cost.

This is the central lesson of the powders aisle. Strong evidence and absurd marketing are not opposites that sort products into good and bad shelves. They coexist on the same label, for the same compound, at the same time. A supplement can be genuinely effective and still be sold to you with claims that are false. Learning to hold both ideas at once, the real mechanism and the marketed fantasy, is the consumer skill the next chapter builds directly, and the risk it conceals is what the chapter after that confronts.

Case study: the cabinet full of confidence

Case

Derek is 29, lifts four days a week, and wants to add muscle. He is serious enough to spend money on it, so his shelf reflects that seriousness: a tub of whey, a pre-workout he scoops before every session, a jar of BCAAs he sips during training, glutamine "for recovery," a test booster he started after a podcast ad, and a fat burner he takes on the days he feels soft. He hits the gym hard and eats reasonably, roughly 150 grams of protein a day at 85 kilograms. After eight months his progress has stalled and his monthly supplement bill rivals his grocery budget. He is convinced he needs a better stack. What he actually needs is to read his own shelf the way this chapter reads a label.

Run Derek's cabinet through the categories and most of it collapses on contact. Start with the protein, the only purchase doing clear work, and even that is worth checking. At 85 kilograms and training hard, his target lands around 1.6 to 2.0 g/kg, roughly 136 to 170 grams. He is already inside that range from food and whey, which means he is at or past the plateau near 1.62 g/kg where added protein stops buying extra muscle (Jäger et al., 2017; Morton et al., 2018). The whey is fine. It is hitting a number he is already close to hitting. It is not the missing piece, because there is no protein gap to fill.

Now the dead weight. The BCAAs he sips mid-workout are redundant by design: his whey and his meals already supply more leucine and branched-chain aminos than the BCAA jar does, inside a complete protein that can actually build tissue, while the isolated three cannot (Wolfe, 2017). The glutamine is filling a pool that a well-fed lifter never empties, which is why a controlled trial found it did nothing for strength, mass, or muscle breakdown in trained young adults (Candow et al., 2001). The test booster is aiming at a healthy man's normal hormones with an ingredient class that does not reliably raise testosterone and would not reliably build muscle even if it did (Melville et al., 2017). The fat burner is a mild stimulant pretending to be a fat-loss strategy, useful only if he were in an energy deficit, which a man trying to add muscle deliberately is not (Stohs, 2017). Four products, four different mechanistic reasons for failure, all predictable without a single new study.

The pre-workout is the instructive one, because it is not useless, just misunderstood. The kick Derek credits to the whole formula is almost entirely the caffeine (Harty et al., 2018). The tingle is beta-alanine, which genuinely helps the mid-length hard sets he does but only at a real dose he cannot verify behind the proprietary blend (Saunders et al., 2016). The "pump" is mostly training, with citrulline riding along on thin evidence (Gough et al., 2021). He could replace the whole tub with measured caffeine and a separate 3-to-5-gram scoop of beta-alanine and lose nothing but the markup.

Here is the part that actually answers his stall. Derek has spent eight months and a fortune optimizing the wrong tier. The one ergogenic compound with the strongest evidence for exactly his goal, creatine, is not even on his shelf (Kreider et al., 2017; Wax et al., 2021). He has bought confidence by the jarful and skipped the cheap, proven monohydrate that would help. The fix is not a better stack. It is a smaller, honest one: keep the protein, add 3 to 5 grams of creatine, dose caffeine deliberately before hard sessions, optionally run beta-alanine if his training fits the window, and put the BCAA, glutamine, test-booster, and fat-burner money toward food, sleep, and a smarter program. The cabinet shrinks, the bill shrinks, and the results finally have room to show up.

Key Takeaways

  • Protein powder is convenient food, not magic. The right question is whether you have a protein gap, not which tub has the best label.
  • The evidence-based protein range for active people is about 1.4 to 2.0 g/kg/day; supplementation's training benefit plateaus near 1.62 g/kg/day, and total daily intake matters more than timing or source (Jäger et al., 2017; Morton et al., 2018).
  • Whey, casein, and plant blends are formats with practical tradeoffs in speed and completeness, not a moral hierarchy. The animal-over-plant edge for muscle protein synthesis is modest and context-dependent (Pinckaers et al., 2025).
  • Creatine is an ergogenic compound with some of the strongest evidence in the field: real gains in strength, power, repeated-effort capacity, and lean mass at 3 to 5 g/day, and it is safe in healthy people (Kreider et al., 2017; Wax et al., 2021).
  • Creatine does not damage healthy kidneys, is not a steroid, does not reliably cause cramps, and the hair-loss claim is unreplicated. Its water effect is real and beneficial, not "bloating."
  • Caffeine is a dose, not a habit: 3 to 6 mg/kg improves performance and alertness, but its roughly five-hour half-life and tolerance create a genuine sleep tradeoff (Guest et al., 2021; Silva et al., 2025; McLellan et al., 2016).
  • Beta-alanine has a small, real benefit (it buffers acidity) only for hard efforts lasting roughly 30 seconds to 10 minutes, at about 3 to 6 g/day; outside that window it does little (Saunders et al., 2016; Georgiou et al., 2024).
  • Citrulline, the "pump" ingredient, has thin and inconsistent performance evidence, and a pre-workout is a delivery vehicle whose felt effect is mostly the caffeine, often hidden inside an undosed proprietary blend (Gough et al., 2021; Harty et al., 2018).
  • BCAAs cannot build muscle alone because they supply only 3 of the 9 essential amino acids; complete protein already contains them plus the rest (Wolfe, 2017). Glutamine adds nothing for a well-fed, healthy lifter (Candow et al., 2001).
  • "Test boosters" like D-aspartic acid do not reliably raise testosterone in healthy men, and small in-range changes would not build muscle anyway (Melville et al., 2017). Fat burners are mild stimulants that cannot replace an energy deficit (Stohs, 2017).
  • Strong evidence and absurd marketing live on the same label. Effectiveness and false claims are not mutually exclusive.
Looking Ahead

You have now met the first sharp case of real science wrapped in unreal claims. Next, we turn that observation into a skill set: how to read a supplement label, decode a proprietary blend, spot the rhetorical moves that turn a modest finding into a miracle, and decide what a claim is actually worth before your money leaves your hand.
