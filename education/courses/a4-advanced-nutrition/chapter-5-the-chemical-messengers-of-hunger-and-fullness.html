The Chemical Messengers of Hunger and Fullness

How ghrelin, leptin, insulin, and the gut's satiety peptides feed a hunger circuit in your brain, why palatable food overrides fullness, and what GLP-1 medications actually do.

36 min read 14 cited sources

You have spent a week eating carefully. The scale is moving, your clothes fit better, and then on day eight something shifts. You are not just hungry, you are preoccupied. Food shows up in your thoughts at odd hours. The same lunch that satisfied you in week one leaves you scanning the kitchen forty minutes later. Nothing about your willpower changed overnight. What changed is chemical. Your body noticed that stored energy is leaving, and it started sending messages.

This chapter is about those messages. In Chapter 1 you met the body that fights back, the system that defends its fat stores when you try to shed them. Now we go one level deeper, to the signaling layer that does the fighting. By the end you will be able to read a wave of hunger not as a character flaw but as a hormone doing exactly what it evolved to do, and you will be able to dismantle the single most popular myth in nutrition with actual evidence in hand.

Why hormones, and why now

You have met insulin three times already, always in passing. That was deliberate. Insulin is the hormone everyone thinks they understand and almost no one does, so before giving it a proper treatment we needed you to see it in context: in digestion, in nutrient handling, in the rhythm of fed and fasted states. Now we give the whole signaling system its overview-level treatment.

A note on scope. This is not an endocrinology course, and we are not going to drown you in receptor subtypes and second-messenger cascades. The goal is different and more useful: enough mechanism to reason about real behavior. When you finish, you should be able to explain to a skeptical friend why hunger predictably intensifies during a sustained diet, why a bad night of sleep makes you ravenous and unsatisfied the next day, and why the slogan "insulin makes you fat" collapses the moment you account for total energy.

The unifying idea is simple. These hormones are an information channel between your peripheral tissues, where energy is stored and burned, and your hypothalamus, where hunger and satiety are generated (Somogyi et al., 2011). Fat tissue, the gut, the pancreas, and the thyroid are constantly reporting upward, and the brain is constantly adjusting appetite and energy expenditure in response. When you understand that channel, the body that fights back stops being mysterious. It becomes a control system you can predict.

Insulin: the necessary signal everyone loves to hate

Let us start with the hormone that needs rehabilitating. Insulin is a peptide hormone released by the beta cells of the pancreas, and its core job is to manage nutrient traffic after you eat. When blood glucose rises, insulin rises with it, and it tells your tissues to take up glucose, store glycogen in liver and muscle, build a little fat, and pause the breakdown of existing fat. That last function is where the trouble starts in people's minds.

Here is the chain of reasoning behind the myth. Insulin suppresses fat breakdown, also called lipolysis. Carbohydrates raise insulin more than fat or protein do. Therefore, the story goes, eating carbs keeps insulin high, which locks fat in your cells, which makes you gain weight. It sounds airtight. It is also wrong, and understanding exactly why it is wrong is one of the most clarifying things you can learn in nutrition.

The kernel of truth

We are going to be fair to the other side, because there is a real mechanism here and pretending otherwise makes you a worse thinker. The most serious version of the insulin argument is the carbohydrate-insulin model (CIM), articulated in detail by Ludwig and colleagues (2021). Their claim is not the cartoon version. They argue that diets with a high glycemic load provoke an exaggerated insulin response, that this insulin response shunts ingested energy toward storage and away from circulating availability, and that the resulting drop in available fuel in the bloodstream registers in the brain as hunger. On this view, hormones partly drive the energy imbalance rather than merely responding to it. It is a coherent physiological hypothesis from serious scientists, and you should be able to state it accurately before you critique it.

Think About It

Before reading on, try to state the carbohydrate-insulin model in one sentence without using the word "bad." If you can only describe it as "carbs are bad," you do not yet understand the claim well enough to evaluate it.

Why the strong version fails

Now the rebuttal. Hall and colleagues (2022) assembled a comprehensive response, and the heart of it is devastatingly simple to state. Fat can be stored without dietary carbohydrate and without insulin rising above its basal level. People accumulate fat on low-carbohydrate, high-fat diets when they are in a calorie surplus. If insulin were the gatekeeper the strong CIM requires, that should be difficult. It is not. The body's hormonal machinery ensures that an energy surplus shows up as stored fat regardless of the macronutrient mix that delivered it.

The decisive experiments are the controlled feeding studies that hold total calories constant and vary carbohydrate. When you do that, swapping carbs for fat does not reliably produce the fat loss the CIM predicts. The variable that moves fat balance is total energy, not the carbohydrate fraction and not the insulin curve. This is why the field increasingly frames obesity through an updated energy balance model, which does not ignore hormones but places them in their actual role: as part of the system that regulates intake and expenditure, not as a single lever that overrides energy accounting (Hall et al., 2022).

This is the inflation that trips people up. They take a real signaling function, insulin's role in suppressing lipolysis after a meal, and they inflate it into a whole-system outcome, fat gain over weeks and months. But fat balance is the integral of energy in minus energy out across all of those fed and fasted cycles. Yes, insulin suppresses fat release for a few hours after lunch. It also rises after a protein shake. And in the fasted state between meals and overnight, insulin falls and fat is released and burned. Over a full day, what determines whether you net-store or net-burn fat is whether you ate more energy than you spent. Insulin organizes the timing of storage and release. It does not rewrite the total.

Adipose fat storage can occur without dietary carbohydrate or elevated insulin, and hormonal adaptations ensure that energy imbalances are reflected as changes in fat storage regardless of diet composition.

Paraphrasing Hall et al. (2022)

Coaching translation. Insulin is not your enemy and it is not optional. It is a normal, necessary traffic-control signal that you cannot live without, literally. People with type 1 diabetes who cannot make insulin lose fat and waste away precisely because the storage signal is gone. That should tell you something. So when someone tells you to fear bread because of insulin, you can say, calmly and correctly: insulin governs when fuel gets stored and released, but your total energy balance governs how much fat you carry. Manage the total, and insulin sorts itself out.

Where insulin genuinely matters. None of this means insulin is irrelevant. Chronically elevated insulin and insulin resistance are real metabolic problems with real consequences, and the body's insulin response to food affects how satisfied you feel and how steady your energy is. For some people, the rapid blood-sugar swings from certain high-glycemic meals do drive a faster return of hunger. That is worth respecting. The error is jumping from "insulin influences the experience of an individual meal" to "insulin is the master cause of body fat." The first is defensible. The second collapses.

Leptin and ghrelin: the long and short of hunger

If insulin manages nutrient traffic, leptin and ghrelin manage the conversation about hunger itself, on two very different timescales. Klok and colleagues (2006) reviewed the human evidence and framed the pair cleanly, and that framing is worth memorizing because it organizes everything that follows.

Leptin: the slow, long-term signal

Leptin is secreted by fat tissue, and its blood level is roughly proportional to how much fat you carry. Think of it as the body's fuel gauge averaged over days and weeks. When fat stores are ample, leptin is high, and high leptin tells the hypothalamus that energy is plentiful, which suppresses appetite and permits normal energy expenditure. When fat stores shrink, leptin falls, and the brain reads that fall as a warning: reserves are dropping. Leptin is the slow hand on the clock. It does not spike before lunch and fall after. It tracks your overall energy state.

This is the mechanism behind a phenomenon that surprises almost everyone. You might assume that since leptin suppresses appetite, giving people more leptin would be a fantastic weight-loss drug. For the rare individuals who genuinely cannot make leptin, it is miraculous. But for ordinary people with normal fat stores, adding leptin does very little, because they are already leptin-replete. The system is built to react far more strongly to leptin falling than to leptin rising. That asymmetry is not a bug. It is the signature of a system evolved to defend against starvation, not against abundance.

Ghrelin: the fast, short-term signal

Ghrelin is the counterpart, and it works on the opposite timescale. Secreted largely by the stomach, ghrelin rises before meals and falls after you eat. It is the acute, hour-to-hour appetite-stimulating signal, the "it is time to eat" alarm. Klok and colleagues (2006) describe its sharp preprandial rise and postprandial fall, and note something important: leptin and ghrelin appear to operate largely independently. They are not two ends of a single seesaw. They are separate instruments reporting different things, the long-term fuel gauge and the short-term hunger alarm, which the brain integrates.

Putting them together: why diets get harder

Now we can explain the scene from the opening of this chapter, and connect it to adaptive thermogenesis. When you sustain a calorie deficit and lose fat, leptin falls. That falling leptin is read by the hypothalamus as a threat to reserves, and the brain responds on multiple fronts at once. Hunger signaling rises. Satiety weakens, so meals satisfy you less. And expenditure is nudged downward. Müller and Bosy-Westphal (2013) reviewed this defensive response and identified low sympathetic nervous system activity, reduced T3 thyroid hormone, and falling leptin as the likely contributors to adaptive thermogenesis, the drop in energy expenditure during weight loss that is independent of the change in body size. They put its average magnitude around 120 kcal per day, with large individual variation.

Read that again, because it is the mechanistic backbone of this whole course. The body that fights back is not a metaphor. It is leptin falling, ghrelin and hunger climbing, satiety fading, and the metabolic rate quietly settling lower, all coordinated to defend the fat you are trying to lose. Your appetite intensifies on day eight of a diet because your fuel gauge dropped and your brain responded exactly as designed.

Think About It

A client says, "I have no willpower, I was fine for a week and then I fell apart." Using leptin, ghrelin, and adaptive thermogenesis, how would you reframe what actually happened to them physiologically? What did their body do, and why?

Coaching translation. Intensifying hunger during a sustained deficit is not evidence that you are weak. It is evidence that the signaling system is working. This reframe matters enormously, because people who interpret hunger as a personal failure tend to spiral into shame, then abandon the whole effort. People who interpret hunger as a predictable signal can plan for it: build in higher-volume, higher-protein, higher-fiber meals that blunt ghrelin and stretch satiety, take periodic diet breaks at maintenance to let leptin partially recover, and stop treating an empty stomach as a moral test they keep failing. In practice, this means you read hunger as a signal to interpret, not a failure to punish.

How a single meal actually starts and stops

So far we have treated hunger and fullness as two slow dials, the long leptin tide and the daily ghrelin pulse. Zoom in on one meal and the picture gets more detailed, because a meal is governed by a small committee of signals that take turns. Some tell you to start. Others build, minute by minute, until they tell you to stop. Understanding the committee is what lets you build meals that actually satisfy you, instead of meals you finish still wanting more.

Ghrelin and the decision to start eating

Ghrelin is the only circulating hormone known to reliably increase food intake in humans, and the timing of its release is the tell. It is produced mainly by cells in the lining of the stomach, and its blood level climbs in the hour or two before a meal you habitually eat, then drops sharply once food arrives. Klok and colleagues (2007) describe this preprandial rise and postprandial fall as ghrelin's signature. The practical consequence is that a large part of what you experience as "lunchtime hunger" is partly anticipatory and partly learned. Your stomach starts raising ghrelin ahead of the meal your body expects, which is why hunger so often arrives on a schedule rather than because your tank is truly empty.

That detail matters for anyone trying to change when they eat. If you usually eat at noon, your ghrelin has been trained to rise near noon, and the first few days of shifting your meals can feel like genuine starvation even when nothing is wrong with your energy stores. It is the alarm going off on its old schedule. Give it a week or two of consistent new timing, and the ghrelin rhythm largely re-trains to the new pattern. The hunger you feel is real, but it is not always reporting an actual fuel emergency. Sometimes it is reporting a habit.

The satiety peptides: how a meal tells you to stop

Stopping is handled by a different set of messengers, released from the gut as food moves through it. Huda and colleagues (2006) reviewed these gut peptides in detail, and three of them deserve a working understanding because they are the biology underneath almost every practical satiety strategy.

Cholecystokinin (CCK) is the fast responder. It is released from the upper small intestine within minutes of fat and protein arriving, and it does two useful things at once: it slows the rate at which the stomach empties, so the meal stays with you longer, and it signals the brain through the vagus nerve that food is on board. CCK is a within-meal brake. It is one reason a meal with some fat and protein registers as a meal, while the same calories as a fast-absorbed liquid can slip past almost unnoticed.

Peptide YY (PYY) and glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) come from cells further down the gut, in the lower small intestine and colon, and they work on a slightly slower timescale. As nutrients reach these lower segments, PYY and GLP-1 rise and stay elevated for a while after the meal, dialing down appetite over the following hour or two and, in GLP-1's case, also slowing gastric emptying and sharpening the insulin response to the meal. Huda and colleagues (2006) frame CCK, PYY, and GLP-1 together as the core gut-derived satiety signals, an integrated system that reports the size and composition of a meal upward to the appetite centers. Keep GLP-1 in mind in particular. The weight-loss medications covered at the end of this chapter are built directly on it.

Insulin is also a satiety signal

Earlier in this chapter we rehabilitated insulin from its reputation as the fat-storage villain. Here is the other half of its job that almost never gets mentioned: insulin is itself an appetite signal in the brain. Somogyi and colleagues (2011) list insulin alongside leptin as one of the two main adiposity signals that act centrally on the hypothalamus, and the logic mirrors leptin's. Insulin rises with meals and tracks, over the longer run, with body fatness, and when it acts in the brain it tends to reduce food intake rather than increase it. So the same hormone that organizes nutrient storage in your tissues also reports "food has arrived" to your appetite circuitry. This is one more reason the cartoon "insulin makes you fat" is exactly backward at the level of the brain: centrally, insulin is part of how a meal tells you that you have had enough.

Think About It

A protein-and-vegetable lunch and a sweetened coffee drink can carry the same calories, yet one leaves you full for hours and the other leaves you hungry within the hour. Using ghrelin, CCK, PYY, and GLP-1, walk through why. Which signals does each "meal" trigger, and which does it skip?

Coaching translation. Fullness is not one switch. It is a sequence: ghrelin falls when you start, CCK brakes the meal as fat and protein arrive, and PYY and GLP-1 carry the satisfied feeling forward after you finish. In practice, this means the foods and meal structures that hit more of these signals keep you full longer, and the ones that bypass them, fast-absorbed liquids and refined carbohydrate eaten alone, leave the committee mostly silent. That is the mechanism behind the protein, fiber, and volume advice later in this chapter. It is not folklore. It is which buttons the food presses.

The control room: how the brain reads all of this

Every signal we have discussed, ghrelin from the stomach, leptin from fat, insulin from the pancreas, PYY and GLP-1 from the gut, has to land somewhere and be turned into the felt experience of wanting or not wanting food. That somewhere is a small region at the base of the hypothalamus called the arcuate nucleus. This is the control room, and how it is wired explains why appetite behaves like a tug-of-war rather than a simple thermostat.

Vohra and colleagues (2021) describe the arcuate nucleus as containing two opposing populations of neurons with antagonistic jobs. One population, the AgRP/NPY neurons, drives hunger: when they fire, they powerfully increase the motivation to eat. The other population, the POMC neurons, drives satiety: when they fire, they reduce food intake. These two sets of neurons push in opposite directions, and your moment-to-moment appetite reflects which side is winning.

The peripheral hormones are the inputs that tip the contest. Ghrelin activates the hunger-driving AgRP/NPY neurons, which is how a pre-meal ghrelin rise becomes the conscious urge to eat. Leptin and insulin do the reverse: they tend to suppress the AgRP/NPY hunger neurons and stimulate the satiety-promoting POMC neurons, which is how ample fat stores and a recent meal translate into reduced appetite (Vohra et al., 2021). So the abstract idea of "an information channel between the body and the brain" has a concrete address. The hormones converge on the arcuate nucleus and adjust the balance of firing between a hunger circuit and a satiety circuit.

Now the diet scenario from the start of this chapter has a wiring diagram. When you sustain a deficit and leptin falls, you remove a brake that normally restrains the AgRP/NPY hunger neurons and normally supports the POMC satiety neurons. The hunger side gets louder and the satiety side gets quieter, not because you have weakened, but because the input that kept them balanced has dropped. This is the same defensive response we called adaptive thermogenesis, now visible at the level of two competing populations of cells.

Coaching translation. You do not consciously control which way the arcuate nucleus tips, any more than you consciously control your heart rate. What you control are the inputs. Sleep, protein, fiber, food volume, and how aggressive your deficit is all feed into this circuit. This is why it matters: when you treat appetite as a circuit you can influence through inputs rather than a willpower contest you keep losing, you stop fighting the wrong battle. You are not trying to out-discipline a hunger neuron. You are trying to give the satiety side better signals to work with.

Two appetites: the body that needs and the brain that wants

Everything so far describes homeostatic hunger, the system that tracks energy stores and defends them. If that were the whole story, nobody would eat dessert after a filling dinner, because every satiety signal would already be screaming "stop." But people do, routinely, and not because they are broken. There is a second system, and it runs on a different currency.

Lutter and Nestler (2009) describe food intake as governed by two complementary drives: the homeostatic pathway, which increases the drive to eat when energy stores run low, and the hedonic or reward-based pathway, which can override homeostatic signals during times of energy abundance by increasing the desire for foods that are highly palatable. That word override is the whole point. The reward system is not a gentle suggestion that politely yields to fullness. It can talk over the satiety signals entirely.

This is built on the brain's reward circuitry, the same dopamine-based system that evolved to make genuinely valuable things, food when food was scarce, feel worth pursuing. In an environment of scarcity, that wiring kept your ancestors alive. The problem is that the system was calibrated for a world that no longer exists. Modern highly palatable, energy-dense foods, the ones engineered to combine fat, sugar, salt, and a soft, fast-eating texture, light up the reward pathway far more intensely than anything in the ancestral diet did. Lutter and Nestler (2009) note that repeated consumption of these high-fat, high-sugar foods produces changes in reward circuitry that overlap with the changes seen with drugs of abuse, which is part of why "I was full, but I kept eating" is such a universal experience.

Here is the part nobody tells you: the reason you can be physically full and still want the dessert is that fullness and reward are two different signals computed in two different systems. Your gut peptides can be reporting "we have enough" at the very moment your reward system is reporting "but that is worth having anyway." Highly palatable food wins that argument by design, because its entire appeal is engineered to be strong enough to override a full stomach.

Think About It

You finish a large, satisfying meal and could not eat another bite of it, yet the dessert cart still tempts you. Which appetite is full, and which one is talking? What does that tell you about whether "just have more willpower at the table" is a realistic plan?

Coaching translation. If you have ever wondered why you can demolish a pint of ice cream you were not even hungry for, this is the answer, and it is not a verdict on your character. It is two systems, one tracking need and one chasing reward, and the modern food supply is built to crank the reward side past the point where fullness can stop it. In practice, this means the most effective move is rarely "resist harder in the moment," because you are trying to out-argue a system designed to win that argument. It is to manage the environment: keep the most hyper-palatable, easy-to-overeat foods out of arm's reach, so the reward system is not constantly being switched on while a full stomach is being ignored. You are not weak for losing to a hot fudge sundae. You are human, eating food engineered to beat your off-switch.

Eating with the signals instead of against them

Now we can turn all of this into food decisions. Three properties of a meal, protein, fiber, and sheer physical volume, drive satiety through the exact signals we have been mapping. None of them is a trick. Each one presses real buttons on the hunger committee.

Protein: the most satiating macronutrient

Protein does more, per calorie, to make you feel full than carbohydrate or fat. Paddon-Jones and colleagues (2008) reviewed the evidence and concluded that protein generally increases satiety more than carbohydrate or fat, and that under unrestricted eating conditions this can lead people to consume less total energy without trying to. The mechanism runs through the signals already covered: protein is a strong trigger for the gut satiety peptides, including CCK and GLP-1, and it carries a high thermic effect, meaning a meaningful fraction of its energy is burned simply digesting it. Paddon-Jones and colleagues (2008) also note protein's role in preserving lean mass during weight loss, which connects directly to the muscle-sparing concern that runs through this whole field. A higher-protein meal both satisfies you more and protects the tissue you are trying to keep.

Fiber and volume: filling the stomach honestly

Fiber's reputation as a satiety tool is real but more nuanced than the headlines suggest, and being precise here keeps you from overselling it. Viscous, soluble fibers slow gastric emptying and blunt the speed of digestion, which can extend the feeling of fullness and gives the lower-gut peptides like PYY and GLP-1 more time and substrate to act. But not all fiber does this equally. Clark and Slavin (2013) ran a systematic review of acute studies and found that most single fiber treatments did not significantly enhance satiety or reduce food intake, with only certain fibers, such as beta-glucan and rye fiber, showing consistent effects across studies. The honest takeaway is that "add fiber" is not a magic satiety switch. The kind of fiber and the whole-food context matter.

Volume is the more reliable lever, and it works through plain mechanics. Your stomach has stretch receptors that report distension to the brain as part of the satiety signal, and that response keys on the physical bulk of what you eat, not its calorie count. This is why high-volume, low-energy-density foods, vegetables, fruit, broth-based soups, are so useful for feeling full. They fill the stomach and trigger the stretch response while delivering relatively little energy. A large salad and a small handful of crackers can carry the same calories, but the salad fills the stomach and the crackers do not, so the salad recruits a satiety signal the crackers skip entirely.

Coaching translation. Put these together and you get the single most practical eating pattern in nutrition, and now you know exactly why it works rather than just being told to do it. Build meals around protein, plus high-volume fiber-containing whole foods, and you stack several satiety signals at once: protein drives the gut peptides and the thermic effect, volume triggers stomach stretch, and fiber slows things down. In practice, this means the same calorie target feels dramatically easier on a plate of chicken, vegetables, and beans than on a pastry and a latte, not because one is virtuous, but because one presses every satiety button and the other presses almost none. You are not relying on willpower. You are recruiting biology.

GLP-1 medications: pharmacology built on this exact biology

No chapter on appetite hormones written today would be complete without the medications that have reshaped the entire conversation, and the honest framing is the one this chapter has been building toward all along: these drugs are not magic and they are not cheating. They are pharmacology aimed directly at a signal you now understand.

Recall GLP-1, the satiety peptide your lower gut releases after a meal. It does several appetite-relevant things: it slows gastric emptying, sharpens the insulin response, and acts in the brain to reduce food intake. The catch is that natural GLP-1 is broken down within minutes, far too quickly to be a useful appetite tool on its own. The GLP-1 receptor agonists, the class that includes semaglutide and liraglutide, are engineered versions of that signal built to resist breakdown and stay active for far longer, hitting the same receptors the natural peptide does but for hours or days instead of minutes (Ard et al., 2021).

What they do, mechanistically, is amplify and prolong a satiety signal your body already uses. People on these medications report that food is simply less interesting, that they feel full sooner and stay full longer, and that the constant background pull of appetite quiets down. That is exactly what you would predict from a sustained GLP-1 signal acting on the gut and on the appetite circuits in the brain. Ard and colleagues (2021) reviewed the mechanism and the weight outcomes and frame these agents as turning a short-lived endogenous satiety signal into a durable one, which translates into reduced energy intake and meaningful weight loss in clinical trials.

This reframes the whole "willpower" conversation in a way that should land hard. If amplifying one satiety hormone can dramatically reduce how much a person wants to eat, then the original appetite, the relentless hunger that person was fighting before the medication, was never a simple failure of discipline. It was a signaling state. The drug does not install willpower the person was missing. It changes the biology the willpower was losing to. That is the single most important thing this medication proves about appetite, and it is the throughline of this entire chapter stated in pharmacological form.

Coaching translation. Whether or not a given person should use these medications is a medical decision between them and a physician, and there are real considerations, side effects, cost, what happens when you stop, and the same muscle-preservation and nutrition principles that apply to any rapid weight loss. That is genuinely beyond the scope of what a coach decides. This is why it matters for everyone, though, medicated or not: the very existence of a drug that works by amplifying a satiety hormone is the final nail in the "just eat less, it is all willpower" coffin. Appetite is physiological. These medications are simply the most direct proof we have, and understanding the biology is what lets you talk about them honestly instead of moralizing in either direction.

Sleep, stress, and the hunger you did not choose

Here is one of the most practical things in this entire course, because it touches everyone, every week. A bad night of sleep makes you measurably hungrier and less satisfied the next day, and we can name the hormones responsible.

Spiegel and colleagues (2004) ran a tightly controlled randomized crossover study in twelve healthy young men. They compared two nights of sleep restriction, just four hours in bed, against two nights of ten hours in bed, holding caloric intake and physical activity constant. The results are striking precisely because nothing else was allowed to vary. Sleep restriction reduced leptin by 18 percent and raised ghrelin by 28 percent. Subjective hunger rose 24 percent and appetite rose 23 percent. The fuel gauge dropped, the hunger alarm climbed, and the men felt exactly what those signals predict, with no change in how much they had actually eaten.

That is an experiment, which establishes causation but in a tiny, artificial sample. Does it hold in the messy real world? Taheri and colleagues (2004) checked, using 1,024 participants from the Wisconsin Sleep Cohort. Habitually short sleepers showed reduced leptin, elevated ghrelin, and proportionally higher BMI. The same hormonal fingerprint that Spiegel produced in a lab over two nights showed up across a population living ordinary lives over years. The two studies are a model of how evidence should stack: a controlled experiment establishes the mechanism, and an epidemiological study confirms it scales.

Think About It

Spiegel et al. held food intake and activity constant. Why does that detail make the hunger and appetite findings so much harder to dismiss? What alternative explanation does the controlled design rule out?

Stress runs along an overlapping path. Chronic psychological stress and elevated cortisol interact with the same appetite circuitry, tending to push intake upward and steer it toward energy-dense food. We are keeping this at the practical level rather than the full neuroendocrine treatment, but the through-line is the same as sleep: a state you might not think of as "metabolic," a rough week or a short night, reaches into the hormonal channel and changes how hungry and how satisfied you feel.

Coaching translation. When you wake up after five hours of sleep and feel like you could eat the kitchen, that is not gluttony and it is not lack of discipline. That is an 18 percent drop in leptin and a 28 percent rise in ghrelin doing their job on a body that thinks energy is scarce. This is why it matters: sleep is not a "wellness" afterthought sitting outside your nutrition. It is an input to your appetite hormones. If you are coaching someone who is fighting relentless hunger and losing, ask about their sleep before you ask about their willpower. Protecting sleep is one of the highest-leverage things a person can do for appetite regulation, and it costs nothing.

Thyroid and appetite: the practical version

The last messenger is the thyroid, and here we are deliberately staying at the level a coach or informed person needs, not the level a clinician needs. You already know thyroid hormones, primarily T3 and T4, as the body's master regulator of metabolic rate. Higher thyroid hormone, faster resting metabolism; lower thyroid hormone, slower. That is the classic peripheral, thermogenic effect, and it is real.

What is less widely appreciated is that thyroid hormones also act directly on appetite in the brain. Amin and colleagues (2011) reviewed the central effects of thyroid hormones and showed that the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid axis influences food intake directly through the arcuate nucleus of the hypothalamus, beyond merely setting metabolic rate. So thyroid hormones sit in both halves of the energy-balance equation at once: they help set how fast you burn, and they nudge how much you want to eat. Somogyi and colleagues (2011) place thyroid hormones alongside leptin, ghrelin, insulin, and oestrogen as one of the principal peripheral signals feeding the hypothalamic food-intake circuits, reinforcing that no single hormone works alone.

This connects back to adaptive thermogenesis. Recall that Müller and Bosy-Westphal (2013) named reduced T3 as one of the likely drivers of the expenditure drop during weight loss. So thyroid is not a separate topic bolted on at the end. It is part of the same defensive response. When you diet, T3 tends to fall, which contributes both to the lower metabolic rate and, through these central effects, to the broader shift in appetite regulation. The messengers are working together.

Coaching translation. For the general population, the practical takeaway is twofold. First, genuine thyroid dysfunction, clinical hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism, has real and clinically significant consequences for appetite and body weight, and that is a medical matter for a physician, not something to self-diagnose or self-treat. Second, and more relevant to most people, the modest downshift in thyroid output during a sustained diet is a normal part of the body defending its energy, not a sign that your metabolism is "broken." In practice, this means you should respect the thyroid's role without catastrophizing it: if something feels clinically off, see a doctor; if you are simply experiencing the ordinary adaptive dip of a long diet, that is the system working as designed, and the answer is patience and periodic maintenance breaks, not panic.

These hormones act as a dynamic information channel between peripheral tissues and central hypothalamic circuits, influencing food-seeking behaviour, ingestion, and energy expenditure.

Paraphrasing Somogyi et al. (2011)

Bringing the messengers together

Step back and look at the whole system. Insulin manages the timing of nutrient storage and release. Leptin reports your long-term fuel reserves. Ghrelin sounds the short-term hunger alarm. Thyroid hormones set the burn rate and tune appetite centrally. And states like sleep loss and stress reach in and shift the whole conversation. None of these is a master switch. Each is one voice in a coordinated system whose deepest priority, written by hundreds of thousands of years of scarcity, is to defend stored energy.

That is why the single-hormone stories are so seductive and so wrong. "Insulin makes you fat." "Leptin is the weight-loss hormone." "Fix your thyroid and the weight falls off." Each takes one real signal and inflates it into a whole-system explanation. The error is the same every time, and it fits in one sentence: people blame a single hormone for a whole-system outcome. The reality is an integrated control loop, and once you see it as a loop you stop looking for villains and start reading signals.

That shift, from villains to signals, is the entire point of this chapter. When hunger surges on a long diet, you now know it is leptin falling and ghrelin rising in defense of fat stores. When a bad night leaves you ravenous, you know it is an 18 percent leptin drop and a 28 percent ghrelin rise. When someone insists carbs are making them fat, you know that fat balance tracks total energy, not the insulin curve. You are no longer at the mercy of these messages, because you can read them.

Case study: the appetite that would not quit

Case

Kwame is 41, a project manager with two young kids, and he has been trying to lose about twenty pounds for the better part of a year. He is not lazy and he is not uninformed. He knows roughly what a sensible day of eating looks like, and for the first stretch of any diet he executes it well. Then, every time, it falls apart the same way. Around week three the hunger becomes constant and gnawing. He starts thinking about food during meetings. The salads that satisfied him at first leave him rummaging the pantry an hour later, and a few nights a week he ends up standing at the counter finishing the kids' leftover snacks long after he is full. He averages six hours of sleep, less when work is heavy. He has decided the problem is that he simply lacks willpower, and he is close to giving up.

Run Kwame through this chapter and almost none of what he is experiencing is a willpower problem. Start with the timeline. He does fine for two weeks and then unravels around week three, which is the exact signature of a falling leptin level. As he loses fat, his long-term fuel gauge drops, the arcuate nucleus reads that drop as a threat to reserves, and the balance tips: his AgRP/NPY hunger neurons get louder and his POMC satiety neurons get quieter. The constant, gnawing hunger that arrives on schedule is not a character flaw appearing on week three. It is adaptive thermogenesis and the defended fat stores doing precisely what this chapter described.

Now layer on his sleep. Six hours a night, less under stress, puts Kwame squarely in the territory Spiegel and colleagues (2004) studied: lowered leptin and elevated ghrelin, the fuel gauge pushed down and the hunger alarm pushed up, independent of how much he has eaten. His short sleep is not a separate problem from his hunger. It is feeding the same circuit, dragging the same two neuron populations further toward the hunger side. He is fighting a deficit-driven appetite and a sleep-driven appetite at the same time, and calling the sum of the two a personal failing.

His meals make it worse without his realizing it. The salads that "stopped working" were likely light on protein and physical volume, which means they were barely triggering the satiety committee, little CCK and GLP-1 response, little stomach stretch, so the fullness faded fast and the lower-gut peptides had nothing to sustain. And the counter-grazing at night is the hedonic system at work: he is not homeostatically hungry for the kids' snacks so much as the reward pathway is overriding a full stomach with engineered, easy-to-eat palatable food right in front of him at his most depleted, lowest-willpower hour.

The fix follows directly from the diagnosis, and notice that almost none of it is "try harder." Protect sleep first, because it is the highest-leverage input feeding the whole circuit. Rebuild meals around protein and high-volume, fiber-containing whole foods so each meal actually recruits the satiety signals instead of skipping them. Run a more moderate deficit with periodic maintenance breaks so leptin is not in permanent free-fall. And manage the environment at night so the reward system is not handed an easy target when he is most vulnerable. Kwame does not need more discipline. He needs to stop fighting his own signaling and start feeding it better inputs.

Key Takeaways

  • Insulin governs the timing of nutrient storage and release, not the total amount of fat you carry. Fat can be stored without dietary carbohydrate or elevated insulin; fat balance tracks total energy (Hall et al., 2022).
  • The carbohydrate-insulin model (Ludwig et al., 2021) is a serious hypothesis you should be able to state accurately, but its strong claim that insulin is the master cause of fat gain fails under controlled feeding studies.
  • Leptin is the slow, long-term fuel gauge proportional to fat stores; ghrelin is the fast, preprandial hunger alarm. They operate largely independently (Klok et al., 2006).
  • During a sustained deficit, falling leptin drives rising hunger, weakening satiety, and lower expenditure, the adaptive thermogenesis (~120 kcal/day average) that defends body weight (Müller & Bosy-Westphal, 2013).
  • Two nights of short sleep cut leptin ~18%, raised ghrelin ~28%, and increased hunger ~24% with food and activity held constant (Spiegel et al., 2004); the pattern holds across a 1,024-person cohort (Taheri et al., 2004).
  • Thyroid hormones set metabolic rate and act centrally on appetite via the arcuate nucleus (Amin et al., 2011); their dip during dieting is part of the same defensive response, not a broken metabolism.
  • A meal is started and stopped by a sequence of signals: ghrelin triggers the urge to eat, then gut peptides (CCK, PYY, GLP-1) build fullness as food arrives, and insulin acts centrally as a satiety signal too (Huda et al., 2006; Somogyi et al., 2011).
  • These signals converge on the arcuate nucleus, where AgRP/NPY hunger neurons and POMC satiety neurons compete; ghrelin tips it toward hunger, leptin and insulin toward satiety (Vohra et al., 2021).
  • Homeostatic hunger tracks energy need, but the hedonic reward system can override fullness for highly palatable, energy-dense food, which is engineered to win that contest (Lutter & Nestler, 2009).
  • Protein is the most satiating macronutrient and spares lean mass (Paddon-Jones et al., 2008); food volume reliably triggers stomach-stretch satiety, while fiber helps selectively rather than universally (Clark & Slavin, 2013).
  • GLP-1 medications work by amplifying and prolonging a satiety hormone your gut already makes (Ard et al., 2021), which is direct proof that appetite is physiological, not a measure of willpower.
  • The practical reframe: read hunger and energy swings as signals to interpret, never as failures of willpower to punish.
