Chapter 2: Apply It to a Client

Take the hidden ecosystem and use it to explain a real complaint, in plain language, within your scope.

Name: ____________________________ Date: __________ Est. time: 25 min

The Scenario

Client · Andre, 34
Andre finished a 10-day course of antibiotics for a sinus infection about three weeks ago. Since then his digestion has felt "off": sluggish, irregular, more gas than usual. Wanting to recover fast, he has been eating what he calls a "gentle" diet, mostly chicken, white rice, and toast, because beans and raw vegetables seem to make the gas worse. He bought a 50-billion-organism probiotic and has taken it daily, but he says it "isn't doing anything." He asks: "Did the antibiotics wreck my gut bacteria for good? Should I just buy a stronger probiotic?"

Your Tasks

  1. Explain, mechanically, what antibiotics did to Andre's gut community (use the idea of niches and colonization resistance), and why that is different from a permanent "wreck."
  2. Account for why his "gentle," low-fiber diet is working against his recovery. Trace the chain from missing fermentable fiber through fermentation, butyrate, and the colonocytes.
  3. Explain why a stronger probiotic capsule is unlikely to fix this, using what you know about transient strains and an established community.
  4. Give Andre a practical, in-scope plan to rebuild (be specific about fiber variety and pacing), and state clearly where your lane ends and a referral begins.

Your Response

0 words
Coaching boundary
You are demonstrating understanding and client communication, not diagnosing. A strong answer explains the normal mechanism, stays practical and gradual, and names the red flags that would change the answer and require a provider: bloody stool, fever, severe or persistent pain, weight loss, or signs of a serious post-antibiotic infection such as C. difficile.
Instructor / self-check rubric
  • Explains that antibiotics cleared microbial niches and reduced colonization resistance, rather than permanently killing the gut.
  • Identifies the low-fiber "gentle" diet as the real bottleneck, and traces fermentable fiber to fermentation to butyrate to colonocyte fuel.
  • Explains why a stronger probiotic will not rebuild the community: most strains are transient and do not recolonize an established gut.
  • Offers a practical, in-scope plan: reintroduce a variety of fermentable fibers gradually, with fermented foods as a reasonable complement.
  • States the referral boundary and the red flags (bloody stool, fever, severe pain, weight loss, suspected C. difficile or IBD) that would change the call.
