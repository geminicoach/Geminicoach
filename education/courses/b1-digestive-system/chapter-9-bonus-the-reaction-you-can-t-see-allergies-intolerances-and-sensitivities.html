The Reaction You Can't See: Allergies, Intolerances, and Sensitivities

Three words people swap freely, three mechanisms that could not be more different.

33 min read 13 cited sources

Two people eat the same slice of pizza. One breaks out in hives within minutes, lips swelling, throat tightening, reaching for an injector. The other feels fine for an hour, then gets bloated, gassy, and uncomfortable for the rest of the evening. A third person, sitting at the same table, eats the same pizza, feels foggy and tired the next day, and decides she has a "gluten sensitivity." Same food. Three different stories. And those three stories run on three different mechanisms inside the body.

We use one casual phrase for all of it. "That food doesn't agree with me." But "doesn't agree" hides at least three distinct mechanisms, and the difference between them is not hair-splitting. One can kill you. One is a predictable enzyme shortage you manage with a dose. One is a fuzzy, heavily marketed category where the science is thin and the testing industry is loud. If you cannot tell them apart, you cannot make good decisions, and you become a target for some of the worst advice in the wellness space.

The vocabulary problem

Start with clean definitions, then earn each one. The umbrella term for "any bad reaction to a food" is adverse food reaction. Underneath that umbrella sit two fundamentally different families, divided by a single question: is the immune system involved?

A food allergy is an adverse reaction driven by the immune system, reproducible every time, in which the body has misidentified a harmless food protein as a threat (Sicherer & Sampson, 2018). A food intolerance is an adverse reaction that does not involve the immune system at all. It is a metabolic, enzymatic, pharmacological, or transport problem, the digestive equivalent of a missing tool or an overloaded pathway (Lomer, 2014). And then there is the word sensitivity, which is not a clean mechanistic category so much as a placeholder we use when symptoms are real but the pathway is unproven or genuinely uncertain.

A lot of people get this backward. They treat the three as points on a single severity scale, as if intolerance is a "mild allergy" and sensitivity is an "even milder allergy." That is false. They are not one mechanism running at different speeds. They are different mechanisms. An allergy and an intolerance to the same food share nothing except the food. Lock that in and the rest of this chapter follows.

This is not pedantry, and it is not just about using the right word at dinner. The category you are in determines everything downstream: how the reaction is diagnosed, who should be doing the diagnosing, whether a trace amount is dangerous or irrelevant, whether the right move is strict lifelong avoidance or simple dose management, and whether you should be carrying an emergency injector or a digestive enzyme. Misclassify the mechanism and you can do real harm in either direction. You can dismiss a life-threatening allergy as "just a sensitivity," or you can saddle someone with a fearful, needlessly narrow diet over a reaction that was never dangerous in the first place. Naming the mechanism correctly is the whole job.

One more piece of vocabulary worth setting straight, because it trips up almost everyone. The clinical umbrella for the immune-driven reactions, both the fast antibody type and the slower cell-driven type, is food hypersensitivity. In everyday speech people say "sensitivity" to mean something vague and mild, but in the technical literature "hypersensitivity" specifically signals immune involvement. We will use the plain words in this chapter, allergy and intolerance and sensitivity, but keep in mind that the casual meaning of "sensitivity" and the clinical meaning of "hypersensitivity" are pulling in opposite directions, which is a large part of why this whole area is so confused.

Allergy: the immune system makes a mistake

A true food allergy is your immune system treating a food protein the way it would treat a parasite or a pathogen. In the classic and most dangerous form, IgE-mediated allergy, the first exposure produces antibodies of the IgE class that are specific to that food's proteins. Those antibodies arm the body's mast cells and basophils, the immune cells loaded with inflammatory chemicals. The next time that protein shows up, it cross-links the IgE on those cells and they degranulate, dumping histamine and other mediators in seconds to minutes (Sicherer & Sampson, 2018).

That speed is the signature. IgE reactions are fast, often appearing within minutes to two hours, and they are frequently visible and dramatic: hives, swelling of the lips and face, vomiting, wheezing, and in the worst case anaphylaxis, a whole-body reaction that can drop blood pressure and close the airway. This is the reaction that puts an epinephrine auto-injector in a backpack. IgE-mediated food allergy affects roughly five to ten percent of people, with higher rates in children, and the common culprits are a short list: milk, egg, peanut, tree nuts, wheat, soy, fish, and shellfish (Tedner et al., 2021).

There is a quieter branch too. Non-IgE-mediated immune reactions involve other arms of the immune system, typically T cells, and they tend to be slower and gut-centered rather than the sudden hives-and-swelling picture (Sicherer & Sampson, 2018). These are harder to diagnose precisely because the timing is delayed and the symptoms are less specific. Keep that branch in mind, because it is part of why the word "sensitivity" gets so muddy later.

Why the immune system turns on food in the first place

The strange thing about a food allergy is not that it happens. It is that it does not happen far more often. You eat foreign protein every single day. Your gut sees an enormous load of outside material, and most of the time the immune system makes a deliberate decision to stand down. That trained calm has a name, oral tolerance, and it is the immune system actively learning that a given food protein is safe and choosing not to attack it. A food allergy is a failure of that learning. For reasons that involve genetics, the timing of first exposure, the skin barrier, and the gut environment, the immune system files a harmless protein under "threat" instead of "safe," and then defends that misfiling every time the protein returns.

The first encounter is silent. On that first exposure the immune system becomes sensitized: helper T cells of a particular type drive B cells to produce IgE antibodies shaped to fit that food's proteins, and those antibodies go and dock onto the surface of mast cells and basophils throughout the body (Anvari et al., 2019). Nothing visible happens yet. The person feels fine. The trap is simply set. It is the second and later exposures that spring it, which is why a food someone has eaten uneventfully for years can, in principle, become a problem: the sensitizing event and the reacting event are separated in time.

When the protein returns, it bridges two of those docked IgE antibodies at once, and that physical cross-linking is the trigger that tells the mast cell to fire. The cell degranulates, releasing pre-made histamine instantly and manufacturing other inflammatory mediators over the following minutes (Anvari et al., 2019). Histamine is what produces the familiar signs directly: it makes small blood vessels leaky (swelling and hives), tightens airway muscle (wheeze), and stimulates nerves (itch). The whole cascade is fast because the ammunition was already loaded and waiting.

Anaphylaxis: when the reaction goes system-wide

Most allergic reactions stay local and unpleasant. Anaphylaxis is what it is called when the same mediator release happens broadly and fast enough to threaten the whole body at once. Blood vessels dilate and leak across the system, which can drop blood pressure toward shock; the airway can swell and the lower airways can constrict, which compromises breathing; the gut can cramp and empty. Two body systems involved, or a significant blood-pressure drop, is the kind of picture clinicians treat as anaphylaxis (Shaker et al., 2020). It can develop within minutes, and in a fraction of cases it follows a biphasic course, settling and then returning hours later without any new exposure, which is one reason a person is kept under observation after a serious reaction rather than sent straight home (Shaker et al., 2020).

The treatment for anaphylaxis is not an antihistamine and it is not a steroid. It is epinephrine (adrenaline), and it is first-line precisely because of what it physically does: it tightens the leaking blood vessels back up, relaxes the airway, and supports the heart, reversing the very mechanics that make anaphylaxis dangerous. The major practice guidelines are unambiguous that epinephrine is the first-line drug, that antihistamines and glucocorticoids do not reliably prevent or stop the dangerous part of the reaction, and that delay in giving epinephrine is associated with worse outcomes (Shaker et al., 2020). This is why a person diagnosed with a food allergy at risk of anaphylaxis is prescribed an epinephrine auto-injector, taught to use it, and told to use it early rather than wait and see. This chapter is education, not instruction in emergency care, but the headline is worth stating plainly: a severe reaction is a call-emergency-services situation, and the auto-injector is a bridge to that care, not a replacement for it.

Who diagnoses and manages this, and how

True food allergy sits with medical professionals, specifically an allergist (an allergy and immunology specialist), and the reason is not gatekeeping. It is that the diagnosis is genuinely easy to get wrong, in both directions, and the stakes are high. The validated tools are the skin-prick test and the blood test for food-specific IgE, and both have an important limitation that a layperson reading a result would never guess: a positive test shows sensitization, that IgE to the food exists, but it does not by itself prove the person will clinically react, and the size of the result does not reliably predict how severe a reaction would be (Anvari et al., 2019). Plenty of people have detectable IgE to a food they eat without any trouble. That gap is exactly why testing has to be read against the actual history of what happens when the person eats the food, and why the reference-standard confirmation, when the picture is unclear, is a supervised oral food challenge done in a medical setting with rescue treatment on hand. You can see why this is not a do-it-yourself project. The interpretation is the hard part, and getting it wrong means either missing a real risk or branding someone allergic to a food they could safely eat.

Coaching translation: Allergy is the immune system crying wolf about a harmless protein. The reaction is reproducible, it can be fast and severe, and it is not something you experiment with on your own. If anyone has experienced throat tightening, widespread hives, or trouble breathing after a food, that is a medical situation, full stop. The right move is a clinician, ideally an allergist, real validated testing read against the real history, and if anaphylaxis is on the table, an epinephrine auto-injector and a plan for using it. Nothing in this chapter is a substitute for that.

Intolerance: the chemistry just runs out of tools

Now flip to the other family. A food intolerance has no immune antibodies, no mast cells, no histamine dump from an immune trigger. It is a plumbing-and-chemistry problem. The most common and best-understood example is the one this course's earlier chapter on chemical demolition set up perfectly: lactose intolerance.

Recall the principle from this course's chapter on chemical demolition: digestion is fundamentally a series of enzymes cutting big molecules into small ones the gut can absorb. Lactose, the sugar in milk, is a disaccharide, two sugar units bonded together. You cannot absorb it whole. The enzyme lactase, anchored in the brush border of the small intestine, is the scissors that cuts lactose into glucose and galactose, which then absorb easily (Deng et al., 2015).

Now the twist of human biology. Most mammals switch off lactase production after weaning, because in nature no adult animal drinks milk. For most of human history that was true of us too. The ability to keep making lactase into adulthood, called lactase persistence, is actually the genetic mutation, and it spread in populations with a long history of dairy herding. So globally, reduced lactase in adulthood is the default state, not a disease. This is primary lactase deficiency, the genetically programmed decline (Deng et al., 2015).

What actually causes the symptoms

When lactase is low, undigested lactose travels down to the colon intact. And this is where the colon and its microbiome come back into the story, the community of bacteria this course covered earlier. Two things happen. First, lactose is osmotically active: it pulls water into the gut lumen, which loosens stool. Second, the resident bacteria do what bacteria do with an unexpected sugar buffet. They ferment it, producing hydrogen, carbon dioxide, methane, and short-chain fatty acids. That gas is the bloating, the cramping, the audible gurgling, and the flatulence (Deng et al., 2015).

Notice what this means. The symptoms of lactose intolerance are produced partly by your gut bacteria, not by lactose itself. That is why the same person can tolerate a splash of milk in coffee but not a tall glass: it is dose-dependent. Below a threshold, the small amount of lactose that escapes digestion is handled quietly. Above it, the fermentation overwhelms the system. Gut motility and the exact makeup of your microbiome shift that threshold up or down from person to person (Deng et al., 2015).

There are other forms worth naming. Secondary lactase deficiency happens when the small-intestinal lining is damaged, by infection, by celiac disease, by gut inflammation, and lactase production drops temporarily because the brush border is wrecked. Fix the underlying damage and lactase often recovers. Congenital lactase deficiency is a rare inherited absence of the enzyme from birth (Deng et al., 2015). The point of distinguishing them: same symptom, different causes, different management.

Intolerance is broader than lactose

Lactose is the headline, but intolerance is a whole family. FODMAPs, a group of fermentable carbohydrates (fermentable oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides, and polyols), cause symptoms by the same basic mechanism: poor absorption in the small intestine, osmotic water-pulling, and colonic fermentation. FODMAP sensitivity has genuinely strong mechanistic evidence behind it, which makes it different from a lot of vaguer claims (Schnedl et al., 2019). Lactose, by the way, is itself a FODMAP, which is why these stories overlap.

The FODMAP idea is worth slowing down on, because it explains a huge share of the day-to-day gut complaints people blame on random foods. The letters stand for fermentable oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides, and polyols, which is a mouthful that hides a simple shared trait: these are small carbohydrates that the small intestine absorbs poorly. Fructans in wheat and onions, galacto-oligosaccharides in beans, lactose in milk, excess fructose in some fruits, and sugar alcohols like sorbitol and mannitol in sugar-free gum and certain fruits all qualify. Because they pass the small intestine without being fully absorbed, they reach the colon still intact, where they do exactly what undigested lactose does: pull water in by osmosis and feed the resident bacteria, which ferment them into gas. The result is the familiar package of bloating, distension, cramping, and altered stool. The dose dependence is the giveaway again. A small serving slips under the threshold; a large one, or several FODMAP-rich foods stacked in one meal, pushes over it.

Then there are pharmacological intolerances, where a food component acts on the body the way a drug would rather than through digestion at all. Histamine intolerance is the cleanest example, and it is a genuinely useful one because it shows how a non-immune reaction can impersonate an allergic one. Histamine is not only something your mast cells release; it is also present in food, and it builds up as food ages or ferments. Aged cheeses, cured and smoked meats, fermented products, some fish, and certain wines and ferments can carry a substantial histamine load. Normally an enzyme in the gut wall called diamine oxidase (DAO) clears that dietary histamine before it matters. Some people have low DAO activity, so the histamine they eat is not broken down fast enough and spills into circulation, producing flushing, headache, nasal congestion, and gut symptoms (Tedner et al., 2021; Schnedl et al., 2019). The symptoms can look like an allergic reaction because the chemical messenger is literally the same one, histamine. The difference is the route: in allergy the body releases its own histamine after an IgE trigger, while in histamine intolerance the histamine came in on the plate and simply was not cleared. No antibodies, no sensitization, no mast cells crying wolf. It is a clearance problem, and like the other intolerances it is dose-related, which is why the same person can handle a fresh meal but not a plate of aged, fermented, leftover food.

Sucrase-isomaltase deficiency is another missing-enzyme problem, this one affecting the digestion of sucrose and certain starches, and it produces the same osmotic-and-fermentation picture when the unsplit sugar reaches the colon. The unifying theme of the whole intolerance family is now clear: there is no immune attack anywhere in it. Every member is either a missing or low enzyme, a transporter that cannot keep up, or a food chemical the body cannot clear fast enough. Chemistry that has run short of a tool, or run past a threshold. That is the entire family.

Food intolerance, taken together, affects an estimated fifteen to twenty percent of the population, which is far more common than true allergy (Lomer, 2014). That mismatch, common intolerance versus rarer allergy, is exactly why so many genuine reactions get mislabeled as "allergies" in casual speech.

How intolerance is actually confirmed

Because intolerance runs on fermentation rather than antibodies, the validated test for the carbohydrate type measures the gas itself. The hydrogen breath test works on a clean piece of logic: human cells do not produce hydrogen gas, but gut bacteria do when they ferment carbohydrate. Give a measured dose of lactose, then sample the breath over the next few hours. If lactose is being absorbed normally in the small intestine, little reaches the colon and breath hydrogen stays low. If lactase is short, the lactose arrives in the colon, the bacteria ferment it, and the hydrogen they make is absorbed into the blood and breathed out, where it shows up as a rise in the breath sample (Catanzaro et al., 2021). The test is non-invasive, inexpensive, and both sensitive and specific, which is why it is the most common method used in practice (Catanzaro et al., 2021). The major European guideline adds an important refinement: a positive gas reading alone is "malabsorption," and to call it intolerance you also want the test to reproduce the person's actual symptoms, because some people malabsorb a sugar without being bothered by it (Hammer et al., 2022). Other approaches exist, a genetic test for lactase persistence, a blood-sugar lactose tolerance test, but the breath test is the workhorse (Catanzaro et al., 2021).

Notice the contrast that is quietly doing a lot of work here. The validated test for an allergy looks for an antibody or a skin reaction. The validated test for a carbohydrate intolerance looks for fermentation gas. They measure completely different things because the mechanisms are completely different. A test built for one tells you nothing about the other. That single fact is the hinge that the entire back half of this chapter turns on, because the testing industry blurs exactly this line.

Coaching translation: Intolerance is dose-dependent and predictable. There is a threshold, and below it you are usually fine. That is the opposite of allergy, where a trace of the protein can set off the immune system. So a person with lactose intolerance does not need to fear all dairy forever. They can find their threshold, lean on hard aged cheeses and yogurts that are naturally lower in lactose, spread dairy across the day instead of in one large hit, or take a lactase supplement that supplies the missing scissors right when they eat. This is management, not elimination, and the difference in quality of life between those two approaches is enormous.

Celiac disease: not a sensitivity, not an allergy

This is the one that gets mislabeled most often, and most dangerously. Celiac disease is neither a food allergy nor a simple intolerance. It is an autoimmune disease, which builds on the idea this course developed earlier that the gut wall is also an immune organ (Caio et al., 2019).

The mechanism is autoimmune. In genetically susceptible people, those carrying the HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 immune-gene variants, dietary gluten triggers an immune response that does not stop at the gluten. The enzyme tissue transglutaminase modifies gluten fragments in the gut wall, and the immune system mounts both innate and adaptive responses that ultimately attack the person's own intestinal tissue. The autoantigen is the body's own enzyme. The result is villous atrophy: the finger-like villi that absorb nutrients get flattened and destroyed, which is why untreated celiac causes malabsorption, deficiency, and a long tail of downstream problems (Caio et al., 2019).

This is a categorically different thing from an IgE allergy. There is no immediate histamine reaction, no anaphylaxis from the autoimmune process, and no epinephrine injector. It is also categorically different from non-celiac discomfort with wheat, because in celiac the immune system is damaging the body's own tissue, with measurable antibodies and visible structural damage on biopsy. Caio and colleagues are explicit: celiac disease is not an IgE-mediated allergy and it is not merely a "sensitivity" (Caio et al., 2019). The only treatment is strict, lifelong gluten avoidance, and in celiac that is not optional caution, it is preventing ongoing autoimmune damage.

This matters in practice. Someone who casually goes "gluten-free" because they think it is healthier, then later wonders if they might have celiac, has a problem: you cannot reliably test for celiac once gluten is already out of the diet, because the antibodies and the damage fade. The diagnostic window closes. This is one of the clearest reasons that self-directed elimination, done blind, can backfire.

"Sensitivity": the fuzzy middle, and where the marketing lives

So where does "sensitivity" fit? It is a mixed bag, and it demands care. Part of the category is real and mechanistically respectable. Non-celiac gluten or wheat sensitivity describes people who get genuine gut and sometimes systemic symptoms from wheat, who do not have celiac disease and do not have a wheat allergy, but who reliably improve when wheat comes out and relapse when it goes back in (Schnedl et al., 2019). The symptoms are not imaginary. These people are not making it up, and the relief they get from removing wheat is real.

What is genuinely uncertain is the mechanism, and the uncertainty is the interesting part. The obvious assumption is that the gluten protein is the culprit, the way it is in celiac. But carefully controlled studies muddied that story. When some people who improved on a gluten-free diet were later given gluten in disguise, against a background diet low in FODMAPs, many of them did not reliably react to the gluten itself. That pointed suspicion at the FODMAP fraction of wheat, the poorly absorbed fructans, rather than the gluten protein. If that is what is happening for a given person, then what they are calling a gluten sensitivity is really a FODMAP intolerance wearing a different costume, and it would respond to a low-FODMAP approach rather than strict gluten avoidance. For others, the trigger may genuinely be a wheat protein, just not through the celiac pathway. The honest summary is that "non-celiac gluten sensitivity" is probably not one condition at all but a label sitting on top of several different mechanisms we are still untangling (Schnedl et al., 2019). The practical implication is real: someone in this group does not necessarily need to police every trace of gluten the way a person with celiac does, because for them a trace is not driving autoimmune damage. The category is real; the certainty around it is not.

The other part of the category is where the trouble starts. The word "sensitivity" has been adopted by a testing and supplement industry that sells the idea that hidden food reactions explain almost any symptom: fatigue, brain fog, joint aches, mood, weight. The claim sounds plausible and the testing looks scientific. It mostly is not.

The IgG testing problem

The single most common commercial test sold for "food sensitivity" measures food-specific IgG antibodies against dozens or hundreds of foods. You send blood, you get back a colorful chart of "reactive" foods, and you are told to eliminate them. The problem is not subtle. The Canadian Society of Allergy and Clinical Immunology reviewed this and concluded there is no body of research supporting food-specific IgG testing to diagnose adverse food reactions (Carr et al., 2012).

Why not? Because food-specific IgG is a marker of exposure and tolerance, not disease. When you eat a food regularly, your immune system produces IgG to it as a normal, healthy response. So a "positive" IgG result is exactly what you would expect in a healthy person who eats that food often (Carr et al., 2012). The test is essentially detecting your diet. People who eat a lot of eggs test "reactive" to eggs. The chart is measuring what you eat, then telling you to stop eating it. That is backwards.

And IgG panels are just the most popular of a long list. Kelso (2018) reviewed the broader market of unproven methods: IgG and IgG4 panels, cytotoxic testing (looking at whether blood cells change shape when mixed with food extract), provocation-neutralization, hair analysis, electrodermal "energy" testing, and applied kinesiology, the muscle-testing one where a practitioner judges whether your arm gets weaker while you hold a vial of food. His conclusion is blunt: these methods lack biologic plausibility, and where they have been tested under blinded, controlled conditions, they show either no correlation with actual disease or a flat failure to reproduce.

It is worth being concrete about why each kind of test misleads, because the failures are instructive. Hair analysis fails on basic biology: a strand of hair is a record of certain minerals and exposures, and there is no mechanism by which it could encode a food reaction, so a "food sensitivity from hair" result is detecting nothing relevant. Applied kinesiology fails on blinding: when the practitioner cannot see which vial is which, the arm-strength "signal" disappears, which tells you the result was coming from the tester's expectations, not from the food. Electrodermal devices fail the same way, reading skin conductance that has no established link to food reactions. And the IgG panels fail in the most seductive way of all, because they actually measure a real molecule. That is what makes them dangerous. A hair test is easy to dismiss as fringe. A blood test that returns precise antibody numbers across a hundred foods looks exactly like real medicine. The problem is not that the lab made up the numbers; the numbers may be perfectly accurate. The problem is that the thing being measured, IgG to food, is a sign of normal exposure and tolerance, so the test is answering a question nobody should be asking. A confident, quantitative, completely valid measurement of the wrong thing is more persuasive, and therefore more harmful, than an obvious fake.

Think About It

If food-specific IgG rises with regular exposure to a food, what would you predict an IgG panel shows for someone's favorite, most-eaten foods? Now explain why "eliminate everything the test flagged" is not just unhelpful but potentially harmful.

Coaching translation: The blunt version. If a test claims to find dozens of "sensitivities" from one blood draw and the recommendation is to cut out a long list of foods, be skeptical. The major allergy and immunology bodies do not endorse these tests, and the most popular one is essentially measuring your grocery list. This does not mean your symptoms are fake. It means the test is not a valid way to find the cause, and acting on it can push you into needless, broad restriction. There is a better, slower, actually-informative tool, and that is next.

The elimination diet, done as an experiment

An elimination diet, done properly, is not a cleanse and it is not a permanent diet. It is a structured experiment. Its entire purpose is to generate information about whether a specific food is driving specific symptoms. And like any experiment, it only works if it has both halves: the part where you remove something, and the part where you test what happens when you put it back (Raber & McMurray, 2023).

The structure has three phases.

Phase 1: Rule things out first

Before removing any food, the conditions that mimic food reactions need to be considered. Celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, infections, and other pathologies can masquerade as food sensitivity, and many of them require tests that only work while you are still eating the suspect food (Raber & McMurray, 2023). Remember the celiac trap: go gluten-free first and you may lose the ability to diagnose it. This is the step people skip, and it is the step that protects you from chasing the wrong thing for months.

Phase 2: The removal phase

You remove the suspected food, or a small, defined set of foods, completely, for a defined window, commonly a few weeks. The goal is a clean baseline: long enough for symptoms to settle if that food was the driver, short enough that you are not restricting indefinitely. The narrower and more targeted the removal, the better. Pulling out one or two suspects gives you a clean signal. Pulling out twenty foods "to be safe" gives you a tangled mess you cannot interpret, plus a real risk of nutritional gaps.

Phase 3: Systematic reintroduction, the part everyone skips

This is the whole game, and it is the part people abandon. After the removal window, you reintroduce foods one at a time, each on its own, with a washout period between them, while tracking symptoms carefully. Reintroduce a single food, watch for a defined number of days, and if nothing happens, move to the next. If symptoms return, you have found a candidate, and you can confirm it by removing again and re-challenging.

Why does the reintroduction matter so much? Removal alone proves nothing. If you cut out five foods and feel better, you have learned that something changed, not which food, and not whether it was the food at all versus the placebo effect, the change in overall eating, or simple regression to the mean. The information is generated by the controlled re-challenge. One variable at a time. That is what separates an experiment from a guess (Raber & McMurray, 2023).

The common failure modes are exactly the ones that destroy the experiment. Reintroducing several foods at once: now a reaction cannot be assigned to any single food. Using too short a washout: a delayed reaction from food A bleeds into the window for food B and you blame the wrong one. And the biggest one, never reintroducing at all: people remove foods, feel better, get scared, and just stay restricted forever. That is not an elimination diet. That is the start of an unnecessarily narrow diet built on an untested assumption.

The risks nobody mentions on the package

Elimination done carelessly is not harmless. Broad self-restriction carries real nutritional risk, and this connects directly to the deficiency content of Chapter 8. Cut out dairy and you put calcium and vitamin D at risk. Cut out whole grains and you lose fiber and B vitamins. Cut out a long list at once and you can drift into genuine inadequacy, especially in children, in pregnancy, and in anyone already eating a limited diet. This is precisely why the clinical literature stresses that elimination diets cannot replace comprehensive, guideline-directed evaluation, and why registered dietitians are considered essential to prevent deficiencies during the restriction phase (Raber & McMurray, 2023).

There is a psychological hazard too. The longer the list of "bad" foods grows, the more eating becomes a field of fear, and the harder it is to ever add anything back. A tool meant to expand your understanding can quietly shrink your life. The fix is the structure: targeted removal, defined windows, and disciplined reintroduction, ideally with professional guidance.

The same food, three different ways to cause trouble

The idea that ties this chapter together: the same food can cause trouble through completely different pathways, and the only way to manage it well is to know which pathway you are dealing with.

Take milk. One person has an IgE allergy to milk protein: immune-mediated, potentially severe, even a trace matters, manage by strict avoidance and possibly an injector. Another person has lactose intolerance: enzymatic and microbial, dose-dependent, manage with thresholds, lactase supplements, and lower-lactose dairy. A third person reads an IgG panel that flagged milk and removes it for no good reason, taking on calcium risk to chase a result that mostly reflected the fact that they drink milk. Same glass. Three mechanisms. Three completely different correct responses (Tedner et al., 2021; Lomer, 2014; Carr et al., 2012).

And these can coexist. The reviews are explicit that different hypersensitivity diagnoses can overlap in the same person and are not always easy to disentangle (Tedner et al., 2021). That is humbling, and it is the honest scientific position. It is also why the answer is rarely "cut it out forever," and almost always "figure out the mechanism, find the threshold, and protect your nutrition while you do it."

Food allergy is an adverse immune-specific response; food intolerance is a non-immune reaction, metabolic, pharmacologic, or otherwise, that happens to involve the same foods through entirely different machinery.

After Sicherer & Sampson (2018) and Lomer (2014)

In practice, this means you do three things. First, when in doubt about a severe or immune reaction, you get real testing from real clinicians, not a mail-order panel. Second, you treat intolerances as dose problems to manage, not as foods to fear. Third, if you want to investigate a suspected food, you do it as an actual experiment, with targeted removal and disciplined reintroduction, ideally with guidance, and you do not let a temporary tool quietly become a permanently shrunken diet.

Case study: the panel that cost Theo five foods for nothing

Case

Theo is 41 and has felt off for months: low energy by mid-afternoon, occasional bloating, a foggy head he cannot shake. His doctor's visit turned up nothing dramatic, so he searches online and orders a mail-order "food sensitivity panel." He sends a finger-prick of blood and gets back a glossy report grading dozens of foods red, yellow, and green. Eggs, dairy, wheat, almonds, and yeast all come back "highly reactive," and the accompanying guide tells him to eliminate every red food for at least ninety days. He does it, all five at once, immediately. The first two weeks he actually feels a bit better. Encouraged, he keeps the list off the table indefinitely, and over the next few months it quietly grows, because cutting wheat and dairy and eggs at once means cutting most of what he used to grab quickly, and he never adds anything back.

Run Theo through this chapter and the problem is not that his symptoms were fake. They were real, and they may well have had a real cause. The problem is the tool he reached for. The panel measured food-specific IgG, and as the position statement from the allergy and immunology body makes clear, IgG to a food is a marker of exposure and tolerance, not of disease (Carr et al., 2012). The five foods flagged "highly reactive" were almost certainly his five most-eaten foods. Eggs, dairy, and wheat top the list precisely because he ate them constantly. The test handed him his own breakfast back and labeled it the enemy. He paid to be told to stop eating the things he eats.

Then look at what he did with the result, because the method compounded the testing error. He removed five foods simultaneously, which means that even the early improvement he felt tells him almost nothing. Five variables changed at once, alongside the general lift people get from eating more deliberately, the placebo effect of finally "doing something," and ordinary day-to-day variation in how he feels. If wheat was the only real driver, or if none of them were, his experiment cannot tell the difference. Removal without controlled reintroduction generates no usable information, and Theo never reintroduced anything. He skipped the only phase that produces an answer.

And the restriction is not free. By cutting dairy he put calcium and vitamin D at risk; by cutting whole grains he lost fiber and B vitamins; by stacking the whole list he narrowed his diet enough that nutritional gaps and a creeping fear of food both became real hazards, the two risks any careless elimination carries. Months in, Theo is more restricted, no clearer on the cause, and arguably worse off than when he started, all on the strength of a test that was measuring the wrong thing and a protocol with no reintroduction.

The better path was available the whole time and follows directly from this chapter. First, take the symptoms seriously but treat the test as noise, because the major allergy bodies do not endorse it. Second, if a food is genuinely worth investigating, run it as an actual experiment: rule out the conditions that mimic food reactions first (the bloating and fog could be any number of things, some of which need testing while the suspect food is still in the diet), then remove one or two targeted suspects for a defined window, then reintroduce them one at a time with a washout between, tracking symptoms, ideally with a clinician or registered dietitian guiding the design so his nutrition stays intact. That approach might have found a real trigger in weeks, or shown there was none to find, without surrendering five foods and several months to a colorful chart of his own grocery list.

Key Takeaways

  • The dividing line between allergy and intolerance is a single question: is the immune system involved? Allergy is immune-mediated; intolerance is not.
  • IgE-mediated allergy is fast, reproducible, can be severe to the point of anaphylaxis, and is triggered by tiny amounts. It is a medical matter, not a self-experiment.
  • Lactose intolerance is missing-enzyme chemistry from Chapter 4: undigested lactose reaches the colon, where osmosis and microbial fermentation from Chapter 2 generate the gas and bloating. It is dose-dependent and manageable.
  • Celiac disease is autoimmune, not an allergy and not a "sensitivity": gluten triggers an immune attack on the body's own tissue, with measurable antibodies and villous atrophy, tied to the immune content of Chapter 6.
  • "Sensitivity" is a mixed category. Some of it, like FODMAP and possibly non-celiac wheat sensitivity, has real mechanistic support; much of the commercial version does not.
  • Food-specific IgG testing measures dietary exposure and tolerance, not disease. Positive results are normal in healthy people, and major allergy bodies do not support it for diagnosing food reactions.
  • An elimination diet only generates information if you do the reintroduction: one food at a time, with washout periods, tracking symptoms. Removal alone proves nothing.
  • Broad self-restriction carries real nutritional risk (Chapter 8) and psychological risk, which is why targeted design and professional guidance matter.
