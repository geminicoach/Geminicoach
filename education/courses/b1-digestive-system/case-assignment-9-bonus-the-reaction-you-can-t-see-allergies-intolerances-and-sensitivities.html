Case Assignment · B1 Digestive System

Chapter 9 Bonus: Apply It to a Client

A client has self-diagnosed an "allergy." Use the three-mechanism toolkit to respond in plain language, within your scope.

Name: ____________________________ Date: __________ Est. time: 25 min

The Scenario

Client · Maya, 34
Maya tells you she has "figured out she is allergic to dairy." Her evidence: about an hour after a big bowl of ice cream or a tall latte she gets bloated, gassy, and crampy for the evening. It settles on its own by morning. She has noticed that a splash of milk in her coffee causes no trouble at all, and a small scoop of ice cream is usually fine, but a large serving is not. She has never had hives, lip or throat swelling, or any trouble breathing. After reading online she has cut out all dairy "to be safe," and she is now asking you: "Since I am allergic, should I be carrying one of those epinephrine pens, and do I need to avoid every trace of dairy forever?"

Your Tasks

  1. Classify what is most likely happening, mechanically, using the chapter's framework. Name the family and the pathway, and point to the specific features in Maya's story that put it there.
  2. Explain to Maya, in plain language she could actually use, why this reads as an intolerance rather than an allergy, contrasting the two mechanisms (immune vs non-immune, trace-dangerous vs dose-dependent).
  3. Correct the self-diagnosis without overstepping: make clear what you can and cannot tell her, who actually diagnoses or rules out a true allergy, and why she should not test it by eating dairy to "see what happens."
  4. Give Maya practical, in-scope next steps for a dose-dependent intolerance, and state plainly the red flags that would change your answer and send her to a clinician immediately.

Your Response

0 words
Coaching boundary
You are demonstrating understanding and client communication, not diagnosing. Do not confirm or rule out a food allergy: that is an allergist's job. Never advise a client to self-diagnose, and never suggest testing a reaction by eating a suspected allergen. A strong answer corrects the self-diagnosis gently, explains the mechanism difference, manages the intolerance within scope, and names the red flags (throat tightening, hives, trouble breathing) that make a reaction a medical emergency and an allergist referral.
Instructor / self-check rubric
  • Correctly classifies the bloating-only, clearly dose-dependent picture as lactose intolerance, not an allergy.
  • Contrasts the two mechanisms explicitly: immune vs enzymatic and microbial, trace-dangerous vs dose-dependent.
  • Corrects the self-diagnosis: a coach does not diagnose or rule out an allergy; an allergist does, with validated testing read against the real history.
  • Offers in-scope, practical intolerance management (find the threshold, lean on lower-lactose dairy, spread dairy across the day, consider a lactase supplement) rather than total avoidance.
  • States the red flags (throat tightening, hives, trouble breathing) and the never-test-by-eating-it rule, and explains that no epinephrine pen is indicated for an intolerance.
