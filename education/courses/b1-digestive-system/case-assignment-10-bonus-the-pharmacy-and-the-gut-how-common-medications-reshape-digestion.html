Case Assignment · B1 Digestive System

Chapter 10 Bonus: Apply It to a Client

Use the pharmacology of acid reducers to read a real complaint, hold the medication boundary, and route it correctly.

Name: ____________________________ Date: __________ Est. time: 25 min

The Scenario

Client · Pearl, 58
Pearl started an acid reducer (a PPI) years ago for stubborn reflux and has taken it daily ever since, long enough that she cannot remember the original conversation with her doctor. Lately she has been more tired than usual, a little short of breath on the stairs, and she mentions in passing that her tongue feels oddly sore. She also read an article online claiming acid reducers "destroy your gut," and she has decided to quit hers cold, starting tomorrow. She asks you, her coach: "Is that a good idea, and what should I take instead?"

Your Tasks

  1. Explain, mechanically, why stopping a long-term PPI abruptly can backfire. Name the rebound mechanism and what Pearl would likely experience and misinterpret if she quits cold tomorrow.
  2. Account for Pearl's tiredness, breathlessness, and sore tongue using the chapter's nutrient mechanisms. Which acid-dependent nutrients are most relevant, and why would a deficiency surface slowly, years in?
  3. State clearly what you will and will not do. Pearl asked two things, whether to stop and what to take instead. Show exactly where your scope ends, and why advising her to stop, to continue, or to take a B12 or iron supplement would all cross the line.
  4. Write the actual message you would give Pearl: a single, in-scope communication that routes both the medication question and the symptoms to the right professional, with the two specific things she should raise.

Your Response

0 words
Coaching boundary
You are demonstrating mechanism literacy and client communication, not managing medication. A strong answer explains the rebound and nutrient mechanisms, refuses to advise stopping, continuing, or changing the drug, declines to recommend a supplement before testing, and names the prescriber or pharmacist as the destination. Telling a client to stop, skip, or change a prescribed medication is never within scope.
Instructor / self-check rubric
  • Explains rebound acid hypersecretion (gastrin rises, pumps upregulate) and why quitting cold can make reflux worse than before.
  • Identifies the plausible B12 and non-heme iron deficiency picture and the acid-release mechanism that explains the symptoms, including why it surfaces slowly.
  • Explicitly refuses to advise stopping, continuing, or changing the medication, and names that as a clinical decision for the prescriber.
  • Declines to recommend a B12 or iron supplement and explains why testing must come before supplementing.
  • Routes both the medication question and the symptoms to the prescriber or pharmacist with a concrete, useful message.
