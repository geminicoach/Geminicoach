Cheat-Sheet · B1 Digestive System

Chapter 3 : The Second Brain

One axis, two-way traffic (mostly upward): the gut runs itself, reports to the brain, and reorganizes under stress.

The Gut-Brain Axis in One Pass

Enteric nervous system200 to 600 million neurons in the gut wall; the only large neural network outside the CNS that runs reflexes on its own. Digestion is mostly self-managed.
Myenteric plexusBetween the muscle layers; governs motility (the squeezing that moves contents along).
Submucosal plexusCloser to the lining; governs secretion and local blood flow.
Vagus nerveMain gut-brain cable. Roughly 80% afferent (gut to brain), 20% efferent (brain to gut). Traffic is mostly an upload.

Key Terms

  • Interoception : the body's sense of its own internal state; the vagus is its largest channel.
  • Vagal tone : ongoing parasympathetic activity; higher tone tilts toward calm and smooth digestion.
  • NTS : brainstem hub where vagal afferents arrive, then route to mood and arousal centers.
  • Cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway : vagal output that brakes inflammation.

The Serotonin Story, Honestly

  • About 90 to 95% of the body's serotonin is gut-made, by enterochromaffin cells.
  • It does not cross the blood-brain barrier. Gut and brain serotonin are two separate pools.
  • Gut serotonin drives motility and signals up the vagus; the message reaches the brain, the molecule does not.
  • "Gut serotonin makes you happy" is a myth built on a real mechanism.

One Circuit: Diet to Mood Signaling

  • Fiber feeds microbes; microbes make short-chain fatty acids (butyrate prominent).
  • SCFAs raise TPH1 in EC cells, increasing serotonin synthesis.
  • Serotonin drives motility and activates vagal afferents to the brainstem.
  • Microbes also make and consume other neuroactive molecules (GABA, dopamine), acting locally.

Stress Is a Digestive Event

  • SAM axis : fast; adrenaline inhibits motility, redirects blood from the gut.
  • HPA axis : slow; CRF and cortisol reshape motility, sensitivity, barrier, microbiota.
  • Stress lowers vagal tone: slowed above, hurried below. Acute is adaptive; chronic reshapes function.
  • Managing stress is a real lever on digestion, not a soft add-on.

Where It Meets Your Scope

  • IBS is a disorder of gut-brain interaction; it travels with anxiety and depression, but you do not diagnose it.
  • Gut red flags: blood in stool, unintended weight loss, fever, symptoms that wake from sleep, anemia, trouble swallowing, new symptoms over ~50.
  • Mind red flags: persistent or worsening anxiety or low mood, hopelessness; any sign of self-harm is immediate emergency referral.
  • Never use "it's just stress" to wave symptoms away; stress aggravates serious conditions too.
Coaching boundary   Educate about the mechanism and support healthy habits (sleep, movement, regular meals, slow breathing) as general wellbeing. Refer the diagnosis and treatment of both gut conditions and mental-health conditions to qualified providers. Do not diagnose IBS, do not treat anxiety or depression, and do not sell a probiotic or "gut reset" as a cure.
