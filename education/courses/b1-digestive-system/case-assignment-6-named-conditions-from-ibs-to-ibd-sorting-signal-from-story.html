Chapter 6: Apply It to a Real Case

Take the symptom-cause-effect framework and use it on a real complaint, in plain language, within your role, without diagnosing.

Name: ____________________________ Date: __________ Est. time: 25 min

The Scenario

Case · Mara, 32
Consider Mara, who has felt off for most of a year: bloated most afternoons, tired in a way coffee does not touch, and crampy after some meals. She read posts linking bloating and fatigue to gluten and to "leaky gut," cut gluten out on her own, and felt noticeably better within a couple of weeks. Encouraged, she added an online "gut reset" supplement protocol and now tells people she is "probably a little gluten sensitive." She has not seen a clinician. Her energy still is not what it used to be, and a recent routine blood draw flagged low iron, which she assumes is unrelated. Her question: "I figured out it's gluten, right? Should I bother seeing anyone?"

Your Tasks

  1. Run Mara's picture through the framework. Sort her findings into symptom, cause, and effect, and explain why her surface symptoms alone cannot tell you which condition she has.
  2. Identify the single most important clue in her story and explain, mechanically, why it should move suspicion beyond a functional disorder. Name what column it belongs to and what condition it raises.
  3. Explain the trap she has walked into by removing gluten before testing, and why that specifically compromises an accurate diagnosis.
  4. Decide what is and is not your role here. Communicate with Mara in a way that does NOT diagnose, gives her the correct order of operations, and states clearly where your lane ends and a referral begins.

Your Response

0 words
Where education ends
You are demonstrating understanding and clear communication, not diagnosing. No one outside a clinician can tell Mara she has celiac, or IBS, or that it is "just a sensitivity." A strong answer keeps the columns separate, names the effect-column clue that raises the stakes, refuses to confirm a diagnosis, gives the order of operations (testing before dietary change), and points her to a clinician promptly and plainly.
Instructor / self-check rubric
  • Keeps symptom, cause, and effect separate, and recognizes bloating plus fatigue plus cramping as the shared-symptom zone that cannot identify a condition.
  • Flags the unexplained iron-deficiency anemia as the decisive effect-column clue, a classic outside-the-gut sign of celiac that a functional disorder does not produce.
  • Explains that going gluten-free before testing quiets the gluten-provoked immune response the celiac serology and biopsy depend on, compromising diagnosis.
  • Does not diagnose Mara; explicitly stays in scope and frames the situation as a prompt referral to a clinician.
  • Gives the correct order of operations (testing before dietary change) and notes that the already-removed gluten is information the clinician needs.
  • Communicates supportively and clearly, without false reassurance or alarmism.
