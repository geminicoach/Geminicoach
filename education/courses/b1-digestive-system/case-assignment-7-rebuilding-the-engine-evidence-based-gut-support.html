Chapter 7: Apply It to a Real Situation

Take the four pillars and the claim filter, and use them to work out where a real person is spending money in the wrong place.

Name: ____________________________ Date: __________ Est. time: 30 min

The Scenario

Person · Priya, 38
Consider Priya, who is 38, a project manager with two young kids. She has been bloated and foggy for months and has done what the internet told her: a premium "gut healing" stack at about $140 a month, a daily probiotic with an impressive number of billions on the front (no strain named), a scoop of prebiotic fiber, and a glutamine powder for "leaky gut." She eats reasonably but mostly the same handful of safe foods. She sleeps about five and a half hours because that is what is left after the kids and the inbox, and she calls her stress "constant but manageable." Three months and four hundred dollars in, she feels roughly the same. The question on the table: "Which expensive thing should she add next?"

Your Tasks

  1. Explain, mechanically, why the supplements she is taking were never going to win, using the butyrate engine and what sleep loss, stress, and low plant diversity are doing to her gut barrier.
  2. Run her probiotic through the claim filter: what does "billions, no named strain" tell you, and how does the product-quality problem (Mazzantini et al., 2021) change the conversation before you even debate the strain?
  3. Reorder her plan in evidence-weighted order, naming the single highest-leverage first move and which items you would drop, and show how this costs her less, not more.
  4. Draw the scope line clearly: what falls under general education and self-care here, and where does Priya's situation need a qualified clinician rather than a self-care plan?

Your Response

0 words
Where education ends
Supplements are not treatments. You are demonstrating mechanism and clear communication, not diagnosing or treating. A strong answer explains the foundations first, weighs the supplement claims honestly, treats starting or stacking supplements (especially alongside a condition or other medications) as a reason to involve a qualified clinician, and names the signs that warrant medical evaluation (persistent symptoms, pain, blood, weight loss) that send Priya to a qualified clinician rather than to another bottle.
Instructor / self-check rubric
  • Identifies the inverted stack: money at the top while the load-bearing pillars fail underneath.
  • Explains sleep loss degrading tight junctions, MUC2, and SCFA producers as the highest-leverage fix (Sun et al., 2023).
  • Names stress (mast-cell degranulation / HPA) and low plant diversity as the untouched permeability drivers.
  • Applies the claim filter to the probiotic: strain-specificity and the product-quality / label problem (Mazzantini et al., 2021).
  • Reorders the plan in correct leverage order and shows it costs less, with the premium stack and glutamine dropped.
  • States the boundary: persistent symptoms warrant a qualified clinician; the response builds understanding and points toward care, never diagnoses, treats, or promises a cure.
