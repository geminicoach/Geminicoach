Part C · Reason About the Mechanisms

  1. An IBS patient has a completely normal colonoscopy and normal bloodwork but is in real pain. Explain, using visceral hypersensitivity, how the pain can be genuine while the tissue is intact. Why is "it's all in your head" both partly right and badly wrong?
  2. A clinic says an antibiotic improved a client's symptoms, so the client "had SIBO all along." Name the specific logical gap between "the treatment helped" and "the proposed cause was correct," and explain why that gap should change how you talk about SIBO.
  3. Increased intestinal permeability is real and measurable, and it is found in IBD patients. Explain why that association does not prove a leaky barrier caused the disease, and name the alternative direction the chapter gives.

Part D · Apply the Four Habits to a Claim

A client shows you a post: "90% of disease starts in the gut, and this 30-day protocol resets it." Run it through the chapter's four habits for grading a claim. Give a sentence for each.

  1. Which column is the claim really making (symptom, cause, or effect), and why does that weaken it?
  2. Is it leaning on association or causation? Point to the unproven link.
  3. What is the source and its incentive, and does it acknowledge uncertainty?
  4. What does it conveniently leave out? (Hint: silence about red flags is itself a red flag.)

Part F · Scope of Practice

Scope of practice is a core professional competency, not a formality, and this chapter is condition-heavy, so the boundary matters more than ever. These are diagnoses made by clinicians; coaches educate and support, never diagnose or treat. Coaching boundary
IBS, GERD, SIBO, IBD, and celiac are clinical diagnoses made by qualified providers, not by coaches. Your job is to understand them well enough to discuss them intelligently, to support someone with an existing confirmed diagnosis, and to recognize the moment a pattern moves beyond your scope. Never tell someone they "probably have IBS," never recommend a diagnostic gluten elimination, and never manage alarm features (blood, unintended weight loss, fever, nighttime symptoms, anemia, escalating pain) with lifestyle tips. When the effect column contains tissue damage, the situation belongs to medicine.
