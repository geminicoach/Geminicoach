Coaching boundary (read first)
NEVER advise a client to stop, change, skip, or start a prescribed medication. That decision belongs to the prescriber and the pharmacist, who know why the drug was prescribed and how to manage a change safely. A coach's job is to explain mechanisms, support general patterns within an educational frame, and refer the medication decision and any alarm symptoms to a qualified professional. Every answer below should respect that line.

Part C · Reason Through the Mechanisms

Answer in two to three sentences each. Name the mechanism, not just the outcome.

  1. Antibiotics knock down butyrate producers such as Faecalibacterium prausnitzii. Explain the chain from "fewer butyrate producers" to "a more vulnerable colon lining," and to "antibiotic-associated diarrhea." Why does loss of colonization resistance raise the risk of C. difficile?
  2. NSAIDs injure the gut through a dual mechanism. Describe both the COX/prostaglandin half and the direct enterocyte mitochondrial half, and explain why blocking prostaglandins lowers the stomach's own defenses while acid keeps attacking.
  3. Why can stopping a long-term PPI abruptly make reflux worse than before? Name the mechanism (gastrin, upregulated pumps) and explain why this is the single cleanest reason the stop decision belongs to a clinician.
  4. Opioid-induced constipation is uniquely stubborn. Explain where opioids act in the gut (which receptors, which nervous system) and why the colon's effect does not fade with tolerance the way pain relief does.

Part E · Apply It: Build a Mechanism-to-Message

A client on metformin for type 2 diabetes tells you they get diarrhea and nausea and asks why. Do not advise anything about the drug. Instead, build a short, accurate, in-scope explanation.

  1. What is unusual about metformin compared with the other classes (where does the drug concentrate, and how might the benefit and the side effect share the same door)?
  2. Why might two people on the same dose respond very differently? Name the mechanism.
  3. Write the two or three sentences you would actually say to the client: accurate on mechanism, reassuring where honest, and explicit that any change to dose, timing, or formulation is a conversation for their prescriber.

Coaching boundary
This worksheet builds pharmacological literacy, not a license to manage medication. Coaches explain how a drug reshapes digestion and support general patterns within an educational frame. They never advise a client to stop, change, skip, or start a prescribed medication, and they refer the medication decision to the prescriber or pharmacist and any alarm symptoms (black or bloody stool, coffee-ground vomit, severe pain, unexplained weight loss, severe or bloody diarrhea on antibiotics) to urgent medical care.
