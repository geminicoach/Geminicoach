Part C · Reason About the Mechanisms

  1. Someone says, "I eat plenty of iron, so my iron must be fine." Explain why intake and uptake are two different questions, and name one mechanism that can drain a specific nutrient to empty even on a textbook-perfect diet.
  2. The fat-soluble vitamins have "no independent doorway." Explain what that means mechanistically (lipase, bile, micelles, chylomicrons, lymph), and why a genuinely fat-free salad is a worse delivery system for its A-D-E-K than the same salad with olive oil.
  3. B12 deficiency can arise from a stomach problem, a pancreatic problem, or an ileal problem. List the four separate dependencies B12 absorption stacks, and explain why a failure in any one of three different organs produces the same single deficiency.
  4. Vitamin D "breaks the chapter's rules" in two ways. Name both (its largest source, and what it governs downstream), and explain why a person with a flawless gut can still run low.

Part E · Apply It: Walk the Chain

Consider a vegetarian who logs high dietary iron but reports fatigue, and a routine blood draw flagged low iron. They drink strong tea with meals and have taken a nightly acid reducer for a year. Do not diagnose. Walk the absorption chain out loud.

  1. Form: what type of iron makes up almost all of their intake, and why does that form behave like a different nutrient?

  3. Upstream digestion: explain how a year of acid suppression plausibly lowers iron uptake, and which second nutrient sharing that bottleneck you would also keep in mind.
  4. In-scope move: write one or two sentences of educational meal-composition guidance (form and pairing), and state plainly the one thing in this picture that belongs to a qualified clinician, not to general education.

Where education ends
This worksheet builds absorption literacy, not a license to diagnose or treat, in yourself or anyone else. A suspected deficiency needs laboratory confirmation and a clinician's care. General education explains how absorption works and reasons about meal composition (the form and pairing of foods). Interpreting lab results, deciding a number is "too low," or prescribing supplements as treatment require the relevant license, whatever your role. Neurological symptoms, steatorrhea with weight loss, blood, or any flagged lab are signs that warrant medical evaluation by a qualified clinician.
