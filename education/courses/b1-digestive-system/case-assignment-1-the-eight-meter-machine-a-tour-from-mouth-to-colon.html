Chapter 1: Apply It to a Client

Take the tour of the gut and use it to explain a real complaint, in plain language, within your scope.

Name: ____________________________ Date: __________ Est. time: 20 min

The Scenario

Client · Jordan, 41
Jordan tells you that after large dinners, especially heavy, greasy ones, food "sits like a rock" for a couple of hours and they feel uncomfortably full and sluggish. It settles on its own. There is no pain, no vomiting, no weight loss, and no trouble swallowing. Jordan asks: "Is something wrong with my stomach? Should I be worried?"

Your Tasks

  1. Explain, mechanically, what is happening to that meal using the stations and processes from the chapter (focus on the stomach's job and gastric emptying).
  2. Account for why a heavy, greasy meal in particular would feel like it sits longer than a light one.
  3. Decide whether this reads as normal physiology or something to worry about, and say how you know.
  4. Give Jordan one practical, in-scope suggestion, and state clearly where your lane ends and a referral begins.

Your Response

0 words
Coaching boundary
You are demonstrating understanding and client communication, not diagnosing. A strong answer explains the normal mechanism, stays practical, and names the red flags that would change the answer and require a provider.
Instructor / self-check rubric
  • Identifies the stomach's hold-and-meter role and gradual gastric emptying.
  • Explains that fat slows gastric emptying (duodenal feedback), so a fatty meal lingers.
  • Frames the heavy feeling as normal physiology, not a disorder.
  • Offers a practical, in-scope suggestion (e.g., smaller or less rushed meals if comfort is the goal).
  • States the referral boundary and the red flags that would change the call.
