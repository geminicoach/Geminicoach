Case Assignment · B1 Digestive System

Chapter 5: Apply It to a Real Situation

Take a real digestive complaint, read it backwards to its mechanism, and answer in plain language, within your scope.

Name: ____________________________ Date: __________ Est. time: 25 min

The Scenario

The person · Carla, 34
Consider Carla, who started a new plant-forward eating plan three weeks ago: a lot more beans, lentils, vegetables, and whole grains than she used to eat. Since then she has been bloated by the afternoon and passing gas far more than before, maybe fifteen or so times a day. She feels well otherwise. There is no blood, no weight loss, no trouble swallowing, no vomiting, no nighttime symptoms, and her bowel pattern is normal and unchanged. She is alarmed anyway and asks: "Is my gut broken? Should I just cut out all the gassy foods?"

Your Tasks

  1. Explain, mechanically, why the new diet is producing more gas and afternoon bloating, using the chapter's framing (fermentation, gas transport and motility, and visceral sensitivity) rather than a raw "too much gas" story.
  2. Address Carla's number directly: is roughly fifteen times a day outside the normal range, and what does the measured evidence say about gas being a sign of a working system versus a broken one?
  3. Decide whether this reads as a transient, explainable signal or something to worry about, and say how you know. Then evaluate her plan to "cut out all the gassy foods" and give a better in-scope alternative.
  4. State clearly where your lane ends: name the specific alarm features that, if they appeared, would change your answer and require a referral, and to whom.

Your Response

0 words
Where education ends
You are demonstrating understanding and plain-language communication, not diagnosing. A strong answer reads the signal backwards to its mechanism, normalizes what is normal, stays practical and in-scope, and names the alarm features (blood, unexplained weight loss, anemia, trouble swallowing, persistent vomiting, nighttime symptoms, a sustained change in bowel habit, new symptoms after 50) that would change the answer and warrant evaluation by a qualified clinician.
Instructor / self-check rubric
  • Reframes bloating as three legs (fermentation load, gas transport and motility, visceral sensitivity), not raw gas volume.
  • Explains that a new high-fiber, high-legume diet predictably raises fermentation gas, and that this is the microbiome working, not a broken gut.
  • Places roughly fifteen times a day inside the normal range and uses the measured evidence (gas as a readout of a functioning fermentation system).
  • Explains why blanket elimination is incomplete or counterproductive and offers an in-scope alternative (gradual fiber progression, pacing, observation over time).
  • Uses the transient-versus-pattern distinction to frame this as an explainable signal, not pathology.
  • Names the alarm features and the referral boundary that would change the call.
