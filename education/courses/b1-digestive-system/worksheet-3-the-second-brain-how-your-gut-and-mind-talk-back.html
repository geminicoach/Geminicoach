Part C · Fix the Serotonin Myth

A client repeats the line: "Most of your serotonin is made in your gut, so a healthy gut makes you happy." Use the chapter's two-pool reasoning.

  1. Which half of that sentence is true, and how large is the gut's share of the body's serotonin?
  2. Why is the conclusion ("makes you happy") mechanistically wrong? Name the barrier and the two-pool idea in your answer.
  3. If gut serotonin stays in the gut, how can it influence mood at all? Describe the actual signaling route.
  4. Trace the full circuit from the previous chapter into this one: fiber to microbes to short-chain fatty acids to serotonin to the brain. Name what acts at each step.

Part D · Reason About Stress and Digestion

Use the two stress arms (the fast SAM axis and the slower HPA axis) and the rest-and-digest balance.

  1. Explain why someone can feel a heavy, undigested fullness up top and an urgent need to empty their bowels down below at the same moment. Why is that split not a contradiction?
  2. Distinguish the SAM axis from the HPA axis by speed and by what each releases. Why does acute stress resolve while chronic stress reshapes gut function?
  3. A client says "calm down" is useless advice for a digestive problem. Using vagal tone and autonomic balance, explain why "calm down" is actually a mechanical lever on digestion.

Part E · Apply It: Read Renata's Pattern

Renata's appetite drops on Sunday nights, her cramping peaks on her heaviest workdays, and everything eases by the second day of any real vacation. Reason mechanistically, do not diagnose.

  1. Map each part of her pattern (Sunday-night drop, peak on hard days, relief on vacation) to a mechanism from the chapter.
  2. Why might her relief lag a full day behind logging off rather than arriving the instant she stops working? Tie this to the difference in timescale between the two stress axes.
  3. Renata bought a probiotic online "for her stress gut." Write what you can say honestly about the microbiome and mood, and what you should not claim about her specific product.

Coaching boundary
This worksheet builds gut-brain literacy, not a license to diagnose or treat. Coaches educate about the mechanism and support ordinary healthy habits (sleep, movement, regular meals, slow breathing) as general wellbeing. They do not diagnose IBS, do not treat anxiety or depression, and never use "it's just stress" to wave symptoms away, because stress aggravates serious conditions too. Refer the diagnosis and treatment of both gut and mental-health conditions to qualified providers, and route any sign of self-harm to emergency help immediately.
