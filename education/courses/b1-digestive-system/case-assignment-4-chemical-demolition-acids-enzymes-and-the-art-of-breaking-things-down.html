Case Assignment · B1 Digestive System

Chapter 4: Apply the Mechanism

Use the mechanism of acids and enzymes to untangle a contradictory pattern of self-treatment, in plain language, within your scope.

Name: ____________________________ Date: __________ Est. time: 25 min

The Scenario

Person · Marcus, 48
Consider Marcus, who is 48 and frustrated. For nearly a year he has had a slow, heavy fullness after bigger meals and a sour burn that climbs toward his throat when he lies down at night, worst after large, greasy dinners. He has been managing it himself: an over-the-counter acid reducer most evenings (because one podcast said his acid was "too high"), a betaine HCl supplement before lunch (because another account said it was "too low"), and a drawer of enzyme capsules he takes inconsistently. He has also started avoiding fat. Nothing has resolved. The question he keeps circling: "Which one of these is actually right? Is my acid high, or low? And should I just take all of them to cover my bases?"

Your Tasks

  1. Explain, mechanically, why Marcus's symptom pattern fits a reflux containment story (acid in the wrong room) better than a "too much acid" story, and why lying down after a heavy fatty meal predictably makes it worse.
  2. Address his core confusion: take apart the three real jobs of stomach acid and explain why the popular "acid is the enemy" framing is backwards, then explain why high-acid and low-acid complaints feel the same and cannot be sorted from symptoms alone.
  3. Evaluate his drawer: explain why an acid reducer, a betaine HCl supplement, and uncoated enzyme capsules pull in incompatible directions, so "all of them, just in case" guarantees at least one is working against him.
  4. Decide and communicate where general education ends and clinical care begins: lay out the in-scope education and any harmless self-care suggestion, then state clearly what stays off the table (recommending, starting, or stopping acid or enzyme products; diagnosing) and what signs would warrant a clinician.

Your Response

Where education ends
You are demonstrating understanding and clear communication, not diagnosing, in Marcus or in yourself. A strong answer explains the normal mechanism, stays practical, and names the signs that warrant medical evaluation. Do not recommend, start, or stop acid-suppressing or enzyme products; a year of unresolved reflux, suspected ulcers, or a possible pancreatic-insufficiency pattern belongs with a qualified clinician who can test rather than guess. Work within your role, whatever it is.
Instructor / self-check rubric
  • Explains reflux as a containment problem (acid in the wrong room, leaking valve and pressure), not overproduction, and why lying down after a fatty meal worsens it.
  • States the three real jobs of acid (denature protein, activate pepsin, defend against microbes) and why "acid is the enemy" is backwards.
  • Notes that low-acid and high-acid symptoms overlap, so neither can be self-diagnosed from symptoms alone.
  • Identifies that the acid reducer, betaine HCl, and uncoated enzymes contradict one another, so at least one is working against him.
  • Offers in-scope, mechanism-based education plus a harmless lifestyle suggestion (e.g., not lying down soon after eating, expecting a fatty late dinner to be a trigger).
  • States the referral boundary: does not recommend, start, or stop acid or enzyme products; refers a year of unresolved symptoms and names red flags (nightly reflux, painful swallowing, weight loss, vomiting, black stools, greasy floating stools).
