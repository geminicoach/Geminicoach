Case Assignment · B1 Digestive System

Chapter 3: Apply It to a Real Case

Use the gut-brain axis to explain a real complaint honestly, correct a popular myth, and hold the line between education and clinical care on both the gut and the mind.

Name: ____________________________ Date: __________ Est. time: 25 min

The Scenario

Case · Wesley, 34
Consider Wesley, a 34-year-old emergency dispatcher on a rotating schedule, who says his gut "has a calendar." Before his stretch of night shifts his appetite disappears and his upper belly feels tight and heavy, while his bowel habits turn urgent and irregular on his busiest shifts. Everything settles a day or two into his rest week. He has read online that "stress causes IBS" and that "your gut makes most of your serotonin," and he has started a probiotic he bought online "to fix his stress gut." He has not seen a clinician about any of this. Almost in passing, he mentions that he has felt persistently anxious and a bit numb for the last few months, which he chalks up entirely to the job. He asks: "So do I have IBS, and will the probiotic fix me?"

Your Tasks

  1. Explain, mechanically, what is happening to Wesley's digestion across a hard week, using the gut-brain axis from the chapter (the two stress axes, vagal tone, blood flow, and the split between slowed-above and hurried-below motility).
  2. Correct the "gut serotonin makes you happy" claim accurately, without discarding the real science. Use the blood-brain barrier and the two-pool idea, and say what gut serotonin actually does.
  3. Answer his direct question ("do I have IBS, and will the probiotic fix me?") in a way that is honest, useful, and stays on the education side of the line. Be explicit about what can responsibly be said and what cannot.
  4. Address the disclosure he dropped at the end (persistent anxiety and low mood). Decide how it should be handled, and state clearly where general education ends and a qualified clinician's role begins, for both the gut and the mind.

Your Response

0 words
Where education ends
You are demonstrating understanding and clear communication, not diagnosing or treating. A strong answer explains the real mechanism, corrects the myth without overclaiming, refuses to confirm a self-applied IBS label or present the probiotic as a cure, and takes the mood disclosure seriously: when a situation calls for diagnosis or treatment, that belongs to a qualified clinician, and concerns about mood, anxiety, or mental health warrant a qualified mental-health professional. Never use "it's just stress" to wave symptoms away, because stress aggravates serious conditions too.
Instructor / self-check rubric
  • Explains the stress-on-digestion mechanism: SAM and HPA axes, falling vagal tone, blood flow redirected, and motility split (slowed above, hurried below).
  • Corrects the serotonin myth using the blood-brain barrier and two-pool reasoning, and notes gut serotonin signals up the vagus rather than being delivered to the brain.
  • Frames the pattern as the normal gut-brain axis under stress, not a confirmed diagnosis.
  • Gives honest, in-scope habit support (sleep, movement, regular meals, slow breathing) without overclaiming the probiotic or a "gut reset."
  • Declines to confirm IBS and points the gut diagnosis to a qualified clinician.
  • Takes the persistent low mood and anxiety seriously and points it to a qualified mental-health professional; rejects "it's just stress" as a reason to stop there.
