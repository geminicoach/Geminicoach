Chapter 8: Apply the Absorption Map

Take the absorption map and use it to explain a suspected deficiency, in plain language, staying on the education side of the line.

Name: ____________________________ Date: __________ Est. time: 25 min

The Scenario

Case · Hassan, 38
Consider Hassan, a recreational cyclist who went fully plant-based about eighteen months ago and tracks his food carefully. By his logs he eats more iron now than he ever did as an omnivore: lentils, black beans, tofu, oats, whole-grain bread, and big spinach salads. He drinks two or three strong coffees a day, usually with meals, and rarely adds anything acidic like citrus to his plates. For the past year he has taken a daily over-the-counter acid reducer for reflux. Lately he is wiped out on rides that used to feel easy, and a routine physical came back with a note flagging low iron. He says: "I don't get it. I eat a ton of iron. Is plant iron just useless? Should I just take an iron pill?"

Your Tasks

  1. Explain, mechanically, why Hassan can have high iron intake and still come up low, using the chapter's intake-versus-uptake distinction and the form of iron he is eating.
  2. Account for the meal chemistry: name the inhibitors in his routine and the enhancer he is likely missing, and explain how each one acts on non-heme iron in the duodenum.
  3. Bring in upstream digestion: explain how a year of acid suppression plausibly contributes, and name the second nutrient that shares that same bottleneck and why you would keep it in mind.
  4. Decide what is in your lane and what is not. Give Hassan one or two practical, in-scope meal-composition suggestions, correct his "plant iron is useless" conclusion, and state clearly where your job ends and a provider's begins.

Your Response

Where education ends
You are demonstrating understanding and plain-language communication, not diagnosing. A suspected deficiency needs lab confirmation and a clinician's care. A strong answer explains the absorption mechanisms, reasons about meal composition, names what would change the answer, and refers the flagged lab and the medication question to a qualified clinician. Interpreting the lab, diagnosing, advising stopping medication, or prescribing a supplement dose as treatment require the relevant license, whatever your role.
Instructor / self-check rubric
  • Distinguishes intake from uptake; frames "low iron despite high intake" as predictable, not a paradox.
  • Identifies that nearly all his iron is non-heme (form-sensitive), and why that matters.
  • Names the inhibitors (coffee polyphenols, whole-grain phytates) and the missing vitamin C enhancer, with the mechanism for each.
  • Explains acid suppression as a duodenal iron bottleneck, and flags B12 as the co-affected nutrient (acid liberates B12 and triggers intrinsic factor; B12 has a single ileal address with no backup).
  • Offers practical, in-scope meal-composition guidance without moralizing food, and corrects the "plant iron is useless" belief.
  • States the referral boundary: confirm the lab, weigh the medication, and decide on treatment with a qualified clinician; the educational task is to illuminate the chain and refer.
