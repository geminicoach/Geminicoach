Where the Goods Get Absorbed: Nutrients, Bottlenecks, and Deficiency

Digestion only matters if nutrients actually cross into the body. This is where the loop closes.

37 min read 12 cited sources

Imagine you eat a perfectly balanced meal. Steak, spinach, a baked potato, some olive oil, a glass of milk. Your stomach acidifies it, your pancreas floods it with enzymes, your bile emulsifies the fat. By every measure of the first seven chapters, digestion went exactly as designed. And yet, none of that meal is in your body yet. Not one molecule of iron, not one amino acid, not one drop of fat-soluble vitamin. It is all still in a tube that, topologically speaking, is the outside world threaded through your middle.

Digestion breaks food down. Absorption is the moment it crosses the border and becomes you. Those are two different events, and confusing them is the single most common mistake people make when they reason about nutrition. "I ate plenty of iron" is a statement about intake. Whether that iron is now in your bloodstream is a completely separate question, and the answer depends on geography, chemistry, and the health of a surface you will never see.

Keep that distinction in front of you, because it does a great deal of work. Almost every confusing thing about deficiency dissolves once you stop treating "I eat it" and "I absorb it" as the same sentence. A person can eat a textbook-perfect diet and still drain a specific nutrient reserve to empty, not because the food was wrong, but because something between the plate and the bloodstream quietly failed; and the reverse is true, since a person eating a fairly ordinary diet can stay perfectly replete because every link in their absorption chain is intact. The food on the fork is only the opening move. The rest of the game happens out of sight, in a sequence of chemical handoffs that has to go right in order, every time. This chapter is where the whole course pays out. By the end of it you will be able to point to the exact stretch of gut where iron enters, where B12 enters, where fat and its passenger vitamins enter, and to explain, mechanistically and without fear, how a failure in one place produces a deficiency that shows up somewhere else entirely. That is the difference between guessing about nutrition and reasoning about it.

The border is enormous, and it is not uniform

Let us start with the size of the problem the body has solved. The small intestine is roughly six meters long in a living adult, but its absorptive surface is not a smooth pipe. It is folded into circular ridges, those ridges are carpeted with finger-like villi, and each villus cell is itself fringed with thousands of microvilli forming the brush border. Stack those amplifications and the functional surface area reaches something on the order of a tennis court compressed into your abdomen (Kiela & Ghishan, 2016). The entire architecture exists for one purpose: to maximize the contact between dissolved nutrients and the transport machinery that pulls them across.

That tennis court is not one uniform surface doing one uniform job. Different stretches of the small intestine are specialized for different nutrients. Absorption has an address system. Iron is taken up in one neighborhood, vitamin B12 in another, bile salts get recycled in a third, and if you damage or bypass the wrong segment, you lose a specific nutrient while the others carry on fine.

The classic teaching divides the small intestine into three regions: the duodenum (the first short C-shaped segment), the jejunum (the long, busy middle), and the ileum (the final segment that hands off to the colon). That three-part map is still useful and we will use it throughout this chapter. But it is worth knowing that the real picture is more granular than the textbook diagram suggests.

How the crossing actually happens

Before we place nutrients on the map, it helps to understand the two fundamentally different ways anything gets across the gut wall, because the route a nutrient takes determines how vulnerable it is. The lining of the small intestine is a single layer of cells called enterocytes, and a nutrient can cross either through those cells (the transcellular route) or between them, slipping through the sealed gaps where neighboring cells meet (the paracellular route). Most of the nutrients you care about take the transcellular route, and that route almost always requires a dedicated piece of machinery: a transporter protein embedded in the brush-border membrane that recognizes a specific molecule and ushers it inside.

This is the detail that makes absorption selective rather than indiscriminate. The gut does not simply soak up whatever is dissolved in front of it. It pulls in glucose because there is a glucose transporter, particular amino acids because there are amino-acid carriers, iron because there is an iron transporter. No transporter, no crossing, no matter how much of the nutrient sits in the lumen. And here is the consequence that recurs all chapter: transporters are concentrated in specific regions, they can be saturated (overwhelmed when there is more substrate than carriers), and they can be lost when the cells that carry them are damaged. The selectivity that makes absorption elegant is the same selectivity that makes it fragile. A nutrient is only as available as its transporter, and the transporter only exists where the gut decided to put it. Water and some minerals can cross more passively, but the headline micronutrients of this chapter, the ones that go missing in malabsorption, are nearly all transporter-dependent, which is exactly why they are the ones that fail.

Five domains, not three

In 2024, Zwick and colleagues used single-cell transcriptomics to read the gene-expression programs of nutrient-absorbing enterocytes all the way down the mouse and human small intestine. What they found is that the absorptive lining is organized into at least five discrete metabolic domains, each switching on a different set of transport and metabolism genes, rather than the tidy three regions anatomy gives us (Zwick et al., 2024). The boundaries between these domains are sharp, not gradual, and the patterning is controlled by specific transcription factors such as Ppar-δ and Cdx1 that organize lipid handling in the distal gut. Strikingly, the domains are not fixed in stone: when the researchers changed the animals' diet, the domains adjusted their gene programs in response.

This matters because it tells you the absorptive surface is a responsive, regionally programmed organ, not a passive sponge. The body decides where and how to absorb, and that decision can shift with what you habitually eat. The three-region map is the floor of your understanding. The five-domain finding is the ceiling, and it confirms the deeper principle: location is a feature of absorption, not an accident.

The regional map: who gets absorbed where

Let us walk the tube from top to bottom and place each nutrient where it actually crosses. This is the spatial backbone of the whole chapter, so take your time with it.

Duodenum: iron, calcium, and the fast lane

The duodenum is short but it punches above its weight. It is the primary site for iron absorption and a major site for calcium and other minerals (Kiela & Ghishan, 2016). There is a chemical reason iron sits here at the top. Dietary non-heme iron is mostly in the oxidized ferric (Fe³⁺) form, which the gut cannot absorb well. It needs to be reduced to ferrous (Fe²⁺) iron, and that conversion is favored by the acidic environment arriving fresh from the stomach. The duodenum is the first stop after the stomach, so it catches iron while the contents are still relatively acidic and the iron is still soluble. The transporter that pulls ferrous iron into the enterocyte, DMT1, is concentrated right here.

This is also where you should first feel the entire course connecting. Remember stomach acid from Chapter 5. That acid is not just a digestive solvent; it is the chemistry that makes iron absorbable downstream. Lower the acid, and you blunt iron uptake before the duodenum ever gets a fair shot. Hold that thread.

Iron has one more wrinkle worth understanding in detail, because it is the nutrient whose absorption is most dramatically shaped by the rest of the meal. Dietary iron comes in two forms that behave almost like different nutrients. Heme iron, found in meat, poultry, and fish, is iron already wrapped inside the heme molecule from animal blood and muscle; it is absorbed relatively efficiently through its own pathway and is largely shielded from the meal around it. Non-heme iron, the form in plants, grains, legumes, eggs, and iron-fortified foods, is the troublesome one. It is far more sensitive to what else is on the plate, because it has to be kept soluble and in the right oxidation state to find its transporter. This is why two people eating the "same amount of iron" on paper can absorb wildly different quantities: the form matters, and the company the iron keeps matters even more.

The classic demonstration comes from the iron-bioavailability literature. According to PubMed, Lynch and Cook (1980) showed that the natural inhibitors of non-heme iron absorption, the phytates in bran and whole grains and the polyphenols (tannins) in tea and coffee, suppress iron uptake in a dose-dependent way, and that vitamin C (ascorbic acid) can largely reverse that suppression. A squeeze of citrus eaten with a plant-based iron source measurably improves how much of that iron crosses the duodenal wall, while a strong cup of tea with the same meal does the opposite. The point is purely mechanistic: vitamin C keeps non-heme iron in its soluble, absorbable state, while phytates and tannins bind it into forms the transporter cannot grab. Someone who pairs their beans with citrus rather than washing them down with tea is not following a superstition; they are working with the chemistry of the duodenum.

In practice, this means

If a person eating mostly plant-based iron is running low, the educational lever is not "eat more iron" in the abstract. It is the form and the pairing: combine non-heme iron sources with a vitamin C source, and be aware that tea or coffee taken with the same meal blunts uptake. This is food-as-chemistry, not food-as-morality, and it stays squarely within a coach's scope because it is about meal composition, not diagnosis or treatment.

Jejunum: the workhorse for the big three

The jejunum is the long middle stretch and it does the bulk of the heavy lifting for the macronutrients. Glucose and other monosaccharides, amino acids and small peptides, and the majority of water-soluble vitamins are absorbed across the jejunal brush border (Kiela & Ghishan, 2016; Said, 2011). Sugars come in through sodium-coupled transporters like SGLT1 (glucose and galactose) and facilitated transporters like GLUT5 (fructose). Amino acids ride a whole family of sodium-dependent and independent carriers. Most water-soluble vitamins, B1, B2, B3, B5, B6, B7, B9 (folate), and vitamin C, have dedicated carrier-mediated systems concentrated in the proximal small intestine (Said, 2011).

Folate deserves a specific note because it makes the "address" principle concrete. Folate has a dedicated carrier, the proton-coupled folate transporter, expressed in the proximal small intestine. So folate gets absorbed early and high. Its chemical cousin in the B-family, vitamin B12, does the opposite, as you will see in a moment. Same vitamin family, completely different addresses.

It is worth pausing on why the jejunum can do so much. It is the longest stretch, the bulk of those six meters, and it is where the villi are tallest and most densely packed, so the absorptive amplification is highest right here. That combination, length plus maximal surface, is why the jejunum is the body's default workhorse and why a healthy person extracts the overwhelming majority of their calories and water-soluble micronutrients before the contents ever reach the ileum. It also explains a pattern you will see repeatedly: damage that targets the proximal small intestine, like celiac disease, is so destructive precisely because it strikes the busiest, most amplified region. Lose the jejunum's surface and you lose the headquarters of absorption.

Ileum: B12, bile salts, and the recycling depot

The terminal ileum is the most specialized address in the entire tract, and it is where two irreplaceable jobs happen. First, vitamin B12 is absorbed almost exclusively here, and only here, and only when it is escorted by a protein called intrinsic factor (Said, 2011). Intrinsic factor is made by the same stomach cells that make acid. B12 binds intrinsic factor, the complex travels all the way down to the terminal ileum, and a specific receptor there pulls it in. There is no backup site. If you remove or damage the terminal ileum, or if you cannot make intrinsic factor, B12 absorption collapses regardless of how much B12 you eat.

The B12 story is worth telling slowly, because it is the single best illustration of how many upstream steps a nutrient can depend on. B12 in food arrives bound to animal protein; stomach acid and pepsin first liberate it, it binds a transport protein, gets handed off to intrinsic factor (made back in the stomach), travels the whole small intestine as that complex, and only at the very end, in the terminal ileum, meets the one receptor that recognizes it. Count the dependencies: adequate stomach acid, cells able to make intrinsic factor, an intact pancreas to release the bound vitamin, and a healthy terminal ileum with working receptors. Four separate systems, in four locations, and if any one fails, B12 absorption fails, no matter how much B12 the person eats. There is no other doorway, which is why B12 deficiency can arise from a stomach, pancreatic, or ileal problem alike, three different organs producing the same single deficiency.

Second, the ileum reclaims bile salts. Recall from Chapter 4 that bile emulsifies fat. The body does not make bile fresh for every meal and discard it; it recycles it through the enterohepatic circulation, and the reclamation point is the terminal ileum. Lose that segment and you lose bile salts into the colon, which impairs fat and fat-soluble vitamin handling on every subsequent meal. This is the doubly painful thing about damage to the terminal ileum: it takes out B12 and the fat-recycling system in one stroke, so the consequences land on B12 status and on the entire fat-soluble vitamin family at the same time. One small segment, two irreplaceable jobs, and no backup for either.

Where fat actually goes in

Fat absorption is its own story because it does not work like sugar or amino acids, and it depends entirely on the chemistry you learned in Chapter 4. Dietary triglycerides are hydrolyzed in the lumen by pancreatic lipase into free fatty acids and monoglycerides. Those products are water-repelling, so they cannot just float to the brush border. Bile salts package them into micelles, tiny carrier droplets that ferry the fat products across the watery layer to the enterocyte surface (Iqbal & Hussain, 2009). Inside the enterocyte, the fats are reassembled into triglycerides, packaged into large lipoprotein particles called chylomicrons, and exported, not into the blood directly, but into the lymphatic system, which eventually drains into the bloodstream.

The fat-soluble vitamins, A, D, E, and K, hitch a ride on this exact machinery. They dissolve into the micelles alongside dietary fat and follow it across. Cholesterol absorption uses a dedicated transporter, NPC1L1, with ABCG5 and ABCG8 pumping excess sterol back out (Iqbal & Hussain, 2009). The practical consequence is enormous: anything that breaks the fat pathway, no lipase, no bile, damaged lining, automatically breaks fat-soluble vitamin absorption too. They are chemically chained together.

This is the single most underappreciated fact in everyday nutrition, so it is worth stating bluntly: the fat-soluble vitamins are not absorbed by themselves. They have no independent doorway, no dedicated transporter that works in water like glucose has. They are passengers on dietary fat, and they can only board the micelle if there is fat in the meal to build the micelle. A meal genuinely devoid of fat is, for these four vitamins, a meal with the delivery truck missing. The carotenoids in carrots, the vitamin K in leafy greens, the vitamin E in seeds, all of them depend on co-ingested fat to cross. This is plumbing dressed up as nutrition advice, and it is one of the few absorption facts that translates directly into a concrete, harmless, within-scope suggestion: eat your fat-soluble nutrients with some fat, on purpose.

The export route matters too. Unlike glucose and amino acids, which drain straight into the portal vein toward the liver, the chylomicrons carrying fat and the fat-soluble vitamins are too large for that route; they enter the lymphatic vessels first and only later join the bloodstream. So even conditions that disrupt intestinal lymph flow, not just the lumen, can impair fat-soluble vitamin status. It is one more reason this vitamin family has so many ways to fail: more steps in the journey, more places a problem can hide.

The colon: water and the microbiome's gift

By the time contents reach the colon, most of the calories and micronutrients are gone. The colon's headline job is reclaiming water and electrolytes, turning liquid chyme into formed stool. But Chapter 2 promised you the colon does something more interesting, and here is where it pays off. The colonic microbiota ferment the carbohydrates and proteins that escaped small-intestinal absorption, producing short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), chiefly acetate, propionate, and butyrate (Ríos-Covián et al., 2016). These SCFAs are then absorbed across the colonic wall. Butyrate is the preferred fuel of the colonocytes themselves; propionate and acetate travel to the liver and beyond, participating in host signaling and metabolism.

So the colon is not merely a drying rack. It is a second, microbe-driven absorption site that extracts energy and signaling molecules from material your own enzymes could never touch. That is the entire reason fiber matters: you do not digest it, your bacteria do, and you absorb the products.

Coaching translation

Here is the whole map in plain language. Iron and calcium: top of the tube, acid-dependent. Sugars, amino acids, most B-vitamins and vitamin C: the long middle. Fat and the A-D-E-K vitamins: need bile and lipase, ride micelles, export through lymph. B12: bottom of the tube only, and only with intrinsic factor from the stomach. Water and short-chain fatty acids: the colon, with help from your microbes. In practice, this means a problem in one location knocks out one specific set of nutrients, not all of them. That is why deficiencies are usually selective, not global.

The trace minerals, and why they fight each other

The macronutrient map is the skeleton, but the minerals deserve their own discussion, because they introduce a complication you have not met yet: nutrients can compete with one another for the same crossing. Iron, zinc, calcium, magnesium, and copper are absorbed largely in the upper small intestine, and several share transport machinery or chemical behavior, so a large dose of one can blunt the absorption of another. This is not a defect; it is what happens when a finite number of carriers serves a crowd of similar passengers.

Zinc: small, essential, and easily crowded out

Zinc is required for hundreds of enzymes and for immune function, wound healing, and taste, yet the body holds no large dedicated store of it, which makes a steady supply of absorbed zinc surprisingly important. According to PubMed, Maares and Haase (2020) describe how zinc crosses the intestinal wall mainly through a dedicated importer called ZIP4, and, tellingly, how that transporter is itself regulated by zinc status: when the body is short on zinc, ZIP4 accumulates at the cell surface and uptake rises; when zinc is plentiful, the transporter is pulled back inside and degraded. This is the body actively tuning its own absorption up and down depending on need, the same responsiveness Zwick and colleagues found at the level of whole regions, now operating at the level of a single transporter.

Zinc also illustrates competition cleanly. It shares chemical territory with iron and copper, and the same phytates that bind non-heme iron also bind zinc, lowering its bioavailability from heavily plant-based meals built mostly on unleavened grains and legumes without much variety. The educational takeaway is again about composition, not correction: variety and food preparation methods that reduce phytate (soaking, sprouting, leavening, fermentation) genuinely improve mineral availability, which is exactly why traditional food cultures developed those techniques long before anyone could name a transporter.

Magnesium: two doors, dose-dependent

Magnesium is absorbed by two routes at once, and the split explains why magnesium status is so variable. According to PubMed, Schuchardt and Hahn (2017) describe a saturable, active transcellular pathway (through channel proteins in the TRPM family) that does most of the work when intake is modest, plus a passive paracellular pathway that becomes proportionally more important when intake is high. The practical consequence is that the fraction of magnesium you absorb falls as the dose rises: the body extracts a high percentage from small amounts and a smaller percentage from large ones, because the efficient active door saturates and the leftover relies on the leakier passive route. This is the saturation principle made visible, and the reason that, for minerals, spreading intake across the day and across whole foods tends to deliver more absorbed mineral than a single large dose.

Calcium: acid-sensitive and route-flexible

Calcium, like magnesium, uses both an active transcellular route and a passive paracellular one, and the active route is the one vitamin D controls, the first hint of a theme we are about to develop: a vitamin governing the absorption of a mineral. Calcium also needs to be soluble, so it shares iron's sensitivity to stomach acid and the competitive dynamic too, since very large calcium loads can interfere with iron and magnesium taken at the same time. The lesson is not to fear any of these minerals but to recognize that they are absorbed in a crowded neighborhood, where timing, dose, and meal composition shape how much actually crosses, and that "I took a supplement" is, once more, a statement about intake and not about uptake.

What blocks absorption: five bottlenecks

Now we flip the chapter. You know where nutrients cross. The next question is what stops them. Malabsorption is usually sorted into three categories: problems with the gut lining itself (mucosal disease), problems in the lumen before nutrients reach the lining (luminal defects), and problems with the transport machinery (transport dysfunction) (Zuvarox & Belletieri, 2023). We will walk through five concrete bottlenecks that cover most of what you will encounter in real life.

1. Low stomach acid

Stomach acid does three jobs that matter for absorption downstream. It keeps iron in its soluble, absorbable ferrous-favoring state, it helps liberate B12 and protein-bound nutrients from food, and it triggers intrinsic factor production. When acid is chronically low, whether from aging, certain medications, or disease, iron and B12 absorption both suffer (Zuvarox & Belletieri, 2023). This is the cleanest example of why digestion gates absorption: the acid never crosses the wall itself, but without it, two specific nutrients downstream go uncollected.

People get this wrong constantly. They assume a stomach problem causes a stomach-located deficiency. It does not. The acid problem at the top causes an iron problem in the duodenum and a B12 problem all the way down in the ileum. Cause and effect live in different ZIP codes.

2. Damaged or flattened villi

If you reduce the absorptive surface area, you reduce absorption across the board, with the steepest losses for whatever is absorbed in the damaged region. Celiac disease is the textbook case. In celiac disease, an immune response to gluten flattens the villi of the proximal small intestine, the very region responsible for iron, folate, calcium, and much of the macronutrient and water-soluble-vitamin uptake (Kreutz et al., 2020). The surface area collapses, and so does absorption.

Remember the prediction I asked you to hold earlier? This is the test. Kreutz and colleagues, reviewing nutrient status in celiac patients, found that deficiencies in iron, folate, vitamin B12, vitamin D, zinc, and others are common at diagnosis, driven by both reduced intake and diminished uptake through atrophied villi (Kreutz et al., 2020). And the sobering part for coaches: even after starting a gluten-free diet, full histological recovery of the lining takes a long time, so patients can remain vulnerable to deficiency well after they have removed the trigger. Removing the cause does not instantly restore the surface.

3. Rapid transit

Absorption takes contact time. Nutrients must dwell against the brush border long enough for transporters to do their work. When transit speeds up, as in chronic diarrhea or some post-surgical states, the contents rush past before slow or saturable transport systems can finish (Zuvarox & Belletieri, 2023). The transporters need contact time, and rapid transit does not give it to them. Fat and fat-soluble vitamins are especially vulnerable because their absorption is a multi-step process that needs time to assemble micelles and shuttle products across.

4. Missing lipase

Pancreatic lipase is the enzyme that hydrolyzes dietary fat into the products that can be absorbed. Without it, as in pancreatic exocrine insufficiency, triglycerides arrive at the brush border still intact and unabsorbable. The fat passes through to the stool (causing steatorrhea, fatty stool), and the fat-soluble vitamins riding along with it go too (Zuvarox & Belletieri, 2023; Iqbal & Hussain, 2009). This is the chemistry chain from Chapter 4 breaking at its first link.

5. No bile

Even with perfect lipase, fat products cannot cross the watery layer to the enterocyte without bile salts to package them into micelles. Obstructed bile flow, or loss of bile-salt recycling at a damaged ileum, strands the fat at the doorstep (Iqbal & Hussain, 2009). The result mirrors the missing-lipase scenario: fat and the A-D-E-K vitamins fail to absorb. Two different mechanisms, low lipase and low bile, converge on the same downstream deficiency, because they break the same shared pathway at different points.

This is why it matters

Notice the pattern. Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency can come from at least four separate failures: rapid transit, missing lipase, no bile, or damaged lining. That is because absorption is a chain, and a chain breaks at its weakest link, not at its average strength. When you see a deficiency, the useful question is never just "did they eat enough?" It is "which link in the absorption chain might be broken?"

The Macronutrient Food-Source Reference

This is the course's second major reference tool, and it follows one firm rule: no food is moralized as good or bad, clean or dirty. We talk about smart sources and balance. The goal is to connect each macronutrient to reliable, practical foods so you can reason about sourcing the same way you now reason about absorption: mechanistically.

Protein

Protein supplies the amino acids absorbed across the jejunum. Smart, balanced sources span both animal and plant origins. Animal sources (eggs, dairy, poultry, fish, meat) provide all essential amino acids in one package. Plant sources (legumes, soy, lentils, beans, nuts, seeds, whole grains) are excellent too; eating a variety across the day covers the amino-acid spectrum without any single "complete" food being required. The practical move is variety and adequacy, not anxiety about any one source.

Carbohydrate

Carbohydrate is absorbed as monosaccharides in the jejunum, but the carbohydrate you do not absorb matters just as much. Fiber-rich sources (vegetables, fruit, whole grains, legumes) deliver the fermentable carbohydrate your colonic microbes turn into short-chain fatty acids (Ríos-Covián et al., 2016). So "smart carbohydrate sourcing" is really two goals at once: glucose for you, and fiber for your microbiome. A plate with starches plus plenty of plants serves both addresses, the jejunum and the colon.

Fat

Fat is absorbed via the micelle-and-chylomicron pathway, and it carries the fat-soluble vitamins with it. Smart sources include olive oil, avocado, nuts, seeds, fatty fish, and dairy fat. The absorption-relevant insight here is one most people never hear: because A, D, E, and K need fat to be absorbed, eating those vitamins with some dietary fat improves their uptake. The spinach-and-olive-oil pairing in this chapter's opening meal was not random. The fat is the vehicle that carries the fat-soluble vitamins across.

In practice, this means

A salad eaten completely fat-free is a worse delivery system for its fat-soluble vitamins than the same salad with a drizzle of olive oil or some avocado. This is not about calories or "healthy fats" as a moral category. It is plumbing: no fat in the meal, no micelles, weaker A-D-E-K absorption. Pair fat-soluble nutrients with a fat source on purpose.

Vitamin D: the nutrient that breaks the rules

Vitamin D deserves a section of its own because it quietly violates almost every assumption this chapter has built. We have treated absorption as the gateway to nutrient status: eat it, absorb it, become replete. Vitamin D scrambles that picture in two ways, and understanding why makes you far less likely to reason about it incorrectly.

The first surprise is that the largest source of vitamin D for most people is not food at all. According to PubMed, Holick (2007), in his New England Journal of Medicine review, describes how the skin manufactures vitamin D from cholesterol when exposed to ultraviolet B sunlight, and for people with adequate sun exposure this skin-synthesized vitamin D can supply the majority of their needs, which means dietary intake and intestinal absorption, the entire subject of this chapter, are only part of the story. A person can have a flawless gut and still run low on vitamin D simply because they live at a high latitude, work indoors, or have darker skin that produces it more slowly. Here, for once, intake and absorption are not the bottleneck. Sunlight is.

The second surprise is that vitamin D does not merely get absorbed; it then turns around and governs the absorption of another nutrient. Vitamin D is essential for the active, regulated transport of calcium across the intestinal wall, so a vitamin D shortfall does not just lower vitamin D status, it impairs calcium absorption downstream, which is why vitamin D deficiency shows up in the literature as a bone and muscle problem (Holick, 2007). This is a slightly mind-bending example of the chapter's deepest theme, that a problem in one place produces a deficiency in another. Here the "place" is not a segment of gut but a nutrient that controls a transporter: low vitamin D becomes low absorbed calcium becomes a skeletal consequence, a chain running through three different levels of the body.

For the part of vitamin D that does come from food (and supplements), the ordinary rules reapply: it is fat-soluble, so it rides the micelle-and-chylomicron pathway like its A, E, and K siblings, and anything that breaks fat absorption will impair it too. So vitamin D sits at the intersection of two worlds: a sun-dependent source that this chapter cannot explain, and a dietary fraction that obeys every fat-absorption rule this chapter has taught. It is also one of the most commonly tested and supplemented nutrients, which makes it exactly the kind of thing where a coach can easily overstep. Explaining how vitamin D is made and how it works is education; looking at someone's level, deciding it is too low, and telling them what dose to take is interpreting a lab and prescribing a treatment, which belongs to a provider. The mechanism is yours to teach. The number on the lab report is not yours to act on.

Think About It

A person eats vitamin-D-rich food daily and absorbs fat normally, yet a routine blood test flags low vitamin D. Why does this not contradict anything in this chapter? Where, in the vitamin D story, is the likely bottleneck, and why is interpreting that lab result and deciding what to do about it a job for a qualified clinician rather than a coach?

Deficiency risk, without the fear

Now we can state the chapter's central reframing cleanly. Your nutrient status is determined by absorption capacity, not just intake. Eating a nutrient is necessary but not sufficient. The status you end up with is intake filtered through every bottleneck we just covered.

Some nutrients are simply more vulnerable than others, because their absorption depends on more moving parts or on a single irreplaceable address. Across the clinical literature, the micronutrients that most commonly run low in malabsorption are iron, vitamin B12, calcium, magnesium, folate, and the fat-soluble vitamins (StatPearls, 2025; Zuvarox & Belletieri, 2023). Look at that list and you can now explain every entry mechanistically:

  • Iron is fragile because it depends on stomach acid plus an intact duodenum. Two points of failure.
  • B12 is fragile because it needs acid, intrinsic factor, and an intact terminal ileum, with no alternate absorption site. Three points of failure and no backup.
  • Fat-soluble vitamins are fragile because they ride the fat pathway, which itself needs lipase, bile, intact lining, and adequate transit time. The most links, the most ways to break.
  • Folate is vulnerable specifically when proximal villi are damaged, which is why it appears so often in celiac disease (Kreutz et al., 2020).

What early signs actually look like

Deficiency is not a switch that flips; it is a slow draining of reserves, and the early signs are usually nonspecific. Iron and B12 deficiency both impair red-blood-cell production, producing fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance, and pallor before anything dramatic (StatPearls, 2025). B12 deficiency has a second danger: because B12 is essential to the nervous system, prolonged deficiency can cause neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling, balance and cognitive changes) that may not fully reverse, which is exactly why the terminal-ileum bottleneck deserves respect (Said, 2011). Folate deficiency also produces anemia. Vitamin D deficiency shows up in bone and muscle. The pattern across the water-soluble vitamins is that each one, when it runs low, produces a fairly specific clinical syndrome (Said, 2011).

The non-alarmist framing is this. Early signs are common, vague, and have many possible causes, so a symptom is a prompt to investigate, not a diagnosis. And the mechanistic lens is what keeps you calm and accurate: instead of fearing food or chasing supplements blindly, you ask where in the absorption chain a problem could plausibly sit, and whether intake, digestion, or uptake is the likely bottleneck.

Think About It

A person eats red meat regularly but has low iron, and also reports they have been on long-term acid-suppressing medication. Walk the chain: intake looks adequate, so where is the likely bottleneck, and which other nutrient sharing that bottleneck would you also want to consider? (Answer: low acid impairs iron solubility in the duodenum and B12 liberation and intrinsic-factor signaling. You would consider both iron and B12.)

The trained-individual footnote

For people training hard, the same mechanisms apply with two amplifications. Endurance athletes lose more iron and have higher turnover, so an absorption bottleneck that a sedentary person would tolerate can tip an athlete into deficiency faster. And very high energy intakes mean more food passing through, which usually helps total absorbed quantity, but rapid transit during heavy training or gut distress can work against it. The principles do not change. The margins get tighter.

Absorption is not a passive sponge. It is a regionally programmed, responsive organ that decides where and how each nutrient crosses, and a problem at one address produces a deficiency at another.

The organizing idea of this chapter

Closing the loop

Chapter 1 opened a physiological loop: food goes in one end, becomes part of you, and the remainder leaves the other. Every chapter since has filled in a stage. The mouth and stomach prepared and acidified. The pancreas and bile from Chapter 4 supplied the chemistry. Chapters 5 and 6 dealt with the lining and its disorders. This chapter is where it all cashes out, because absorption is the moment "food" finally becomes "you," and we can now see that the moment depends on everything upstream working in sequence.

You can now do four things you could not do before. You can locate where each major nutrient is absorbed. You can explain how upstream digestion and gut-lining health gate that absorption. You can use the Macronutrient Food-Source Reference to source smartly without moralizing food. And you can reason about deficiency risk mechanistically, by asking which link in the chain might be broken, rather than reaching for fear or for a supplement bottle by default. That is the whole loop, closed.

Where your job ends, stated plainly

Because this chapter touches deficiency, labs, and supplements, the boundary deserves to be stated as cleanly as the mechanisms. Everything you have learned here is educational. It is not medical advice, and it does not turn you into someone who can diagnose or treat a deficiency. That distinction is not a legal footnote; it is the practical core of doing this work responsibly.

A suspected deficiency needs laboratory confirmation and a provider's care. You do not diagnose iron deficiency, B12 deficiency, or any other shortfall from symptoms, because the early signs are vague and the underlying bottleneck, whether it is intake, digestion, or absorption, can only be sorted out with proper testing and clinical judgment. You do not interpret someone's lab results, deciding their number is "too low" and what it means. And you do not prescribe supplements as treatment: telling a person what nutrient to take, in what dose, to fix a deficiency is a clinical act, not a coaching one.

What you do is genuinely valuable and entirely within scope. You explain how absorption works so people understand their own bodies. You help with meal composition, the form and pairing of foods, that supports good absorption for anyone. You recognize the patterns, like Nadia's, that should prompt someone to seek qualified care, and you say so clearly and promptly. And you refer. Coaches educate and refer; they do not diagnose and treat. Holding that line is not a weakness in your usefulness; it is the very thing that makes your education trustworthy.

Case study: Nadia and the disappearing iron

Case

Nadia is 34, a committed distance runner who switched to a mostly plant-based diet two years ago. She has been diligent about it: lentils, beans, tofu, plenty of spinach and whole grains, and she is sure she eats more iron now than she did as an omnivore. She drinks strong black tea with most meals because she finds it settles her stomach, and she has been taking an over-the-counter acid reducer nightly for the past year for occasional reflux. Lately she is exhausted in a way her training does not explain, her usual paces feel impossible, and a routine blood draw came back flagging low iron. She is baffled, because by her own accounting her iron intake is high, and she has started wondering whether plant iron is simply "useless."

Work Nadia's situation through everything this chapter built and the puzzle dissolves into a sequence of plausible bottlenecks, none of which is "she does not eat enough iron." Start with the form. Almost all of Nadia's iron is now non-heme iron, the type most sensitive to the rest of the meal, because it has to be kept soluble and reduced to find its duodenal transporter. Then layer in the company she keeps it in. The polyphenols in her strong tea are among the classic dose-dependent inhibitors of non-heme iron absorption that Lynch and Cook (1980) documented, and her whole-grain and legume-heavy meals add phytates, the other classic inhibitor, while she may not be pairing that iron with a vitamin C source that could counteract them. So before any disease is on the table at all, the chemistry of her meals is plausibly suppressing how much of her abundant dietary iron actually crosses the wall. Her intake is genuinely high; her absorption may be quietly low. That is the intake-versus-uptake distinction made personal.

Then there is the acid reducer. Iron needs an acidic environment in the duodenum to stay absorbable, and chronic acid suppression is one of this chapter's named bottlenecks, so a year of nightly acid reduction is a reasonable contributor to poorer iron uptake, and also a setup for impaired B12 over time. The running matters too: endurance athletes have higher iron turnover and losses, so an absorption shortfall a sedentary person might tolerate can tip a runner into deficiency faster. Nadia has not one bottleneck but a stack of them, each modest on its own, converging on the same result.

This is the line that matters. It is entirely appropriate, and genuinely useful, for an educator to help Nadia see that her conclusion ("plant iron is useless") is mistaken, and to explain the mechanisms: form, inhibitors like tea and phytate, enhancers like vitamin C, meal timing, and the role of stomach acid. That is education about meal composition, squarely within scope. What is not within scope is telling her to stop her acid-reducing medication, interpreting her iron labs, or putting her on an iron supplement as treatment. Her flagged result and her medication both belong in a conversation with a qualified clinician, who can confirm the deficiency, weigh the medication, and decide on treatment. The coach's job is to illuminate the chain and route her to the right person, not to diagnose the broken link or prescribe the fix. Nadia's case is a near-perfect summary of the whole chapter, because it shows that "low iron despite high intake" is no paradox at all. It is the predictable output of intake filtered through form, meal chemistry, stomach acid, and the demands of her training. Once you can see the chain, the mystery becomes a map, and the map points clearly to where education ends and clinical care begins.

Key Takeaways

  • Digestion breaks food down; absorption is the separate event where nutrients cross the gut wall and become part of you. Intake does not guarantee uptake.
  • The small intestine is regionally specialized: iron and calcium in the duodenum; sugars, amino acids, and most water-soluble vitamins in the jejunum; B12 (with intrinsic factor) and bile-salt recycling only in the terminal ileum.
  • Fat and the fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) share one chemistry chain: lipase to hydrolyze, bile to form micelles, chylomicrons to export via lymph. Break any link and they all suffer.
  • The colon reclaims water and absorbs short-chain fatty acids produced when microbes ferment fiber, a second microbe-driven absorption site.
  • Five bottlenecks impair absorption: low stomach acid, flattened villi (celiac), rapid transit, missing lipase, and absent bile, each knocking out a specific, predictable set of nutrients.
  • The most deficiency-prone nutrients (iron, B12, fat-soluble vitamins, folate, calcium, magnesium) are vulnerable because they depend on the most steps or on a single irreplaceable address.
  • Form and meal company shape mineral uptake: non-heme iron and zinc are suppressed by tea polyphenols and grain phytates and helped by vitamin C, while minerals compete for shared pathways and saturable transporters favor smaller amounts spread across the day over single large doses.
  • Vitamin D breaks the chapter's rules: its largest source is sunlight on skin, not food, and it governs calcium absorption, so a vitamin D shortfall becomes a calcium and bone problem downstream.
  • Reason about deficiency by asking which link in the absorption chain is broken, not by fearing food. Status equals intake filtered through absorption capacity.
  • This is education, not medical advice: deficiencies need lab confirmation and a provider's care. Coaches explain mechanisms, help with meal composition, recognize patterns, and refer. They do not diagnose, interpret labs, or prescribe supplements as treatment.
