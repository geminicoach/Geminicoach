Where the Goods Get Absorbed: Nutrients, Bottlenecks, and Deficiency

Digestion only matters if nutrients actually cross into the body. This is where the loop closes.

60 min read 8 cited sources

Imagine you eat a perfectly balanced meal. Steak, spinach, a baked potato, some olive oil, a glass of milk. Your stomach acidifies it, your pancreas floods it with enzymes, your bile emulsifies the fat. By every measure of the first seven chapters, digestion went exactly as designed. And yet, none of that meal is in your body yet. Not one molecule of iron, not one amino acid, not one drop of fat-soluble vitamin. It is all still in a tube that, topologically speaking, is the outside world threaded through your middle.

Digestion breaks food down. Absorption is the moment it crosses the border and becomes you. Those are two different events, and confusing them is the single most common mistake people make when they reason about nutrition. "I ate plenty of iron" is a statement about intake. Whether that iron is now in your bloodstream is a completely separate question, and the answer depends on geography, chemistry, and the health of a surface you will never see.

The border is enormous, and it is not uniform

Let us start with the size of the problem the body has solved. The small intestine is roughly six meters long in a living adult, but its absorptive surface is not a smooth pipe. It is folded into circular ridges, those ridges are carpeted with finger-like villi, and each villus cell is itself fringed with thousands of microvilli forming the brush border. Stack those amplifications and the functional surface area reaches something on the order of a tennis court compressed into your abdomen (Kiela & Ghishan, 2016). The entire architecture exists for one purpose: to maximize the contact between dissolved nutrients and the transport machinery that pulls them across.

Here is the part most people miss. That tennis court is not one uniform surface doing one uniform job. Different stretches of the small intestine are specialized for different nutrients. Absorption has an address system. Iron is taken up in one neighborhood, vitamin B12 in another, bile salts get recycled in a third, and if you damage or bypass the wrong segment, you lose a specific nutrient while the others carry on fine.

The classic teaching divides the small intestine into three regions: the duodenum (the first short C-shaped segment), the jejunum (the long, busy middle), and the ileum (the final segment that hands off to the colon). That three-part map is still useful and we will use it throughout this chapter. But it is worth knowing that the real picture is more granular than the textbook diagram suggests.

Five domains, not three

In 2024, Zwick and colleagues used single-cell transcriptomics to read the gene-expression programs of nutrient-absorbing enterocytes all the way down the mouse and human small intestine. What they found is that the absorptive lining is organized into at least five discrete metabolic domains, each switching on a different set of transport and metabolism genes, rather than the tidy three regions anatomy gives us (Zwick et al., 2024). The boundaries between these domains are sharp, not gradual, and the patterning is controlled by specific transcription factors such as Ppar-δ and Cdx1 that organize lipid handling in the distal gut. Strikingly, the domains are not fixed in stone: when the researchers changed the animals' diet, the domains adjusted their gene programs in response.

Why does this matter for you as a learner or a coach? Because it tells you the absorptive surface is a responsive, regionally programmed organ, not a passive sponge. The body decides where and how to absorb, and that decision can shift with what you habitually eat. The three-region map is the floor of your understanding. The five-domain finding is the ceiling, and it confirms the deeper principle: location is a feature of absorption, not an accident.

The regional map: who gets absorbed where

Let us walk the tube from top to bottom and place each nutrient where it actually crosses. This is the spatial backbone of the whole chapter, so take your time with it.

Duodenum: iron, calcium, and the fast lane

The duodenum is short but it punches above its weight. It is the primary site for iron absorption and a major site for calcium and other minerals (Kiela & Ghishan, 2016). There is a chemical reason iron sits here at the top. Dietary non-heme iron is mostly in the oxidized ferric (Fe³⁺) form, which the gut cannot absorb well. It needs to be reduced to ferrous (Fe²⁺) iron, and that conversion is favored by the acidic environment arriving fresh from the stomach. The duodenum is the first stop after the stomach, so it catches iron while the contents are still relatively acidic and the iron is still soluble. The transporter that pulls ferrous iron into the enterocyte, DMT1, is concentrated right here.

This is also where you should first feel the entire course connecting. Remember stomach acid from Chapter 5. That acid is not just a digestive solvent; it is the chemistry that makes iron absorbable downstream. Lower the acid, and you blunt iron uptake before the duodenum ever gets a fair shot. Hold that thread.

Jejunum: the workhorse for the big three

The jejunum is the long middle stretch and it does the bulk of the heavy lifting for the macronutrients. Glucose and other monosaccharides, amino acids and small peptides, and the majority of water-soluble vitamins are absorbed across the jejunal brush border (Kiela & Ghishan, 2016; Said, 2011). Sugars come in through sodium-coupled transporters like SGLT1 (glucose and galactose) and facilitated transporters like GLUT5 (fructose). Amino acids ride a whole family of sodium-dependent and independent carriers. Most water-soluble vitamins, B1, B2, B3, B5, B6, B7, B9 (folate), and vitamin C, have dedicated carrier-mediated systems concentrated in the proximal small intestine (Said, 2011).

Folate deserves a specific note because it makes the "address" principle concrete. Folate has a dedicated carrier, the proton-coupled folate transporter, expressed in the proximal small intestine. So folate gets absorbed early and high. Its chemical cousin in the B-family, vitamin B12, does the opposite, as you will see in a moment. Same vitamin family, completely different addresses.

Ileum: B12, bile salts, and the recycling depot

The terminal ileum is the most specialized address in the entire tract, and it is where two irreplaceable jobs happen. First, vitamin B12 is absorbed almost exclusively here, and only here, and only when it is escorted by a protein called intrinsic factor (Said, 2011). Intrinsic factor is made by the same stomach cells that make acid. B12 binds intrinsic factor, the complex travels all the way down to the terminal ileum, and a specific receptor there pulls it in. There is no backup site. If you remove or damage the terminal ileum, or if you cannot make intrinsic factor, B12 absorption collapses regardless of how much B12 you eat.

Second, the ileum reclaims bile salts. Recall from Chapter 4 that bile emulsifies fat. The body does not make bile fresh for every meal and discard it; it recycles it through the enterohepatic circulation, and the reclamation point is the terminal ileum. Lose that segment and you lose bile salts into the colon, which impairs fat and fat-soluble vitamin handling on every subsequent meal.

Where fat actually goes in

Fat absorption is its own story because it does not work like sugar or amino acids, and it depends entirely on the chemistry you learned in Chapter 4. Dietary triglycerides are hydrolyzed in the lumen by pancreatic lipase into free fatty acids and monoglycerides. Those products are water-repelling, so they cannot just float to the brush border. Bile salts package them into micelles, tiny carrier droplets that ferry the fat products across the watery layer to the enterocyte surface (Iqbal & Hussain, 2009). Inside the enterocyte, the fats are reassembled into triglycerides, packaged into large lipoprotein particles called chylomicrons, and exported, not into the blood directly, but into the lymphatic system, which eventually drains into the bloodstream.

The fat-soluble vitamins, A, D, E, and K, hitch a ride on this exact machinery. They dissolve into the micelles alongside dietary fat and follow it across. Cholesterol absorption uses a dedicated transporter, NPC1L1, with ABCG5 and ABCG8 pumping excess sterol back out (Iqbal & Hussain, 2009). The practical consequence is enormous: anything that breaks the fat pathway, no lipase, no bile, damaged lining, automatically breaks fat-soluble vitamin absorption too. They are chemically chained together.

The colon: water and the microbiome's gift

By the time contents reach the colon, most of the calories and micronutrients are gone. The colon's headline job is reclaiming water and electrolytes, turning liquid chyme into formed stool. But Chapter 2 promised you the colon does something more interesting, and here is where it pays off. The colonic microbiota ferment the carbohydrates and proteins that escaped small-intestinal absorption, producing short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), chiefly acetate, propionate, and butyrate (Ríos-Covián et al., 2016). These SCFAs are then absorbed across the colonic wall. Butyrate is the preferred fuel of the colonocytes themselves; propionate and acetate travel to the liver and beyond, participating in host signaling and metabolism.

So the colon is not merely a drying rack. It is a second, microbe-driven absorption site that extracts energy and signaling molecules from material your own enzymes could never touch. That is the entire reason fiber matters: you do not digest it, your bacteria do, and you absorb the products.

Coaching translation

Here is the whole map in plain language. Iron and calcium: top of the tube, acid-dependent. Sugars, amino acids, most B-vitamins and vitamin C: the long middle. Fat and the A-D-E-K vitamins: need bile and lipase, ride micelles, export through lymph. B12: bottom of the tube only, and only with intrinsic factor from the stomach. Water and short-chain fatty acids: the colon, with help from your microbes. In practice, this means a problem in one location knocks out one specific set of nutrients, not all of them. That is why deficiencies are usually selective, not global.

What blocks absorption: five bottlenecks

Now we flip the chapter. You know where nutrients cross. The next question is what stops them. Malabsorption is usually sorted into three categories: problems with the gut lining itself (mucosal disease), problems in the lumen before nutrients reach the lining (luminal defects), and problems with the transport machinery (transport dysfunction) (Zuvarox & Belletieri, 2023). We will walk through five concrete bottlenecks that cover most of what you will encounter in real life.

1. Low stomach acid

Stomach acid does three jobs that matter for absorption downstream. It keeps iron in its soluble, absorbable ferrous-favoring state, it helps liberate B12 and protein-bound nutrients from food, and it triggers intrinsic factor production. When acid is chronically low, whether from aging, certain medications, or disease, iron and B12 absorption both suffer (Zuvarox & Belletieri, 2023). This is the cleanest example of why digestion gates absorption: the acid never crosses the wall itself, but without it, two specific nutrients downstream go uncollected.

Here is where people get this wrong. They assume a stomach problem causes a stomach-located deficiency. It does not. The acid problem at the top causes an iron problem in the duodenum and a B12 problem all the way down in the ileum. Cause and effect live in different ZIP codes.

2. Damaged or flattened villi

If you reduce the absorptive surface area, you reduce absorption across the board, with the steepest losses for whatever is absorbed in the damaged region. Celiac disease is the textbook case. In celiac disease, an immune response to gluten flattens the villi of the proximal small intestine, the very region responsible for iron, folate, calcium, and much of the macronutrient and water-soluble-vitamin uptake (Kreutz et al., 2020). The surface area collapses, and so does absorption.

Remember the prediction I asked you to hold earlier? This is the test. Kreutz and colleagues, reviewing nutrient status in celiac patients, found that deficiencies in iron, folate, vitamin B12, vitamin D, zinc, and others are common at diagnosis, driven by both reduced intake and diminished uptake through atrophied villi (Kreutz et al., 2020). And the sobering part for coaches: even after starting a gluten-free diet, full histological recovery of the lining takes a long time, so patients can remain vulnerable to deficiency well after they have removed the trigger. Removing the cause does not instantly restore the surface.

3. Rapid transit

Absorption takes contact time. Nutrients must dwell against the brush border long enough for transporters to do their work. When transit speeds up, as in chronic diarrhea or some post-surgical states, the contents rush past before slow or saturable transport systems can finish (Zuvarox & Belletieri, 2023). Think of it as a conveyor belt moving too fast for the workers to grab every item. Fat and fat-soluble vitamins are especially vulnerable because their absorption is a multi-step process that needs time to assemble micelles and shuttle products across.

4. Missing lipase

Pancreatic lipase is the enzyme that hydrolyzes dietary fat into the products that can be absorbed. Without it, as in pancreatic exocrine insufficiency, triglycerides arrive at the brush border still intact and unabsorbable. The fat passes through to the stool (causing steatorrhea, fatty stool), and the fat-soluble vitamins riding along with it go too (Zuvarox & Belletieri, 2023; Iqbal & Hussain, 2009). This is the chemistry chain from Chapter 4 breaking at its first link.

5. No bile

Even with perfect lipase, fat products cannot cross the watery layer to the enterocyte without bile salts to package them into micelles. Obstructed bile flow, or loss of bile-salt recycling at a damaged ileum, strands the fat at the doorstep (Iqbal & Hussain, 2009). The result mirrors the missing-lipase scenario: fat and the A-D-E-K vitamins fail to absorb. Two different mechanisms, low lipase and low bile, converge on the same downstream deficiency, because they break the same shared pathway at different points.

This is why it matters

Notice the pattern. Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency can come from at least four separate failures: rapid transit, missing lipase, no bile, or damaged lining. That is because absorption is a chain, and a chain breaks at its weakest link, not at its average strength. When you see a deficiency, the useful question is never just "did they eat enough?" It is "which link in the absorption chain might be broken?"

The Macronutrient Food-Source Reference

This is the course's second major reference tool, and it follows one firm rule: no food is moralized as good or bad, clean or dirty. We talk about smart sources and balance. The goal is to connect each macronutrient to reliable, practical foods so you can reason about sourcing the same way you now reason about absorption: mechanistically.

Protein

Protein supplies the amino acids absorbed across the jejunum. Smart, balanced sources span both animal and plant origins. Animal sources (eggs, dairy, poultry, fish, meat) provide all essential amino acids in one package. Plant sources (legumes, soy, lentils, beans, nuts, seeds, whole grains) are excellent too; eating a variety across the day covers the amino-acid spectrum without any single "complete" food being required. The practical move is variety and adequacy, not anxiety about any one source.

Carbohydrate

Carbohydrate is absorbed as monosaccharides in the jejunum, but the carbohydrate you do not absorb matters just as much. Fiber-rich sources (vegetables, fruit, whole grains, legumes) deliver the fermentable carbohydrate your colonic microbes turn into short-chain fatty acids (Ríos-Covián et al., 2016). So "smart carbohydrate sourcing" is really two goals at once: glucose for you, and fiber for your microbiome. A plate with starches plus plenty of plants serves both addresses, the jejunum and the colon.

Fat

Fat is absorbed via the micelle-and-chylomicron pathway, and it carries the fat-soluble vitamins with it. Smart sources include olive oil, avocado, nuts, seeds, fatty fish, and dairy fat. The absorption-relevant insight here is one most people never hear: because A, D, E, and K need fat to be absorbed, eating those vitamins with some dietary fat improves their uptake. The spinach-and-olive-oil pairing in this chapter's opening meal was not random. The fat is the vehicle that carries the fat-soluble vitamins across.

In practice, this means

A salad eaten completely fat-free is a worse delivery system for its fat-soluble vitamins than the same salad with a drizzle of olive oil or some avocado. This is not about calories or "healthy fats" as a moral category. It is plumbing: no fat in the meal, no micelles, weaker A-D-E-K absorption. Pair fat-soluble nutrients with a fat source on purpose.

Deficiency risk, without the fear

Now we can state the chapter's central reframing cleanly. Your nutrient status is determined by absorption capacity, not just intake. Eating a nutrient is necessary but not sufficient. The status you end up with is intake filtered through every bottleneck we just covered.

Some nutrients are simply more vulnerable than others, because their absorption depends on more moving parts or on a single irreplaceable address. Across the clinical literature, the micronutrients that most commonly run low in malabsorption are iron, vitamin B12, calcium, magnesium, folate, and the fat-soluble vitamins (StatPearls, 2025; Zuvarox & Belletieri, 2023). Look at that list and you can now explain every entry mechanistically:

  • Iron is fragile because it depends on stomach acid plus an intact duodenum. Two points of failure.
  • B12 is fragile because it needs acid, intrinsic factor, and an intact terminal ileum, with no alternate absorption site. Three points of failure and no backup.
  • Fat-soluble vitamins are fragile because they ride the fat pathway, which itself needs lipase, bile, intact lining, and adequate transit time. The most links, the most ways to break.
  • Folate is vulnerable specifically when proximal villi are damaged, which is why it appears so often in celiac disease (Kreutz et al., 2020).

What early signs actually look like

Deficiency is not a switch that flips; it is a slow draining of reserves, and the early signs are usually nonspecific. Iron and B12 deficiency both impair red-blood-cell production, producing fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance, and pallor before anything dramatic (StatPearls, 2025). B12 deficiency has a second danger: because B12 is essential to the nervous system, prolonged deficiency can cause neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling, balance and cognitive changes) that may not fully reverse, which is exactly why the terminal-ileum bottleneck deserves respect (Said, 2011). Folate deficiency also produces anemia. Vitamin D deficiency shows up in bone and muscle. The pattern across the water-soluble vitamins is that each one, when it runs low, produces a fairly specific clinical syndrome (Said, 2011).

The non-alarmist framing is this. Early signs are common, vague, and have many possible causes, so a symptom is a prompt to investigate, not a diagnosis. And the mechanistic lens is what keeps you calm and accurate: instead of fearing food or chasing supplements blindly, you ask where in the absorption chain a problem could plausibly sit, and whether intake, digestion, or uptake is the likely bottleneck.

Think About It

A person eats red meat regularly but has low iron, and also reports they have been on long-term acid-suppressing medication. Walk the chain: intake looks adequate, so where is the likely bottleneck, and which other nutrient sharing that bottleneck would you also want to consider? (Answer: low acid impairs iron solubility in the duodenum and B12 liberation and intrinsic-factor signaling. You would consider both iron and B12.)

The trained-individual footnote

For people training hard, the same mechanisms apply with two amplifications. Endurance athletes lose more iron and have higher turnover, so an absorption bottleneck that a sedentary person would tolerate can tip an athlete into deficiency faster. And very high energy intakes mean more food passing through, which usually helps total absorbed quantity, but rapid transit during heavy training or gut distress can work against it. The principles do not change. The margins get tighter.

Absorption is not a passive sponge. It is a regionally programmed, responsive organ that decides where and how each nutrient crosses, and a problem at one address produces a deficiency at another.

The organizing idea of this chapter

Closing the loop

Chapter 1 opened a physiological loop: food goes in one end, becomes part of you, and the remainder leaves the other. Every chapter since has filled in a stage. The mouth and stomach prepared and acidified. The pancreas and bile from Chapter 4 supplied the chemistry. Chapters 5 and 6 dealt with the lining and its disorders. This chapter is where it all cashes out, because absorption is the moment "food" finally becomes "you," and we can now see that the moment depends on everything upstream working in sequence.

You can now do four things you could not do before. You can locate where each major nutrient is absorbed. You can explain how upstream digestion and gut-lining health gate that absorption. You can use the Macronutrient Food-Source Reference to source smartly without moralizing food. And you can reason about deficiency risk mechanistically, by asking which link in the chain might be broken, rather than reaching for fear or for a supplement bottle by default. That is the whole loop, closed.

Key Takeaways

  • Digestion breaks food down; absorption is the separate event where nutrients cross the gut wall and become part of you. Intake does not guarantee uptake.
  • The small intestine is regionally specialized: iron and calcium in the duodenum; sugars, amino acids, and most water-soluble vitamins in the jejunum; B12 (with intrinsic factor) and bile-salt recycling only in the terminal ileum.
  • Fat and the fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) share one chemistry chain: lipase to hydrolyze, bile to form micelles, chylomicrons to export via lymph. Break any link and they all suffer.
  • The colon reclaims water and absorbs short-chain fatty acids produced when microbes ferment fiber, a second microbe-driven absorption site.
  • Five bottlenecks impair absorption: low stomach acid, flattened villi (celiac), rapid transit, missing lipase, and absent bile, each knocking out a specific, predictable set of nutrients.
  • The most deficiency-prone nutrients (iron, B12, fat-soluble vitamins, folate, calcium, magnesium) are vulnerable because they depend on the most steps or on a single irreplaceable address.
  • Reason about deficiency by asking which link in the absorption chain is broken, not by fearing food. Status equals intake filtered through absorption capacity.
