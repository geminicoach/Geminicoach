Chemical Demolition: Acids, Enzymes, and the Art of Breaking Things Down

How the body dismantles food into pieces small enough to actually use.

39 min read 12 cited sources

Picture a brick wall delivered to your front door. You ordered the wall because you want the bricks, but you cannot use a wall. You need individual bricks, and you need them small enough to carry through a doorway that is, frustratingly, narrower than a single brick. So you bring out the tools: a sledgehammer to crack the mortar, a chisel for precision, an acid bath for the stubborn joints. Demolition first, then transport.

That is digestion. The food you eat is built from molecules that are far too large to cross the wall of your intestine. A steak is a scaffold of proteins thousands of amino acids long. A bowl of rice is starch, a branching chain of glucose units. Butter is fat, packed into droplets that refuse to mix with water. None of it can be absorbed in that form. Before a single nutrient enters your bloodstream, your body has to take the wall apart, brick by brick, using one of the most precise chemical toolkits in biology.

Meet Marcus. He is forty-eight, eats what he considers a sensible diet, and has spent the last year quietly convinced something in his digestion is broken. After bigger meals he feels a slow, heavy fullness that sits for hours, sometimes a sour burn that climbs toward his throat when he lies down. A wellness podcast told him his stomach acid was "too high," so he takes an over-the-counter acid reducer most evenings. A different account told him it was "too low," so he also takes a betaine HCl supplement before lunch. He has a drawer of enzyme capsules he uses inconsistently, and he has begun avoiding fat because fatty meals seem to make everything worse. Marcus is not careless. He is doing what a motivated person does with symptoms and no framework: collecting tactics. What he lacks is the mechanism. We will build it with him, because once you understand how chemical demolition is supposed to work, almost every piece of advice Marcus has collected sorts itself into "plausible," "harmless," or "actively working against him." That sorting is the skill this chapter teaches.

The Size Problem, and Why Chewing Is Not Enough

Absorption happens across the lining of your small intestine, and the cells of that lining will only let through molecules of a certain size. Polymers stay out. Monomers get in. The entire job of chemical digestion is to convert the first into the second.

Mechanical breakdown, the chewing and churning we covered earlier in the course, helps by increasing surface area. But surface area alone does not break a chemical bond. You can grind a steak into paste and not free a single amino acid. The covalent bonds holding those molecules together have to be cut, and cutting them requires hydrolysis: inserting a water molecule across the bond to split it in two. Every major digestive enzyme is, at heart, a hydrolase. It is a molecular scissor that uses water as its blade.

Hydrolysis happens on its own, but absurdly slowly. Left to spontaneous chemistry, the protein in your lunch would take years to break down. Enzymes solve the speed problem. They are protein catalysts that hold a target molecule in exactly the right position and lower the energy barrier for the reaction, accelerating it by factors of millions. The art of digestion is the art of deploying the right enzyme, in the right place, at the right pH, at the right moment.

Two features of enzymes matter for everything that follows. The first is specificity. An enzyme is not a generic shredder; it is shaped to fit one kind of bond in one kind of molecule, the way a key is cut for one lock. The enzyme that cuts starch will not touch protein, and the one that cuts protein will not touch fat. This is why the body needs a whole toolkit rather than one universal tool, and why a digestive problem is almost never "digestion is weak" in some vague global sense. It is far more often a specific tool that is missing, mislocated, or working in the wrong conditions. When Marcus says his "digestion is bad," the useful question is never whether digestion is bad, but which specific step, on which macronutrient, is failing, because each one fails differently and produces a different downstream signal.

The second feature is that enzymes are themselves proteins, and proteins are fragile. The same folded shape that lets an enzyme grip its target is held together by weak forces that come apart when the surroundings change. Heat unfolds them; extremes of acidity or alkalinity unfold them. An enzyme outside its preferred conditions does not work slowly; past a point it stops working entirely, because it has lost the shape that made it an enzyme. Hold onto that: it is the hidden reason pH governs the entire tract, and the reason a swallowed enzyme is not automatically a working one.

The demolition also has to be fast and complete. A bond left uncut is a nutrient left behind, and that nutrient does not vanish; it travels onward to the colon, where bacteria ferment it into the gas, bloating, and looseness people describe when a step upstream has quietly failed. Every symptom in the back half of this chapter is, at root, a story about bonds that should have been cut and were not.

The Stomach: An Acid Bath With a Purpose

The stomach is where most intuitions about digestion go wrong, so we will spend real time here. The dominant feature of the stomach is its acidity. The parietal cells embedded in the gastric lining secrete hydrochloric acid (HCl), and they do it aggressively. Resting stomach pH sits around 1.5 to 3.5, roughly the acidity of battery acid diluted a little. The machine that produces this is the H+/K+-ATPase, the proton pump, which trades potassium ions for hydrogen ions and drives protons into the stomach lumen against a concentration gradient steeper than almost any other in the body (Schubert & Peura, 2008).

Why would your body invest so much energy building such a punishing environment inside itself? Three reasons, and none of them is "to digest food directly." Acid does not chop up your lunch on its own.

Reason one: denaturation

Proteins are folded into intricate three-dimensional shapes. Those folds hide most of the chemical bonds an enzyme would want to cut. Strong acid disrupts the weak interactions holding the fold together, causing the protein to unravel, a process called denaturation. Unfolding a tightly wound chain lets the enzyme reach bonds that were buried inside. A denatured protein is not yet digested, but it is now accessible.

Reason two: activating pepsin

The stomach's protein-cutting enzyme is pepsin, and here is an elegant piece of biological engineering. The chief cells of the stomach do not secrete pepsin. They secrete pepsinogen, an inactive precursor called a zymogen. If chief cells made active pepsin, the enzyme would digest the very cells that produced it. Instead, pepsinogen is harmless until it hits the acid bath. Low pH cleaves a blocking segment off the pepsinogen molecule, exposing its active site and converting it to working pepsin. Once a little pepsin exists, it activates more pepsinogen in an accelerating chain called autocatalysis (StatPearls, 2023). Acid is the trigger. No acid, no pepsin.

Pepsin itself is an endopeptidase: it cuts proteins at internal bonds, preferentially next to certain amino acids, chopping long protein chains into shorter fragments called peptides. It does not finish the job. It does the rough demolition, breaking the wall into manageable chunks that the small intestine will later dismantle into individual bricks.

Reason three: defense

An environment that unfolds proteins and shreds peptides is also lethal to most bacteria. Stomach acid is a frontline immune barrier, killing a large fraction of the microbes that ride in on food before they ever reach the gut (Schubert & Peura, 2008). This matters for a later chapter: when people suppress acid long term, they are also lowering this defensive wall.

If the stomach is acidic enough to denature proteins and kill bacteria, why does it not digest itself? Because it defends its own lining with a thick layer of mucus and a coat of bicarbonate held against the cell surface, so the cells live in a thin neutral microclimate even as the lumen a fraction of a millimeter away sits near pH 2. The acid and enzyme are aimed outward at the food, never inward at the tissue. When that defensive layer is overwhelmed, by certain bacteria, some medications, or other insults, the acid and pepsin that were doing useful work start eroding the wall itself, and we call the result an ulcer. One principle runs through all digestive dysfunction: the same tool that is essential in the right place becomes the injury when the boundary that contained it fails. The acid was not reclassified as bad. The containment failed.

Think About It

Pepsin is made and stored as inactive pepsinogen, then switched on only in the stomach lumen. Why is the "make it inactive, activate it on site" strategy a recurring theme you should expect to see again when we reach the pancreas?

The myth: "stomach acid is the enemy"

This is the claim the wellness world gets wrong, and gets wrong loudly. The popular story says acid is a problem, that reflux means too much acid, that you should be neutralizing or suppressing it to feel better. Read the mechanism again. Acid is not a malfunction. It is the on-switch for protein digestion and a primary defense against ingested pathogens. A stomach that is doing its job is supposed to be intensely acidic.

Reflux is real and uncomfortable, but the symptom is acid in the wrong place, the esophagus, not necessarily too much acid in the right place. We will take apart the reflux story properly in Chapters 6 and 10, including what acid-reducing drugs actually do and the tradeoffs of long-term suppression. For now, plant this flag: the goal of digestion is not less acid. It is acid contained where it belongs.

The practical translation. When someone tells you stomach acid is bad for you, they have mistaken the tool for the injury. The acid is doing essential work. The discomfort of reflux is a containment problem, not a "you have too much of this nasty stuff" problem. In practice, this means you should be skeptical of any advice that treats suppressing acid as automatically healthy. It has costs, and we will count them later.

The opposite problem: too little acid

If the popular error is treating acid as the enemy, the equal and opposite error, increasingly fashionable in wellness spaces, is assuming that everyone has too little of it. Low stomach acid is a real condition. It has a clinical name, hypochlorhydria, and a more severe form, achlorhydria, in which acid production is essentially absent. But it is not the universal explanation for indigestion that the supplement market would like it to be, and understanding the difference is exactly the kind of judgment that separates educated thinking from guessing.

According to PubMed, Fatima and colleagues (2024) describe achlorhydria and hypochlorhydria as conditions in which hydrochloric acid production is absent or reduced, almost always secondary to an underlying cause rather than arising on its own. The classic drivers are autoimmune destruction of the acid-producing parietal cells, the chronic inflammation called atrophic gastritis, certain infections, and, very commonly today, the long-term use of acid-suppressing medication itself. In other words, the single most frequent reason a modern adult has genuinely low stomach acid is that they are taking a drug specifically designed to lower it.

Why does low acid matter? Run the mechanism backward. Without enough acid, proteins are not fully denatured and are harder for pepsin to reach; pepsinogen is not efficiently activated, so protein demolition weakens; the microbial barrier drops, letting more swallowed bacteria survive into the gut; and several nutrients that depend on an acidic stomach to be released or converted are absorbed less well. Fatima and colleagues (2024) note that low-acid states are linked to iron-deficiency anemia and, through the loss of intrinsic factor when parietal cells are destroyed, to vitamin B12 deficiency. Acid is not only a digestive trigger; it is the opening move in absorbing specific minerals and vitamins, a thread we pick up in the absorption chapter.

Think About It

Marcus is doing two contradictory things at once: taking an acid reducer some evenings and a betaine HCl supplement before lunch. Using the mechanism, explain why these two products cannot both be the right answer for the same person at the same time, and why "both, just in case" is not a neutral hedge but a way of guaranteeing at least one of them is working against him.

The practical translation, and a hard boundary. The symptoms people attribute to low acid, fullness, bloating, food that seems to just sit there, overlap almost perfectly with the symptoms people attribute to high acid and reflux. The same complaint points in opposite directions, and you cannot tell which is true from the symptom alone. This is precisely why diagnosing low stomach acid, and choosing acid supplements to correct it, belongs to a qualified clinician, not to general education or self-experiment. Genuine hypochlorhydria is identified through clinical testing and usually signals an underlying condition that itself needs attention. The useful move with a picture like Marcus's is not to pick a side in the high-acid versus low-acid debate. It is to recognize that the question is medical, understand why a symptom-based self-diagnosis is shakier than the internet implied, and treat that as a signal to bring in a clinician who can test rather than guess. Reaching for betaine HCl to self-treat undiagnosed reflux crosses the line, of scope and of safety, whether you are doing it for yourself or pointing anyone else toward it.

Why pH Is Not a Detail

If you played with the explorer, you saw the core principle: every enzyme has an optimal pH, a narrow window where its shape is stable and its active site works. Push the pH outside that window and activity collapses. Pepsin is happiest around pH 1.5 to 3.5 and is essentially dead above pH 5. The pancreatic enzymes are the mirror image, tuned for the alkaline conditions of the small intestine and useless in acid.

This is not an inconvenience the body tolerates. It is a design feature. By assigning each enzyme a different pH optimum, the body controls where each step of digestion happens. Pepsin can only act in the stomach because only the stomach is acidic enough. Pancreatic enzymes can only act in the duodenum because that is the only place neutralized enough for them. pH is the body's way of writing addresses on its chemistry.

It also explains a fact that surprises people. Salivary amylase, the carbohydrate enzyme that starts working in your mouth, is destroyed by stomach acid (Butterworth et al., 2011). Carbohydrate digestion essentially pauses in the stomach and restarts in the small intestine with a fresh supply of pancreatic amylase. The handoff is dictated entirely by pH.

The practical translation. When you hear "take a digestive enzyme supplement," the right first question is: will it survive the pH where it lands, and will it reach the pH where it works? An enzyme in the wrong environment is a key in the wrong lock. This is why pH, not just the presence of an enzyme, determines whether digestion actually happens.

This is where Marcus's enzyme drawer earns a second look. The supplement market sells "digestive enzymes" as though swallowing them guarantees they will do something. But a raw enzyme dropped into the stomach faces the same gauntlet salivary amylase faces: a wash of acid that can denature it before it reaches the small intestine where most digestion happens. This is not a reason to dismiss every enzyme product, because some are formulated with acid-resistant coatings to survive the trip, and there are genuine conditions for which prescribed enzyme replacement is transformative, as we will see shortly. It is a reason to be unimpressed by the bare claim that a capsule "contains enzymes." Containing an enzyme and delivering an active one to the place it works are very different promises, and the gap is measured in pH.

Think About It

The pH explorer shows pepsin near peak at pH 2 and effectively dead at pH 5, while pancreatic enzymes are the reverse. If a single enzyme had a broad optimum that worked equally well from pH 2 to pH 8, the body would lose something important. What would it lose? Hint: think about what pH is being used to control.

Reflux: Acid in the Wrong Room

We promised to take the reflux story apart, and Marcus's evening burn makes this the right moment, because reflux is the single most common reason people decide their digestion is broken and then reach for exactly the wrong mental model.

Start with the geography. Between the esophagus and the stomach sits a ring of muscle, the lower esophageal sphincter, meant to stay closed and open only to let food pass downward. The esophagus is built to handle food, not acid; the stomach handles acid but is not meant to leak it upward. Reflux happens when stomach contents travel backward across that ring into a tube with no defenses against them. The burn is not the stomach being too acidic. It is the esophagus exposed to an acidity that is perfectly normal one room down and intolerable here.

According to PubMed, Clarrett and Hachem (2018) describe the dominant mechanism of reflux as transient relaxations of that lower esophageal sphincter, brief inappropriate openings of the ring that should have stayed shut, along with situations where pressure inside the abdomen overwhelms the barrier. Note what is and is not on that list. The cause is a failure of the valve and the pressure gradient across it, not an oversupply of acid. A stomach producing an entirely normal amount of acid will produce reflux symptoms if the valve leaks or the pressure pushes the wrong way. This is the mechanistic heart of why "too much acid" is the wrong frame: the problem is location, not quantity.

Why, then, do acid-reducing drugs help reflux at all? Because if you cannot reliably keep the acid in the right room, the next best thing is to make the acid that leaks less corrosive. Reducing acidity does not fix the leaking valve; it makes the leak hurt less and gives the irritated esophagus a chance to heal. That is legitimate and often valuable. But it tells you exactly what these drugs do and do not address: they manage the consequence, the burn and the tissue damage, while leaving the mechanical cause, the failing barrier, untouched. Holding that distinction lets you talk about reflux honestly instead of repeating either the wellness slogan that acid is poison or the reflex that more suppression is always better.

Clarrett and Hachem (2018) also note that reflux does not always present as classic heartburn. It can show up as a chronic cough, a hoarse voice, a lump-in-the-throat sensation, or disrupted sleep, the so-called atypical presentations. Because these do not look like "heartburn," people and sometimes clinicians chase the wrong explanation for months. The practical consequence is humility: a chronic throat-clearing is not something to confidently attribute to reflux, or to anything else, from the outside.

Think About It

If reflux is fundamentally a containment problem rather than an overproduction problem, why might lying down after a large, fatty meal, exactly Marcus's evening pattern, make symptoms worse independent of how much acid his stomach makes? Think about pressure, gravity, and how long the stomach stays full.

The practical translation, and a firm referral line. Occasional reflux after a heavy late meal is common and ordinary. But persistent reflux, reflux severe enough to wake someone at night, difficulty or pain on swallowing, unintended weight loss, vomiting, black stools, or any reflux that has been quietly self-treated for months without resolving, these are signs that warrant medical evaluation, not problems to work through on your own. Long-standing untreated reflux can damage the esophagus in ways that need medical surveillance, and the symptoms can also mask conditions that are not reflux at all. Understanding the mechanism is squarely within general education: why the "too much acid" model is upside down, why not lying down soon after eating helps, why a fatty late dinner is a predictable trigger. Starting or stopping an acid-reducing medication is not, in yourself or anyone else, and a year of unresolved symptoms belongs with a clinician rather than a podcast.

The Pancreas: The Enzyme Cocktail and the Acid Neutralizer

When the acidic, partially demolished slurry called chyme leaves the stomach and enters the duodenum, the first segment of the small intestine, two things must happen fast. The acid has to be neutralized, and a full set of enzymes has to arrive. The pancreas handles both, which is why it is the engine room within the engine room.

Bicarbonate: turning down the acid

The pancreatic duct cells secrete a watery juice loaded with bicarbonate, the same buffering chemistry your blood uses, producing one to two liters of alkaline fluid per day (Lee et al., 2020). Bicarbonate neutralizes incoming gastric acid and lifts duodenal pH into the range where pancreatic enzymes can function. The molecular machinery involves carbonic anhydrase generating bicarbonate inside the cell and the CFTR channel helping move it out (Lee et al., 2020). The same CFTR is the protein defective in cystic fibrosis, which is why that disease devastates pancreatic function.

This is the missing half of the pepsin story. Pepsin needs acid. Pancreatic enzymes need that acid gone. The transition from stomach to duodenum is a controlled chemical sign change, acidic to alkaline, orchestrated by bicarbonate.

The enzyme cocktail

The pancreatic acinar cells produce the workhorse enzymes that handle all three macronutrients:

Pancreatic amylase finishes what salivary amylase started, cleaving the internal α-1,4 bonds of starch into shorter oligosaccharides and maltose. In practice this enzyme, not the salivary one, does most of the real carbohydrate work because it operates in the right pH and has time and quantity on its side (Butterworth et al., 2011).

Pancreatic proteases, chiefly trypsin and chymotrypsin, continue protein demolition where pepsin left off. Like pepsin, they are secreted as inactive zymogens, trypsinogen and chymotrypsinogen, for the same self-protection reason. We will see the activation cascade in a moment, because it is genuinely clever. The stakes of getting this safety system right are not academic. If pancreatic proteases switch on while still inside the pancreas, they begin digesting the organ that made them, which is the violent core of acute pancreatitis. The pancreas carries multiple redundant safeguards against premature activation precisely because the consequence of failure is the gland eating itself.

Pancreatic lipase is the primary fat-digesting enzyme, cutting triglycerides into free fatty acids and monoglycerides. But lipase has a problem that the other enzymes do not, and solving it requires the liver.

Timing: the pancreas does not fire blindly

The pancreas releases its products on cue, coordinated by two hormones. When acidic chyme hits the duodenum, the gut releases secretin, which tells the pancreas to send bicarbonate. When fat and protein arrive, the gut releases cholecystokinin (CCK), which tells the pancreas to send enzymes and signals the gallbladder to release bile (Chandra & Liddle, 2020). Secretion is organized into phases, cephalic, gastric, and intestinal, so that the digestive juices arrive in proportion to what and how much you ate (Chandra & Liddle, 2020). The system is not a faucet left running. It meters its output to demand.

Think About It

Secretin responds to acid; CCK responds to fat and protein. Given that, predict which signal dominates after a glass of water versus after a fatty meal. Why does it make sense that fat is one of the strongest triggers for both enzyme release and bile release?

There is a practical insight hiding in this timing. The system is demand-driven: a small simple meal calls for a modest release, a large fatty meal calls for the full orchestra. So when someone feels fine after light meals but unwell after big rich ones, they are reporting that their capacity is tested hardest exactly when demand is highest, which is the expected place for a marginal step to reveal itself. Marcus's observation that fatty meals hit him worst is mechanistically coherent: fat is the strongest call for both pancreatic enzymes and bile, so any weakness in the fat-handling chain announces itself loudest after the fattiest meals. That does not tell you which link is weak. It tells you where to look.

Bile: Solving the Fat Problem Without Cutting a Single Bond

Lipase has an awkward limitation. Fat does not dissolve in water, and the contents of your gut are watery. Dietary fat arrives as large globules, the way oil beads up in a vinaigrette. Lipase can only work at the surface of a fat droplet, where water and fat meet. A big globule has very little surface relative to its volume, so lipase working on it would be agonizingly slow. The fat is not protected by a chemical bond. It is protected by geometry.

This is where bile earns its keep. Bile is made by the liver, concentrated and stored in the gallbladder, and released into the duodenum when CCK signals that fat has arrived (Hundt et al., 2022). Its key components are bile salts, which are amphipathic: one part of the molecule loves water, the other part loves fat. Bile salts coat large fat globules and break them into a fine suspension of tiny droplets, a process called emulsification (Chen & Cassaro, 2023).

Notice what emulsification does and does not do. It does not break any chemical bonds. It does not digest fat. It takes one big droplet and turns it into thousands of small ones, multiplying the total surface area enormously. Now lipase has acres of fat-water interface to work on, and digestion accelerates dramatically (Chen & Cassaro, 2023). Bile is the prep crew that lays out the work so the enzyme can do it fast.

After lipase cuts the triglycerides, bile salts assemble the products, free fatty acids and monoglycerides, into mixed micelles, tiny ferry-like packages that shuttle the otherwise water-hating products across the watery gap to the intestinal lining for absorption (Chen & Cassaro, 2023). Bile is also recycled with remarkable thrift: most bile salts are reabsorbed downstream and returned to the liver in the enterohepatic circulation, to be used again (Hundt et al., 2022).

The practical translation. Bile works like dish soap. Soap does not chemically destroy grease on your plate; it surrounds it and lifts it into the water so it can be rinsed away. Bile does the same to dietary fat so lipase can reach it. This is why people without a working gallbladder, or with poor bile flow, often struggle specifically with fatty meals. The enzyme may be fine; the prep crew is short-staffed. We will return to this in the malabsorption chapter.

Think About It

Emulsification and hydrolysis are two completely different operations. One changes the physical arrangement of fat; the other breaks chemical bonds. Which does bile perform, and which does lipase perform? If you can answer cleanly, you understand why both are required and neither can substitute for the other.

When the Enzyme Cocktail Runs Dry: Pancreatic Insufficiency

We have built the pancreas up as the engine room. Now ask the obvious question: what happens when the engine room cannot deliver enough enzyme? This is not a hypothetical, and it is the most important enzyme-deficiency condition to recognize, because it sits right at the boundary of what enzyme supplements can and cannot legitimately do.

The condition is exocrine pancreatic insufficiency, or EPI. According to PubMed, Singh and Haupt (2023) define it as a state in which the pancreas fails to produce or deliver enough digestive enzymes, leading to maldigestion and malabsorption. The hallmark, predictably, is fat. Because lipase is the primary fat-digesting enzyme and the pancreas is its only meaningful source, a shortfall shows up first and most dramatically as undigested fat, producing the symptom clinicians call steatorrhea: stools that are pale, bulky, greasy, foul-smelling, and that may float or leave an oily film. Alongside it come bloating, abdominal discomfort, and, over time, weight loss and the deficiencies of fat-soluble vitamins that ride along with fat.

Notice how cleanly this maps onto the mechanism. Singh and Haupt (2023) note that EPI most often follows chronic pancreatitis, cystic fibrosis, pancreatic surgery, or pancreatic cancer, conditions that physically reduce the gland's ability to make and ship enzymes. Diagnosis is typically supported by a stool test for pancreatic elastase, a marker that drops when output falls. And the treatment is exactly the logic this chapter has built toward: pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy, prescribed preparations dosed to the meal and formulated to survive the trip and release in the small intestine. This is the legitimate, evidence-based, sometimes life-changing use of supplemental enzymes, and it could not be further from a wellness capsule taken on a hunch.

Think About It

Both genuine EPI and simple overeating of a very fatty meal can produce loose, greasy-seeming stools and bloating. Using what you know about how the system scales to demand, why is the pattern over time, not any single bad day, the thing that distinguishes a person who needs medical evaluation from a person who just ate too much fried food at a party?

This is the part Marcus is least equipped to handle alone. He has noticed fat makes him worse and responded by avoiding it and self-prescribing enzyme capsules. If his problem is occasional overindulgence, that is harmless tinkering. But if it is genuine pancreatic insufficiency, then avoiding fat masks a signal that should be evaluated, his capsules may be the wrong type or dose, and a potentially serious cause goes unexamined while he treats a symptom. The distance between "I bloat after rich meals" and "I have exocrine pancreatic insufficiency" is enormous, and it can only be crossed by testing.

The practical translation, and the cleanest boundary in this chapter. Pancreatic enzyme replacement is a prescribed medical therapy for a diagnosed condition. It is not a self-care intervention, not a supplement to reach for on a hunch, and not something to be inferred from symptoms. When the EPI pattern shows up, persistent greasy or floating stools, ongoing fat-meal distress, unexplained weight loss, the move is not a product. It is to understand, in the plain mechanism this chapter gave you, why these signs warrant medical evaluation, and to treat them as a reason to involve a clinician. Recognizing the pattern and knowing where it belongs is what the education in this chapter is for. Diagnosing or treating it is where general education ends and clinical care begins.

The Brush Border: The Finishing Crew

By the time chyme has been through stomach acid, pepsin, pancreatic enzymes, and bile, the macronutrients are mostly reduced to small fragments: disaccharides, short peptides, fatty acids, monoglycerides. But "mostly" is not "fully." Disaccharides like maltose, sucrose, and lactose still cannot be absorbed. Short peptides still need to become free amino acids or very small units. The last cuts happen at the wall itself.

The lining of the small intestine is covered in microscopic projections called microvilli, collectively the brush border. Embedded in this surface is a set of brush border enzymes anchored directly in the membrane, so digestion finishes at the exact spot where absorption begins (Hooton et al., 2015). These include the disaccharidases, such as maltase, sucrase, and lactase, which split disaccharides into absorbable monosaccharides, and various peptidases that trim short peptides into amino acids (Hooton et al., 2015).

The brush border also holds the master switch for the entire pancreatic protease system. Recall that the pancreas ships its proteases as inactive zymogens. They cannot be allowed to activate inside the pancreas, or the organ would digest itself, a catastrophe that actually occurs in pancreatitis. So how do they switch on safely in the gut? A brush border enzyme called enteropeptidase (also called enterokinase) cleaves trypsinogen into active trypsin. Trypsin then activates more trypsinogen and, crucially, activates chymotrypsinogen and the other protease zymogens as well (Hooton et al., 2015). One brush border enzyme pulls the trigger, and the whole protein-digesting cascade comes online, but only after it has safely arrived in the small intestine.

This is the same "make it inactive, activate it on site" logic you saw with pepsinogen, now scaled up into a multi-stage cascade with a single failsafe trigger located exactly where activation is supposed to happen. It is one of the better-engineered safety systems in physiology.

Lactase deserves a specific note because it explains a phenomenon you have certainly encountered. Lactase activity is high in infancy and declines in much of the world's adult population. When lactase is low, lactose passes undigested into the colon, where bacteria ferment it, producing the gas and discomfort of lactose intolerance. This is not a disease of the stomach or pancreas. It is a single missing brush border enzyme. We will use this case again when we discuss malabsorption.

Lactose intolerance is the cleanest illustration of why "my digestion is bad" is the wrong unit of analysis. A lactose-intolerant person has a flawless stomach, pancreas, and bile, and an intact brush border in every respect except one enzyme on one sugar. They digest protein, fat, and starch perfectly. Their entire problem is a single missing tool. Internalize that and you stop hearing "I can't digest things" as a global verdict and start hearing it as a question: which tool, on which food? That reframing is most of the diagnostic humility this course is trying to build, and the opposite of the scattershot approach Marcus brought to his symptoms.

The list in plain text

Salivary amylase. Source: salivary glands. Target: starch. Product: maltose and oligosaccharides. Works at near-neutral mouth pH; inactivated by stomach acid.

Pepsin. Source: stomach chief cells (secreted as pepsinogen, activated by HCl). Target: proteins. Product: smaller peptides. Optimal pH about 1.5 to 3.5.

Pancreatic amylase. Source: pancreas. Target: starch. Product: maltose and oligosaccharides. Works in the alkaline duodenum and does the bulk of carbohydrate digestion.

Trypsin. Source: pancreas (as trypsinogen, activated by enteropeptidase). Target: proteins and peptides. Product: smaller peptides. Also activates other pancreatic proteases. Alkaline optimum.

Chymotrypsin. Source: pancreas (as chymotrypsinogen, activated by trypsin). Target: proteins and peptides. Product: smaller peptides. Alkaline optimum.

Pancreatic lipase. Source: pancreas. Target: triglycerides (after emulsification by bile). Product: free fatty acids and monoglycerides. Alkaline optimum.

Brush border enzymes. Source: small intestine microvilli. Targets: disaccharides (maltase, sucrase, lactase) and short peptides (peptidases); enteropeptidase activates trypsinogen. Products: monosaccharides and amino acids. Operate at the absorptive surface.

Note that bile is not on this list. Bile is not an enzyme. It emulsifies fat but breaks no bonds. Keeping bile off the enzyme list is itself a test of whether you understand the difference between emulsification and hydrolysis.

Putting It Together: A Worked Example

Follow a bite of a cheese-and-turkey sandwich, which conveniently contains all three macronutrients: starch (bread), protein (turkey and cheese), and fat (cheese).

Mouth. Chewing increases surface area. Salivary amylase begins cutting the bread's starch into maltose and oligosaccharides. Nothing yet happens to the protein or fat.

Stomach. HCl floods in. The acid denatures the turkey and cheese proteins, unfolding them, and converts pepsinogen to pepsin, which begins chopping those proteins into peptides. The acid inactivates the salivary amylase, so starch digestion pauses. Fat sits largely untouched, churned into a coarse mixture. The result is acidic chyme, released in controlled squirts into the duodenum.

Duodenum. Acid here triggers secretin, which calls bicarbonate from the pancreas to neutralize the acid and raise the pH. Fat and protein trigger CCK, which calls pancreatic enzymes and squeezes the gallbladder to release bile. Bile emulsifies the cheese fat into fine droplets. Enteropeptidase on the brush border activates trypsin, which activates chymotrypsin and the rest. Now the cocktail runs: pancreatic amylase finishes the starch, trypsin and chymotrypsin finish the protein, lipase cuts the emulsified fat into fatty acids and monoglycerides, which bile packages into micelles.

Brush border. The final cuts. Disaccharidases split maltose into glucose, lactase splits any lactose from the cheese into glucose and galactose, peptidases trim the last peptides into amino acids. Everything is now small enough to absorb, which is exactly where Chapter 8 picks up the story.

The practical translation. Every later problem we study, low stomach acid, pancreatic insufficiency, gallbladder removal, lactose intolerance, reflux and acid suppression, is a specific break in this exact sequence. Once you can run this worked example from memory, every dysfunction chapter becomes "which step broke and what is downstream of it." That is the whole point of building the mechanism first.

The Cost of Turning the Acid Down for Years

We have circled the acid-suppression question several times. Now we can close it, because you have the mechanism to see the full ledger rather than just the headline. Acid reducers, particularly the proton pump inhibitors that shut down the very H+/K+-ATPase pump we opened the chapter with, are among the most widely used medications in the world. For genuine indications, healing an inflamed esophagus, treating ulcers, protecting the gut lining from certain drugs, they are effective and important. The issue is not the drug. It is what happens when a tool meant for a defined problem is taken indefinitely, often without anyone revisiting whether it is still needed.

Recall the three jobs of acid: denature protein, activate pepsin, defend against microbes. Suppress acid for years and you blunt all three. The protein-digestion consequences are usually mild for a well-nourished adult, but the absorption and defense consequences are where the literature focuses. According to PubMed, Sheikh-Taha and colleagues (2025), in a systematic review of long-term proton pump inhibitor use in older adults, found that chronic acid suppression is associated with lowered status of several nutrients, including vitamin B12, magnesium, and iron, because an acidic stomach is part of how each of these is liberated from food or converted into an absorbable form. This is the exact mirror image of the hypochlorhydria discussion: the body uses acid as the opening step in absorbing specific micronutrients, so removing the acid removes that step.

The mechanism is direct. Vitamin B12 is bound to protein in food and must be freed by acid and pepsin first; less acid means less freed B12. Iron is better absorbed in a reduced form that an acidic environment favors; less acid shifts the balance unfavorably. None of this means acid reducers are dangerous or that anyone should stop one, and it does not mean general education is the place to advise stopping a prescribed medication, in yourself or anyone else. Sheikh-Taha and colleagues (2025) frame the takeaway as monitoring: in long-term users, periodically checking these nutrient levels is reasonable, a decision that belongs to the prescribing clinician.

The practical translation. The honest summary is neither "acid reducers are bad" nor "acid reducers are free." It is that suppressing a tool the body relies on has predictable downstream costs worth weighing against the benefits, by a clinician, on an ongoing basis. For Marcus, who has taken an over-the-counter acid reducer most evenings for nearly a year on his own initiative, the relevant fact is not that he should stop. It is that an unsupervised year of acid suppression for an undiagnosed problem is exactly the situation where a clinician should be involved, to find out what is actually wrong and decide whether the medication still makes sense.

Case

Put Marcus's whole picture through the mechanism. His symptoms, fullness and a sour burn that climbs when he lies down, worse after big fatty meals, are most consistent with a reflux pattern, an acid-in-the-wrong-room containment story, not a quantity-of-acid story. His three self-prescribed fixes pull in incompatible directions: an acid reducer that lowers acid, a betaine HCl supplement that adds acid, and enzyme capsules that may not survive the stomach. At least one is working against the others, and a year of taking them together has produced no resolution, only a more complicated situation. The mechanism does not tell you Marcus's diagnosis; you cannot give him one. It tells you his self-experiment has reached its limit, that contradictory over-the-counter tactics are no substitute for finding out what is actually happening, and that the most useful response is to understand why his model is upside down and to treat that as a reason to involve a clinician who can test rather than guess. That is education doing its job, not diagnosis, and it is the conversation Marcus most needs to have about his gut.

The acid is not the disease. It is the signal, the trigger, and the gatekeeper. Mistake the tool for the injury and every downstream recommendation goes wrong.

A working principle for the chapters ahead

Key Takeaways

  • Chemical digestion is hydrolysis: enzymes use water to cut large polymers into absorbable monomers. Chewing alone breaks no chemical bonds.
  • Stomach acid (HCl) does three jobs: it denatures proteins, it activates pepsin from pepsinogen, and it defends against ingested microbes. It does not digest food directly.
  • Every enzyme has an optimal pH. pH determines where each step happens: pepsin in the acidic stomach, pancreatic enzymes in the alkaline duodenum. Salivary amylase is destroyed by stomach acid.
  • The pancreas delivers both bicarbonate, to neutralize acid and reset the pH, and the enzyme cocktail: amylase for carbs, trypsin and chymotrypsin for protein, lipase for fat. Secretin and CCK time the delivery.
  • Bile emulsifies fat, multiplying surface area so lipase can work, but bile breaks no bonds and is not an enzyme.
  • The brush border finishes digestion at the absorptive surface, and enteropeptidase there triggers the entire pancreatic protease cascade safely on site.
  • The Enzymes Reference List, organized by source, target, and product, is your working instrument for the malabsorption, enzyme-disorder, and absorption chapters to come.
  • Myth busted: stomach acid is not the enemy. The goal of digestion is acid contained where it belongs, not less acid everywhere.
  • Reflux is a containment problem, acid in the wrong room, driven by a leaking valve and pressure, not by overproduction. Low stomach acid is a real but over-claimed condition whose symptoms overlap with reflux, so neither can be self-diagnosed from symptoms alone.
  • Exocrine pancreatic insufficiency is the major enzyme-deficiency condition; its treatment, prescribed enzyme replacement, is the legitimate medical use of supplemental enzymes and is entirely distinct from over-the-counter capsules taken on a hunch.
  • Scope of practice: this course builds understanding, not a license to diagnose or treat, in yourself or anyone else. Persistent reflux, suspected ulcers, the EPI pattern, or any plan involving acid-suppressing or enzyme products belongs to a qualified clinician. Work within your role, whatever it is: education and signposting toward care, never diagnosis or treatment.
Looking Ahead

You now know what is supposed to happen. Next, in Chapter 5, we ask what happens when it does not: malabsorption. When an enzyme is missing, bile is short, or the brush border is damaged, nutrients pass through undigested and unabsorbed, and the consequences show up as everything from bloating to nutrient deficiency. Keep the Enzymes Reference List open. We are about to break it on purpose, one step at a time, to see what each failure produces downstream.
