The Body's Budget: Where Your Energy Goes

Energy balance is the master equation, but "calories out" is a budget with four very different line items.

60 min read 8 cited sources

Two people sit across from each other at lunch. They are the same height, the same weight, the same age, the same sex. They order identical sandwiches. By every measure a tape and a scale could capture, they are twins. And yet, over the course of an ordinary day, one of them will burn perhaps five hundred more calories than the other, not because she runs marathons, but because she is the kind of person who bounces a heel under the table, takes the stairs without deciding to, paces while on the phone, and gestures when she talks. Her companion is a stiller creature, economical in his movements, content in his chair.

Neither of them is exercising. Neither is even aware that anything metabolically interesting is happening. But the gap between them is real, it is large, and it is invisible. To understand it, and to understand why "I worked out, so I earned this" is one of the most expensive misconceptions in all of nutrition, we need to stop thinking about "calories burned" as a single number and start thinking about it as an account with four separate ledgers.

The Master Equation

In Chapter 1 we defined the calorie as a unit of energy: the heat required to warm a gram of water by one degree Celsius, scaled up to the kilocalorie we casually call a "Calorie" on food labels. That was the currency. This chapter introduces the accounting system that currency flows through.

The system rests on a single relationship, the energy balance equation. In its barest form it says that the change in your body's energy stores equals what comes in minus what goes out:

Δ Energy stores = Energy intake − Energy expenditure

When intake exceeds expenditure, the surplus is stored, predominantly as fat, but also as glycogen and lean tissue. When expenditure exceeds intake, the body draws on those stores to cover the shortfall. When the two sides match over time, body energy is stable. This is not a theory open to dispute; it is the first law of thermodynamics applied to a warm-blooded animal. Energy is neither created nor destroyed inside you, so it has to be accounted for somewhere (Hall et al., 2012).

It is tempting to stop here, treat the body as a bank ledger, and declare nutrition solved: eat less than you burn, lose weight; the rest is willpower. That intuition is half right and dangerously incomplete. The phrase that does the damage is energy expenditure, that innocent-looking term on the right side of the equation. Most people picture it as "the calories I burn at the gym." In reality, deliberate exercise is usually the smallest of four contributors, and the other three behave in ways no ledger ever does: they shift, they respond, and they actively push back. We will spend the bulk of this chapter taking that single term apart.

Think About It

Before reading on, make a private guess: of all the energy your body spends in a typical non-exercise day, what fraction goes to simply keeping you alive, heartbeat, breathing, brain, organs, body temperature, while you do nothing at all? Hold that number in mind. Most people guess far too low.

Total Daily Energy Expenditure: The Four Line Items

The sum of everything you burn in 24 hours is called total daily energy expenditure (TDEE). Physiologists conventionally split it into four components, and learning to rank them is the single most useful intuition this chapter offers:

1. Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR), the cost of being alive

Your basal metabolic rate is the energy your body spends at complete rest, in a thermoneutral environment, fasted, just to run the machinery of staying alive: pumping blood, ventilating lungs, maintaining ion gradients across billions of cell membranes, filtering blood at the kidneys, and, expensively, running a brain that consumes roughly a fifth of resting energy despite weighing about three pounds. For most sedentary adults, BMR accounts for roughly 60 to 70 percent of total daily expenditure. It is, by a wide margin, the largest line item in the budget. If you guessed low in the callout above, you are in good company; the dominance of BMR is genuinely counterintuitive.

2. Thermic Effect of Food (TEF), the cost of digesting

Eating is not free. Breaking food down, absorbing its components, and processing them all consume energy, an effect variously called the thermic effect of food or diet-induced thermogenesis. Over a mixed diet at energy balance, TEF accounts for roughly 8 to 15 percent of daily expenditure (Westerterp, 2004). It is real but modest, and as we will see, not all calories are equally expensive to process.

3. NEAT, the cost of incidental movement

Non-exercise activity thermogenesis, or NEAT, is James Levine's term for "the energy expended for everything we do that is not sleeping, eating or sports-like exercise" (Levine, 2002). Walking to the bus, standing at the counter, fidgeting, gesturing, doing dishes, chasing a toddler, shifting your weight, all of it. NEAT is the wild card of the entire system: in some people it is a trickle, in others a torrent, and the difference between them can be enormous.

4. Exercise Activity Thermogenesis, the cost of deliberate training

Finally, the component everyone overestimates: the calories burned during purposeful, planned exercise, the run, the spin class, the weights session. For the average person who is not a competitive athlete, this is frequently the smallest wedge of the four, and on most days it is zero. Even for someone who trains regularly, an hour of moderate exercise rarely rivals what BMR quietly spends over the same day without any conscious effort at all.

Why BMR Is So Large, and Why It Varies

If basal metabolism is the biggest line item, what determines its size? The popular folklore, "some people are just born with a fast metabolism", turns out to contain a kernel of truth wrapped in a great deal of error. The single most rigorous answer comes from a cross-sectional study of 150 adults by Johnstone and colleagues (2005), who partitioned the between-person variance in BMR into its sources. Their finding is clean and worth memorizing:

  • Fat-free mass (lean mass) explained roughly 63 percent of the variation in BMR between people.
  • Fat mass explained about 6 percent.
  • Age explained roughly 2 percent.
  • Notably, sex, circulating leptin, and triiodothyronine added little once fat-free mass was accounted for.

The headline is unambiguous: lean body mass is the primary engine of basal metabolism. Muscle, organs, and other metabolically active non-fat tissue are what cost energy to maintain. This is why a larger person generally has a higher BMR than a smaller one, more tissue to run, and why men, who on average carry more lean mass than women, tend to have higher absolute BMRs. It is not a mysterious gift; it is mostly a question of how much living, non-fat machinery you are operating.

This reframing matters for two reasons. First, it explains the slow downward drift of BMR with age: as people lose lean mass over the decades (often through inactivity rather than aging per se), the metabolic engine shrinks. Second, it clarifies what resistance training does for metabolism. The direct calorie cost of a weights session is modest, but by building and preserving lean tissue, training nudges up the much larger, around-the-clock BMR line item. The effect is real but gradual, and far more about long-term tissue maintenance than about any post-workout "afterburn" the fitness magazines love.

Fat-free mass was the single best predictor of basal metabolic rate, explaining 63% of its variance… underscoring that lean tissue is the principal driver of daily energy demand.

Johnstone et al. (2005)

For the new coach, the practical translation is this: when someone asks why their friend "can eat anything," the answer is rarely a magical metabolism. It is some combination of more lean mass, a larger body, and, as we are about to see, a quietly enormous NEAT.

The Thermic Effect of Food: Not All Calories Cost the Same

Of the four components, TEF is the smallest most days, but it carries a lesson that resonates through the rest of the course: the macronutrient composition of a meal changes how much energy it costs to process. Westerterp's (2004) review establishes a clear hierarchy of diet-induced thermogenesis by nutrient:

alcohol > protein > carbohydrate > fat

Protein is metabolically expensive: somewhere around 20 to 30 percent of the calories in a protein-rich meal are spent simply handling it, assembling and disassembling amino acids, synthesizing proteins, and disposing of nitrogen waste. Carbohydrate sits in the single digits to low teens, and fat is the cheapest to store, costing only a few percent. This is why a meal heavy in protein has a higher overall thermic effect than an isocaloric meal heavy in fat.

Two cautions keep this from being oversold. First, TEF is a fraction of a fraction, shifting your protein intake will not, by itself, rewrite your energy budget. Second, and more importantly, Westerterp highlights a finding we will develop in Chapters 5 and 6: protein-induced thermogenesis is accompanied by notably enhanced satiety. The bigger story is not that protein burns more calories in the gut, but that protein-rich meals tend to make people feel full and eat less afterward. The TEF and the satiety are two faces of the same physiology, and the satiety is the part that moves the needle.

NEAT: The Body's Quiet Thermostat

Now we arrive at the component that, more than any other, separates two outwardly identical people, and that overturns the bank-ledger intuition most thoroughly. NEAT is the energy of incidental movement, and its defining feature is its astonishing variability.

How variable? Levine's work documents that NEAT can differ by as much as 2,000 kilocalories per day between two people of the same body weight (Levine et al., 2006). That is not a rounding error; it is larger than the entire BMR of many adults. The variation comes from occupation, a warehouse worker versus a software engineer, but also from something subtler and more constant: spontaneous, low-grade physical activity that the person never consciously chooses. Fidgeting. Posture changes. The decision, made below awareness, to stand rather than sit. In the same body of work, Levine found that adults with obesity spent roughly 2.5 more hours per day seated than their lean counterparts, and estimated that adopting a more active "NEAT-o-type" posture pattern could expend on the order of an additional 350 kcal/day.

The Overfeeding Experiment That Made NEAT Famous

The most striking demonstration of NEAT's power is a landmark 1999 study published in Science (Levine et al., 1999). The design was elegantly brutal: sixteen non-obese volunteers were deliberately overfed by 1,000 kilocalories per day for eight weeks, a large, sustained surplus. On a simple ledger model, everyone should have stored roughly the same excess as fat. They did not.

The fat gained ranged across a tenfold spread between individuals. What explained the difference? Not BMR, and not the thermic effect of all that extra food. The dominant factor was NEAT. Of the total increase in daily energy expenditure under overfeeding, about two-thirds was attributable to increased non-exercise movement, the body, in effect, "fidgeting away" a chunk of the surplus without anyone deciding to. Changes in NEAT predicted resistance to fat gain with a correlation of r = 0.77, a remarkably strong relationship in human physiology. The authors concluded that NEAT activation is a mechanism the body uses to dissipate excess energy and preserve leanness.

Two-thirds of the increase in total daily energy expenditure was accounted for by an increase in nonexercise activity thermogenesis… changes in NEAT predicted resistance to fat gain with overeating.

Levine, Eberhardt, & Jensen (1999)

Sit with what this means. The right side of the energy balance equation is not a passive constant you set with your gym schedule. It is partly an automatic, unconscious response to the left side. Eat a large surplus, and in many people the body quietly turns up the dial on incidental movement. This is our first concrete encounter with a theme that will recur throughout the course: intake and expenditure are not independent. They are coupled by an active, defending physiology.

A systematic review by Chung and colleagues (2018) confirms the broader pattern: NEAT is the main variable component of total daily energy expenditure, swinging widely both within and between individuals. The review also makes a point relevant to most non-athletes: for people who do little structured exercise, the majority, almost all of their physical-activity energy expenditure is NEAT. The gym is not where their movement calories come from. The grocery run, the stairs, the standing, the pacing, that is the real engine.

The Exercise Component: Smaller Than You Think

We can now confront the misconception named at the start of the chapter directly. The cultural script says: exercise is how you burn calories; the harder the workout, the more you "earn." The physiology says something humbler.

A brisk 30-minute walk burns on the order of 120 to 180 kilocalories for a typical adult. A vigorous spin class might reach 400 to 500. These are real and worthwhile, but compare them to the BMR line item, which for the same person quietly spends 1,500 to 1,800 kilocalories over the day with no effort whatsoever. The exercise wedge, even on a hard day, is usually dwarfed by the cost of merely being alive.

This matters most when exercise is treated as a license to eat. The arithmetic is unforgiving. A single muffin can carry 400 to 500 kilocalories; a large flavored coffee or a "healthy" smoothie can match or exceed it. It is entirely ordinary for the post-workout reward to contain more energy than the workout expended, meaning the session that felt like progress actually ended the day in surplus. This is the "I exercised, so I earned this" trap, and it is mathematical, not moral. Intake is dense and fast; the exercise component of expenditure is modest and slow.

Estimate, in your head, how many minutes of brisk walking it would take to "burn off" a 480-kcal muffin. Now ask how long it took to eat the muffin. The ratio between those two numbers is the whole lesson.

The System Pushes Back: Why Expenditure Is Dynamic

We have now seen two ways the right side of the equation refuses to behave like a fixed number: NEAT swings unconsciously, and overfeeding can trigger compensatory movement. But the deepest challenge to the ledger model comes from research on what happens at the other end, when people exercise more and more.

The naive, additive model says: add exercise, and your TDEE rises by exactly the calories that exercise costs, every time. Pontzer and colleagues (2016) tested this directly. Using doubly labeled water, the gold-standard method for measuring real-world energy expenditure, and accelerometry across 332 adults from five populations, they found something the additive model does not predict. At low levels of physical activity, total energy expenditure did rise with more movement, as expected. But at higher activity levels, TDEE plateaued: the body appeared to adapt, holding total expenditure within a relatively narrow band rather than letting it climb without limit.

This is the constrained total energy expenditure model. Its mechanism is thought to involve the body trimming energy spent elsewhere, quietly reducing NEAT, immune activity, reproductive function, and other "background" costs, to make room for the energy demanded by heavy training. The body, in other words, does not simply stack exercise calories on top of everything else. It negotiates.

The implications are sobering for anyone who assumed that more exercise mechanically equals more total burn. Beyond a point, it may not. Pontzer's findings directly challenge the additive assumptions baked into many obesity-prevention guidelines, which estimate weight loss by simply adding up the calories a prescribed exercise program "should" burn. The real system compensates.

Total energy expenditure increases with physical activity at low activity levels but plateaus at higher levels… consistent with a constrained model in which the body adapts to maintain expenditure within a narrow range.

Pontzer et al. (2016)

Put NEAT compensation, overfeeding-induced thermogenesis, and constrained expenditure together, and a picture emerges that the bank-ledger metaphor cannot hold. Energy balance is a dynamic, self-adjusting system. The two sides of the equation are linked by feedback: change one, and the body often quietly nudges the other. This is exactly the warning the Hall et al. (2012) consensus review sounds, that we must understand how the components of energy balance interact over time, rather than treating intake and expenditure as fixed, independent quantities (Hall et al., 2012).

None of this repeals the first law of thermodynamics. Energy is still conserved; surpluses are still stored and deficits still drawn down. The point is more subtle and more important: the numbers on the expenditure side are not constants you control by spreadsheet. They are outputs of a living, defending system. We will pick up that thread of active defense, and what it means for the difficulty of sustained weight loss, in earnest in Chapter 6.

Putting the Budget Back Together

Return now to the two diners from the opening, same size, same lunch, different days' totals. We can finally explain them precisely. Their BMRs are similar (similar lean mass), and their TEF is identical (same sandwich). The gap is almost entirely NEAT: one is a high-NEAT phenotype, burning hundreds of extra calories through movement she never schedules or notices, exactly as Levine's work would predict. If you watched only their gym memberships, you would learn nothing about this difference. It lives in the un-exercised hours of an ordinary day.

For the aspiring coach, three practical lessons follow. First, respect BMR: it is the largest line item and the one lean mass protects, which is why preserving muscle through adequate protein and resistance training matters far more for long-term metabolism than chasing post-workout "afterburn." Second, take NEAT seriously as a lever, encouraging more standing, walking, and incidental movement throughout the day can rival or exceed the impact of a structured workout, and it is the one component most people can expand. Third, retire "I earned it" math: a single session rarely offsets a dense reward, and the body may compensate for the session anyway. The honest message is that what you eat, the left side of the equation, is usually the more powerful lever, precisely because the right side defends itself.

You can now write the energy balance equation, name and rank its four expenditure components, and explain why the system is dynamic rather than ledger-like. That framework is the spine of everything ahead: the macronutrients of Chapter 3 supply the "in," food quality and satiety in Chapters 5 and 6 act on both sides at once, and the habit-building of Chapter 8 is, at bottom, a strategy for working with a self-adjusting system rather than against it.

In Practice

Here is where people get this wrong: they credit exercise with most of the calories they burn, so they eat a big reward after a workout and wonder why the scale will not move. The math rarely works out. For most people the deliberate-exercise slice is small next to resting metabolism and everyday movement, and a single meal can wipe out an hour of effort.

So do not try to out-train the fork. Use training to build and protect muscle, and let resting metabolism, daily movement, and food choices do the real work on the energy budget.

Key Takeaways

  • Energy balance is the master equation: change in body energy stores equals intake minus expenditure, an application of the first law of thermodynamics, not an opinion.
  • "Calories out" (total daily energy expenditure) has four components: Basal Metabolic Rate, the Thermic Effect of Food, NEAT, and deliberate exercise.
  • BMR is the largest line item (~60–70% of TDEE) and is driven primarily by lean mass, fat-free mass explained ~63% of BMR variance between people (Johnstone et al., 2005).
  • TEF is modest (~8–15%) but varies by macronutrient (alcohol > protein > carbohydrate > fat); protein's bigger contribution is enhanced satiety (Westerterp, 2004).
  • NEAT is the great variable: it can differ by up to ~2,000 kcal/day between same-weight people, and accounted for two-thirds of the rise in expenditure under overfeeding, predicting resistance to fat gain (r = 0.77) (Levine et al., 1999, 2006).
  • Deliberate exercise is usually the smallest component and rarely "cancels" a dense reward food, intake outpaces a single workout easily.
  • Expenditure is dynamic and self-adjusting: at high activity levels, total expenditure plateaus as the body compensates (Pontzer et al., 2016). Intake and expenditure are coupled, not independent (Hall et al., 2012).
Looking Ahead

If this chapter dissected the "out" side of the equation, Chapter 3 turns to the "in": the macronutrients, carbohydrate, fat, and protein, that supply every calorie you spend. We will see why a gram of fat carries more than twice the energy of a gram of carbohydrate, how each macronutrient is actually used by the body, and why "a calorie is a calorie" is true thermodynamically yet incomplete physiologically.
