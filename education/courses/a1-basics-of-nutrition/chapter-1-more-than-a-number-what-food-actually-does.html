More Than a Number: What Food Actually Does

Why every bite is two things at once, fuel to burn and substance to become.

60 min read 9 cited sources

Picture the back of a cereal box. Somewhere in the small print sits a number, 240, maybe, beside the word "Calories." For most people that number is the whole story of food: a quantity to be counted, budgeted, feared, or burned off on a treadmill. It is the most famous figure in all of nutrition, and it is also one of the most misleading, because it tells you only one of the two things that food does to your body, and arguably not the more interesting one.

Here is the reframing that this entire course is built on. The almond you just ate is not merely a packet of energy. Part of it will indeed be oxidized, broken down and burned to keep your heart beating and your neurons firing. But another part of it will be incorporated: its fatty acids slotted into the membranes of your cells, its protein dismantled into amino acids and reassembled into your own tissue, its minerals deposited into bone. The almond does not just power you. In a slow, literal sense, it becomes you. Hold that dual identity in your mind. Once you see food this way, almost every confusion in nutrition starts to dissolve.

What nutrition is, as a science

Nutrition is the scientific study of how organisms obtain and use the substances in food to sustain life, to release energy, to build and repair structure, and to regulate the thousands of chemical processes that keep a body running. It sits at the intersection of biochemistry, physiology, and public health, and unlike many sciences it has an unusually precise birthday. We can date the modern discipline to the laboratory of Antoine Lavoisier in the 1780s, who showed that the slow combustion of food inside an animal and the rapid combustion of a candle were fundamentally the same chemistry: both consume oxygen, both release carbon dioxide and heat (Carpenter, 2003a). With that single insight, that respiration is combustion, the metaphor "food as fuel" stopped being a metaphor and became a measurable, thermodynamic fact.

That history matters because it explains a quirk we will meet repeatedly: nutrition borrowed many of its core ideas from physics and chemistry, not from biology. The calorie is a unit of heat. Energy balance is an accounting identity from thermodynamics. The discipline grew up speaking the language of engines and furnaces, and only later learned to speak the language of cells and tissues. Keeping both languages in view, the engineering language of fuel and the biological language of building material, is exactly the skill this chapter is trying to install.

Why we need an outside supply at all

It is worth pausing on a question so basic it usually goes unasked: why must we eat? A car needs refueling because it cannot manufacture gasoline. A body needs feeding for two analogous but distinct reasons. First, the laws of thermodynamics are unforgiving, living systems are highly ordered, and maintaining that order against the universe's relentless drift toward disorder costs energy continuously. We cannot create energy; we can only capture it from chemical bonds in food and convert it into the forms our cells use. Second, and this is the part the fuel metaphor hides, the body cannot synthesize all of its own raw materials. There are essential nutrients, certain amino acids, two fatty acids, every vitamin and mineral, that human biochemistry simply cannot build from scratch. They must arrive, pre-made, from the outside. A diet that supplied abundant energy but lacked these materials would let you burn brightly for a while and then fall apart structurally, which is precisely what deficiency diseases are.

The calorie: a unit of heat, borrowed

We use the word "calorie" so casually that its strangeness is invisible. We talk about the calories in an apple the way we talk about the price of an apple, as though it were a property of the apple itself. But a calorie is not a property of food at all. It is a unit of heat: formally, the energy needed to raise the temperature of one gram of water by one degree Celsius. It describes what happens to water, not what happens to you. When we say an almond "contains" 7 calories, what we really mean is that burning that almond completely would release enough heat to warm a specified amount of water by a specified amount. The number is a measurement of combustion, not of nourishment.

This is not a pedantic aside, it is the literal history of the unit. The calorie was coined in the 1820s by the French engineer Nicolas Clément, in lectures on the efficiency of steam engines. It entered English through a physics textbook in 1863 and was sitting in dictionaries as a unit of heat decades before anyone applied it to food (Hargrove, 2006). The transfer to nutrition came in 1887, when the American chemist Wilbur Atwater, who had trained in the German tradition of bomb calorimetry, borrowed the unit wholesale to describe food energy, and even capitalized it ("Calorie") to signal the larger kilocalorie quantity used on labels (Hargrove, 2006; Hargrove, 2007). The "Calorie" you count is really a kilocalorie: a thousand of the physicist's small calories. The capital-C convention has caused a century of confusion, and some nutrition scientists still argue we should have adopted the SI joule instead (Hargrove, 2007).

How the number gets made: the bomb calorimeter

To measure the energy in a food, a chemist seals a dried, weighed sample inside a sturdy steel vessel, the "bomb", surrounded by a known mass of water. The chamber is filled with pressurized oxygen and the sample is ignited electrically. It burns completely and almost instantly, dumping all its chemical energy into the surrounding water as heat. By measuring how much the water's temperature rises, and knowing the water's mass, you can read off the energy released directly in calories. This is where the canonical numbers come from. Per gram, carbohydrate and protein each release roughly 4 kilocalories; fat releases about 9, more than double, because its carbon-hydrogen bonds are more reduced and yield more energy on oxidation (Carpenter, 2003b).

But Atwater understood something the raw calorimeter cannot: the body is a leaky engine. We do not extract every joule. Protein, in particular, is not burned cleanly in us, we excrete nitrogen as urea, carrying energy out with it, so the body's usable, or metabolizable energy, from protein is lower than its bomb value. Atwater's enduring contribution was the system of corrected factors (the familiar 4-4-9) that estimate physiological energy yield rather than total combustion heat (Carpenter, 2003b). When you read "4 kcal per gram of protein," you are reading an Atwater factor, a number adjusted for the fact that a body is not a furnace.

Fuel and building material: the dual identity

Now we arrive at the conceptual spine of the course. Every food you eat is doing two jobs at once, in proportions that differ from food to food. Some of what you swallow gets oxidized, broken down to release energy that powers everything from muscle contraction to the active pumping of ions across nerve membranes. The rest gets assimilated, chemically rebuilt into the physical substance of your body. The protein in a chicken breast can be torn apart for fuel when energy is scarce, but under ordinary conditions much of it is dismantled into amino acids and reassembled into your muscle, enzymes, antibodies, and skin. This recognition, that food proteins are structural raw materials and not merely fuel, was itself a hard-won discovery of late-nineteenth-century nutrition science, arriving alongside Atwater's energy work (Carpenter, 2003b).

The split is not fixed; it is a budget that the body adjusts according to need. After resistance exercise, dietary amino acids are preferentially routed toward building tissue. During a fast, structural protein is increasingly diverted to fuel. But as a first approximation, it helps to ask of any food: is this mostly something I will burn, or mostly something I will become? Pure fats and sugars lean heavily toward fuel, they carry little structural value and a great deal of energy. Protein-rich and mineral-rich foods lean toward building material. Body composition: why the scale lies

If food is partly building material, then what we are building deserves attention. The bathroom scale reports a single number, total mass, and treats a kilogram of muscle as identical to a kilogram of fat. Physiologically they could hardly be more different. Body composition is the distinction the scale erases: the partition of body mass into fat mass (energy stored in adipose tissue) and lean mass (muscle, bone, organs, and water, the metabolically active, structural you).

Two people can weigh exactly the same and be metabolically worlds apart: one carrying substantial muscle and modest fat, the other the reverse. Lean tissue is expensive to maintain, it burns energy around the clock, while fat is largely storage. This is why two diets that deliver identical calories can produce very different bodies depending on what materials they supply and what activity demands them. It is also why "weight loss" is a crude goal. Losing a kilogram of fat and losing a kilogram of muscle both move the scale the same distance downward, yet one outcome is broadly desirable and the other is not. Throughout this course we will favour the language of body composition over the language of weight, because composition is where food's building-block role becomes visible on a human scale.

The four domains: where nutrition ripples outward

Once we accept that food is both fuel and material, its consequences spread predictably into four broad domains of lived experience. These four, energy, health, body composition, and mood and cognition, organize the rest of the course. Three of them connect directly to sensations you have already felt.

1. Energy: the rhythm of the day

The most immediate domain is the one you experience by mid-afternoon. The 3 p.m. slump, the fog after a skipped breakfast, the heavy drowsiness following a large refined-carbohydrate lunch, these are not character flaws or signs of laziness. They are the surface expression of fuel supply and blood-glucose dynamics. When fuel availability to the brain drops or swings sharply, alertness and concentration follow it down. Energy is the domain where nutrition's fuel role is felt most directly and most quickly, sometimes within an hour of eating.

2. Health: the slow accumulation

The second domain operates over years and decades rather than hours, and its stakes are enormous. The Global Burden of Disease study, the largest comparative risk assessment ever conducted, estimated that suboptimal diet was responsible for roughly 11 million deaths and 255 million disability-adjusted life-years in 2017, ranking diet among the leading modifiable causes of non-communicable disease worldwide (Afshin et al., 2019). Strikingly, the largest dietary risks were not the usual villains of excess but patterns of deficiency and imbalance: too much sodium, too few whole grains, too little fruit (Afshin et al., 2019). This is the building-material role written at the scale of populations, chronic disease as the long-term consequence of supplying the body with the wrong raw materials, day after day.

3. Body composition: the visible architecture

The third domain we have already met. Over months and years, the balance of fuel intake against demand, and the supply of building materials such as protein, sculpts the fat-to-lean ratio of the body. Composition is the slow, structural ledger of the dual identity, energy surplus stored as fat, building blocks deployed as lean tissue when activity calls for them.

4. Mood and cognition: food for thought

The fourth domain was, until recently, the most neglected, and is now among the most exciting frontiers in the field. The "hangry" state is the everyday face of it: irritability and short temper that track falling blood sugar. But the connection runs far deeper than transient mood swings. The emerging field of nutritional psychiatry assembles observational and interventional evidence that diet quality is a modifiable risk factor for depression and other mental disorders across countries and age groups (Jacka, 2017; Marx et al., 2017). Proposed mechanisms are biological and specific: the gut-brain axis, neuroinflammation, and the brain's capacity for plasticity (Jacka, 2017; Spencer et al., 2017). Caloric intake and diet composition exert large and lasting effects on cognition and emotion, especially during critical developmental windows, with dietary fats and omega-3 balance implicated in particular (Spencer et al., 2017). The newest syntheses in nutritional cognitive neuroscience trace how specific nutrients, B-vitamins, omega-3 fatty acids, polyphenols, shape brain structure and function (Wu & Barbey, 2026). This matters all the more given that current pharmacological and psychological treatments address fewer than half of the global mental-health burden, which is exactly why researchers see nutrition as an underused lever (Marx et al., 2017).

A note of scientific honesty is owed here. Much of the mood-and-cognition evidence is observational, and observational data cannot by themselves prove that food causes changes in mood, people who eat well differ from those who don't in many ways. The field's strength comes from the convergence of mechanism, observation, and a growing handful of intervention trials, not from any single decisive study (Marx et al., 2017; Jacka, 2017). We will hold this domain with appropriate care: promising, mechanistically plausible, and not yet as settled as the energy and health domains.

A worked example: two breakfasts, same calories

Let us put the whole framework to work. Imagine two breakfasts, each delivering about 350 kilocalories. Breakfast A is a large glass of sweetened fruit juice and a plain bagel. Breakfast B is two eggs, a slice of whole-grain toast, and a handful of almonds. By the calorie-counting view, these are interchangeable, same number on the box. By the framework of this chapter, they are nearly opposites.

Fuel role: Both supply energy, but A is almost entirely rapidly-absorbed fuel, prone to a sharp blood-glucose rise and the rebound dip that shows up as a mid-morning slump (energy domain). B releases its fuel more gradually because protein, fat, and fibre slow digestion. Building-block role: A contributes almost no structural material, little protein, few essential fats, modest micronutrients. B delivers high-quality protein for tissue building, essential fatty acids for cell membranes, and a fuller spread of vitamins and minerals (body-composition and health domains). Mood: the steadier fuel curve and richer nutrient supply of B align with what nutritional-psychiatry research associates with more stable mood and cognition (mood domain) (Spencer et al., 2017; Jacka, 2017).

Same number. Four domains' worth of difference. This is what we mean when we say food is more than a number, and it is the reasoning you will deploy in every chapter that follows.

In Practice

Here is where a lot of people get this wrong: they think eating well means eating less. It does not. It means giving the body enough of the right material to actually run and rebuild. That is exactly why a person can eat almost nothing, feel terrible, lose muscle, and end up softer at a lighter bodyweight. They cut the fuel and starved the building material at the same time, while only ever watching the calorie number.

So the practical move is to ask two questions of a meal, not one. Not just "how many calories," but "what is this actually giving the body to work with." That single shift does more for how someone eats than any diet rule.

Key Takeaways

  • Nutrition is the science of how the body obtains and uses food substances for energy, structure, and regulation, a discipline born from Lavoisier's discovery that respiration is combustion.
  • A calorie is a borrowed unit of heat (the energy to warm one gram of water by 1°C), coined for steam engines and adapted to food by Atwater in 1887; the food "Calorie" is actually a kilocalorie.
  • Bomb-calorimeter values give roughly 4, 4, and 9 kcal/g for carbohydrate, protein, and fat; Atwater's corrected factors account for the fact that the body, unlike a furnace, does not extract every joule.
  • Every food plays two roles at once, fuel to be oxidized and building material to be assimilated, in proportions that differ by food and that the body adjusts to need.
  • Body composition (fat mass vs. lean mass) captures the structural consequence of food and matters far more than the single number on a scale.
  • Nutrition ripples into four domains: energy (felt in hours), health (over decades; ~11 million deaths from poor diet in 2017), body composition (over months), and mood and cognition (emerging, mechanistically plausible, not yet fully settled).
Looking Ahead

If food is fuel, the obvious next question is one of accounting: what happens when fuel coming in does not match fuel going out? Chapter 2 takes up energy balance, the thermodynamic ledger behind weight change, and shows why this seemingly simple equation is far subtler in a living body than it is in a calorimeter.
