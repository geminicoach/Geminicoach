GLP-1 Medications Education

GLP-1 Medications Explained:
What They Are and How They Work

April 2026 8 min read Education Series
Featured Image

GLP-1 receptor agonists have moved rapidly from specialized diabetes treatments into mainstream conversations about weight management, metabolic health, and physique development. Before forming any opinion about whether these medications belong in a health strategy, it helps to start with the actual biology — not the headlines.

What Is GLP-1?

GLP-1 stands for glucagon-like peptide-1. It is an incretin hormone — a type of hormone produced in the small intestine in response to eating. Specifically, it is secreted by L-cells in the gut lining when nutrients, particularly carbohydrates and fats, are detected after a meal.

Under normal physiological conditions, GLP-1 is produced naturally every time you eat. It plays a direct role in regulating blood sugar, modulating appetite, and coordinating the body's response to a meal. In people with type 2 diabetes or significant insulin resistance, this signaling is often impaired or insufficient — which is part of where the medications come in.

"GLP-1 is not a foreign substance — it is a hormone your body already produces every time you eat. GLP-1 medications work within an existing biological system, amplifying signals that are already there."

How GLP-1 Medications Work

GLP-1 receptor agonists bind to the same receptors in the body that natural GLP-1 binds to, but they are engineered to be significantly more stable and long-lasting. Natural GLP-1 has a biological half-life of roughly two minutes — it is produced, acts briefly, and is then degraded almost immediately by an enzyme called DPP-4. Pharmaceutical formulations have been designed to resist that degradation, allowing them to remain active for days or, in the case of weekly injections, up to seven days.

When these medications bind to GLP-1 receptors throughout the body, they trigger several coordinated effects that work together to influence blood sugar regulation and appetite — which is what makes them clinically meaningful beyond simply suppressing hunger.

The Three Core Effects

GLP-1 medications don't work through a single mechanism. They produce effects across multiple systems simultaneously, which is why their clinical impact spans both diabetes management and weight reduction.

1. Appetite and Satiety Signaling

GLP-1 receptors exist in the hypothalamus and other brain regions involved in hunger regulation. When activated, they send signals that reduce appetite and increase feelings of fullness. Most people on these medications report eating significantly less — not because they are forcing themselves to, but because the neurological drive to eat is genuinely reduced. This is meaningfully different from willpower-based dietary restriction.

2. Insulin Secretion and Blood Sugar Regulation

GLP-1 stimulates the pancreas to release insulin in response to elevated blood glucose, and simultaneously suppresses glucagon — the hormone responsible for raising blood sugar. Critically, this response is glucose-dependent, meaning insulin release is triggered by actual elevated blood sugar rather than a constant pharmacological signal. This reduces the risk of hypoglycemia compared to some other diabetes medications.

3. Gastric Emptying

GLP-1 medications slow the rate at which food moves from the stomach into the small intestine. This contributes to prolonged feelings of fullness after meals and blunts post-meal blood sugar spikes. It is also responsible for some of the gastrointestinal side effects — nausea in particular — that some people experience early in treatment.

Current Medications on the Market

There are several GLP-1 receptor agonists currently available, with different formulations, dosing schedules, and approved indications.

Semaglutide (brand names Ozempic and Wegovy) is perhaps the most widely discussed. Ozempic was originally approved for type 2 diabetes management, while Wegovy is the higher-dose formulation approved specifically for chronic weight management. Both are weekly subcutaneous injections.

Tirzepatide (Mounjaro for diabetes, Zepbound for weight management) is technically a dual agonist — it targets both GLP-1 receptors and GIP receptors simultaneously. Clinical trials have shown it produces somewhat greater average weight loss than semaglutide alone, though the mechanisms are closely related.

Liraglutide (Saxenda, Victoza) was an earlier-generation daily injectable GLP-1 agonist. It remains in use but has largely been superseded by the convenience of weekly formulations.

What to Realistically Expect

Clinical trials for semaglutide at weight management doses have shown average weight reductions of 15–20% of body weight over 68 weeks in people with obesity — significantly higher than previously available medications. Tirzepatide trials have shown reductions approaching 22% in certain populations.

However, averages don't tell the full individual story. Response rates vary considerably based on starting weight, metabolic health, adherence, and — critically — lifestyle factors. Some people experience dramatic results. Others see more modest outcomes. A meaningful minority are non-responders. Side effects, most commonly nausea and gastrointestinal discomfort, are most pronounced early in treatment and typically diminish as the body adjusts over weeks to months.

"Average trial results are important context, but they are averages. Individual response varies considerably — and lifestyle factors consistently separate the best outcomes from average ones."

The Bottom Line

GLP-1 medications are powerful, legitimate pharmacological tools with well-documented clinical outcomes for specific populations. Understanding the biology behind them — what they actually do and through which mechanisms — is the foundation for evaluating whether they are appropriate for a given situation.
